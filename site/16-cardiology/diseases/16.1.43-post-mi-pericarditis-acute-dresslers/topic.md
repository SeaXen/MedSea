# Post-MI Pericarditis

## 1. Definition
**Pericarditis after MI** has two distinct forms: (1) **Early / peri-infarction pericarditis** (within days) from transmural necrosis-induced inflammation; (2) **Dressler's syndrome** (weeks-months later) from autoimmune mechanism.

## 2. Key Features
- **Early peri-infarction pericarditis** (1–3 days post-MI):
  - Transmural MI → inflammation of adjacent pericardium
  - Pleuritic chest pain, pericardial rub (~10–20%)
  - ECG: persistent ST↑, may have PR depression
  - Treatment: **high-dose aspirin** (avoid NSAIDs/steroids — impair healing, ↑ risk of LV rupture/anastomotic leak)
- **Dressler syndrome** (2–10 weeks post-MI):
  - Autoimmune; serum sickness-like
  - Fever, pleuritic chest pain, pericardial effusion, ↑ESR, leukocytosis
  - Risk ↓ with reperfusion (rare in PCI era)
  - Treatment: **NSAIDs (ibuprofen) ± colchicine**; steroids if refractory
- **Differential**: recurrent ischaemia, pulmonary embolism, aortic dissection

## 3. Management
- **Early**: aspirin 750–1000 mg q6h, taper over 1–2 weeks; **avoid** indomethacin/steroids
- **Dressler**: NSAIDs + colchicine; rarely steroids
- **Large effusion/tamponade**: pericardiocentesis (rare)
- Anticoagulation: cautious if effusion (risk of haemopericardium)
- **Recurrent Dressler**: may need pericardial window

> **Pearls**
> - **Early post-MI pericarditis → high-dose aspirin** (avoid NSAIDs/steroids)
> - **Dressler** = weeks later, autoimmune, treat with NSAIDs
> - **Persistent ST↑ post-MI** may be pericarditis, not re-infarction
> - Dressler is **rare** in the reperfusion era
