# P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's

> [!info] **Topic Classification**
> **Section:** 05_Valvular_Heart_Disease | **Category:** Cardiology

---

## 1. HIGH-YIELD SUMMARY (30-Second Review)

**P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's** (Mitral Valve Prolapse, MVP): systolic displacement of one or both MV leaflets >2mm beyond the mitral annular plane into the LA in **parasternal long-axis view** (echocardiographic). **Primary** (Barlow disease, fibroelastic deficiency, Marfan) vs **secondary** (chordal rupture, IE, rheumatic). **Prevalence**: 2-3% (population). **Most common cause of primary MR in developed countries**. **Mid-systolic click** and **late systolic murmur** is the classic finding.


## 2. ETIOLOGY & PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Barlow disease**: diffuse leaflet thickening, redundancy, myxomatous degeneration, chordal elongation, annular dilation, multi-segment prolapse. **Fibroelastic deficiency**: focal prolapse, thin leaflets, chordal rupture (often P2). **Marfan** (FBN1): myxomatous, redundancy. **Ehlers-Danlos**. **Loeys-Dietz**. **Pathology**: myxomatous degeneration, disruption of fibrosa, proteoglycan accumulation, collagen type III excess.


## 3. CLINICAL FEATURES

**Often asymptomatic** (incidental echo). **Symptoms**: palpitations, atypical chest pain, dyspnoea, fatigue, syncope, panic/anxiety. **MVP syndrome**: autonomic dysfunction, panic attacks. **Complications**: MR progression (10-15% severe MR), IE (mitral regurgitant jet), sudden death (rare, <1%/year, often with severe MR or LV dysfunction), chordal rupture (acute MR, 10-20%/year in Barlow), TIA/stroke (FND, debated, less than previously thought). **Examination**: **mid-systolic click** (chordae tendineae sudden tension, after S1), **late systolic murmur** (MR), **earlier with Valsalva** (↓LV size → earlier prolapse, murmur starts earlier, louder). **Auscultation**: standing, left lateral decubitus.


## 4. DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH

**TTE**: prolapse >2mm beyond annular plane (parasternal long-axis), MR severity, LV function, leaflet thickening (>5mm = Barlow, <5mm = fibroelastic), chordal rupture, flail, annular calcification. **3D echo** for surgical planning. **TOE** for repair planning. **CMR** (if echo limited, MR quantitation). **ECG**: usually normal, may have biphasic T V4-V6, AF (late). **CXR**: usually normal. **Genetic testing** (Marfan, etc.).


## 5. SEVERITY ASSESSMENT & RISK STRATIFICATION

**Mild**: prolapse, no MR, asymptomatic. **Moderate**: MR moderate. **Severe**: MR severe, LV dilation/dysfunction, AF, PASP >50 mmHg — surgery. **Complications**: chordal rupture (acute MR, 10-20%/year in Barlow). **SCD risk** very low (1-2%/year with severe MR or LV dysfunction). **IE** 1-3% (not as high as previously thought).


## 6. MANAGEMENT ALGORITHM

**Asymptomatic, mild MR**: monitor (echo every 3-5y), reassurance, lifestyle (avoid dehydration in autonomic dysfunction). **Symptomatic MR**: surgery. **Severe MR with symptoms, LVEF 30-60%, LVESD ≥40mm, AF, PASP >50 mmHg**: MV repair (preferred, >95% success, especially posterior P2, durability >20y). **Replacement** if not repairable. **Beta-blocker** for palpitations, atypical chest pain, anxiety. **Avoid** decongestants, caffeine (palpitations). **IE prophylaxis**: NOT routinely recommended (only prior IE, prosthetic valve, unrepaired cyanotic CHD, transplant valvulopathy). **Stroke**: not enough evidence for anticoagulation if no AF. **Exercise**: most can participate; restriction if severe MR, LV dysfunction, family history SCD.


## 7. COMPLICATIONS & PROGNOSIS

**MR progression** (10-15% severe MR by 10y). **Chordal rupture** (acute MR). **IE** (1-3%). **Stroke** (debated, low risk). **SCD** (very rare, <1-2%/year, mostly with severe MR). **AF** (late, 5-10%). **Anxiety, panic disorder, autonomic dysfunction** (MVP syndrome). **Reduced QoL**.


## 8. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

**Marfan**: 25% have MVP, monitor for aortic root disease, avoid strenuous exercise if root >45mm. **Ehlers-Danlos**: MVP, monitor. **Pregnancy**: severe MR symptomatic = contraindication, repair before pregnancy. **Children**: usually asymptomatic, monitor. **Elderly**: chordal rupture common (P2).


## 9. LATEST GUIDELINES & EVIDENCE

**ESC 2021 VHD (EHJ)**: MVP, MR. **2020/2024 ACC/AHA VHD**: similar. **AHA 2007 IE prophylaxis**: not routine for MVP. **NICE CG64 (2008)**: prophylaxis.


## 10. CONFUSIONS & COMMON PITFALLS

**MVP is overdiagnosed** — old M-mode criteria, multiple views. Use parasternal long-axis. **SCD risk** is very low. **IE prophylaxis** is NOT routine. **Stroke** no clear association. **MVP syndrome** controversial. **Repair** preferred over replacement (95% success for posterior). **3D echo** for repair planning. **Flail** = chordal rupture, urgent surgery if severe MR.


## 11. MNEMONICS & MEMORY AIDS

**MVP**: leaflet >2mm beyond annulus, mid-systolic click, late systolic murmur, Valsalva earlier. **Barlow**: diffuse, redundant, thick (>5mm). **Fibroelastic deficiency**: focal, thin, chordal rupture. **P2**: most common prolapse. **Repair >95% success posterior, 80% anterior**. **IE prophylaxis not routine**. **SCD** very rare.


## 12. DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

HCM (ejection systolic, LLSB, Valsalva louder), MR from other causes, papillary muscle dysfunction, chordal rupture (acute), MS, ASD, anxiety/panic, TIA, papillary muscle fibroma.


## 13. INVESTIGATIONS

TTE (essential, serial, 3D for repair planning), TOE (procedural), CMR (quantitative, if echo limited), ECG, CXR, BNP, exercise echo, coronary angio (pre-surgery), genetic testing (Marfan, connective tissue).

## 14. EXAM DRILLS

### 14.1 MCQs

**Q1.** What is the most common aetiology of P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's?
A. Genetic predisposition
B. Acquired disease
C. Mixed aetiology
D. Idiopathic
E. Familial

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Most cases are acquired with genetic/environmental interaction. Recognise risk factors, presentation, and management.

**Q2.** First-line investigation for P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's:
A. ECG
B. Echocardiography
C. Cardiac MRI
D. Coronary angiography
E. Cardiac biomarkers

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Echo is first-line for structural/functional assessment; additional tests as indicated.

**Q3.** Most appropriate first-line therapy for P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's:
A. Lifestyle modification
B. Pharmacological therapy
C. Device therapy
D. Surgical intervention
E. Multidisciplinary care

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Guideline-directed medical therapy is cornerstone; other modalities as indicated.

**Q4.** Which is a contraindication to therapy in P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's?
A. Renal dysfunction
B. Hepatic dysfunction
C. Drug interactions
D. Patient preference
E. All of the above

> **Answer:** E
> **Explanation:** All must be considered. Individualise therapy.

**Q5.** The most common complication of P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's is:
A. Heart failure
B. Arrhythmia
C. Thromboembolism
D. Death
E. Quality of life reduction

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Arrhythmia common; severity determines outcome.

### 14.2 SBAs

**SBA1.** A 55-year-old presents with features of P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's. Most appropriate next step?
A. Discharge
B. Outpatient follow-up
C. Investigation
D. Empirical treatment
E. Multidisciplinary referral

> **Answer:** C
> **Rationale:** Confirm diagnosis before treatment.

**SBA2.** Patient with P2 flail, chordal rupture, Barlow's on optimal therapy develops new symptoms. What is the next step?
A. Add another medication
B. Refer for invasive assessment
C. Reassess diagnosis
D. Multidisciplinary team discussion
E. All of the above

> **Answer:** E
> **Rationale:** Comprehensive reassessment with MDT input.
