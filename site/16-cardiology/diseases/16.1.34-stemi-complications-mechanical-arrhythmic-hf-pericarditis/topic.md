# STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis

> [!info] **Topic Classification**
> **Section:** 02_Acute_Coronary_Syndromes | **Category:** Cardiology

---

## 1. HIGH-YIELD SUMMARY (30-Second Review)

**STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis**: acute pericarditis — inflammation of the pericardium with characteristic chest pain, pericardial friction rub, ECG changes (diffuse concave STE, PR depression), and pericardial effusion (variable). **Aetiology** (idiopathic 80-90% presumed viral, infection viral/bacterial/fungal, autoimmune — post-MI early/Dressler, RA, SLE, SSc, Sjögren, sarcoid, IBD; uraemia, post-cardiac injury — post-MI, post-cardiac surgery, post-PCI, ablation; malignancy, radiation, drug — hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid, methyldopa, minoxidil, amiodarone; trauma, aortic dissection, hypothyroidism, post-vaccination).


## 2. ETIOLOGY & PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Inflammation** of pericardium → ↑vascular permeability → **fibrin deposition** (fibrinous pericarditis, bread and butter appearance) → **fluid accumulation** (serous, sanguineous, purulent, chylous) → **constriction** (chronic, fibrotic, calcific). **Effusion rate** >production rate → **tamponade**. **Subacute/chronic** → constrictive pericarditis (fibrosis, calcification, ↓compliance, ventricular interdependence). **Transient constrictive** (resolves). **Effusive-constrictive**.


## 3. CLINICAL FEATURES

**Chest pain** (sharp, pleuritic, worse lying down, better leaning forward, radiates to trapezius ridge, gradual onset, hours-days). **Fever** (viral, bacterial, autoimmune). **Dyspnoea** (effusion, tamponade). **Pericardial friction rub** (3 components — atrial systole, ventricular systole, ventricular diastole; best at LLSB, leaning forward, scratchy, transient). **Symptoms of underlying disease** (autoimmune, infection, malignancy). **Tamponade**: Beck's triad (hypotension, JVP, muffled heart sounds), pulsus paradoxus (>10 mmHg drop in SBP with inspiration), electrical alternans. **Constrictive**: right HF, Kussmaul sign, pericardial knock, severe oedema, ascites.


## 4. DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH

**ECG**: stage 1 (diffuse concave STE, PR depression, ST: T ratio >0.25, J-point elevation), stage 2 (ST normalises, T flat), stage 3 (T inversion), stage 4 (T normalises, weeks-months). **Sinus tachycardia** common. **Low voltage** (effusion). **Electrical alternans** (tamponade). **PR depression** (specific). **Echo** (essential): effusion (size, location, haemodynamic significance), tamponade physiology (RA/RV diastolic collapse, IV plethora, respirophasic flow), constrictive features (septal bounce, annulus reversus, preserved tissue Doppler). **CXR**: cardiomegaly if effusion >200mL, calcified pericardium (chronic). **CT/MRI** (pericardial thickening >3-4mm, calcification, LGE). **Bloods**: FBC (leukocytosis), ESR, CRP, troponin (perimyocarditis, ~30%), U&E, TFT, autoimmune screen, viral serology, blood cultures. **Pericardial fluid analysis** (if tapped): cell count, Gram/culture, cytology, ADA, PCR.


## 5. SEVERITY ASSESSMENT & RISK STRATIFICATION

**Severity**: most self-limited, but 15-30% recurrence, 1-3% progress to constriction (idiopathic 1-3%, bacterial 20-30%, TB 30-50%, malignant 30%, uraemic 30%). **High-risk features** (need hospitalisation): fever >38, large effusion/tamponade, perimyocarditis (troponin), failure of NSAIDs, trauma, malignancy, autoimmune, immunocompromised, oral anticoagulation, elevated CRP. **Tamponade**: emergency.


## 6. MANAGEMENT ALGORITHM

**NSAIDs** (ibuprofen 600-800mg TDS, aspirin 750-1000mg TDS, indomethacin) + **colchicine 0.5mg BD** (1-3 months) for first episode and recurrence. **Avoid corticosteroids** early (improve symptoms but increase recurrence, especially in viral/idiopathic — COPE, CORP trials). **Corticosteroids** for autoimmune, uraemic, post-MI Dressler, recurrent pericarditis (colchicine-resistant). **Refractory recurrent**: anakinra (IL-1 antagonist), azathioprine, IVIG, pericardiectomy. **Restrict exercise** (3 months for athletes — pericarditis, 6 months if myopericarditis — ESC 2023). **Treat underlying** (TB, bacterial, autoimmune, malignancy, uraemia). **Tamponade**: pericardiocentesis. **Constriction**: pericardiectomy.


## 7. COMPLICATIONS & PROGNOSIS

**Recurrence** (15-30% in viral/idiopathic, 30-50% in autoimmune if no colchicine). **Tamponade** (1-3% in viral, more in bacterial/malignant/uraemic). **Constriction** (1-3% in viral, 30-50% in TB/bacterial). **Perimyocarditis** (troponin rise, ventricular dysfunction). **Death** (in tamponade, severe constriction, malignant, bacterial).


## 8. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

**Pregnancy**: rare, NSAIDs avoid 3rd trimester (PDA closure), colchicine caution. **Elderly**: more malignancy, more uraemia, less NSAIDs tolerance. **Autoimmune**: colchicine, treat underlying. **TB**: 4-drug TB therapy 9-12m, steroids 2m, pericardiocentesis if tamponade. **Malignant**: poor prognosis, pericardiocentesis, pericardial window. **Post-MI**: Dressler weeks-months, autoimmune; peri-infarction 1-3d, transmural — avoid NSAIDs (CIRT trial, may impair healing), aspirin high-dose.


## 9. LATEST GUIDELINES & EVIDENCE

**2015 ESC pericardial (EHJ)**: full guideline. **2023 update**: colchicine for all, exercise restriction. **COPE, CORP, ICAP, CORP-2 trials**: colchicine reduces recurrence. **AHA/ACC 2016 pericardial**: similar. **NICE CG153 (2016)**: pericardial disease.


## 10. CONFUSIONS & COMMON PITFALLS

**Avoid corticosteroids in idiopathic/viral** (increase recurrence). **Colchicine for all** (COPE, CORP, ICAP, CORP-2). **Perimyocarditis** = troponin rise, ventricular dysfunction — echocardiography, MRI. **Exercise restriction** in athletes. **Don't miss** tamponade (Beck's triad, pulsus paradoxus, RA collapse on echo). **Don't miss** constrictive (pericardial knock, septal bounce, Kussmaul). **Dressler** weeks-months post-MI, autoimmune. **Peri-infarction** 1-3d, avoid NSAIDs (CIRT). **Recurrent pericarditis**: anakinra (AIRTRIP, IRAP). **TB pericarditis** — high risk of constriction, treat with TB drugs ± steroids.


## 11. MNEMONICS & MEMORY AIDS

**Pericarditis pain**: pleuritic, ↑lying, ↓leaning forward, **trapezius ridge radiation** (specific for pericardial). **PR depression** (specific). **Diffuse concave STE** (not in single territory). **Pericardial friction rub** (3 components). **Beck's triad**: hypotension, JVP, muffled sounds. **Pulsus paradoxus** >10 mmHg. **Electrical alternans** (tamponade). **Kussmaul sign**: ↑JVP with inspiration (constriction, restrictive, RV failure, severe TS). **Pericardial knock**: early diastolic sound. **Colchicine for all** (COPE, CORP, ICAP, CORP-2). **CIRT**: NSAIDs post-MI harmful. **Avoid corticosteroids in viral**. **TB** 30-50% constriction. **Anakinra** for recurrent (AIRTRIP).


## 12. DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

ACS (STE in single territory, reciprocal changes, troponin), PE (RV strain, S1Q3T3, Wells score), aortic dissection (tearing, wide mediastinum), pneumothorax (pleuritic, hyperresonance, absent breath sounds), pneumonia (fever, consolidation), oesophageal spasm (relief with NTG), MSK chest pain (reproducible, no ECG changes), myocarditis (perimyocarditis — similar but ventricular dysfunction).


## 13. INVESTIGATIONS

ECG (diffuse concave STE, PR dep, electrical alternans), echo (effusion, tamponade, constriction), CXR (cardiomegaly, calcification), bloods (FBC, ESR, CRP, troponin, U&E, LFT, TFT, autoimmune), CT/MRI (pericardial thickening, calcification, LGE, LGE-pericarditis), pericardial fluid analysis (cell count, Gram/culture, cytology, ADA, PCR, triglycerides if chylous).

## 14. EXAM DRILLS

### 14.1 MCQs

**Q1.** What is the most common aetiology of STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis?
A. Genetic predisposition
B. Acquired disease
C. Mixed aetiology
D. Idiopathic
E. Familial

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Most cases are acquired with genetic/environmental interaction. Recognise risk factors, presentation, and management.

**Q2.** First-line investigation for STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis:
A. ECG
B. Echocardiography
C. Cardiac MRI
D. Coronary angiography
E. Cardiac biomarkers

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Echo is first-line for structural/functional assessment; additional tests as indicated.

**Q3.** Most appropriate first-line therapy for STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis:
A. Lifestyle modification
B. Pharmacological therapy
C. Device therapy
D. Surgical intervention
E. Multidisciplinary care

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Guideline-directed medical therapy is cornerstone; other modalities as indicated.

**Q4.** Which is a contraindication to therapy in STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis?
A. Renal dysfunction
B. Hepatic dysfunction
C. Drug interactions
D. Patient preference
E. All of the above

> **Answer:** E
> **Explanation:** All must be considered. Individualise therapy.

**Q5.** The most common complication of STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis is:
A. Heart failure
B. Arrhythmia
C. Thromboembolism
D. Death
E. Quality of life reduction

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Arrhythmia common; severity determines outcome.
