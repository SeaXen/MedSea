# Peripartum cardiomyopathy

Related: [[../Cardiology MOC|Cardiology MOC]] · [[../Cardiomyopathies and Myocardial Disease|Cardiomyopathies and Myocardial Disease]] · [[Myocardial inflammation and infiltrative disease]] · [[Heart Failure]]

## Definition
Peripartum cardiomyopathy (PPCM) is an idiopathic cardiomyopathy with **heart failure due to LV systolic dysfunction** developing toward the end of pregnancy or in the months following delivery, when no other clear cause of cardiomyopathy is found.

## Anatomy
- LV is often dilated with reduced systolic function.
- Functional MR may occur due to annular dilatation.
- RV may also be involved in severe disease.

## Physiology
- Reduced contractility lowers cardiac output.
- Pregnancy/postpartum hemodynamic stress can unmask failure.
- Thromboembolism risk is increased because of low EF plus peripartum hypercoagulability.

## Classification
- Antepartum onset near late pregnancy
- Early postpartum onset
- Recovered PPCM
- Persistent LV dysfunction PPCM

## Etiology / risk factors
- Multiparity
- Advanced maternal age
- Multiple pregnancy
- Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy / pre-eclampsia
- African ancestry in epidemiologic studies
- Proposed mechanisms: vascular-hormonal injury, inflammation, genetic susceptibility

## Pathophysiology
1. Susceptible myocardium is injured by peripartum stressors.
2. LV systolic dysfunction develops.
3. Congestion, low output, and arrhythmia may follow.
4. Recovery may occur, but some patients remain dilated with chronic HF.

## Clinical features
### Symptoms
- Progressive dyspnea
- Orthopnea / PND
- Cough, fatigue
- Palpitations
- Edema
- Chest discomfort sometimes

### Signs
- Tachycardia
- Elevated JVP, pulmonary crackles
- S3 gallop
- Functional MR murmur
- Hypotension in severe cases

## Investigations
- **ECG**: sinus tachycardia, nonspecific ST-T change, AF/arrhythmia occasionally
- **Chest X-ray**: cardiomegaly/pulmonary edema if needed and appropriate postpartum
- **Echo**: key test showing LV systolic dysfunction ± dilatation, MR, RV involvement, thrombus
- **BNP/NT-proBNP** elevated
- **Troponin** may be mildly elevated but does not by itself indicate ACS
- Routine labs to exclude anemia, thyroid disease, sepsis, ischemia, and other secondary causes

## Interpretation frameworks
### Heart-failure / hemodynamic profile
- Often wet/warm; severe cases may become wet/cold or shock.
- Distinguish physiologic pregnancy breathlessness from true HF red flags: orthopnea, PND, pulmonary edema, elevated JVP.

### Troponin / ACS logic
- Mild troponin rise can occur in myocardial strain.
- PPCM is not diagnosed by troponin; use echo and clinical context.

### Murmur / valve clues
- Murmur is usually functional MR from LV dilatation, not primary valvular disease.

## Diagnosis
Diagnosis requires:
- late-pregnancy or postpartum onset,
- LV systolic dysfunction,
- no better alternative explanation.

## Differential diagnosis
- Physiologic dyspnea of pregnancy
- Pulmonary embolism
- Severe pre-eclampsia with pulmonary edema
- Myocarditis
- Takotsubo cardiomyopathy
- Previously unrecognized valvular/congenital heart disease

## Management
- Standard HF therapy adjusted for pregnancy/postpartum status.
- Diuretics for congestion.
- Vasodilator/HF drugs according to maternal status and whether pregnant or postpartum.
- **Avoid ACEi/ARB/ARNI during pregnancy**; they may be used postpartum if appropriate.
- Beta-blockers commonly used.
- Anticoagulation considered in severe LV dysfunction/thrombus risk.
- Obstetric-cardiology joint care.
- Counsel regarding future pregnancy risk if LV function remains impaired.

## Complications
- Acute pulmonary edema
- Cardiogenic shock
- LV thrombus / embolism
- Ventricular arrhythmia
- Persistent dilated cardiomyopathy
- Maternal death in severe disease

## Red flags / emergencies
- Postpartum breathlessness with orthopnea and crackles
- Shock or severe hypoxia
- Markedly reduced EF
- LV thrombus
- Recurrent arrhythmia/syncope

## One-Page Revision Summary
- PPCM = **peripartum onset LV systolic failure** without another clear cause.
- Think in late pregnancy/postpartum dyspnea that is more than “normal pregnancy breathlessness.”
- Echo is the key test.
- Treat as HF with pregnancy-safe adjustments.
- Major exam trap: **ACE inhibitors contraindicated during pregnancy but useful postpartum**.
