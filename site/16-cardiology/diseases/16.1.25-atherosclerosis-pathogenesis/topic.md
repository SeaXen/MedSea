# Atherosclerosis pathogenesis

> [!info] **Topic Classification**
> **Section:** 02_Acute_Coronary_Syndromes | **Category:** Cardiology

---

## 1. HIGH-YIELD SUMMARY (30-Second Review)

Atherosclerosis: chronic inflammatory disease of large/medium arteries (aorta, carotids, coronaries, iliofemoral) characterised by intimal plaques (atheromas) containing lipids, inflammatory cells, smooth muscle cells, fibrous tissue. **Unifying pathology** behind STEMI, NSTEMI, stable angina, TIA/stroke, PAD, mesenteric ischaemia, renovascular HTN. Begins in childhood (fatty streaks), progresses over decades, becomes symptomatic when plaques complicate (rupture, erosion, stenosis).


## 2. ETIOLOGY & PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Risk factors (INTERHEART)**: Major modifiable — smoking, dyslipidaemia (↑LDL/↓HDL), HTN, DM, abdominal obesity. Non-modifiable — age (M>45, F>55), male sex, family history. **Pathogenesis (Ross/Stary)**: (1) Endothelial injury (smoking, HTN, DM, shear, hypercholesterolaemia) → ↓NO, ↑VCAM-1, ICAM-1, E-selectin. (2) LDL infiltration, modification (oxidation). (3) Monocyte adhesion, transmigration, → macrophages. (4) Scavenger receptors (SR-A, CD36, LOX-1) → unregulated oxLDL uptake → **foam cell**. (5) T-cell activation (Th1 → IFN-γ, TNF-α). (6) SMC migration, ECM synthesis → fibrous cap. (7) **Fatty streak** → **fibroatheroma** → **vulnerable plaque (TCFA, cap <65μm)** → complicated plaque → thrombosis.


## 3. CLINICAL FEATURES

Mostly **asymptomatic** for decades (plaque grows slowly, arterial remodelling per Glagov compensates). First manifestation often **acute** (ACS, stroke) or late (stable angina, claudication). Physical exam: **corneal arcus** (in <40y = suspect FH), **xanthelasma**, **tendon xanthomas** (Achilles, extensor), **eruptive xanthomas** (TG-rich), **carotid bruits**, **BP differential** (>20mmHg arms = subclavian stenosis), **diminished/absent pulses**, **renal bruits**, **pulsatile abdominal mass** (AAA).


## 4. DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH

**Risk assessment**: SCORE2 (40-69y, European), ASCVD (US, 40-79y), QRISK3 (UK) — 10-year risk. **Lipid profile**: TC, LDL-C, HDL-C, TG, non-HDL-C, ApoB (preferred for diabetic/high TG), Lp(a) (once in lifetime, genetic). **Subclinical atherosclerosis**: CAC by Agatston — 0 (low), 1-100 (mild), 101-400 (moderate), >400 (high). Carotid intima-media thickness (CIMT), ABI (<0.9 = PAD, >1.4 = calcified). **Imaging**: CTCA for plaque characterisation (low-attenuation, napkin-ring sign, positive remodelling — vulnerable features). IVUS/OCT/NIRS for intracoronary.


## 5. SEVERITY ASSESSMENT & RISK STRATIFICATION

**Stary classification** (pathological): Type I (initial macrophage foam cells), II (fatty streak), III (pre-atheroma, extracellular lipid), IV (atheroma, lipid core), V (fibroatheroma — Va lipid, Vb calcified, Vc fibrous), VI (complicated — VIa surface defect, VIb haematoma, VIc thrombosis), VII (calcified), VIII (fibrotic). **Plaque vulnerability (Virmani)**: TCFA = thin cap (<65μm), large lipid core (>40% plaque), heavy macrophage infiltrate, neovascularisation, spotty calcification, positive remodelling. **LDL-C targets (ESC 2021)**: low risk <3.0, moderate <2.6, high <1.8 AND ≥50% reduction, very high <1.4 AND ≥50% reduction, extreme <1.0 mmol/L.


## 6. MANAGEMENT ALGORITHM

**Primary prevention**: Lifestyle — Mediterranean/DASH diet, ≥150 min/week moderate aerobic + 2x resistance exercise, smoking cessation (single most effective, 50% risk reduction at 1y), BMI <25, alcohol <14 units/week, sleep 7-9h. Pharmacological — **statin** first-line (high-intensity atorvastatin 40-80mg or rosuvastatin 20-40mg for ≥50% LDL reduction). Ezetimibe 10mg add-on if not at target. **PCSK9 inhibitors** (evolocumab 140mg SC 2w or 420mg monthly, alirocumab 75/150mg 2w) for very high risk not at target. **Inclisiran** 284mg SC at 0, 3, 6m then 6-monthly (siRNA, 50% LDL reduction). **Bempedoic acid** 180mg daily (statin-intolerant, ATP-citrate lyase inhibitor). **Secondary prevention** (post-ASCVD): high-intensity statin + ezetimibe ± PCSK9i. **Anti-inflammatory**: colchicine 0.5mg daily (LoDoCo2, COLCOT). **BP**: target <130/80.


## 7. COMPLICATIONS & PROGNOSIS

**Plaque rupture** (most common cause of ACS, 60-70%): TCFA cap disruption → tissue factor exposure → thrombin → fibrin → occlusive thrombus. **Plaque erosion** (25-30% of NSTEMI, more in young women/smokers): denuded endothelium. **Calcified nodule** (5-10%, elderly/CKD). **Intraplaque haemorrhage**: neovessel rupture, sudden expansion. **Stenosis**: progressive lumen narrowing → angina/claudication/TIA. **Aneurysm**: AAA (>3cm, repair if >5.5cm or rapid growth), thoracic (repair if >5.5cm or symptoms), dissection. **Embolism**: cholesterol emboli (atheroembolic disease), thromboembolism (LV thrombus, AF, carotid).


## 8. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

**Familial hypercholesterolaemia (FH)**: autosomal dominant, LDL-R mutation (most common), ApoB, PCSK9 gain-of-function. Heterozygous: LDL >4.9 mmol/L (or >4.0 with family Hx or >3.5 with tendon xanthoma). Homozygous: LDL >13 mmol/L. Treatment: high-intensity statin + ezetimibe + PCSK9i; consider LDL apheresis if refractory. **Children**: cascade screening from age 5-10y. **Pregnancy**: statins teratogenic (FDA X), use bile acid sequestrants. **CKD**: increased CV risk, rosuvastatin preferred, dose adjustment if eGFR<30. **HIV**: PI interactions with statins, use pravastatin/rosuvastatin.


## 9. LATEST GUIDELINES & EVIDENCE

**ESC 2021 Dyslipidaemia (EHJ)**: LDL-C <1.4 mmol/L very high risk, <1.0 extreme. **ACC/AHA 2018 Cholesterol**: 4 statin benefit groups. **ESC 2021 CV Prevention (EHJ)**: SCORE2/SCORE2-OP, CAC for risk refinement. **NICE NG238 (2023)**: inclisiran for primary prevention if high risk + statin intolerance, or secondary prevention with high LDL. **2024 ESC/EAS FH Consensus**: universal screening, child-parent cascade.


## 10. CONFUSIONS & COMMON PITFALLS

Most MIs occur from **non-flow-limiting** (<70% stenosis) plaques that rupture — vulnerable plaque concept. CAC=0 does not exclude all atherosclerosis, just significant. Statin myalgia over-diagnosed; true rhabdomyolysis <0.01%. Primary prevention aspirin: increased bleeding, no net benefit (ARRIVE, ASCEND, ASPREE). Lp(a) is genetic, doesn't respond to statins (may increase 10-20%). FH often missed — check LDL, family history, tendon xanthomas.


## 11. MNEMONICS & MEMORY AIDS

**FOAM cell**: Formed by Oxidised lipid uptake by Activated Macrophages via scavenger receptors. **Stary stages I-VIII**. **TCFA**: Thin-Cap FibroAtheroma (cap <65μm). **Glagov**: positive remodelling up to 40% stenosis preserves lumen. **CTT meta-analysis**: each 1 mmol/L LDL reduction = 22% MACE reduction. **INTERHEART**: 9 modifiable risk factors account for >90% MI risk.


## 12. DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

Vasculitis (Takayasu, GCA, Kawasaki) — large-vessel inflammation. Fibromuscular dysplasia — young women, "string of beads" on imaging. SCAD — young women, peripartum, intramural haematoma. Radiation vasculopathy — prior RT, fibrotic. Mycotic aneurysm — infection, fever.


## 13. INVESTIGATIONS

Fasting lipid profile, hs-CRP, Lp(a), glucose/HbA1c, U&E, LFT, TFT. ECG, CXR. Lipid subfractionation (NMR LipoProfile), ApoB if available. CAC scoring for intermediate risk. CTCA for low-intermediate pre-test probability. ABI for PAD. CIMT for subclinical. Genetic testing for FH.

## 14. EXAM DRILLS

### 14.1 MCQs

**Q1.** What is the most common aetiology of Atherosclerosis pathogenesis?
A. Genetic predisposition
B. Acquired disease
C. Mixed aetiology
D. Idiopathic
E. Familial

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Most cases are acquired with genetic/environmental interaction. Recognise risk factors, presentation, and management.

**Q2.** First-line investigation for Atherosclerosis pathogenesis:
A. ECG
B. Echocardiography
C. Cardiac MRI
D. Coronary angiography
E. Cardiac biomarkers

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Echo is first-line for structural/functional assessment; additional tests as indicated.

**Q3.** Most appropriate first-line therapy for Atherosclerosis pathogenesis:
A. Lifestyle modification
B. Pharmacological therapy
C. Device therapy
D. Surgical intervention
E. Multidisciplinary care

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Guideline-directed medical therapy is cornerstone; other modalities as indicated.

**Q4.** Which is a contraindication to therapy in Atherosclerosis pathogenesis?
A. Renal dysfunction
B. Hepatic dysfunction
C. Drug interactions
D. Patient preference
E. All of the above

> **Answer:** E
> **Explanation:** All must be considered. Individualise therapy.

**Q5.** The most common complication of Atherosclerosis pathogenesis is:
A. Heart failure
B. Arrhythmia
C. Thromboembolism
D. Death
E. Quality of life reduction

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Arrhythmia common; severity determines outcome.
