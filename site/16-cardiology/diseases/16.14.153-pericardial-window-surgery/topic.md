# Pericardial window surgery

## 1. Definition
**Pericardial window**: Surgical procedure creating a permanent opening in the pericardium to allow continuous drainage of pericardial fluid into the pleural or peritoneal cavity. Used for **recurrent** or **malignant** effusions not amenable to repeated pericardiocentesis.

## 2. Indications
- **Recurrent pericardial effusion** (after multiple pericardiocenteses)
- **Malignant pericardial effusion** (most common indication)
- **Purulent pericarditis** (failed closed drainage)
- **Traumatic hemopericardium** (clotted, not amenable to needle)
- **Type A aortic dissection** (with tamponade)
- **Diagnostic** when fluid analysis needed but failed pericardiocentesis

## 3. Approaches
### Subxiphoid (Subxiphoid Pericardiotomy)
- **Most common** approach
- **Infracostal incision** (5–7 cm)
- Open pericardium, create 2–3 cm window
- Can place drain
- **Local anesthesia** possible in high-risk patients
- Lower morbidity than thoracotomy

### Left Anterior Thoracotomy
- 4th–5th ICS, anterolateral
- For **larger window** or combined procedure
- General anesthesia required

### VATS (Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery)
- **Minimally invasive**
- Excellent visualization
- Pleuropericardial window
- Faster recovery

### Pericardioperitoneal Window
- Window into peritoneal cavity
- Used in some centers

## 4. Technique (Subxiphoid)
1. **General or local anesthesia**
2. **Supine position**
3. **Midline incision** from xiphoid extending 5 cm
4. **Dissect** through subcutaneous tissue, linea alba
5. **Identify pericardium** (often thickened)
6. **Incise pericardium** (small initial)
7. **Evacuate fluid** (send for analysis)
8. **Create window** (2×2 cm to 4×4 cm)
9. **Place drain**
10. **Close** in layers

## 5. Postoperative Care
- **Monitor hemodynamics** (re-accumulation)
- **Drain** until <25 mL/24 h
- **Repeat echo** before discharge
- **Treat underlying cause**
- **Pain control**
- **Pulmonary toilet**

## 6. Complications
- **Bleeding, infection**
- **Pneumothorax, hemothorax**
- **Re-accumulation** (~5–10%)
- **Phrenic nerve injury**
- **Constrictive pericarditis** (rare, late)
- **Anesthesia complications**

## 7. Outcomes
- **Subxiphoid window**: 95%+ success, low recurrence
- **VATS**: Excellent visualization, slightly higher morbidity
- **Malignant effusion**: Window + systemic therapy → palliation

## 8. Comparison: Pericardiocentesis vs Window
| Feature | Pericardiocentesis | Window |
|---------|-------------------|--------|
| **Invasiveness** | Bedside, percutaneous | Surgical |
| **Anesthesia** | Local | Local or general |
| **Recurrence** | Higher (40–60%) | Lower (<10%) |
| **Drainage duration** | 24–48 h | Days–weeks |
| **Complications** | Lower | Higher (surgical) |
| **Best for** | First episode, tamponade | Recurrent, malignant |

> **Pearls**
> - **Subxiphoid window** is the **gold standard** for recurrent effusions
> - **Malignant effusion**: window + systemic therapy = best palliation
> - **VATS** for diagnostic uncertainty + window
> - **Type A dissection**: surgical repair, not window
> - **Constriction risk** low but possible
