# Antipsychotics (typical-atypical) and clozapine

---

## Definition

Antipsychotics are dopamine D2 receptor antagonists used to treat psychotic disorders. They are classified as typical (first-generation) or atypical (second-generation) based on side effect profiles and serotonin/dopamine receptor activity.

## Core Clinical Content

### Typical (first-gen)
Haloperidol, chlorpromazine, fluphenazine, zuclopenthixol; higher EPS risk

### Atypical (second-gen)
Risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole, clozapine; lower EPS risk

### Dopamine pathways
Mesolimbic (positive symptoms), mesocortical (negative), nigrostriatal (EPS), tuberoinfundibular (prolactin)

### Mechanism of antipsychotics
D2 receptor antagonism; atypicals also block 5-HT2A

### Extrapyramidal side effects (EPS)
Acute dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia

### Metabolic side effects (atypicals)
Weight gain, dyslipidaemia, diabetes (especially olanzapine, clozapine)

### Hyperprolactinaemia
Risperidone, amisulpride, paliperidone

### QTc prolongation
Ziprasidone, sertindole; caution in cardiac disease

### Clozapine — reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia
Requires regular FBC (agranulocytosis risk); register monitoring

### Clozapine side effects
Agranulocytosis, myocarditis, weight gain, diabetes, sedation, sialorrhoea, seizures
