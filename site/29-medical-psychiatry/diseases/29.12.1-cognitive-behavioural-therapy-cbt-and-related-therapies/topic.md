# Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) and related therapies

---

## Definition

Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) is a structured, time-limited, present-focused psychotherapy that identifies and modifies unhelpful thinking patterns (cognitions) and behaviours to improve emotional wellbeing. Related therapies include DBT, ACT, and mindfulness-based CBT.

## Core Clinical Content

### CBT principles
Cognitive model (thoughts → feelings → behaviour); collaborative empiricism; Socratic questioning

### CBT techniques
Thought records, behavioural activation, exposure, cognitive restructuring, problem-solving

### CBT duration
Usually 12-20 sessions; structured; homework between sessions

### CBT effectiveness
Strong evidence for depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD, eating disorders

### Cognitive distortions
Catastrophising, all-or-nothing thinking, mind-reading, overgeneralisation, personalisation

### Behavioural activation
Schedule activities; counteracts withdrawal in depression

### Exposure therapy
Graded exposure to feared stimuli; for phobias, OCD, PTSD

### Dialectical behaviour therapy (DBT)
For BPD; combines CBT + mindfulness; 4 modules: mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness

### Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)
Acceptance + values-based action; for chronic conditions

### Mindfulness-based cognitive therapy (MBCT)
For relapse prevention in recurrent depression; combines mindfulness + CBT
