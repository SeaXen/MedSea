# Acute behavioural disturbance and rapid tranquilisation

---

## Definition

Acute behavioural disturbance is a clinical state of severe agitation, aggression, or violence posing immediate risk to the patient or others, often requiring rapid tranquilisation with antipsychotics and/or benzodiazepines after verbal de-escalation fails.

## Core Clinical Content

### Stepwise management
Verbal de-escalation → oral medication → IM medication → physical restraint (last resort)

### Verbal de-escalation
Calm tone, clear language, offer choices, time, place of safety

### Rapid tranquilisation (RT)
Use of medication to rapidly calm agitated patient; lowest effective dose

### RT medications
IM lorazepam + haloperidol; olanzapine IM; aripiprazole IM; midazolam IM

### Avoid benzodiazepines in
Respiratory depression risk; substance use, frailty

### Avoid haloperidol in
Parkinsonism, Lewy body dementia (severe sensitivity), prolonged QT

### Antipsychotic + benzodiazepine
Often combined; haloperidol 5 mg + lorazepam 2 mg IM is common RT combination

### Dosing interval
Repeat every 30-60 min as needed; do not exceed BNF max

### Monitoring post-RT
BP, pulse, SpO2, temperature, GCS, hydration; ECG if possible

### Physical restraint
Last resort; rapid tranquilisation preferred; face-to-face review within 1 h
