# Squamous cell carcinoma (cutaneous)

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Skin Tumours

## Definition

Cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (cSCC) is the second most common skin cancer, arising from keratinocytes. Locally invasive with metastatic potential (5% overall, higher in immunosuppressed, lip, ear). Premalignant: AK, Bowen disease. Risk factors: cumulative UV, immunosuppression (transplant), HPV, arsenic, chronic wounds (Marjolin), xeroderma pigmentosum. Treatment: surgical excision (4-6mm margin), Mohs (high-risk), radiotherapy, cemiplimab (advanced).

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Squamous cell carcinoma (cutaneous) presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Common | Second most common skin cancer |
| Metastasis | 5% overall; higher in immunosuppressed, lip, ear, high-risk features |
| Sites | Sun-exposed: face, ears, scalp, dorsal hands, lower lip |
| Morphology | Scaly, hyperkeratotic papule/nodule, often ulcerated/crusted |
| Dermoscopy | Glomerular vessels, white circles, keratin masses, looped vessels |
| Risk factors | Cumulative UV, immunosuppression (transplant), HPV, arsenic, chronic wounds, XP, albinism |
| Marjolin ulcer | SCC arising in chronic wound, scar, or ulcer |
| High-risk features | Size >2cm, depth >6mm, ear, lip, immunosuppression, poor differentiation, perineural invasion |
| Treatment | Surgical excision (4-6mm low-risk, 6-10mm high-risk); Mohs; radiotherapy; cemiplimab (advanced) |
| Hedgehog | Hedgehog pathway implicated; vismodegib also used for advanced BCC |
