# Behcet syndrome (multisystem vasculitis)

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Emergencies

## Definition

Behçet syndrome is a chronic, relapsing, multisystem vasculitis (both arteries and veins) characterised by recurrent oral aphthous ulcers (mandatory, 100%), genital ulcers (80%), ocular inflammation (uveitis, 50%), skin lesions (erythema nodosum-like, papulopustular, pathergy), arthritis, neurologic (parenchymal, 5-10%), vascular (thrombosis, aneurysm), GI (ulceration). More common along ancient Silk Road (Turkey, Japan, Mediterranean). HLA-B*51 associated. Pathogenesis: neutrophil hyperactivation, endothelial dysfunction. Diagnosis: International Study Group (ISG) criteria (oral ulcers + 2 of: genital ulcers, eye lesions, skin lesions, pathergy+). Treatment: mucocutaneous: colchicine, topical steroids, sucralfate; severe: azathioprine, thalidomide, interferon-α, anti-TNF (adalimumab, infliximab), tocilizumab, JAK inhibitors; ocular: urgent immunosuppression.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Behcet syndrome (multisystem vasculitis) presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29 (and standard dermatology curriculum). The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures, trigger avoidance, and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment
