# Varicella (chickenpox)

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Skin Infections

## Definition

Varicella (chickenpox) is the primary VZV infection, most common in children. Characterised by fever and pruritic vesicular rash with lesions in different stages (macule → papule → vesicle → crust) on the same body region ('dewdrops on a rose petal'). Highly contagious (respiratory droplets, vesicle fluid). Complications: bacterial superinfection, pneumonia (adults, immunocompromised), cerebellar ataxia, encephalitis, Reye syndrome (aspirin). Vaccine: live attenuated.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Varicella (chickenpox) presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Cause | Primary VZV |
| Age | Children 1-10 years (most common) |
| Prodrome | Fever, malaise, anorexia 1-2 days before rash |
| Rash | Macule, papule, vesicle, crust (all stages simultaneously) |
| Distribution | Centripetal (face, trunk > limbs); scalp, mucosa |
| Pruritus | Yes (intense) |
| Contagious | 2 days before to 5 days after rash (until all crusted) |
| Complications | Bacterial superinfection, pneumonia (adults/immunosuppressed), cerebellar ataxia, encephalitis, Reye (aspirin) |
| Pregnancy | Maternal varicella 5 days before to 2 days after delivery → severe neonatal varicella |
| Treatment | Aciclovir (immunocompromised, adults, neonates, secondarily infected); symptomatic for children |
| Vaccine | Live attenuated (Varilrix, Varivax); 2 doses |
