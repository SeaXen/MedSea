# Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (cutaneous)

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Drug Eruptions

## Definition

Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) is an immune-mediated adverse reaction to heparin (unfractionated > LMWH), with onset 5-14 days after exposure. Type II HIT (immune-mediated, IgG against heparin-PF4 complex) causes thrombocytopenia and paradoxically thrombosis. Skin findings: erythematous plaques or skin necrosis at injection sites (less severe than warfarin necrosis). Diagnosis: 4Ts score, anti-PF4 antibodies, serotonin release assay.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (cutaneous) presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Types | Type I (mild, early, self-limiting); Type II (immune, 5-14d, severe) |
| Type II mechanism | IgG against heparin-PF4 complex → platelet activation → thrombosis |
| Onset | 5-14 days (or within 24h if recent heparin exposure) |
| Skin | Erythematous plaques or skin necrosis at injection sites |
| Thrombosis | Paradoxical (despite thrombocytopenia); venous and arterial |
| 4Ts score | Thrombocytopenia, Timing, Thrombosis, oTher causes (higher = more likely) |
| Diagnosis | Anti-PF4 antibody ELISA, serotonin release assay (SRA) |
| Treatment | STOP all heparin (including LMWH); switch to non-heparin anticoagulant (argatroban, bivalirudin, fondaparinux); avoid warfarin acutely |
| Warfarin | Contraindicated acutely (worsens microthrombosis in protein C deficiency) |
| Cross-reactivity | LMWH may cross-react; use direct thrombin inhibitors |
