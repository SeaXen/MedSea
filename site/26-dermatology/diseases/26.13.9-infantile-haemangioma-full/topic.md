# Infantile haemangioma

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Special Populations

## Definition

Infantile haemangioma (strawberry naevus) is the most common tumour of infancy (5-10% of infants), with characteristic evolution: absent or precursor lesion at birth → rapid proliferation (0-12 months, especially 6-9w) → plateau → slow involution (years, complete by 5-10y in most). Superficial (red, strawberry), deep (blue/purple, subcutaneous), or mixed. Complications: ulceration (most common), bleeding, visual/airway obstruction, PHACE syndrome, high-output cardiac failure (large hepatic).

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Infantile haemangioma presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Prevalence | 5-10% of infants; F>M (3:1); premature |
| Course | Proliferate 0-12 months, involute years |
| Types | Superficial (red), deep (blue), mixed |
| Complications | Ulceration (most common), bleeding, visual obstruction, airway, PHACE, LUMBAR |
| PHACE | Posterior fossa malformation, Haemangioma, Arterial anomalies, Cardiac defects, Eye abnormalities |
| LUMBAR | Lower body haemangioma + Urogenital anomalies, Myelopathy, Bony deformities, Anorectal malformations, Renal anomalies |
| High-risk | Face (periorbital, perioral, nasal, lip), airway, anogenital, segmental, large |
| Propranolol | First-line; oral 2-3mg/kg/day; standard of care |
| Topical timolol | Small superficial haemangiomas |
| Treatment indications | Functional impairment, ulceration, PHACE, cosmetic risk |
