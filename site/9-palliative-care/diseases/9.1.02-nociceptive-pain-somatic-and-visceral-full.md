> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Pain Physiology & Assessment**

## 1. 1. Definition

Nociceptive pain is pain from actual or threatened tissue damage activating nociceptors. Subtypes: somatic (sharp, well-localised, Aδ fibres — skin, bone, muscle, joint) and visceral (dull, deep, poorly localised, referred, C fibres — organs, hollow viscera). Common causes: trauma, surgery, inflammation, cancer infiltration, obstruction. Treatment: paracetamol, NSAIDs, opioids, treatment of underlying cause.

## 2. 2. Clinical Features and Presentation

Nociceptive pain arises from actual or threatened damage to non-neural tissue with intact somatosensory system. Somatic pain: well-localised, sharp, aching, throbbing; worsens with movement; examples: bone metastases, post-operative, fracture, osteoarthritis. Visceral pain: poorly localised, deep, cramping, squeezing, pressure; often referred to dermatomes (e.g., diaphragmatic irritation → shoulder tip via phrenic C3, C4, C5; cardiac → left arm T1-T2; gallbladder → right shoulder/scapula); associated autonomic features (sweating, nausea, vomiting, pallor); examples: bowel obstruction, pancreatic cancer, liver capsule distension, ureteric colic. Clinical features include guarding, rigidity, tenderness, and signs of underlying pathology. Visceral pain often has 'silent' areas with hyperalgesia in the referred zone (Head's zones).


## 3. 3. Investigation and Management Summary

### 1. Investigations
History (SOCRATES — Site, Onset, Character, Radiation, Associations, Timing, Exacerbating/relieving, Severity), examination (inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation), vital signs. Disease-specific: full blood count (infection, inflammation), ESR/CRP, U&E (renal colic, metabolic), LFTs, amylase (pancreatitis), troponin (cardiac), imaging (X-ray, CT, ultrasound, MRI), endoscopy (OGD, colonoscopy), laparoscopy if indicated. Pain assessment tools: NRS, VAS, McGill, BPI.

### 2. Management
Treat underlying cause (surgery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy for cancer; antibiotics for infection). WHO analgesic ladder: paracetamol ± NSAID for mild; weak opioid (codeine, tramadol) for moderate; strong opioid (morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl) for severe. Adjuvants: corticosteroids for liver capsule pain, bone pain, raised ICP; antispasmodics (hyoscine, mebeverine) for smooth muscle colic; laxatives for opioid-induced constipation; antiemetics. Non-pharmacological: TENS, heat, massage, positioning. Interventional: coeliac plexus block (pancreatic), nerve blocks, epidural.

## 6. 6. Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: What is the difference between somatic and visceral pain? A: Somatic = well-localised, sharp, from skin/muscle/bone; visceral = poorly localised, deep, cramping, often referred, from organs.

2. 2. Q: What is referred pain and examples? A: Visceral pain perceived in somatic dermatome; e.g., diaphragmatic irritation → shoulder (C3, C4, C5); cardiac ischaemia → left arm T1-T2; gallbladder → right scapula.

3. 3. Q: First-line for visceral pain? A: Treat cause + WHO ladder + adjuvant (corticosteroid for organ capsule, antispasmodic for colic, opioid for severe).

4. 4. Q: Coeliac plexus block indication? A: Pancreatic cancer pain; often >70% pain relief.

5. 5. Q: What is Head's zone? A: Dermatome of cutaneous hyperalgesia corresponding to a visceral organ's nerve supply.
