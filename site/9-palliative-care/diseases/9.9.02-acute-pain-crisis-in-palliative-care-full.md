# Acute pain crisis in palliative care

Related: [[../../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 8: Pain and Palliative Care** (24th Edition, Colvin, Fallon) — Palliative Care Emergencies

## Definition

Acute pain crisis (or pain emergency) is sudden, severe, uncontrolled pain in a palliative care patient requiring urgent intervention. Causes: pathological fracture, bone metastasis progression, spinal cord compression, biliary/ureteric colic, bowel obstruction, perforated viscus, infection, procedure-related. Management: rapid-onset strong opioid (SC morphine 5-10mg or IV/SC diamorphine, repeat at 30-60min, titrate), adjuvant (NSAID, dexamethasone for bone pain/colic, anticholinergic for colic), non-pharmacological (positioning, hot/cold, distraction), identify and treat cause, consider CSCI opioid if multiple doses, breakthrough plan.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Identify cause (history, examination, imaging). Pain assessment.

### Management
Rapid-onset strong opioid (SC morphine 5-10mg or diamorphine 5-10mg, repeat at 30-60 min), adjuvant (NSAID, dexamethasone for bone pain/colic/MSCC, anticholinergic for colic, ketamine for refractory), non-pharm (positioning), identify and treat cause (RT for bone mets, stent for obstruction, surgery for fracture), CSCI, PCA for severe.

## Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: First-line? A: Rapid-onset strong opioid (SC morphine 5-10mg or diamorphine 5-10mg), repeat.

2. 2. Q: Adjuvants? A: NSAID, dexamethasone, anticholinergic, ketamine.

3. 3. Q: Treat cause? A: RT, stent, surgery, ABx.

4. 4. Q: CSCI vs PCA? A: CSCI for sustained; PCA for severe uncontrolled.

5. 5. Q: Dexamethasone indication? A: Bone pain, MSCC, raised ICP, colic.
