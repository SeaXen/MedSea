# Depression in palliative care

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Non-Pain Symptoms in Palliative Care**

## Definition

Depression in palliative care is common (5-50%) but underdiagnosed. Symptoms: persistent low mood, anhedonia, hopelessness, guilt, suicidal ideation, sleep/appetite changes, withdrawal. Distinguish from normal grief (which is reactive, transient, fluctuates). Screening: Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS), Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9). Treatment: psychological (CBT, supportive, dignity therapy, meaning-centred therapy); pharmacological (SSRIs — sertraline, citalopram, 4-6 weeks for effect; mirtazapine for sleep, appetite, anxiety; TCAs — amitriptyline — if neuropathic pain). Methylphenidate for rapid effect (2-4 days). For severe/refractory: ECT.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Depression in palliative care is common (15-50%), under-recognised. Persistent low mood, anhedonia (loss of pleasure), hopelessness, guilt, worthlessness, suicidal ideation, sleep/appetite changes, fatigue, psychomotor retardation. Differentiate from normal grief (which has waves), anticipatory grief, adjustment disorder. Risk factors: prior depression, advanced disease, poor pain control, social isolation, functional decline. Suicidal ideation is a key marker (not normal in grief).


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Screening: PHQ-2 (Patient Health Questionnaire-2, ≥3), PHQ-9, HADS (Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale). Clinical interview. Risk assessment: suicidal ideation, intent, plan.

### Management
Psychological: CBT, supportive psychotherapy, dignity therapy, mindfulness, meaning-centred therapy. Pharmacological: SSRIs (sertraline, citalopram, escitalopram — first-line, 4-6 weeks onset); mirtazapine (also appetite, sleep, weight gain); TCAs (amitriptyline, useful with neuropathic pain); psychostimulants (methylphenidate 5-10mg BD — rapid onset, end of life). Combination: psychological + pharmacological. ECT for severe/refractory. Social and spiritual support.

## Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: First-line antidepressant in palliative care? A: SSRI (sertraline, citalopram).

2. 2. Q: How to differentiate depression from normal grief? A: Depression: persistent anhedonia, hopelessness, worthlessness, suicidal ideation; grief: waves, can enjoy moments, no suicidal ideation.

3. 3. Q: When to use mirtazapine? A: Depression with insomnia, weight loss, poor appetite (sleep, weight gain side effects).

4. 4. Q: Rapid-onset antidepressant? A: Methylphenidate (psychostimulant), 5-10mg BD, onset 1-2 days, useful at end of life.

5. 5. Q: Suicidal ideation in pall care? A: Always assess; often related to uncontrolled pain, depression, existential distress; not normal.
