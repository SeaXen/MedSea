# Delirium in palliative care

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Non-Pain Symptoms in Palliative Care**

## Definition

Delirium in palliative care is a common (28-90%), serious, often missed complication. Acute onset, fluctuating course, inattention, altered consciousness, disorientation, hallucinations, agitation or hypoactivity. Reversible causes (40-50%): drugs (opioids, corticosteroids, anticholinergics), metabolic (hypercalcaemia, hyponatraemia, uraemia, liver failure), infection, dehydration, hypoxia, urinary retention, constipation. Terminal delirium (last days) often irreversible. Treatment: identify and treat reversible causes; non-pharmacological (reorientation, family, familiar objects); antipsychotics (haloperidol 0.5-2mg q4-8h, olanzapine 2.5-5mg nocte, levomepromazine 6.25-25mg), benzodiazepines (midazolam 2-5mg SC) for severe agitation/refractory.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Delirium is acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention, awareness, and cognition, with reduced ability to focus, sustain attention, or shift attention. Common in palliative care (up to 90% in last days/weeks), often under-recognised. Three subtypes: hyperactive (agitation, hallucinations — 25%), hypoactive (lethargy, withdrawal — 75%, missed), mixed. Causes: drugs (opioids, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, steroids), metabolic (Ca, Na, urea, glucose, liver failure), infection (UTI, pneumonia, sepsis), hypoxia, hypercapnia, dehydration, constipation, urinary retention, pain, brain (metastases, stroke). Terminal delirium: in last days, often irreversible.


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Bedside assessment: 4AT, CAM (Confusion Assessment Method), time-of-day variation. Reversible causes: drugs, bloods (U&E, Ca, LFT, glucose, FBC, cultures), infection screen, O2/CO2, bladder, bowels, pain, brain imaging if focal signs. Avoid unnecessary investigations at end of life.

### Management
Identify and treat reversible causes (drugs, metabolic, infection, pain, urinary retention, constipation). Non-pharm: reorientation, family presence, familiar environment, clocks, lighting, soft music, avoid restraints. Pharmacological: haloperidol 0.5-5mg SC/PO (first-line, for hyperactive or distressing), levomepromazine 12.5-50mg (second-line, broader, more sedating), olanzapine 2.5-5mg (alternative), benzodiazepines (only if alcohol withdrawal or seizures, midazolam SC prn or CSCI for refractory/agitated). Terminal delirium: address cause, family support, comfort care.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Differential | Distinguishing Feature |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Delirium vs dementia | Acute, fluctuating, attention impaired |
| Depression | Slow onset, attention preserved |
| Drug effect | Benzodiazepines, opioids |
