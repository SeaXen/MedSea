# Opioid tolerance, dependence, addiction, pseudo-addiction

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Opioid Management**

## Definition

Four related but distinct concepts: (1) Tolerance: pharmacological right-shift of dose-response, requiring higher doses for same effect. (2) Physical dependence: physiological adaptation causing withdrawal on abrupt cessation/reduction. (3) Addiction (psychological dependence): compulsive use despite harm, loss of control, cravings, continued use despite negative consequences. (4) Pseudo-addiction: behavioural changes (clock-watching, requesting more medication) due to UNDER-treated pain that resolves with adequate analgesia. Patients with chronic pain on long-term opioids are often tolerant and physically dependent but NOT addicted.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Definitions: Tolerance: physiological adaptation, decreased effect of same dose over time; reversed by dose increase. Physical dependence: withdrawal on abrupt discontinuation or dose reduction; not addiction. Addiction (substance use disorder): psychological dependence, compulsive use, harm despite consequences; rare in pain patients (less than 0.1% without prior history). Pseudo-addiction: drug-seeking behaviour due to inadequate analgesia; resolves with adequate dose. Distinguishing is important to avoid undertreating pain.


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
History of substance use, opioid use (prescribed, OTC, illicit), behaviour (e.g., 'clock-watching', multiple prescriptions), functional impact.

### Management
Tolerance: increase dose (cancer pain, progressive disease). Physical dependence: taper slowly to avoid withdrawal. Addiction: refer to addiction services, consider opioid-sparing, methadone/buprenorphine (addiction indication). Pseudo-addiction: increase dose. Use universal precautions (screening, agreements, monitoring, prescription drug monitoring).
