# Malignant spinal cord compression (MSCC)

Related: [[../../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 8: Pain and Palliative Care** (24th Edition, Colvin, Fallon) — Palliative Care Emergencies

## Definition

Malignant spinal cord compression (MSCC) is an oncological emergency with compression of the spinal cord and/or cauda equina by tumour, leading to neurological deficit. Most commonly from vertebral metastases (prostate, breast, lung, kidney, myeloma, lymphoma). Clinical features: back pain (>95%, often first symptom, worse on movement, lying flat, Valsalva), sensory level, motor weakness, autonomic dysfunction (sphincter, bladder, bowel). Diagnosis: MRI whole spine (urgent, within 24h of clinical suspicion). Treatment: high-dose dexamethasone (10mg IV bolus then 4mg q6h), urgent neurosurgical/radiation oncology referral, definitive treatment (surgical decompression or radiotherapy, depending on suitability — Tokuhashi score, NOMS framework). Prognosis depends on pretreatment neurological status (ambulatory before = 80% remain ambulatory; paraplegic before = 5% recover).

## Clinical Features and Presentation

MSCC is oncological emergency: compression of spinal cord/cauda equina by tumour causing neurological deficit. Causes: vertebral metastases (prostate, breast, lung, kidney, myeloma, lymphoma). Site: thoracic 60%, lumbar 30%, cervical 10%. Features: back pain (>95%, often first, worse on movement/Valsalva/lying flat), sensory level, motor weakness, autonomic dysfunction (sphincter, bladder/bowel — late, poor prognosis). Cord compression is medical emergency; pretreatment function = main prognostic factor (ambulatory 80% remain; paraplegic 5% recover).


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
MRI whole spine URGENT (within 24h of suspicion) — gold standard. CT if MRI contraindicated. X-ray (late changes).

### Management
High-dose dexamethasone 10mg IV bolus then 4mg q6h PO/IV (immediately, before imaging if high suspicion). Urgent neurosurgical + radiation oncology referral. Definitive: surgical decompression (posterior ± anterior) or radiotherapy (RT alone for multi-level, radio-resistant tumour, no surgical option). Indications surgery: solitary, single level, good prognosis, mechanical instability, radio-resistant. Post-treatment: rehabilitation, pressure area care, bladder/bowel, psychological support.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Differential | Distinguishing Feature |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Cauda equina | Lower motor signs, saddle anaesthesia, late bladder |
| Bone metastasis without compression | Pain without neurology |
| Spinal stenosis | Older, degenerative, gradual |

2. 2. Q: Indications for surgery vs RT? A: Surgery: solitary, single level, good prognosis, mechanical instability, radio-resistant. RT: multi-level, radio-sensitive, palliative, no surgical option.

3. 3. Q: Prognostic factor? A: Pretreatment neurological function (ambulatory 80% remain; paraplegic 5% recover).

4. 4. Q: Most common site? A: Thoracic (60%), then lumbar (30%), cervical (10%).

5. 5. Q: Most common cancers? A: Prostate, breast, lung, kidney, myeloma, lymphoma.
