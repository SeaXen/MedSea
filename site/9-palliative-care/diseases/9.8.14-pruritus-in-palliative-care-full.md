# Pruritus in palliative care

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Non-Pain Symptoms in Palliative Care**

## Definition

Pruritus (itch) in palliative care affects 10-30%. Causes: cholestasis (obstructive jaundice — common in pancreatic/liver cancer), uraemia, opioids (μ-receptor), paraneoplastic (lymphoma), xerosis, drugs, scabies, eczema. Treatment: identify and treat cause; cholestatic pruritus (cholestyramine 4g q6-12h, rifampicin 150-300mg daily, sertraline, naltrexone); uraemic pruritus (gabapentin, pregabalin, mirtazapine, UV-B phototherapy); opioid-induced (ondansetron 8mg, opioid rotation, naloxone); emollients, topical corticosteroids, antihistamines (limited benefit).

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Pruritus (itch) affects 20-30% of palliative care. Causes: cholestasis (obstructive jaundice — pancreatic, cholangiocarcinoma, liver mets), uraemia (renal failure), opioid-induced (especially morphine; intrathecal morphine, fentanyl; less with methadone, hydromorphone), paraneoplastic (Hodgkin, leukaemia, solid tumours), dermatological (xerosis, scabies, drug eruption), iron deficiency, thyroid disease, psychogenic. Often distressing, disrupts sleep.


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
History (timing, relationship to drugs, dialysis, jaundice), examination (skin — primary lesion, scratch marks, xerosis, jaundice, excoriations, papules), bloods (LFT, renal, Ca, ferritin, TFT, FBC), drug review. Skin biopsy if unclear.

### Management
Treat cause: cholestasis (bile acid sequestrants — cholestyramine, colestipol, colesevelam; rifampicin; naltrexone; sertraline), uraemia (antihistamines, gabapentin, pregabalin, phototherapy), opioid (rotation — methadone, hydromorphone, fentanyl patch), dermatological (emollients, topical steroids, calcineurin inhibitors, menthol cream). Antihistamines: hydroxyzine 25mg, cetirizine, loratadine (non-sedating for daytime), chlorpheniramine. Other: gabapentin 300-900mg/day (uraemic, cholestatic, opioid), pregabalin, ondansetron (cholestatic), low-dose paroxetine or mirtazapine (cholestatic, paraneoplastic).

## Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: Common causes of pruritus in palliative care? A: Cholestasis, uraemia, opioid (especially morphine), paraneoplastic.

2. 2. Q: First-line for cholestatic pruritus? A: Cholestyramine (bile acid sequestrant), then rifampicin, naltrexone, sertraline.

3. 3. Q: First-line for opioid-induced pruritus? A: Opioid rotation (to methadone, hydromorphone, fentanyl); antihistamine; ondansetron.

4. 4. Q: First-line for uraemic pruritus? A: Gabapentin 300-900mg/day; phototherapy; antihistamines.

5. 5. Q: Why sedating antihistamines? A: Pruritus has both histamine and central (serotonergic) components; sedating H1 (chlorpheniramine, hydroxyzine) more effective.
