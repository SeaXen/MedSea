# Constipation in palliative care

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Non-Pain Symptoms in Palliative Care**

## Definition

Constipation in palliative care is common (50-90% with advanced cancer). Causes: opioids (universal effect, prophylactic laxatives essential), decreased mobility, low fibre/fluid intake, drugs (5-HT3 antagonists, anticholinergics, opioids), metabolic (hypercalcaemia, hypokalaemia), bowel obstruction. Bristol Stool Chart 1-2 = constipation. Treatment: prophylactic laxatives for all opioid patients; stimulant (senna) + softener (docusate); escalating to macrogol, lactulose, opioid receptor antagonists (methylnaltrexone, naloxegol — peripheral, for opioid-induced constipation), rectal (suppositories, enemas) if oral fails.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Constipation in palliative care is common (50-90%), reduced bowel frequency, hard stools, straining, sensation of incomplete evacuation, abdominal discomfort, distension. Causes: opioid (most common, all opioids, no tolerance), other drugs (5-HT3 antagonists, anticholinergics, diuretics, iron, calcium), decreased mobility, poor intake, dehydration, metabolic (hypothyroid, hypercalcaemia), mechanical (obstruction by tumour), neurological (spinal cord compression). Complications: overflow diarrhoea, faecal impaction, urinary retention, delirium, pseudo-obstruction, bowel perforation.


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
History (frequency, consistency, Bristol stool scale), examination (abdomen — distension, masses, bowel sounds; PR — stool, masses, impaction, anal tone), abdominal X-ray (faecal loading, obstruction), bloods (Ca, TSH, glucose, K).

### Management
Prevention: co-prescribe stimulant laxative (senna) + softener (docusate) on opioid initiation. Treat: osmotic (macrogol/Movicol), stimulant (senna, bisacodyl, sodium picosulfate), prokinetic (prucalopride). Combination: stimulant + osmotic (best evidence). Refractory: methylnaltrexone SC (μ-opioid receptor antagonist, doesn't cross BBB — only peripheral side effects), naloxegol (oral), opioid rotation (less constipating = fentanyl, methadone). Bowel management plan: regular, proactive, rescue (enema, suppository, manual evacuation if impaction).

## Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: First-line for opioid-induced constipation? A: Senna + docusate (stimulant + softener), escalate to macrogol.

2. 2. Q: Methylnaltrexone mechanism? A: Peripheral μ-opioid receptor antagonist; doesn't cross BBB, so doesn't reverse analgesia or cause withdrawal.

3. 3. Q: Why prophylactic laxative on opioid? A: All opioids cause constipation, no tolerance, common, disabling.

4. 4. Q: Most constipating opioid? A: Morphine (no advantage); less: fentanyl, methadone.

5. 5. Q: Complications? A: Overflow diarrhoea, impaction, urinary retention, delirium, pseudo-obstruction, perforation.
