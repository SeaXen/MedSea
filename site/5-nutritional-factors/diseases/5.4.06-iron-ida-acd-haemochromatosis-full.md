# Iron- IDA, ACD & Haemochromatosis

**Related:** [[Nutritional Factors in Disease MOC]], [[Davidson Chapter 22 - Nutritional Factors in Disease Hierarchy]], [[../00_Index/Medicine MOC|Medicine MOC]]

> [!important]
> **Iron = Hb/myoglobin/cytochrome Fe-Sulphur; 3–4 g body Fe (65% Hb, 25% ferritin/haemosiderin, 5% myoglobin, 0.1% transferrin); dietary Fe²⁺ (heme, 15–20%) > Fe³⁺ (non-heme, 5–10%); DMT1 enterocyte; ferroportin (hephaestin); hepcidin (master regulator); IDA (microcytic ↓Fe/↓ferritin/↑TIBC) vs ACD (↓Fe/normal-ferritin/normal-TIBC) vs HH (↑Fe/↑ferritin/normal-TIBC).**

## Definitions / Key Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Iron (Fe)** | Transition metal; Fe²⁺ (ferrous, absorbable) vs Fe³⁺ (ferric, requires reduction); stored as Fe³⁺ in ferritin |
| **Heme Iron** | From animal sources (myoglobin, Hb); Fe²⁺ in porphyrin; bioavailability 15–35% (high) |
| **Non-Heme Iron** | Plant sources, supplements; Fe³⁺; bioavailability 5–12% (lower); requires acidification + reduction |
| **DMT1 (Divalent Metal Transporter 1, SLC11A2)** | Apical enterocyte transporter; Fe²⁺ uptake; also Mn²⁺, Co²⁺ |
| **DCYTB (Duodenal Cytochrome B)** | Apical ferrireductase; Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺ for DMT1 |
| **Ferroportin (FPN1, SLC40A1)** | Basolateral enterocyte/macrophage Fe²⁺ exporter; **hepcidin target** |
| **Hephaestin (HEPH)** | Enterocyte basolateral ferroxidase; Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ for transferrin binding |
| **Hepcidin (HAMP)** | 25-aa hepatic peptide; **master Fe regulator**; binds ferroportin → internalisation/degradation → ↓Fe release |
| **Ferritin** | Intracellular Fe storage; apoferritin + Fe³⁺; acute phase reactant (↑in inflammation) |
| **Transferrin (Tf)** | Plasma Fe²⁺-binder (delivers to bone marrow); saturation 20–45% normal |
| **Total Iron Binding Capacity (TIBC)** | Plasma transferrin Fe-binding capacity; ↑in IDA (transferrin ↑); ↓in ACD/HH |
| **Soluble Transferrin Receptor (sTfR)** | Cleaved Tf receptor; ↑in IDA (proliferating erythroid); normal in ACD |
| **Reticuloendothelial Macrophages** | Recycle Fe from senescent RBC (Hb → amino acids + Fe²⁺ → ferroportin → transferrin) |
| **Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA)** | Microcytic (MCV <80), hypochromic; ↓Fe, ↓ferritin, ↓transferrin saturation, ↑TIBC |
| **Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)** | Normocytic (early) or microcytic; ↓Fe, normal/↑ferritin, normal/↓TIBC; hepcidin-mediated |
| **Hereditary Haemochromatosis (HH)** | HFE C282Y (most common) → ↓hepcidin → Fe overload; transferrin saturation >45%, ferritin >200 M/>150 F |
| **Plummer-Vinson** | IDA + dysphagia + oesophageal web/cricopharyngeus; ↑SCC risk; iron repletion resolves |
| **Dysphagia Iron Studies (DI)** | Chronic severe iron deficiency in children; Pica, pagophagia |
| **MDS with Ring Sideroblasts** | SF3B1 mutation; defective Fe utilisation; SF3B1 mitochondrial Fe ring; ring sideroblasts on Perl's stain |
| **Sideroblastic Anaemia** | Iron-loaded mitochondria around nucleus; acquired (MDS, drugs, alcohol) or inherited (ALAS2, SLC25A38) |
| **Restless Legs Syndrome (RLS)** | ↑In IDA; iron supplementation improves (CSF iron/co-factor for tyrosine hydroxylase) |

## Core Content

### Section 1: Iron Metabolism & Absorption
**Body iron (3–4 g, adult):**
- **Hb (65%):** 2.5 g in circulating erythrocytes (2500 mL blood × 1.34 mL O₂/g Hb)
- **Storage (25%):** 0.8 g ferritin (soluble) + haemosiderin (insoluble aggregate) in liver, spleen, marrow
- **Myoglobin (5%):** 150 mg in muscle
- **Cytochromes/Fe-S (0.1%):** Mitochondrial, electron transport
- **Transferrin (0.1%):** 3 mg plasma-bound; "free" Fe negligible (bound)
- **Daily loss:** 1–2 mg (desquamation, menstruation 30–60 mL/month, GI tract, urinary)

**Absorption (duodenum/proximal jejunum):**
- **Heme iron:** Heme oxygenase → porphyrin + Fe²⁺ in enterocyte; absorbed via heme carrier protein 1 (HCP1)
- **Non-heme:** Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺ (DCYTB, ascorbate) → DMT1 (SLC11A2) apical uptake
- **Intracellular:** Fe²⁺ stored in ferritin OR exported (ferroportin FPN1)
- **Basolateral:** Ferroportin exports Fe²⁺ → hephaestin oxidises to Fe³⁺ → transferrin binding
- **Iron loss:** Only 1–2 mg/day; no physiological excretion (vs daily ~20–25 mg turnover via recycling)

**Enhancers of absorption:**
- Ascorbic acid (Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺)
- Meat/fish (MFP factor — promotes non-heme Fe absorption)
- Acidic gastric pH (Fe²⁺ stability)
- Citrus/fruit

**Inhibitors:**
- **Phytates** (whole grains, legumes — soaking/fermenting reduces)
- **Polyphenols/tannins** (tea, coffee, red wine)
- **Calcium** (dairy, supplements)
- **Antacids/PPIs** (↓gastric acid → ↓Fe solubilisation)
- **Oxalates** (spinach, rhubarb)

### Section 2: Hepcidin — Master Iron Regulator
**Hepcidin (HAMP gene):** 25-aa hepatic peptide; ferroportin antagonist.
**Regulation:**
- **↑ Hepcidin (↓ Fe absorption, sequester Fe in macrophages):** Iron overload, inflammation (IL-6 → JAK/STAT3), infection, liver disease (decreased clearance)
- **↓ Hepcidin (↑ Fe absorption, mobilise stores):** Iron deficiency, anaemia, hypoxia, erythropoietic drive (EPO)

**Mechanism:** Hepcidin binds ferroportin → JAK2/β-catenin phosphorylation → ferroportin internalisation/degradation → ↓Fe release from enterocyte/macrophage → ↑intracellular Fe storage.

**Clinical implications:**
- **ACD:** IL-6 → ↑hepcidin → hypoferraemia despite adequate stores
- **HH:** HFE mutation → ↓hepcidin → Fe overload (despite Fe in macrophages)
- **Iron-deficiency anaemia of chronic kidney disease:** ↑hepcidin (CKD = chronic inflammation) → ↓EPO response + ↓Fe utilisation
- **Iron-refractory IDA (IRIDA):** TMPRSS6 mutation → ↑hepcidin → oral Fe ineffective; IV iron required

### Section 3: Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA)
**Stages (progressive):**
1. **Depletion (Stage 1):** ↓Ferritin (iron stores); normal Hb/MCV; transferrin saturation normal
2. **Deficient erythropoiesis (Stage 2):** ↓Serum Fe, ↑TIBC (transferrin), normal Hb initially
3. **Iron deficiency anaemia (Stage 3):** Microcytic (MCV <80), hypochromic RBCs, ↑RDW, penicillate/elliptocytic cells, target cells

**Causes:**
| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Blood loss (chronic)** | **GI:** cancer (gastric, colon, GIST), peptic ulcer, IBD, angiodysplasia, PUD, NSAID; **GU:** menstruation, menorrhagia; **Other:** haematuria, epistaxis |
| **Malabsorption** | Coeliac, atrophic gastritis, H. pylori, RYGB, IBD |
| **Poor intake** | Vegetarian/vegan, elderly, poverty, anorexia |
| **↑Demand** | Pregnancy, infancy, adolescence, EPO therapy, IDA of chronic kidney disease |
| **Drugs** | NSAIDs, PPIs (↓acid → ↓Fe absorption), antacids |
| **Chronic blood donation** | 250 mL/3 months (typical donor) |

**Clinical features:** Fatigue, dyspnoea, palpitations, pica (pagophagia = ice eating), koilonychia (spoon nails), glossitis, angular cheilosis, Plummer-Vinson (web + dysphagia), restless legs syndrome; in children: ↓cognition, growth, immunity.

**Investigations:**
- ↓Hb, MCV <80 (microcytic), hypochromic
- ↓Serum ferritin (<30 µg/L male, <20 µg/L female)
- ↓Serum iron, ↓transferrin saturation (<16%)
- ↑TIBC, ↑sTfR (soluble transferrin receptor)
- ↑RDW, ↑ZPP (zinc protoporphyrin)
- Bone marrow (gold standard): absent iron stores; rarely done

**Treatment:**
| Form | Dose | Elemental Fe | Frequency |
|------|------|--------------|-----------|
| **Ferrous sulphate** | 200 mg | 65 mg | 2–3×/day |
| **Ferrous gluconate** | 300 mg | 35 mg | 3×/day |
| **Ferrous fumarate** | 200 mg | 106 mg | 2×/day |
| **Polysaccharide-Iron Complex (Ferric maltol)** | — | 30 mg/capsule | 2×/day (newer, better tolerated) |
| **IV iron** (Fe sucrose, ferric carboxymaltose, iron isomaltoside) | Varies by preparation | 100–1000 mg per infusion | 1–2 infusions |

**Duration:** Continue **3 months after Hb normal** (replete stores, ferritin >50–100); monitor response (reticulocytes 5–7d, Hb 2–3w, ferritin 2–3m).

**Side effects (oral):** GI upset, constipation, dark stools; take with **vitamin C** (enhance absorption), avoid tea/coffee/calcium within 1–2 h.

**IV iron indications:** Oral intolerance/non-compliance, malabsorption, severe ongoing loss, CKD with ESA, bariatric surgery, IRIDA, IBD; **risk:** anaphylaxis (older dextran > newer formulations), hypophosphataemia (carboxymaltose).

### Section 4: Anaemia of Chronic Disease (ACD)
**Pathophysiology:** Chronic inflammation (cancer, RA, IBD, infection, CKD) → IL-6 → ↑hepcidin → ↓ferroportin → Fe sequestered in macrophages → functional Fe deficiency despite adequate stores.
**Iron studies:**
- ↓Serum Fe (hypoferraemia)
- Normal/↑Ferritin (acute phase reactant; iron stored in macrophages)
- Normal/↓TIBC (transferrin ↓ in inflammation)
- Normal transferrin saturation
- ↑Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR)
- ↑sTfR (less elevated than IDA, distinguishes; **sTfR/log ferritin >2 = IDA**)

**Types:**
- Normocytic normochromic (early, common)
- Microcytic hypochromic (chronic, severe, overlapping with IDA)

**Treatment:** Treat underlying disease; EPO; **IV iron** if functional Fe deficiency (ferritin <100, TSAT <20%); oral iron often ineffective (hepcidin blocks absorption); some response after inflammation resolves.

### Section 5: Iron Overload & Hereditary Haemochromatosis (HH)
**Causes:**
- **Hereditary (HH):** HFE C282Y/H63D mutations (autosomal recessive; Northern European); C282Y homozygosity = 1:200; **TMPRSS6** (regulates hepcidin), HJV, TFR2, ferroportin mutations (rare)
- **Secondary:** Chronic transfusions (thalassaemia major, sickle, MDS, dialysis), ineffective erythropoiesis (sideroblastic, thalassaemia), haemolytic anaemia
- **Parenteral iron overload:** Excess IV iron
- **African iron overload:** Dietary + genetic
- **Neonatal iron overload** (gestational diabetes, rare)

**HFE C282Y/H63D Mechanism:** HFE binds β2-microglobulin on cell surface → interacts with transferrin receptor → regulates hepcidin transcription. C282Y disrupts HFE-β2-M binding → ↓hepcidin → ↑Fe absorption.

**HH Clinical Features (4 "C's" + DM + arthropathy):**
- **Cirrhosis/HCC** (Fe deposition in liver; HCC risk 200× with cirrhosis)
- **Cardiomyopathy** (restrictive, dilated; arrhythmias; sudden death)
- **Chondrocalcinosis** (CPPD arthropathy; 2nd/3rd MCP joint, hook osteophytes)
- **C**utaneous hyperpigmentation ("bronze diabetes" = skin + DM)
- **Diabetes mellitus** (Fe in pancreas; "bronze diabetes" = 3 features)
- **Hypogonadism** (pituitary Fe; ↓LH/FSH)
- **Fatigue, arthralgia, hepatomegaly**
- **Infections:** Listeria, Yersinia, Vibrio vulnificus, *Aeromonas* (siderophilic bacteria thrive in Fe-rich environment)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Transferrin saturation >45%** (most sensitive for HH; should be checked with ferritin)
- **Ferritin >200 µg/L (M) / >150 µg/L (F)**
- **HFE gene testing** (C282Y/H63D)
- **MRI liver** (quantitative Fe assessment; avoids biopsy)
- **Liver biopsy** (Perl's stain, hepatic iron index >1.9)
- **Hepcidin** (research, low in HH)

**Treatment:**
- **Therapeutic phlebotomy** (1 unit 450 mL weekly = 250 mg Fe per unit) until ferritin <50 (target)
- **Iron chelation** (if phlebotomy contraindicated — anaemia, cardiac disease): deferasirox (oral), deferoxamine (SC infusion), deferiprone
- **Low-iron diet** (limit red meat, vitamin C, raw shellfish; tea/coffee inhibit Fe)
- **Avoid alcohol** (synergistic liver damage)
- **HCC surveillance** (cirrhosis; 6-monthly US + AFP)
- **Family screening** (1st-degree relatives; HFE testing)

### Section 6: Sideroblastic Anaemia
**Mitochondrial Fe loading** (ring sideroblasts on Perl's stain); ineffective erythropoiesis.
| Type | Cause | Treatment |
|------|-------|-----------|
| **Inherited** | ALAS2, SLC25A38, ABCB7 mutations; X-linked most common | Pyridoxine (some), transfusions |
| **Acquired - Myelodysplastic** | SF3B1, ASXL1 mutations (MDS-RS) | EPO, luspatercept, transfusion |
| **Acquired - Reversible** | Alcohol, lead poisoning, isoniazid, chloramphenicol, copper deficiency | Remove toxin, pyridoxine |
| **Copper deficiency** | Bariatric, zinc excess, Menkes → ↓hephaestin | Copper repletion |

### Section 7: Other Iron Disorders
**Anaemia of CKD:** Combination of ↓EPO + functional Fe deficiency; treat with ESA + IV iron.
**Iron Refractory IDA (IRIDA):** TMPRSS6 mutation → constitutive hepcidin overexpression; **oral Fe ineffective**; IV iron may help partially.
**Restless Legs Syndrome:** Fe is cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase (dopamine synthesis); ferritin <75 → supplementation (oral or IV); improves RLS in 30–50%.
**Pica (Pagophagia):** Cravings for ice, dirt, starch, paper; resolves with Fe repletion; **highly specific for IDA**.
**Pulmonary Haemosiderosis:** Heiner syndrome (cow's milk hypersensitivity); chronic GI/lung Fe loss; pulmonary infiltrates + IDA.
**Iron & Infection:** Fe supports bacterial growth; withholding Fe in active sepsis sometimes considered (controversial).

## Common Confusions / Exam Traps

| Trap | Correction |
|------|------------|
| IDA = always nutritional | **MUST exclude GI/GU blood loss in adults** (malignancy until proven otherwise) |
| Ferritin normal in IDA | **Ferritin <30 µg/L M, <20 F** = deficient; ferritin is acute phase reactant (↑in inflammation/malignancy) — interpret with CRP |
| ACD = IDA | **ACD: normal/↑ferritin, normal TIBC, hepcidin-mediated**; IDA: ↓ferritin, ↑TIBC |
| HH: give Fe supplementation | **CONTRAINDICATED** — phlebotomy removes Fe |
| Iron supplement with tea | **AVOID tea/coffee/calcium within 1–2 h** — ↓absorption |
| Always IV iron for severe IDA | **Oral Fe effective if tolerated**; IV for intolerance, malabsorption, ongoing loss, CKD |
| Microcytic = iron deficient | **Other causes:** thalassaemia (normal Fe/ferritin), sideroblastic, ACD, lead poisoning |
| RBC transfusion for IDA | **Reserved for severe haemodynamic compromise**; oral/IV Fe preferred; transfusion risks |
| Sideroblastic = IDA | **Sideroblastic: ↑Fe, ↑ferritin, ring sideroblasts**; IDA: ↓Fe, ↓ferritin |
| Pica is psychological | **Pagophagia (ice eating) is HIGHLY SPECIFIC for IDA**; resolves with Fe |

## Mnemonics

- **Body Fe:** **HEMS** = **H**b 65%, **E**rythropoiesis (transferrin 0.1%), **M**yoglobin 5%, **S**torage 25% (ferritin)
- **Fe absorption:** **DCYTB → DMT1** (apical, Fe²⁺) → **Ferritin** (store) → **Ferroportin + Hephaestin** (basolateral, Fe²⁺→Fe³⁺) → **Transferrin**
- **Hepcidin:** **HI** = **H**epcidin **I**ncreased in Inflammation/Fe overload; **D**ecreased in IDA/Hypoxia
- **IDA iron studies:** **↓↓↓** = **↓Fe, ↓Ferritin, ↓Transferrin saturation, ↑↑TIBC, ↑sTfR**; microcytic
- **ACD iron studies:** **↓↓ = Fe/Fe sat** but **Ferritin N/↑, TIBC N/↓, ↑Hepcidin**
- **HH:** **HFE C282Y** → **↓Hepcidin** → **Fe overload**; TSAT >45% (early), Ferritin ↑
- **HH complications (4 C's):** **Cirrhosis, Cardiomyopathy, Chondrocalcinosis, Cutaneous (bronze) + DM + hypogonadism**
- **Iron stages:** **Depletion → Deficient erythropoiesis → Anaemia**
- **Sideroblastic:** **Ring Sideroblast = Iron ring around nucleus** (Perl's); acquired (MDS, alcohol, B6) or inherited (ALAS2)
- **Oral Fe brands:** **S**ulphate (65), **G**luconate (35), **F**umarate (106) = elemental mg/tablet
- **IV Fe:** **Sucrose, Carboxymaltose, Isomaltoside** (newer, fewer anaphylaxis)
- **Pica:** **P**agophagia (ice), **P**ica (dirt), **P**ica (starch) — IDA

## Answer Key with Explanations

### MCQs
1. **B** — Hepcidin (HAMP gene) is the master iron regulator; binds ferroportin → internalisation → ↓iron release.
2. **C** — IDA: iron stores depleted (↓ferritin), transferrin upregulated (↑TIBC), low transferrin saturation.
3. **B** — ACD: iron sequestered in macrophages (↓Fe) but stores normal/↑ (ferritin); hepcidin-mediated.
4. **C** — HFE C282Y (chromosome 6) is the most common HH mutation in Northern European populations.
5. **B** — Therapeutic phlebotomy (1 unit 450 mL = 250 mg Fe weekly) is first-line; chelation only if anaemia prohibits phlebotomy.
6. **B** — Pagophagia (compulsive ice eating) is highly specific for iron deficiency anaemia; resolves with iron repletion.
7. **B** — DMT1 (SLC11A2) on apical enterocyte membrane transports Fe²⁺ (ferrous form); DCYTB reduces Fe³⁺ first.
8. **C** — Ferroportin exports Fe²⁺ across basolateral membrane; hephaestin oxidises to Fe³⁺ for transferrin binding.
9. **C** — Transferrin saturation >45% is the diagnostic threshold for HH screening (with ferritin).
10. **D** — Plummer-Vinson: iron deficiency + dysphagia + oesophageal web + ↑SCC risk; NOT hypercoagulability.
