# Acute hypoxemia approach

> [!important]
> **Acute hypoxemia** (PaO₂ <60 mmHg / SpO₂ <90%) is a **time-critical emergency**. Identify and treat underlying cause (PTX, PE, pneumonia, APO, ARDS). Distinguish **V/Q mismatch (responds to O₂)** from **shunt (refractory)**. ABG + A-a gradient guide the differential.

Related: [[Acute severe breathlessness approach]], [[ABG Interpretation]], [[Oxygen Therapy and NIV]], [[A-a gradient and oxygenation failure]], [[Respiratory Failure]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: Acute hypoxemia + normal A-a gradient = **hypoventilation** (e.g. opioid, NM). Acute hypoxemia + ↑A-a gradient = **V/Q mismatch, shunt, or diffusion limitation**. **Shunt does not respond to O₂** (PaO₂ <500 on 100% O₂). **V/Q mismatch does** (PaO₂ >500 on 100% O₂).

## 1. Definition

**Acute hypoxemia** = PaO₂ <60 mmHg (8 kPa) on room air, or SpO₂ <90% (in absence of chronic lung disease).

### Severity
- **Mild**: PaO₂ 60–80 mmHg (8–10.7 kPa), SpO₂ 90–94%
- **Moderate**: PaO₂ 40–60 mmHg (5.3–8 kPa), SpO₂ 75–89%
- **Severe**: PaO₂ <40 mmHg (<5.3 kPa), SpO₂ <75%

## 2. Pathophysiology

### 5 mechanisms of hypoxemia
1. **Hypoventilation** (low PaCO₂ → low PaO₂; **normal A-a gradient**)
2. **V/Q mismatch** (most common; **↑A-a gradient**; improves with O₂)
3. **Shunt** (perfusion without ventilation; **↑↑A-a**; **refractory to O₂**)
4. **Diffusion limitation** (↑A-a; improves with O₂)
5. **Low FiO₂** (altitude; **normal A-a gradient**)

### Mnemonic: "CHIEF" or "HiLVD"
- **H**ypoventilation
- **L**ow FiO₂
- **V**/Q mismatch
- **D**iffusion limitation
- **S**hunt

## 3. Clinical Assessment

### Symptoms
- **Dyspnoea** (acute)
- **Confusion, agitation, drowsiness** (severe)
- **Cyanosis** (severe)

### Signs
- **Tachypnoea** (compensation)
- **Tachycardia** (compensation)
- **Cyanosis** (severe)
- **Use of accessory muscles**
- **Altered mental status** (severe)

## 4. Diagnosis

### Immediate
- **SpO₂** (continuous monitoring)
- **ABG** (within 30–60 min of O₂)
- **CXR** (bedside)
- **ECG**
- **CTPA** if PE suspected
- **Bedside echo** if RV strain
- **Bloods**: FBC, U&E, CRP, troponin, BNP, D-dimer

### A-a gradient
- **Normal A-a** (5–15 mmHg) + ↑PaCO₂ = **hypoventilation**
- **↑A-a** = V/Q mismatch, shunt, or diffusion
- **↑A-a + ↓PaCO₂** = V/Q mismatch or shunt
- **↑A-a + ↑PaCO₂** = V/Q mismatch with hypoventilation (severe COPD)

## 5. Differential by Mechanism

| Mechanism | A-a | PaCO₂ | O₂ response | Causes |
|-----------|-----|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Hypoventilation** | Normal | ↑ | Improves | Opioid, NM, chest wall |
| **V/Q mismatch** | ↑ | Variable | Improves | Asthma, COPD, pneumonia, PE |
| **Shunt** | ↑↑ | Normal/low | **No** | ARDS, lobar pneumonia, R→L |
| **Diffusion** | ↑ | Variable | Improves | ILD, oedema, PH |
| **Low FiO₂** | Normal | ↓ (hypervent) | Improves | Altitude, suffocation |

## 6. Management

### General
- **Position**: sitting up
- **O₂** to target SpO₂ 94–98% (88–92% if hypercapnic risk)
- **IV access**, monitoring
- **Treat cause**

### Specific
| Cause | Rx |
|-------|-----|
| **Hypoventilation** | Support ventilation, antidote (naloxone), NM Rx |
| **V/Q mismatch** | O₂ + treat cause (bronchodilators, diuretics) |
| **Shunt** | PEEP, prone, treat cause (lung-protective if ARDS) |
| **Diffusion** | O₂ + treat cause (steroids for ILD, diuretics for oedema) |
| **PTX** | Chest drain |
| **PE** | Anticoagulation ± thrombolysis |
| **Pneumonia** | Antibiotics + O₂ |
| **APO** | O₂ + diuretics + NIV |
| **ARDS** | Lung-protective ventilation, prone, treat cause |

### Refractory hypoxemia
- **HFNC** → **NIV** → **intubation** (lung-protective)
- **Prone ventilation** (ARDS)
- **ECMO** (severe refractory)

## 7. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Domain | Key points |
|--------|------------|
| **Definition** | PaO₂ <60 mmHg |
| **5 mechanisms** | Hypoventilation, V/Q, shunt, diffusion, low FiO₂ |
| **Shunt** | Refractory to O₂ (no response) |
| **V/Q mismatch** | Improves with O₂ |
| **A-a gradient** | ↑ in V/Q, shunt, diffusion; normal in hypoventilation |
| **First Rx** | Position + O₂ + treat cause |
| **Refractory** | NIV/HFNC → intubation → prone → ECMO |

## 8. MCQs (10)

1. Acute hypoxemia with normal A-a gradient suggests:
   A. PE
   B. **Hypoventilation**
   C. Shunt
   D. ILD
   E. ARDS
   **Answer: B** — Hypoventilation.

2. Acute hypoxemia refractory to 100% O₂ suggests:
   A. V/Q mismatch
   B. **True shunt**
   C. Hypoventilation
   D. Altitude
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Shunt.

3. The most common cause of acute hypoxaemia in lung disease is:
   A. Shunt
   B. **V/Q mismatch**
   C. Diffusion
   D. None
   E. Hypoventilation
   **Answer: B** — V/Q mismatch.

4. The most sensitive sign of hypoxaemia is:
   A. Cyanosis
   B. **Tachycardia + tachypnoea**
   C. Confusion
   D. Wheeze
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Tachycardia + tachypnoea.

5. Cyanosis becomes visible at SpO₂:
   A. <95%
   B. **<85% (PaO₂ <6.7 kPa)**
   C. <90%
   D. <70%
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — <85%.

6. The most useful bedside test for acute hypoxemia:
   A. ABG only
   B. **SpO₂ (pulse oximetry) + ABG**
   C. CXR only
   D. CT
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — SpO₂ + ABG.

7. Treatment of refractory hypoxemia in ARDS includes:
   A. Steroids
   B. **Prone ventilation, lung-protective, ECMO if needed**
   C. Antibiotics
   D. Theophylline
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Prone, LPV, ECMO.

8. Acute hypoxemia + pleuritic pain + tachycardia. First:
   A. CXR
   B. **Wells + D-dimer → CTPA (PE)**
   C. Discharge
   D. Steroids
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Workup PE.

9. The first-line O₂ device in severe hypoxemia:
   A. Nasal cannula
   B. **High-concentration (15 L NRB mask)**
   C. Simple mask
   D. Venturi
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — NRB.

10. A-a gradient of 50 mmHg in hypoxaemic patient suggests:
    A. Hypoventilation
    B. **V/Q mismatch, shunt, or diffusion limitation**
    C. Altitude
    D. Normal
    E. None
    **Answer: B** — ↑A-a.

## 9. SBA Questions (10)

1. A 60-year-old with APO is hypoxaemic. Best O₂ delivery:
   A. Nasal cannula
   B. **High-flow + NIV (CPAP) + diuretics + GTN**
   C. Venturi
   D. Wait
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — HF + NIV + diuretics.

2. ARDS with refractory hypoxemia despite optimal ventilation:
   A. Increase FiO₂
   B. **Prone ventilation 12–16 h/day, ECMO if needed**
   C. Steroids
   D. Stop
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Prone + ECMO.

3. Hypoxemia in opioid overdose:
   A. Shunt
   B. **Hypoventilation (normal A-a gradient)**
   C. PE
   D. ARDS
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Hypoventilation.

4. The most common cause of hypoxemia in hospitalised patients:
   A. PE
   B. **Pneumonia**
   C. ARDS
   D. Asthma
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Pneumonia.

5. Cyanosis in a patient with SpO₂ 88% indicates:
   A. Mild hypoxemia
   B. **Moderate-severe hypoxemia**
   C. Normal
   D. None
   E. Cardiac cause
   **Answer: B** — Moderate.

6. A patient on HFNC has persistent hypoxemia, RR 35, exhaustion. Next:
   A. Continue HFNC
   B. **Intubate (failure of HFNC)**
   C. Discharge
   D. Wait
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Intubate.

7. The most accurate way to assess oxygenation:
   A. SpO₂ alone
   B. **ABG (PaO₂)**
   C. CXR
   D. CT
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — ABG.

8. A 50-year-old on 100% O₂ has PaO₂ 60 mmHg. P/F ratio:
   A. 100
   B. **60 (severe ARDS)**
   C. 200
   D. 400
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — 60.

9. A normal A-a gradient in hypoxemia points to:
   A. PE
   B. **Hypoventilation or low FiO₂ (altitude)**
   C. ARDS
   D. ILD
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Hypoventilation or altitude.

10. The threshold for severe hypoxemia:
    A. SpO₂ <90%
    B. **PaO₂ <60 mmHg (8 kPa)**
    C. RR >30
    D. None
    E. Cyanosis
    **Answer: B** — PaO₂ <60.

## 10. Flashcards

- **Q: 5 mechanisms of hypoxemia?**
  A: Hypoventilation, low FiO₂, V/Q mismatch, diffusion, shunt.

- **Q: A-a gradient in hypoventilation?**
  A: Normal.

- **Q: A-a gradient in V/Q mismatch/shunt?**
  A: ↑.

- **Q: Shunt response to O₂?**
  A: No (refractory).

- **Q: V/Q mismatch response to O₂?**
  A: Yes (improves).

- **Q: Cyanosis threshold?**
  A: SpO₂ <85% (PaO₂ <6.7 kPa).

- **Q: First-line in refractory ARDS?**
  A: Prone ventilation 12–16 h/day.

- **Q: ARDS Berlin P/F <100?**
  A: Severe.

- **Q: Threshold PaO₂ for severe?**
  A: <60 mmHg (8 kPa).

- **Q: Most common cause in hospital?**
  A: Pneumonia.

## 12. Summary

Acute hypoxemia: SpO₂ <90% / PaO₂ <60 mmHg. 5 mechanisms: hypoventilation, V/Q, shunt, diffusion, low FiO₂. A-a gradient: normal = hypoventilation/altitude; ↑ = V/Q/shunt/diffusion. Shunt refractory to O₂; V/Q improves. Treat underlying cause + O₂. Refractory: prone, ECMO.
