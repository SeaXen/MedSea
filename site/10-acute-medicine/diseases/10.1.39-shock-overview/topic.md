> [!important]
> Shock = **inadequate tissue perfusion and oxygenation** despite adequate circulating volume or cardiac output. It is a **time-critical emergency** — early recognition and resuscitation save lives.

## 1. Learning Objectives
- Define shock and classify its types
- Understand the common final pathway of cellular hypoxia
- Apply a rapid clinical approach to any shocked patient
- Recognise compensated vs decompensated shock
- Initiate immediate resuscitation while identifying the underlying cause

## 2. Definition
Shock is a state of **acute circulatory failure** leading to **inadequate tissue perfusion** and **cellular hypoxia**, resulting in cellular dysfunction and, if untreated, organ failure and death.

Key concept: **Blood pressure may be normal** in early/compensated shock.

## 3. Core Physiology
- Oxygen delivery (DO₂) = Cardiac Output (CO) × Arterial Oxygen Content (CaO₂)
- CO = Heart Rate (HR) × Stroke Volume (SV)
- SV depends on preload, afterload, and contractility
- Shock occurs when DO₂ is insufficient to meet tissue oxygen demand

## 4. Normal Values / Important Cut-offs
- Lactate: >2 mmol/L → tissue hypoperfusion (≥4 mmol/L = severe)
- MAP target in septic shock: ≥65 mmHg
- ScvO₂ target: ≥70%
- Urine output: ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr
- Base deficit: >6 → significant hypoperfusion

## 5. Classification
| Type                  | Mechanism                          | Examples                          | Skin          | CVP   | CO    |
|-----------------------|------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|---------------|-------|-------|
| **Hypovolaemic**      | Reduced preload                    | Haemorrhage, dehydration          | Cool, clammy  | Low   | Low   |
| **Cardiogenic**       | Pump failure                       | MI, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmia    | Cool, clammy  | High  | Low   |
| **Obstructive**       | Mechanical obstruction             | PE, tamponade, tension PTX        | Cool, clammy  | High  | Low   |
| **Distributive**      | Vasodilatation + capillary leak    | Sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic   | Warm          | Low   | High  |
| **Septic**            | Distributive + myocardial depression | Gram-negative / positive sepsis | Warm → cold   | Low   | High → Low |

## 7. Pathophysiology (Common Final Pathway)
1. Inadequate perfusion → cellular hypoxia
2. Anaerobic metabolism → lactate production
3. Cellular swelling, mitochondrial dysfunction
4. Inflammatory cascade activation
5. Endothelial damage, capillary leak
6. Worsening hypovolaemia and myocardial depression (vicious cycle)
7. Irreversible cell death and multi-organ failure

## 8. Clinical Features
**Compensated shock** (early)
- Tachycardia, tachypnoea
- Narrow pulse pressure
- Cool peripheries, delayed capillary refill
- Anxiety, mild confusion
- Normal or slightly low BP

**Decompensated shock** (late)
- Hypotension
- Altered mental status
- Oliguria / anuria
- Cold, mottled skin
- Metabolic acidosis, high lactate

## 9. Approach / Algorithm (ABCDE + Source Control)
1. **Airway** — protect if GCS low
2. **Breathing** — high-flow O₂, consider intubation
3. **Circulation** — 2 large-bore IV cannulae, bloods, lactate, blood gas
4. **Disability** — AVPU/GCS, blood glucose
5. **Exposure** — full exposure, look for source (rash, wound, abdomen, legs)

Simultaneously:
- Fluid challenge (250–500 mL crystalloid)
- Identify type of shock clinically + bedside echo/US
- Early source control (antibiotics, drainage, surgery)

## 10. Investigations
- **Immediate**: VBG/ABG + lactate, FBC, U&E, LFT, coagulation, glucose, troponin, ECG, CXR
- **Targeted**: Echo (bedside), CT (if stable), cultures, procalcitonin, etc.

## 11. Interpretation Frameworks
- Lactate clearance: aim for ≥10–20% reduction every 2 hours
- ScvO₂ or SvO₂: low in most shocks except distributive (may be high)

## 12. Management (General Principles)
- **Oxygen** — target SpO₂ 94–98% (or 88–92% in COPD)
- **Fluids** — crystalloids first (balanced solutions preferred)
- **Vasopressors** — start early if fluid-refractory (noradrenaline first-line in most cases)
- **Inotropes** — dobutamine if low cardiac output
- **Source control** — within 1–3 hours for sepsis
- **Monitoring** — arterial line, central line, urine output, lactate

## 13. Red Flags / Emergencies
- Lactate ≥4 mmol/L
- Hypotension not responding to 1–2 L fluid
- Altered consciousness
- Signs of tamponade, massive PE, or tension pneumothorax

## 14. Prognosis
- Mortality rises sharply with duration of shock and number of organs failing
- Early goal-directed therapy + source control significantly improves survival

## 15. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- Shock can exist with **normal blood pressure** (especially in young patients)
- Warm peripheries + high CO classically point to **distributive shock**
- Always measure **lactate** — it is the single best bedside marker of tissue hypoperfusion
- In any shocked patient, think **"Is this hypovolaemic, cardiogenic, obstructive, or distributive?"**

## 17. Common Confusions / Exam Traps
- Assuming hypotension is required for diagnosis of shock
- Treating all shock with large-volume fluids (dangerous in cardiogenic/obstructive)
- Delaying antibiotics/source control while chasing "more fluids"

## 18. One-Page Revision Summary
