> [!important]
> The **ABCDE** approach is the universal systematic method for assessing and treating any acutely ill or deteriorating patient. It must be repeated until the patient is stable.

## 1. Learning Objectives
- Perform a rapid, structured ABCDE assessment
- Identify life-threatening problems in order of priority
- Initiate immediate life-saving interventions
- Know when to call for help / escalate

## 2. The ABCDE Sequence
**A – Airway**
- Assess patency (talking, stridor, secretions, GCS)
- Interventions: head tilt-chin lift, jaw thrust, suction, airway adjuncts, intubation if needed

**B – Breathing**
- Look, listen, feel (RR, SpO₂, effort, chest movement, breath sounds)
- Interventions: high-flow O₂, bag-mask ventilation, chest drain for tension PTX

**C – Circulation**
- Pulse, BP, capillary refill, skin colour, JVP, heart sounds, ECG
- Interventions: IV access, fluid challenge, haemorrhage control, CPR if pulseless

**D – Disability**
- AVPU or GCS, pupils, blood glucose, seizures
- Interventions: glucose, benzodiazepines for seizures

**E – Exposure**
- Full exposure while preventing hypothermia
- Look for rash, wounds, drains, abdomen, legs, back

## 3. Key Principles
- Treat problems as you find them (do not complete full assessment before acting)
- Reassess after every intervention
- Call for help early (MET / ICU / senior)
- Use monitoring (pulse oximeter, ECG, BP) from the start

## 4. Red Flags Requiring Immediate Escalation
- Airway compromise
- SpO₂ <90% despite O₂
- SBP <90 mmHg
- GCS <12 or falling
- Lactate >4 mmol/L

## 5. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- The ABCDE approach is **not** a one-time assessment — it is repeated
- Always assume cervical spine injury in trauma until proven otherwise
- Hypoglycaemia is a reversible cause of altered consciousness — check glucose early

  A: Airway → Breathing → Circulation → Disability → Exposure

  A: Talking normally = patent
