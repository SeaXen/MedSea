# Polycystic ovary syndrome in adolescents

---

## Definition

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is the commonest endocrine disorder in women of reproductive age, with onset commonly in adolescence. It is defined by the Rotterdam criteria (>=2 of: oligo/anovulation, hyperandrogenism [clinical or biochemical], polycystic ovaries on ultrasound), with adult criteria requiring exclusion of other causes. In adolescents, ultrasound is less reliable (PCOM appearance is normal in 30% of adolescents), so diagnosis is clinical - persistent menstrual irregularity >2 years post-menarche plus hyperandrogenism. PCOS is associated with insulin resistance, obesity, type 2 diabetes, infertility, and increased cardiovascular risk. Management is lifestyle first, hormonal regulation, and metformin in select cases.

## Core Clinical Content

### Prevalence
8-13% of reproductive-age women

### Rotterdam criteria
>=2 of: oligo/anovulation, hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries

### Adolescent diagnosis
Clinical: persistent menstrual irregularity >2 yrs post-menarche + hyperandrogenism

### Hyperandrogenism clinical
Hirsutism (modified Ferriman-Gallwey >=8), acne, alopecia

### Hyperandrogenism biochemical
Increased free testosterone, increased androstenedione; LH:FSH ratio (2:1)

### PCOS phenotypes
A (all 3), B (anovulation+HA), C (HA+PCOM), D (anovulation+PCOM)

### Insulin resistance
Common; check fasting glucose/insulin, OGTT in obese

### Differential
CAH (17-OH progesterone), androgen-secreting tumour, Cushing, thyroid

### First-line management
Lifestyle: weight loss 5-10% restores ovulation

### Pharmacological
OCP (cyclical regulation), metformin (IR/T2DM), spironolactone (hirsutism)

**Q6. What are the key complications or red flags of Polycystic ovary syndrome in adolescents?**
> Red flags and complications: Pharmacological - OCP (cyclical regulation), metformin (IR/T2DM), spironolactone (hirsutism). Always consider safeguarding, mental health comorbidity, and transition planning in adolescent presentations.
