# Disorders of sex development

---

## Definition

Disorders of sex development (DSD) are congenital conditions in which chromosomal, gonadal, or anatomical sex development is atypical. The 2006 Chicago Consensus classifies DSD into: sex chromosome DSD (Turner 45,X; Klinefelter 47,XXY; mixed gonadal dysgenesis 45,X/46,XY), 46,XY DSD (androgen insensitivity syndrome, 5-alpha reductase deficiency, gonadal dysgenesis), and 46,XX DSD (congenital adrenal hyperplasia, aromatase deficiency, MRKH). Incidence ~1 in 4500 births. Multidisciplinary team management is essential (endocrinology, urology, psychology, genetics). Most present in neonates with ambiguous genitalia or in adolescents with primary amenorrhoea/delayed puberty. Management: sex assignment (now deferred where possible), hormone replacement, surgical timing, psychological support, disclosure counselling.

**Q2. What are the most important FCPS/MRCP high-yield points for Disorders of sex development?**
> DSD definition: Congenital conditions with atypical chromosomal/gonadal/anatomical sex development. Incidence: ~1 in 4500 births. Sex chromosome DSD: Turner 45X, Klinefelter 47XXY, mixed 45X/46XY. See FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table below.

**Q3. How is Disorders of sex development recognised clinically?**
> Presentation and key clinical features include: 46,XY DSD - Androgen insensitivity, 5-alpha reductase deficiency, gonadal dysgenesis; 46,XX DSD - CAH (most common), aromatase deficiency, MRKH. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q4. What investigations are required for Disorders of sex development?**
> Investigations include: CAH classic - 21-hydroxylase deficiency; virilisation; salt-wasting (1 in 10,000); AIS complete - 46,XY female phenotype; primary amenorrhoea; testes intra-abdominal. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q5. What is the first-line management of Disorders of sex development?**
> First-line management: MRKH - 46,XX female; absent uterus/vagina; normal ovaries; primary amenorrhoea; MDT - Endocrinology, urology, psychology, genetics, gynaecology. Refer to specialist adolescent services for definitive management where indicated.

**Q6. What are the key complications or red flags of Disorders of sex development?**
> Red flags and complications: Sex assignment - DSD consensus: deferred where possible; individualised. Always consider safeguarding, mental health comorbidity, and transition planning in adolescent presentations.
