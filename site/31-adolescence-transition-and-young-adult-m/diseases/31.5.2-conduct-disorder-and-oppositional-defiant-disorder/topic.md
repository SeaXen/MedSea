# Conduct disorder and oppositional defiant disorder

---

## Definition

Conduct disorder (CD) is a persistent pattern of behaviour in adolescents that violates the basic rights of others or major age-appropriate societal norms, including aggression to people/animals, destruction of property, deceitfulness/theft, and serious rule violations. Oppositional defiant disorder (ODD) is a pattern of angry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant behaviour, or vindictiveness lasting >=6 months, but without the serious violations of CD. CD affects 3-5% of adolescents, M:F 3:1, often comorbid with ADHD, depression, substance use. Management: multimodal — parent management training (PMT), CBT, social skills training, school intervention; severe cases may need residential or forensic placement. Prognosis improves with early intervention; ~50% desist by adulthood.

## Core Clinical Content

### Conduct disorder DSM-5
>=3 criteria in 15 months from 4 groups: aggression, property, deceit, rules

### ODD DSM-5
Angry/irritable, argumentative/defiant, vindictive >=6 months

### Prevalence
CD 3-5%; ODD 2-16%; M:F 3:1

### Comorbidity
ADHD (50%), depression, anxiety, substance use, learning disability

### Risk factors
Genetics, harsh parenting, ACEs, poverty, school failure

### Severity specifiers
Mild, moderate, severe; age of onset (before/after 10 yrs)

### First-line therapy
Parent management training (PMT), CBT, social skills, school intervention

### Pharmacotherapy
Risperidone for severe aggression; stimulants for comorbid ADHD

### Prognosis
50% desist by adulthood; worse with early onset, comorbidity, criminality

### Differential
ODD, ADHD, bipolar, ASD, depression, substance use

**Q2. What are the most important FCPS/MRCP high-yield points for Conduct disorder and oppositional defiant disorder?**
> Conduct disorder DSM-5: >=3 criteria in 15 months from 4 groups: aggression, property, deceit, rules. ODD DSM-5: Angry/irritable, argumentative/defiant, vindictive >=6 months. Prevalence: CD 3-5%; ODD 2-16%; M:F 3:1. See FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table below.

**Q3. How is Conduct disorder and oppositional defiant disorder recognised clinically?**
> Presentation and key clinical features include: Comorbidity - ADHD (50%), depression, anxiety, substance use, learning disability; Risk factors - Genetics, harsh parenting, ACEs, poverty, school failure. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q4. What investigations are required for Conduct disorder and oppositional defiant disorder?**
> Investigations include: Severity specifiers - Mild, moderate, severe; age of onset (before/after 10 yrs); First-line therapy - Parent management training (PMT), CBT, social skills, school intervention. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q5. What is the first-line management of Conduct disorder and oppositional defiant disorder?**
> First-line management: Pharmacotherapy - Risperidone for severe aggression; stimulants for comorbid ADHD; Prognosis - 50% desist by adulthood; worse with early onset, comorbidity, criminality. Refer to specialist adolescent services for definitive management where indicated.

**Q6. What are the key complications or red flags of Conduct disorder and oppositional defiant disorder?**
> Red flags and complications: Differential - ODD, ADHD, bipolar, ASD, depression, substance use. Always consider safeguarding, mental health comorbidity, and transition planning in adolescent presentations.

- **'HEEADSSS' assessment** - Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression, Safety (3.0: + Eating, Sleep, Screen) - always use in adolescent history
- **'Gillick competence'** - under-16 with sufficient understanding to make own medical decisions
- **'Fraser guidelines'** - specific criteria for contraceptive/sexual health advice without parental knowledge
- **'HEADSS' / 'SSHADESS'** - alternative psychosocial assessment frameworks (Strengths-based)
