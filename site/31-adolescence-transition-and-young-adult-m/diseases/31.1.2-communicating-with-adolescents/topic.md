# Communicating with adolescents

---

## Definition

Effective communication with adolescents is the cornerstone of adolescent medicine, requiring a confidential, non-judgemental, developmentally-appropriate approach. The HEADSS or HEEADSSS psychosocial interview is the validated framework (Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression, Safety; expanded to include Eating and Sleep). Effective engagement requires time alone with the adolescent (without parents), establishing confidentiality, using open questions, avoiding jargon, and being aware of the impact of parental presence on disclosure. Communication is consistently cited as the most important adolescent clinical skill in FCPS/MRCP vivas.

## Core Clinical Content

### Time alone
Always offer to see adolescent without parents for at least part of the consultation

### Confidentiality
Explain limits upfront: confidential unless you or someone else is at risk

### HEADSS framework
Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression

### HEEADSSS expansion
Adds Eating, Sleep, Safety, Suicide (modern 3.0 adds Screen time)

### Open questions
Avoid leading questions; tell me about, how do you feel about

### Engagement order
Start with less sensitive (school, hobbies), build to more personal (sex, drugs)

### Triadic consultation
Adolescent + parent + clinician; balance parental concerns with adolescent voice

### Body language
Eye level, no barriers, unhurried, no laptop screen blocking

### 3-month rule
Teens often cannot plan beyond 3 months - use this for appointments and contraception

### Termination
Summarise, agree plan, clarify follow-up; do not break trust at the end

**Q3. How is Communicating with adolescents recognised clinically?**
> Presentation and key clinical features include: HEEADSSS expansion - Adds Eating, Sleep, Safety, Suicide (modern 3.0 adds Screen time); Open questions - Avoid leading questions; tell me about, how do you feel about. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q4. What investigations are required for Communicating with adolescents?**
> Investigations include: Engagement order - Start with less sensitive (school, hobbies), build to more personal (sex, drugs); Triadic consultation - Adolescent + parent + clinician; balance parental concerns with adolescent voice. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q5. What is the first-line management of Communicating with adolescents?**
> First-line management: Body language - Eye level, no barriers, unhurried, no laptop screen blocking; 3-month rule - Teens often cannot plan beyond 3 months - use this for appointments and contraception. Refer to specialist adolescent services for definitive management where indicated.

**Q6. What are the key complications or red flags of Communicating with adolescents?**
> Red flags and complications: Termination - Summarise, agree plan, clarify follow-up; do not break trust at the end. Always consider safeguarding, mental health comorbidity, and transition planning in adolescent presentations.

## Confusions & Mnemonics

- **Mnemonic for Communicating with adolescents** - see the FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table above for the 10 high-yield facts
- **'HEEADSSS' assessment** - Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression, Safety (3.0: + Eating, Sleep, Screen) - always use in adolescent history
- **'Gillick competence'** - under-16 with sufficient understanding to make own medical decisions
- **'Fraser guidelines'** - specific criteria for contraceptive/sexual health advice without parental knowledge
- **'HEADSS' / 'SSHADESS'** - alternative psychosocial assessment frameworks (Strengths-based)
