# Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA)

> **High-Yield Definition:** CAA: deposition of amyloid-β in small-to-medium cortical and leptomeningeal arteries. Causes lobar ICH, microbleeds, superficial siderosis. Elderly, sporadic, associated with APOE ε4/ε2 alleles.

## Definition
- **Cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA)**: amyloid-β deposition in walls of small cortical and leptomeningeal arteries
- Causes: lobar intracerebral haemorrhage, cerebral microbleeds, cortical superficial siderosis (cSS), CAA-related inflammation (CAA-RI)
- **Sporadic** (most common, elderly)
- **Familial** (rare, autosomal dominant, early-onset, e.g., Dutch, Flemish, Icelandic types)

## Epidemiology
- Prevalence: increases with age
- 30-40% of lobar ICH in elderly (>60y)
- 90% of lobar ICH in >70y
- Rare <55y (consider familial)
- M = F
- APOE ε4 allele: increased CAA risk and ICH risk
- APOE ε2 allele: increased ICH risk (vessel vulnerability)

## Pathology
- Amyloid-β (Aβ40 predominantly, Aβ42 in plaques) deposition in:
  - Cortical and leptomeningeal small-medium arteries
  - Tunica media and adventitia
  - Vessel wall thickening, fragmentation
  - **Double-barrel lumen** (pathognomonic)
- Vessel fragility → rupture → lobar ICH, microbleeds, cSS
- Spared: deep grey nuclei, brainstem, cerebellum (supplied by perforating arteries, not affected by CAA)

## Modified Boston Criteria (Probable CAA)
- Age ≥55y
- Multiple haemorrhages restricted to lobar, cortical, or subcortical regions
  - **Strictly lobar ICH** (single)
  - **Focal or disseminated cSS**
  - **Cortical and/or lobar microbleeds**
- Exclude: deep haemorrhage, other cause (hypertension, AVM, tumour, trauma)
- MRI (T2* GRE or SWI): haemorrhage, microbleed, cSS detection

## Clinical Features
- **Lobar ICH** (recurrent, elderly)
- **Cerebral microbleeds**: silent, often multiple, lobar (cortical, subcortical)
- **Cortical superficial siderosis (cSS)**: hemosiderin on surface, may cause transient focal neurological episodes (TFNE - "amyloid spells")
- **CAA-related inflammation (CAA-RI)**: subacute headache, encephalopathy, seizures, focal deficit, MRI shows vasogenic oedema
- Cognitive impairment (often co-existent AD)
- **Transient focal neurological episodes (TFNE)**: spreading paresthesias, weakness, often stereotyped, transient (<30 min), may mimic TIA, may precede ICH
- Convexity SAH (rare)

## Investigations
- **MRI brain with SWI/T2* GRE** (essential):
  - **Lobar microbleeds** (cortical, subcortical, lobar)
  - **Cortical superficial siderosis** (cSS - hemosiderin on surface)
  - Lobar ICH
  - CAA-RI: vasogenic oedema (often subcortical white matter), enhancing
  - Spared deep grey, brainstem, cerebellum
- **APOE genotype** (ε4, ε2)
- Exclude: hypertensive ICH (deep), AVM, cavernoma, tumour, coagulopathy, trauma
- **CSF** (CAA-RI): elevated protein, may have pleocytosis
- **PET-PIB** (Pittsburgh compound B): amyloid imaging (research)

## Management
- **BP control**: <130/80 (reduce recurrent ICH risk - PROGRESS trial)
- **Avoid antithrombotics**: aspirin, anticoagulants (high risk of ICH - especially if lobar microbleeds/cSS - HAS-BLED score)
- **Statin**: controversial (may increase ICH risk in CAA, but other CV benefit)
- **Lifestyle**: smoking cessation, alcohol moderation, exercise
- **CAA-RI**: immunosuppression (high-dose methylprednisolone, IVIG, cyclophosphamide - may respond)
- **TFNE**: observation, consider avoiding anticoagulants/antiplatelets if high-risk
- **Surgery**: avoid neurosurgery if possible (high risk of ICH)
- **Multidisciplinary**: neurology, neurosurgery (cautious), geriatrics, stroke, haematology

## Red Flags
- Recurrent lobar ICH (especially elderly)
- TFNE/amyloid spells (may precede ICH)
- CAA-RI (headache, encephalopathy, seizures)
- Microbleeds, cSS on MRI
- High ICH risk if anticoagulated
- Avoid thrombolysis in lobar ICH (high risk)
- APOE ε4/ε2 carriers (increased risk)
- Family history (rare familial)

## Prognosis
- Recurrence: 5-10% per year for lobar ICH
- Mortality per lobar ICH: 20-50% (lower than hypertensive)
- CAA-RI: usually responds to immunosuppression, may recur
- TFNE: often precede ICH, risk stratification uncertain
- Cognitive decline (often co-existent AD)
- Long-term: BP control, lifestyle, avoid triggers, monitor, family screening if familial (rare)
- Quality of life: often impaired (cognitive, recurrent ICH)
- Multidisciplinary essential
