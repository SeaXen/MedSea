# Subarachnoid Haemorrhage: Core Concept

## Learning Objectives
- Define spontaneous SAH and distinguish from traumatic SAH.
- Identify the relative frequency of aneurysmal vs non-aneurysmal causes.
- Recognise the typical demographic and risk-factor profile.
- Outline the natural history and rationale for urgent investigation.

## Definition
- **Subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH)** is bleeding into the subarachnoid space (between the arachnoid and pia mater) from a ruptured intracranial vessel.
- **Spontaneous (non-traumatic) SAH** is the focus of this section; ~85 % is aneurysmal.
- Traumatic SAH is managed under head-injury pathways and has different prognostic implications.

## Epidemiology
- Annual incidence: ~6–10 per 100 000 population in most Western series.
- Peak age: 50–60 years; women affected more than men (~1.5:1).
- Accounts for ~5 % of all strokes but disproportionate mortality (~30–40 % 30-day case fatality).
- Aneurysmal SAH carries a hereditary component: 5–10 % have a first-degree relative with SAH.

## Aetiology — Davidson 24e Perspective
- **Aneurysmal (85 %)** — saccular ("berry") aneurysm rupture, typically at arterial bifurcations of the circle of Willis.
- **Perimesencephalic non-aneurysmal (PNSAH) (~10 %)** — venous or capillary bleed with normal angiography.
- **Other (~5 %)** — AVM, dural arteriovenous fistula, mycotic aneurysm, arterial dissection, pituitary apoplexy, reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome (RCVS).

## Risk Factors
- **Modifiable**: hypertension, cigarette smoking, heavy alcohol, sympathomimetic drugs (cocaine, amphetamines).
- **Non-modifiable**: family history of SAH, autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD), Ehlers–Danlos type IV, Marfan syndrome, coarctation of the aorta, fibromuscular dysplasia.
- Female sex, Black ethnicity, low socioeconomic status.

## Natural History
- 10–15 % die before reaching hospital.
- 30-day mortality ~35 %; 50 % of survivors have long-term cognitive or functional disability.
- Rebleeding risk is highest in the first 24 h (~4 %); risk falls after aneurysm securing.
- Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks days 4–14.

## Clinical Clues
- Sudden "thunderclap" headache — peak intensity within seconds.
- Often described as "the worst headache of my life" or "exploding head".
- Nausea/vomiting, photophobia, neck stiffness (meningismus).
- Transient or persistent loss of consciousness in ~50 %.
- Focal deficits (e.g., third-nerve palsy with posterior communicating artery aneurysm).

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- The exam tests the **diagnostic pathway** (CT → LP → CTA), **severity grading** (WFNS, Hunt & Hess, modified Fisher scale), and **management sequencing** (secure aneurysm first, then address DCI and hydrocephalus).
- Pitfall: missing a sentinel headache (warning leak) 6–20 days before major SAH in up to 30–50 % of patients.

## Viva Questions

**Q1. Define SAH and the most common cause.**
> Spontaneous SAH = bleeding into the subarachnoid space from a ruptured intracranial vessel; 85 % is aneurysmal (berry aneurysm at arterial bifurcations, most commonly ACoA).

**Q2. What is the investigation pathway for suspected SAH with negative CT?**
> Lumbar puncture 12 h after ictus, looking for xanthochromia. If positive → CTA → DSA. If CT positive → CTA directly.

**Q3. Why is nimodipine given in SAH?**
> To reduce delayed cerebral ischaemia (DCI). Mechanism: neuroprotective and microcirculatory, not primarily vasodilation. Dose: 60 mg PO 4-hourly × 21 days.

**Q4. Differentiate cerebral salt wasting from SIADH.**
> CSW: hypovolaemic hyponatraemia from natriuresis, treat with isotonic saline + salt ± fludrocortisone. SIADH: euvolaemic hyponatraemia from ADH excess, treat with fluid restriction. CSW has high urine output + hypovolaemia; SIADH has euvolaemia.

**Q5. What is the role of EVD in SAH?**
> Treats acute obstructive hydrocephalus (within 24 h) by draining CSF to control ICP. Long-term CSF diversion (VP shunt) needed for chronic communicating hydrocephalus (typically 2–4 weeks).

**Q6. Compare coiling vs clipping per ISAT.**
> ISAT (2002) showed coiling has better 1-year functional outcome than clipping when both techniques feasible. However, MCA aneurysms are under-represented; clipping is still preferred for MCA bifurcation. Decision is MDT-led.

**Q7. What is Terson syndrome?**
> Vitreous haemorrhage on fundoscopy from raised ICP, indicating severe SAH and worse prognosis. Often resolves spontaneously.

**Q8. Why is BP controlled differently pre- and post-securing?**
> Pre-securing: SBP <160 to reduce rebleed. Post-securing: liberalise to SBP ≤200 to maintain cerebral perfusion, especially with DCI risk.

## Confusions & Mnemonics

- **"ACoA → bitemporal hemianopia"** — Anterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses the optic chiasm from above.
- **"PCoA → pupil-involving third"** — Posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III, classically producing a "surgical" third nerve palsy (pupil fixed/dilated, eye down-and-out, ptosis).
- **"MCA bifurcation = surgical"** — Most amenable to surgical clipping; coiling less preferred.
- **"PNSAH = perimesencephalic = positive prognosis"** — Perimesencephalic non-aneurysmal SAH has excellent prognosis.
- **"Triple-H = Euvolaemia + Hypertension only"** — Hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out of favour.
- **"Cushing's triad"** — Hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respiration = raised ICP, imminent herniation.
- **"Cerebral T waves"** — Deep symmetric T-wave inversions = SAH/Takotsubo from catecholamine surge.
- **"Day 7 = DCI peak"** — Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks day 7, range 4–14.

1. A 52-year-old woman presents with sudden severe "thunderclap" headache, neck stiffness, and photophobia. CT head shows hyperdensity in the basal cisterns. What is the most likely cause?

## Investigation and Management Summary

This overview file does not duplicate the detailed sub-files. For specific investigation and management details, see the dedicated sub-files. Brief summary:

### Investigation
- **Non-contrast CT** within 6 h (sensitivity ~98-100%)
- **Lumbar puncture** at 12 h if CT negative (xanthochromia)
- **CTA** immediately after SAH confirmed
- **DSA** if CTA negative or for definitive vascular anatomy
- **Modified Fisher scale** to predict vasospasm risk
- **ECG**, **troponin**, **daily U&Es** (CSW vs SIADH)

### Management (key principles)
- **Nimodipine 60 mg PO 4-hourly × 21 days** (DCI prophylaxis)
- **BP control** (SBP < 160 pre-securing, liberalise post-securing)
- **Isotonic IV fluids** (avoid hypotonic)
- **Analgesia + laxatives** (avoid Valsalva, NSAIDs)
- **VTE prophylaxis**: mechanical from admission, LMWH 24 h post-securing
- **Secure aneurysm within 72 h** (coiling or clipping, MDT decision)
- **Monitor for DCI, hydrocephalus, hyponatraemia**
