# Myoclonus

Related: [[Movement Disorders Hub]], [[Hyperkinetic Movement Disorders Hub]], [[Epilepsy]], [[CJD]], [[Corticobasal Degeneration]]

> [!tip] **Myoclonus = sudden, brief, shock-like involuntary jerks** from muscle contraction (positive) or loss of tone (negative/asterixis). Classify by **physiology (cortical, subcortical, spinal, peripheral)** AND **aetiology** for treatment.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] Define myoclonus and classify by physiology, aetiology, distribution
- [ ] Distinguish cortical, subcortical (brainstem), spinal, peripheral myoclonus
- [ ] Identify key causes: CJD, Lance-Adams, post-hypoxic, metabolic, drug, epileptic, essential
- [ ] Order: MRI brain, EEG, EEG-EMG back-averaging, SEP, bloods, metabolic, autoimmune
- [ ] Treat: valproate, levetiracetam, clonazepam, piracetam, ± immunomodulation

## 1. Definition / Epidemiology / Classification

**Definition:** Sudden, brief (<100 ms), involuntary, shock-like muscle jerks (positive) or sudden loss of muscle tone (negative myoclonus = asterixis). Can be focal, segmental, multifocal, or generalised.

**Epidemiology:** Common; cortical myoclonus most frequent, especially in epilepsy, dementia, metabolic, drug-induced.

**Classification (by physiological origin):**
| Type | Generator | Clinical | EEG-EMG | Examples |
|------|-----------|----------|---------|----------|
| **Cortical** | Sensorimotor cortex | Focal/multifocal distal, stimulus-sensitive | Polyspike/sharp wave preceding jerk by 10-30 ms (back-averaging) | Epilepsy (JME, PME), post-anoxic (Lance-Adams), CJD, AD, CBD, Rett, coeliac, anti-GAD |
| **Cortical-subcortical** | Cortex + thalamus | Generalised, bilateral | Generalised polyspike | Myoclonic seizures, JME |
| **Subcortical/brainstem (reticular)** | Brainstem reticular formation | Generalised, axial, startle, reflex | No consistent cortical correlate | Startle, hyperekplexia, palatal myoclonus, brainstem myoclonus |
| **Spinal** | Spinal cord | Segmental, often persistent | No cortical correlate | Spinal cord injury, MS, tumours, syrinx, propriospinal myoclonus, post-anoxic |
| **Peripheral** | Peripheral nerve, root, plexus | Focal, follows distribution | No cortical correlate | Hemifacial spasm, nerve injury, post-traumatic |

**Classification (by aetiology):**
- **Physiological:** Sleep jerks (hypnic), hiccup, exercise, anxiety
- **Essential:** Hereditary or sporadic, no other neurological signs
- **Epileptic:** Seizure disorder (EPC, JME, PME, absences with myoclonus)
- **Symptomatic (secondary):** Metabolic, drug, neurodegenerative, autoimmune, infectious, paraneoplastic, post-hypoxic, toxic, focal CNS lesion

## 2. Aetiology / Pathophysiology

**Key aetiologies:**
- **Post-hypoxic (Lance-Adams):** Action myoclonus after hypoxic brain injury; cortical reflex myoclonus
- **Neurodegenerative:** CJD (startle myoclonus), Alzheimer's, DLB, CBD, PSP, Huntington's
- **Metabolic:** Uraemia, hepatic failure, hyponatraemia, hypoglycaemia, hypocalcaemia, CO2 retention
- **Drugs/toxins:** SSRIs, MAOIs, lithium, anticonvulsants (phénytoin, carbamazepine, gabapentin, pregabalin), opioids, levodopa, dopaminergics, bismuth, heavy metals
- **Autoimmune:** Anti-GAD, anti-NMDA, anti-LGI1, coeliac disease (anti-GAD), MS, PANDAS
- **Infectious:** CJD, HIV, subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE), Whipple's
- **Paraneoplastic:** Anti-Ma, anti-Hu, anti-CV2/CRMP5
- **Genetic:** Lafora disease, Unverricht-Lundborg, mitochondrial (MERRF), Huntington's, DRPLA, GM1/GM2

**Pathophysiology:** Hyperexcitability of sensorimotor cortex (cortical), brainstem reticular formation (reticular), or local spinal/nerve circuits. Loss of GABAergic inhibition and excessive glutamate drive.

## 3. Clinical Features

| Feature | Cortical | Subcortical/Brainstem | Spinal | Peripheral |
|---------|----------|----------------------|--------|------------|
| Distribution | Focal/distal | Generalised/axial | Segmental | Single nerve territory |
| Stimulus-sensitive | Yes | Yes (startle) | Variable | No |
| Consciousness | Preserved | Preserved | Preserved | Preserved |
| EEG correlate | Yes (spike-shifted) | No | No | No |

**Examination:** Observe at rest, with posture, with action; assess for stimulus sensitivity (touch, sound, light); assess distribution; check for ataxia, dementia, signs of underlying disease.

**Key syndromes:**
- **Lance-Adams syndrome:** Chronic post-hypoxic action myoclonus + cerebellar ataxia
- **Myoclonic epilepsies (PME):** Lafora, Unverricht-Lundborg, MERRF, DRPLA
- **CJD:** Startle myoclonus, periodic sharp wave complexes on EEG
- **EPC (Epilepsia partialis continua):** Continuous focal cortical myoclonus, Kozhevnikov's
- **Palatal myoclonus (tremor):** Rhythmic 1-3 Hz, due to dentato-rubro-olivary pathway lesion

## 4. Diagnostic Approach

**First line:**
- MRI brain (with DWI for CJD)
- EEG (with back-averaging for cortical myoclonus)
- Bloods: U&E, LFT, glucose, Ca2+, Mg2+, ammonia, ABG, lactate
- Drug/toxin screen
- TFTs, B12, HIV, syphilis serology

**Second line (cause unclear):**
- LP: cell count, protein, OCB, anti-GAD, anti-NMDA, paraneoplastic panel
- Anti-tTG/gliadin (coeliac)
- Genetic (PME panel, mitochondrial)
- DAT-SPECT (look for synucleinopathy)
- Brain biopsy (CJD, SSPE)

## 5. Investigations

| Test | Purpose | Key Findings |
|------|---------|--------------|
| **MRI brain (DWI, FLAIR)** | Structural | Cortical ribboning/high signal basal ganglia in CJD; atrophy patterns in NDD |
| **EEG** | Cortical vs non-cortical | Spike-wave preceding jerk (back-averaging); PSWCs in CJD (1 Hz); polyspike in JME |
| **EMG** | Characterise jerks | Burst duration <100 ms; synchronous agonist/antagonist |
| **SEP (giant)** | Cortical hyperexcitability | Giant P25/N33 in cortical myoclonus |
| **LP** | Autoimmune, infection | 14-3-3, RT-QuIC (CJD); antibodies |
| **Genetics** | Hereditary | EPM1, EPM2A, mitochondrial |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Tic** | Suppressible, premonitory urge, stereotyped, semi-voluntary |
| **Chorea** | Continuous, flowing, non-stereotyped; longer duration |
| **Tremor** | Rhythmic, oscillatory, 4-12 Hz |
| **Dystonia** | Sustained twisting/posturing, longer duration |
| **Functional jerks** | Variable, distractible, incongruous, entrainable |
| **Startle disease (hyperekplexia)** | Exaggerated startle, glycine receptor mutation |
| **Clonus** | Rhythmic, sustained stretch-induced |

## 7. Management

**General principles:** Treat underlying cause; symptomatic pharmacotherapy; supportive.

**Symptomatic drug ladder:**

| Agent | Indication | Dose | Notes |
|-------|------------|------|-------|
| **Valproate** | First-line cortical, generalised | 600-2000 mg/day | Watch LFT, weight, pregnancy |
| **Levetiracetam** | Cortical, post-anoxic, Lance-Adams | 1000-3000 mg/day | First-line, well tolerated |
| **Clonazepam** | Brainstem, spinal, startle | 0.5-6 mg/day | Sedation, tolerance |
| **Piracetam** | Cortical, post-anoxic | 4.8-24 g/day | Cognitive benefit |
| **Zonisamide** | Refractory cortical | 200-600 mg/day | Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor |
| **Topiramate** | Refractory | 200-400 mg/day | Cognitive side effects |
| **Brivaracetam** | Cortical | 100-200 mg/day | Newer analogue of levetiracetam |
| **Perampanel** | Refractory generalised | 4-12 mg/day | Behavioural effects |

**Cause-specific:**
- **CJD:** No disease-modifying therapy; clonazepam/valproate for myoclonus
- **Autoimmune:** IVIG, methylprednisolone, plasma exchange, rituximab
- **Metabolic:** Correct underlying; clonazepam
- **Drug-induced:** Stop offending drug

**Special situations:**
- **Refractory:** Polytherapy, VNS, deep brain stimulation
- **Lance-Adams:** Levetiracetam + clonazepam + piracetam, often combined

## 8. Drug Interactions
- Valproate + carbamazepine: complex kinetics; valproate + lamotrigine: risk of Stevens-Johnson
- Clonazepam + opioids: respiratory depression
- Levetiracetam: minimal interactions, renally cleared

## 9. Procedures
- **Lumbar puncture:** Diagnostic; check opening pressure, cells, OCB, antibodies
- **EMG/EEG:** Diagnostic
- **VNS (vagus nerve stimulation):** Refractory cases (off-label for myoclonus)

## 10. Complications
- Falls and injury (especially in elderly, cortical action myoclonus)
- Cognitive impairment (drugs and underlying disease)
- Aspiration (pharyngeal myoclonus in CJD)
- Status myoclonicus: prolonged myoclonic seizures (treated as status epilepticus)

## 11. Red Flags
- Rapid cognitive decline + startle myoclonus = suspect CJD
- New myoclonus + confusion = autoimmune encephalitis
- New myoclonus + ataxia + opsoclonus = paraneoplastic
- Status myoclonicus
- Lance-Adams after cardiac arrest

## 12. Prognosis
- **Physiological:** Benign
- **Essential:** Slow progression, often non-disabling
- **CJD:** Rapidly fatal (months)
- **Post-anoxic:** Variable; Lance-Adams may improve over years
- **PME:** Progressive, severe disability

## 13. Topic Correlation
- [[Epilepsy]] - myoclonic seizures
- [[CJD]] - startle myoclonus
- [[Alzheimers Disease]] - late-stage myoclonus
- [[Corticobasal Degeneration]] - cortical myoclonus
- [[Movement Disorders Hub]] - classification

## 14. Special Situations
- **Pregnancy:** Valproate contraindicated; prefer levetiracetam, clonazepam
- **Paediatric:** PME, infantile spasms (West syndrome)
- **Elderly:** Drug-induced common; renal dosing for levetiracetam
- **Post-cardiac arrest:** Lance-Adams syndrome (recovery phase)

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary
- **Definition:** Brief shock-like jerks (positive) or loss of tone (negative)
- **Classification:** Cortical, subcortical, spinal, peripheral
- **EEG back-averaging:** Spike precedes jerk by 10-30 ms in cortical
- **Lance-Adams:** Action myoclonus after hypoxic injury
- **CJD:** Startle myoclonus, PSWCs 1 Hz, periodic sharp wave complexes
- **First-line drugs:** Valproate, levetiracetam, clonazepam
- **Avoid:** Carbamazepine in progressive myoclonic epilepsies (worsens)

## Viva Questions
1. Classify myoclonus by physiological origin — Cortical, subcortical, spinal, peripheral
2. EEG features of cortical myoclonus — Spike/sharp wave preceding jerk 10-30 ms (back-averaging), giant SEP
3. CJD EEG — Periodic sharp wave complexes at ~1 Hz
4. Lance-Adams syndrome — Chronic post-hypoxic action myoclonus + ataxia
5. First-line drugs — Valproate, levetiracetam, clonazepam

## Common Confusions
| Confusion | Clarification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Myoclonus vs tic | Myoclonus is involuntary and brief; tic has premonitory urge and is suppressible |
| Negative myoclonus vs ataxia | Negative myoclonus = sudden tone loss; asterixis is negative myoclonus |
| Cortical myoclonus vs epilepsia partialis continua | EPC is a form of cortical myoclonus that is continuous for hours/days |

## Mnemonics
- **CJD** = Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease: **C**ortical ribboning, **J**erk (startle), **D**ementia, periodic EEG
- **Lance-Adams** = **L**ate action **A**fter anoxia = cortical myoclonus
- **PME causes** = **M**yoclonic epilepsy: **M**ERRF, **U**nverricht-Lundborg, **L**afora, **D**RPLA, **N**CL
