# Reversible Cerebral Vasoconstriction Syndrome (RCVS)

> **High-Yield Definition:** RCVS: acute-onset, severe thunderclap headache with reversible cerebral vasoconstriction (segmental narrowing of medium-large arteries). Often affects young women. Associated with vasoactive drugs (cannabis, cocaine, SSRIs), postpartum.

## Definition
- Acute-onset severe headache (thunderclap)
- Reversible cerebral vasoconstriction (multifocal segmental narrowing)
- Angiography: string-of-beads appearance, sausage-string
- Reversal within 3 months (key feature)
- Also called Call-Fleming syndrome

## Epidemiology
- F:M 2-3:1
- Mean age 40-50y (younger than SAH)
- Under-recognised (often misdiagnosed as SAH, migraine, thunderclap)

## Aetiology
- **Idiopathic** (often)
- **Vasoactive drugs**: cannabis (increasingly common), cocaine, amphetamines, ecstasy, LSD, sympathomimetics
- **Medications**: SSRIs, SNRIs, triptans, ergot derivatives, bromocriptine, sumatriptan
- **Postpartum** (1-6 weeks, especially first postpartum week)
- **Eclampsia, preeclampsia**
- **Head injury, neurosurgery**
- **Hypercalcaemia, thyrotoxicosis**
- **Pheochromocytoma, carcinoid**
- **Sexual activity, exertion, Valsalva** (trigger)

## Clinical Features
- **Thunderclap headache** (sudden, severe, reaches maximum in seconds-minutes)
- **Recurrent thunderclap headaches** (over 1-4 weeks) - characteristic
- Nausea, vomiting, photophobia
- Focal neurological deficit (transient ischaemia, infarct 5-10%, often watershed)
- Seizures (5-10%)
- **No meningismus** (vs SAH, meningitis)
- **No fever** (vs meningitis, encephalitis)
- Trigger: exertion, sexual activity, Valsalva, emotion, drugs, postpartum
- Visual disturbance (amaurosis fugax, hemianopia, cortical blindness)

## Investigations — EMERGENCY
- **Non-contrast CT head** (often normal or subtle)
- **CT angiography (CTA)**: segmental narrowing, "string of beads", "sausage string" - multifocal
- **MR angiography (MRA)**: similar findings, may also show PRES
- **MRI brain**: PRES pattern (vasogenic oedema, often parieto-occipital), infarcts (watershed), small SAH (rare)
- **Digital subtraction angiography (DSA)**: gold standard, may be needed if CTA/MRA equivocal
- **Lumbar puncture** (if SAH suspected, exclude): typically normal, may have mild pleocytosis
- **Bloods**: FBC, ESR, toxicology, drug screen, pregnancy test, autoimmune
- Exclude: SAH (most important), arterial dissection, vasculitis, migraine, PRES, eclampsia, pituitary apoplexy

## Management
- **Stop offending drugs** (cannabis, cocaine, SSRIs, triptans, ergot, sympathomimetics)
- **Calcium channel blockers** (KEY):
  - **Nimodipine 60mg PO q4-6h** × 4-12 weeks (then taper)
  - OR verapamil, diltiazem
  - Reduces headache, may prevent stroke
- **Magnesium** (postpartum, eclampsia): magnesium sulphate 4g IV loading, then 1-2g/h
- **Glucocorticoids** (controversial, may worsen - avoid)
- **Endovascular** (intra-arterial vasodilators, balloon angioplasty): severe, refractory
- **Supportive**: analgesia, hydration, BP control (avoid hypotension), seizure control (levetiracetam)
- **Multidisciplinary**: neurology, neurosurgery (interventional neuroradiology), ICU, obstetrics (postpartum), toxicology

## Monitoring
- Daily clinical review
- **MRI/MRA at 3 months** (confirm reversal)
- BP, neurological, headache
- Adverse drug avoidance
- Pregnancy (avoid in subsequent)

## Red Flags
- EMERGENCY: thunderclap headache, focal deficit, seizure, stroke, ICH, PRES, pregnancy/postpartum, reversible, recurrence, vasoactive drugs
- Complications: ischaemic stroke (5-10%), ICH (rare), SAH (rare), PRES (often)
- Misdiagnosis: SAH (most common), migraine, thunderclap, dissection, vasculitis

## Prognosis
- **Reversible** within 3 months (by definition)
- Headache resolves within days-weeks
- Vasoconstriction reverses within 3 months
- Recurrence: 5-10% (especially with continued trigger exposure)
- Outcome: usually good
- Stroke: 5-10% (often watershed)
- Mortality: very low
- Multidisciplinary essential
- Long-term: avoid triggers, monitor, family screening if familial
