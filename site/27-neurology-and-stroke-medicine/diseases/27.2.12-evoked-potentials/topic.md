# Evoked Potentials

> [!tip] **Evoked potentials (EPs) measure conduction along sensory pathways.** Abnormalities suggest demyelination, compression, or degeneration — even when MRI is normal. Less used now due to MRI but still **useful for occult lesions, intraoperative monitoring, prognostication in coma**.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] Describe types of EPs (VEP, BAEP, SSEP, MEP)
- [ ] Identify normal waveforms and peak latencies
- [ ] Apply EPs to MS, optic neuritis, brainstem lesions, cord disease
- [ ] Recognise role in intraoperative monitoring
- [ ] Interpret SSEP in prognostication after cardiac arrest

---

## 1. Definition / Classification

### Definition
Time-locked cortical (or peripheral) electrical responses to repetitive sensory (or motor) stimulation. Small signals extracted from background EEG by averaging many trials.

### Types
| EP | Stimulus | Pathway | Best For |
|----|----------|---------|----------|
| **VEP** (Visual) | Reversing checkerboard pattern | Retina → optic nerve → chiasm → tract → LGN → occipital cortex | Optic neuritis, MS |
| **BAEP** (Brainstem Auditory) | Click sounds | Cochlea → CN VIII → cochlear nuclei → pons → midbrain → cortex | Acoustic neuroma, MS brainstem, brain death |
| **SSEP** (Somatosensory) | Electrical stimulation of peripheral nerve (median, tibial) | Peripheral nerve → dorsal columns → medial lemniscus → thalamus → sensory cortex | Spinal cord lesions, MS, intraoperative monitoring, coma prognostication |
| **MEP** (Motor) | Transcranial magnetic/electrical stimulation | Cortex → corticospinal tract → peripheral nerve → muscle | Intraoperative monitoring, MS, ALS (assessing corticospinal) |
| **ERG** (Electroretinogram) | Light flash | Retina | Differentiate retinal from optic nerve disease |

## 2. Visual Evoked Potentials (VEP)

### Methodology
- Patient watches reversing black-and-white checkerboard pattern (one eye at a time)
- Records from occipital electrodes (Oz)
- 100+ stimuli averaged

### Normal Response
- **P100** (positive peak at 100 ms) — from primary visual cortex
- Normal latency: **95-115 ms** (inter-eye difference <8 ms)
- Amplitude 5-25 µV

### Abnormal Patterns
| Finding | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| **↑ P100 latency (delayed)** | **Demyelination** (MS, optic neuritis, NMO, Leber's) |
| **↓ P100 amplitude** | Axonal loss, optic nerve atrophy, severe compression |
| **Absent P100** | Optic nerve transection, severe glaucoma, blindness |
| **Asymmetry >8 ms** | Unilateral optic nerve pathology |

### Clinical Use
- **MS diagnosis (McDonald):** Delayed P100 supports dissemination in space
- **Optic neuritis:** Delayed P100 in 70-80% (may persist after recovery)
- **NMO:** Bilateral severe delay
- **Functional visual loss:** Normal VEP despite reported blindness
- **Optic nerve compression (meningioma, glioma)**

## 3. Brainstem Auditory Evoked Potentials (BAEP / ABR)

### Methodology
- Click stimulus (monaural) at 60-70 dB above threshold
- Records from vertex (Cz) with reference to ear
- 1000-2000 trials averaged

### Normal Peaks
| Peak | Generator | Latency (ms) |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **I** | Distal CN VIII / cochlear nerve | 1.5 |
| **II** | Proximal CN VIII / cochlear nucleus | 2.5 |
| **III** | Pons (superior olive) | 3.5 |
| **IV** | Midbrain (lateral lemniscus) | 5.0 |
| **V** | Midbrain (inferior colliculus) | 5.5 |

### Key Intervals
- **I-III interval:** Peripheral to lower pons (acoustic neuroma, demyelination)
- **III-V interval:** Pons to midbrain (MS, tumours, herniation)
- **I-V interval:** Total brainstem conduction (↑ in MS, brainstem lesions)

### Abnormal Patterns
| Finding | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| **↑ I-III interval / Loss of III** | Cerebellopontine angle lesion (acoustic neuroma) |
| **↑ III-V interval** | Upper brainstem lesion (MS, tumour, hydrocephalus) |
| **Loss of all peaks** | Severe brainstem dysfunction, brain death |
| **↑ I-V with preserved waves** | Demyelination, ischaemia |

### Clinical Use
- **Acoustic neuroma / vestibular schwannoma:** ↑ I-III interval or absent waves (especially with contralateral ear stimulation)
- **MS:** ↑ I-V interval, asymmetric waveforms
- **Brain death:** Loss of all waves above wave I (or all)
- **Intraoperative monitoring:** CN VIII preservation during posterior fossa surgery
- **Hearing screening in infants**

## 4. Somatosensory Evoked Potentials (SSEP)

### Methodology
- **Median nerve** (upper limb) — stimulates at wrist
- **Tibial nerve** (lower limb) — stimulates at ankle
- Records at: Erb's point, cervical spine (N13), contralateral cortex (N20)
- 200-500 trials averaged

### Normal Peaks (Median)
| Peak | Generator | Latency (ms) |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **N9** | Brachial plexus | 9 |
| **N13** | Cervical cord | 13 |
| **P14** | Medulla (cuneate nucleus) | 14 |
| **N20** | Sensory cortex | 20 |

### Normal Peaks (Tibial)
| Peak | Generator | Latency (ms) |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **P37 / N37** | Sensory cortex | ~37 |

### Abnormal Patterns
| Finding | Significance |
|---------|-------------|
| **Loss / delay N13 → N20** | Cervical cord lesion, MS, hypoperfusion |
| **Central conduction time (CCT = N20-N14) ↑** | MS, subcortical lesion, hydrocephalus |
| **Absent cortical responses bilaterally** | Severe cortical injury, brain death |
| **Preserved peripheral, absent cortical** | Cortex / subcortical lesion |

### Clinical Use
- **MS:** Delayed central conduction
- **Spinal cord lesions:** Localisation (peripheral preserved, central lost)
- **Intraoperative monitoring:** Prevent iatrogenic cord injury (spinal deformity, aortic surgery)
- **Coma prognostication:** **Bilateral absent N20 after cardiac arrest = poor prognosis (very high specificity)** — per AAN guidelines
- **Brain death:** Absent cortical responses

---

## 5. Motor Evoked Potentials (MEP)

### Methodology
- **Transcranial magnetic stimulation** (TMS) over motor cortex
- Records CMAP from contralateral muscle (e.g., abductor pollicis brevis for upper limb, tibialis anterior for lower limb)

### Use
- **Intraoperative monitoring** (corticospinal tract integrity)
- **MS:** ↑ central motor conduction time
- **ALS:** Assess corticospinal involvement
- **Stroke:** Predict motor recovery

---

## 6. Multimodal Evoked Potentials in MS (McDonald Era)

Historically, EP abnormalities (delayed VEP, BAEP, SSEP) supported **dissemination in space** for MS diagnosis. Now largely superseded by **MRI**, but still useful when:
- MRI equivocal
- Functional (psychogenic) symptoms suspected (normal EPs argue against organic)
- Spinal cord lesion localisation

### Typical MS Findings
| EP | Finding |
|----|---------|
| **VEP** | Delayed P100 (often asymmetric) |
| **BAEP** | ↑ I-V interval, wave V delay |
| **SSEP (median)** | ↑ CCT, N20 delay |

---

## 7. SSEP in Coma Prognostication

### Post-Cardiac Arrest
- **Bilateral absent N20** (cortical SSEP) at **≥72h post-arrest** = poor neurological outcome (very high specificity, low false-positive rate)
- Per AAN 2019 prognostication algorithm: absent N20 + GCS-M ≤3 + bilateral absent pupillary/ corneal reflexes = high specificity for poor outcome
- Use in conjunction with **EEG, NSE, MRI, pupillary reflexes**

---

## 8. Intraoperative Monitoring (IONM)

### Modalities
| Modality | Monitors | Surgery |
|----------|----------|---------|
| **SSEP** | Dorsal columns | Spinal deformity, thoracoabdominal aortic |
| **MEP** | Corticospinal tract | Spinal, intracranial |
| **BAEP** | CN VIII / brainstem | Posterior fossa, vestibular schwannoma |
| **Free-running EMG** | Nerve roots | Spinal |
| **EEG** | Cortical perfusion | Carotid endarterectomy |

### Alarm Criteria
- **SSEP:** ↓ amplitude >50% or ↑ latency >10%
- **MEP:** Complete loss or ↓ amplitude >80%

---

## 9. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| **VEP** | P100 latency >115 ms = demyelination; MS, optic neuritis |
| **VEP side difference** | >8 ms = unilateral optic nerve disease |
| **BAEP** | Waves I-V generated from CN VIII to midbrain; I-III peripheral, III-V central |
| **Acoustic neuroma** | ↑ I-III interval, often with absent waves III-V |
| **SSEP median** | N9 plexus, N13 cervical, N20 cortex |
| **SSEP in coma** | Bilateral absent N20 at ≥72h post-arrest = poor outcome |
| **MS historical** | VEP delay = dissemination in space (now MRI-dominant) |
| **MEP** | Use in spinal / intracranial surgery |
| **Intraoperative SSEP** | ↓ amplitude >50% / latency ↑ >10% = alarm |
| **BAEP in brain death** | Loss of all waves above I (or all) |

---

## 10. Special Situations

| Situation | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Pregnancy** | EPs safe (no radiation); SSEP/BAEP routinely used |
| **Severe visual impairment** | VEP may be unobtainable; use flash VEP or rely on MRI |
| **Hearing impairment** | BAEP requires ≥30 dB above threshold for adequate waves |
| **Pacemaker** | Avoid MEP via TMS (rare concern with modern devices) |
| **Cooperative patient needed** | VEP requires fixation; BAEP needs cooperation |
| **Children** | Normative data differs; sedation may be needed |

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** What does VEP P100 delay indicate?
   **A:** Demyelination of optic nerve (MS, optic neuritis).
2. **Q:** Where is the lesion if BAEP shows delayed III-V interval?
   **A:** Upper pons to midbrain (central auditory pathway).
3. **Q:** What is the SSEP finding that predicts poor outcome after cardiac arrest?
   **A:** Bilateral absent N20 (cortical) at ≥72h post-arrest.
4. **Q:** Name the SSEP peaks for median nerve stimulation.
   **A:** N9 (plexus), N13 (cervical cord), N20 (cortex).
5. **Q:** What is the alarm criterion for SSEP in spinal surgery?
   **A:** ↓ amplitude >50% or ↑ latency >10%.

## Common Confusions

| Confusion | Clarification |
|-----------|---------------|
| VEP abnormal = MS | Delayed P100 indicates demyelination, not specific to MS |
| Normal EPs rule out MS | MRI is more sensitive |
| Absent VEP = blindness | Functional blindness can have normal VEP; absent VEP = organic |
| BAEP absent in brain death | Wave I may persist (peripheral CN VIII); absent above wave I |

---

## Mnemonics

1. **VEP P100 = VISION** — posterior cortex, ~100 ms
2. **BAEP I-III-V** — peripheral CN VIII → pons → midbrain
3. **SSEP N9-N13-N20** — plexus → cord → cortex
4. **N20 ABSENT = BAD** — bilateral absent N20 = poor outcome post-arrest
5. **50/10 RULE** — SSEP alarm: ↓ 50% amplitude or ↑ 10% latency

## MCQs (10)

1. **Q:** VEP P100 latency is delayed in:
   **A:** Cataract **B:** Optic neuritis **C:** Refractive error **D:** Corneal disease
   **Ans:** B — Optic neuritis causes demyelination → P100 delay.

2. **Q:** BAEP wave I represents:
   **A:** Cochlear nucleus **B:** Distal CN VIII / cochlear nerve **C:** Pons **D:** Midbrain
   **Ans:** B

3. **Q:** Increased I-III BAEP interval suggests:
   **A:** Acoustic neuroma **B:** Midbrain lesion **C:** Cortical lesion **D:** Cochlear pathology
   **Ans:** A — Acoustic neuroma affects peripheral CN VIII (waves I-III).

4. **Q:** SSEP N20 is generated from:
   **A:** Brachial plexus **B:** Cervical cord **C:** Sensory cortex **D:** Thalamus
   **Ans:** C

5. **Q:** Bilateral absent N20 SSEP at 72h after cardiac arrest suggests:
   **A:** Good recovery **B:** Poor neurological outcome (very high specificity) **C:** Need for EEG **D:** Brainstem lesion
   **Ans:** B

6. **Q:** VEP normal inter-eye difference is:
   **A:** <2 ms **B:** <8 ms **C:** <20 ms **D:** <30 ms
   **Ans:** B

7. **Q:** Median nerve SSEP normal central conduction time is approximately:
   **A:** 2 ms **B:** 7 ms (N14 to N20) **C:** 20 ms **D:** 50 ms
   **Ans:** B — CCT = N20 - N14 ≈ 6-7 ms.

8. **Q:** Which EP is most useful for monitoring CN VIII during posterior fossa surgery?
   **A:** VEP **B:** SSEP **C:** BAEP **D:** MEP
   **Ans:** C

9. **Q:** In MS, the most commonly abnormal EP is:
   **A:** BAEP **B:** VEP **C:** SSEP **D:** MEP
   **Ans:** B — VEP most sensitive for MS (optic nerve involvement).

10. **Q:** TMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation) is used for:
    **A:** VEP **B:** SSEP **C:** MEP **D:** BAEP
    **Ans:** C — Motor EP.

## SBA Questions (10)

1. **Scenario:** A 25-year-old with optic neuritis, normal MRI brain. VEP shows P100 latency 145 ms in the affected eye. Interpretation?
   **A:** Functional visual loss **B:** Demyelination of optic nerve **C:** Retinal disease **D:** Cortical lesion
   **Ans:** B — Delayed P100 = demyelination (consistent with optic neuritis).

2. **Scenario:** A 50-year-old with unilateral sensorineural hearing loss. BAEP shows absent waves III-V on affected side, normal wave I. Localisation?
   **A:** Cochlea **B:** Cerebellopontine angle (e.g., acoustic neuroma) **C:** Midbrain **D:** Cortex
   **Ans:** B — Loss of waves III-V with preserved wave I = CN VIII lesion proximal to cochlea.

3. **Scenario:** A 60-year-old in coma post-cardiac arrest. At 72h, GCS-M 1, pupils fixed, corneal absent, EEG burst-suppression, SSEP bilateral absent N20. Prognosis?
   **A:** Likely good recovery **B:** Poor neurological outcome (multi-modal) **C:** Need more time **D:** Locked-in
   **Ans:** B — Multi-modal (absent pupillary, absent corneal, absent N20) = very poor prognosis.

4. **Scenario:** During spinal deformity surgery, SSEP amplitude drops by 60% and latency increases by 15%. Action?
   **A:** Continue surgery **B:** Alert surgeon, check positioning, perfusion **C:** Wake patient **D:** Stop case permanently
   **Ans:** B — Alarm criteria (↓ amp >50%, ↑ latency >10%) — immediate action: check perfusion, hardware, position; consider wake-up test.

5. **Scenario:** A 30-year-old with suspected MS, MRI brain shows one periventricular lesion. To support dissemination in space, most useful EP?
   **A:** BAEP **B:** VEP **C:** SSEP **D:** MEP
   **Ans:** B — VEP most sensitive for MS.

6. **Scenario:** A 70-year-old in ICU post cardiac arrest. Brainstem reflexes absent. BAEP shows only wave I. Interpretation?
   **A:** Cochlear lesion **B:** Brain death (preserved peripheral, absent central) **C:** CN VIII neuroma **D:** Hearing loss
   **Ans:** B — Brain death compatible (wave I = peripheral CN VIII; absent above = central pathway failure).

7. **Scenario:** A patient with progressive weakness, myelopathy on MRI. SSEP shows preserved N9 and N13, absent N20. Localisation?
   **A:** Brachial plexus **B:** Peripheral nerve **C:** Above cervical cord (central) **D:** Cortex
   **Ans:** C — N9-N13 preserved → peripheral and cord entry OK; absent N20 → lesion between cord and cortex.

8. **Scenario:** A 35-year-old with suspected demyelination. VEP normal bilaterally. BAEP shows I-V interval 5.0 ms (prolonged). Interpretation?
   **A:** No demyelination **B:** Brainstem demyelination (central) **C:** Cochlear pathology **D:** Peripheral CN VIII
   **Ans:** B — Prolonged I-V interval = central conduction delay in brainstem.

9. **Scenario:** A 45-year-old with severe bilateral optic atrophy. VEP shows absent P100 bilaterally. Likely cause?
   **A:** MS **B:** Severe optic nerve damage (axonal loss) **C:** Functional blindness **D:** Cataract
   **Ans:** B — Severe bilateral optic atrophy = absent P100 due to axonal loss.

10. **Scenario:** MEP during thoracoabdominal aortic repair: complete loss of MEPs and SSEPs bilaterally. Action?
    **A:** Continue **B:** Wake patient **C:** Alert surgeon immediately, increase perfusion / spinal cord protection **D:** Wait
    **Ans:** C — Complete loss of MEPs/SSEPs = spinal cord ischaemia; urgent action (↑BP, drain CSF, steroids per protocol).
