# Febrile Seizures

> **High-Yield Definition:** Febrile seizures: seizures in children 6 months-5 years associated with fever (>38°C), without CNS infection, metabolic cause, or prior afebrile seizure. Common (2-5%), usually benign.

## Definition
- **Seizure** with fever (>38°C) in children 6 months-5 years
- **Excluded**: CNS infection (meningitis, encephalitis), metabolic disturbance, afebrile seizure history
- **Simple febrile seizure** (70-75%): generalised, <15 min, single in 24 hours
- **Complex febrile seizure** (25-30%): focal, >15 min, multiple in 24 hours, or with post-ictal Todd's paresis
- **Febrile status epilepticus**: >30 min

## Epidemiology
- 2-5% of children
- Peak: 18-24 months
- F:M 1:1
- Often viral illness (HHV-6, influenza, RSV, adenovirus)
- Post-vaccination (MMR, DTaP - rare, not causal)
- Family history: 25-40% (autosomal dominant with reduced penetrance)
- Genes: SCN1A (Dravet), SCN1B, GABRG2, GABRD (some familial febrile seizures)

## Aetiology
- **Genetic predisposition**: polygenic, channelopathies
- **Fever**: viral infection (most common), bacterial infection, post-vaccination, teething (controversial)
- Mechanism: immature brain, fever-induced neuronal hyperexcitability, lowered seizure threshold
- Risk factors: family history, developmental delay, NICU admission, daycare, viral exposure

## Clinical Features

### Simple Febrile Seizure (70-75%)
- **Generalised tonic-clonic**
- **<15 min duration**
- **Single seizure in 24 hours**
- No focal features
- No post-ictal Todd's paresis
- No prior afebrile seizure
- No CNS infection

### Complex Febrile Seizure (25-30%)
- **Focal** features
- **>15 min** duration
- **Multiple** in 24 hours
- Post-ictal Todd's paresis
- Higher risk of epilepsy later

### Febrile Status Epilepticus
- >30 min (or serial seizures without recovery)
- 5-10% of febrile seizures
- Often focal, prolonged, subtle (especially in young)
- Risk factor for mesial temporal sclerosis and temporal lobe epilepsy

## Investigations
- **Clinical diagnosis** (usually)
- **LP (lumbar puncture)**: mandatory if <12 months (meningitis signs), strongly consider 12-18 months, optional >18 months
- **EEG**: not routinely indicated (simple), consider for complex or recurrent
- **MRI brain**: not routine, consider for complex, focal, prolonged, abnormal neuro exam, developmental delay, consider Dravet, MElkersson-Rosenthal
- Exclude: meningitis, encephalitis (LP if signs)
- Bloods: glucose, electrolytes, FBC (if unwell)

## Management
- **Acute seizure**:
  - First aid: protect from injury, recovery position, don't restrain, don't put anything in mouth
  - Time the seizure
  - If >5 min: **buccal midazolam 0.3 mg/kg** OR rectal diazepam 0.5 mg/kg OR IV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (if IV access)
  - Treat fever: paracetamol 15 mg/kg PO/PR, ibuprofen 10 mg/kg PO (not proven to prevent recurrence but comfort)
  - If meningitis suspected: IV antibiotics + aciclovir, LP
  - Reassurance, education
- **Long-term**:
  - **Simple**: no continuous ASMs (high rate of side effects vs low recurrence risk)
  - **Complex**: consider intermittent ASMs (clobidogrel/valproate, buccal midazolam) for prolonged or recurrent, no routine prophylaxis
  - Antipyretics: do NOT prevent recurrence (NICE guidance), use for comfort
- **Recurrence risk**:
  - 30-35% overall
  - Higher with: first seizure <18 months, family history, lower fever, short duration fever, multiple episodes
  - Cumulative risk of epilepsy: 1-2% (simple), 4-12% (complex)
  - Risk factors for epilepsy: complex, neurodevelopmental abnormality, family history of epilepsy, first seizure <12 months, multiple seizures, prolonged

## Red Flags
- Signs of meningitis (bulging fontanelle, neck stiffness, irritability, lethargy, persistent fever, focal signs)
- Prolonged (>15 min)
- Focal features
- Multiple seizures
- Post-ictal focal deficit
- Abnormal development
- Recurrent
- Status epilepticus

## Multidisciplinary
- Paediatrician
- Paediatric neurologist (complex, recurrent, atypical)
- Emergency medicine
- General practice
- Education (parents)

## Prognosis
- **Simple**: benign, 30-35% recurrence, 1-2% epilepsy
- **Complex**: 4-12% epilepsy
- **Febrile status epilepticus**: higher risk mesial temporal sclerosis, TLE later
- Mortality: very low (if CNS infection excluded)
- Quality of life: usually normal
- Education: key to reduce parental anxiety
- Multidisciplinary essential
