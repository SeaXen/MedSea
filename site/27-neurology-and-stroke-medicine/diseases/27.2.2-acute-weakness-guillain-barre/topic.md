# Acute Weakness & Guillain-Barre

> [!tip] **Acute flaccid paralysis = emergency.** Differentiate **spinal cord (UMN + sensory level)**, **peripheral nerve (LMN, areflexia)**, **NMJ (descending / fatigable)**, **muscle (pain, CK)**. GBS = most common acute neuromuscular emergency.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] Localise acute weakness (cortex, cord, peripheral nerve, NMJ, muscle)
- [ ] Diagnose and treat Guillain-Barré syndrome
- [ ] Recognise respiratory failure indications
- [ ] Differentiate GBS variants (AIDP, AMAN, MFS, Bickerstaff)
- [ ] Manage autonomic dysfunction in GBS
- [ ] Identify mimics (myelitis, cord compression, myasthenia, botulism, periodic paralysis)

---

## 1. Definition / Classification

### Definition
**Acute weakness** = sudden-onset loss of muscle power (<4 weeks) affecting one or more limbs, often progressing. **Acute flaccid paralysis (AFP)** = acute lower motor neuron weakness with reduced tone and reflexes.

### Classification by Localisation
| Site | Pattern | Tone | Reflexes | Examples |
|------|---------|------|----------|----------|
| **Cortex** | Hemiparesis | ↑ | ↑ | Stroke, tumour |
| **Brainstem** | Crossed (ipsilateral CN + contralateral limb) | Variable | Variable | Stroke, demyelination |
| **Spinal cord** | Bilateral, sensory level, bladder/bowel | ↑ (LMN at level) | ↑ below, ↓ at level | Myelitis, compression |
| **Anterior horn cell** | Asymmetric, distal, fasciculations | ↓ | ↓ | Polio, West Nile, ALS (later) |
| **Root** | Myotomal | ↓ | ↓ | Radiculopathy |
| **Peripheral nerve** | Distal, sensory, areflexia | ↓ | ↓ | GBS, vasculitis |
| **NMJ** | Fatigable, ocular, bulbar | Normal | Normal | MG, LEMS, botulism |
| **Muscle** | Proximal, painful, ↑CK | Normal/↓ | Normal | Myositis, periodic paralysis |

## 2. Acute Flaccid Paralysis — Differential

### Infectious / Inflammatory
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS)**
- **Acute flaccid myelitis** (enterovirus D68, polio-like)
- **Transverse myelitis**
- **ADEM** (post-infectious demyelination)
- **Poliomyelitis** / West Nile

### Spinal Cord Compression
- Trauma, tumour, abscess, haematoma, disc

### Neuromuscular Junction
- **Myasthenic crisis**
- **Botulism** (descending paralysis, autonomic)
- **Organophosphate poisoning** (cholinergic crisis)
- **Tick paralysis**

### Muscle
- **Periodic paralysis** (hypo/hyperkalaemic)
- **Inflammatory myopathy** (acute severe)
- **Rhabdomyolysis**
- **Acute myopathy** (critical illness)

### Metabolic / Toxic
- **Hypokalaemia / hyperkalaemia**
- **Hypophosphataemia** (refeeding)
- **Heavy metals, drugs** (statins, steroids)

### Vascular
- **Spinal cord infarction** (anterior spinal artery)
- **Stroke** (focal weakness, not AFP)

## 3. Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)

### Definition
**Acute inflammatory demyelinating polyradiculoneuropathy** — acute-onset, ascending, symmetrical flaccid paralysis with areflexia, often post-infectious. Most common cause of acute flaccid paralysis worldwide.

### Epidemiology
- Incidence: 1-2/100,000/year
- Age: bimodal (young adults + elderly)
- Male predominance
- **Antecedent infection:** Campylobacter jejuni (30%), CMV, EBV, Mycoplasma, hepatitis E, Zika, influenza, **SARS-CoV-2**
- **Trigger:** Vaccination (rare; influenza historically); surgery

### Pathophysiology
- **Molecular mimicry** — antibodies against pathogen cross-react with peripheral nerve gangliosides (GM1, GD1a, GQ1b)
- Demyelination (AIDP) or axonal damage (AMAN/AMSAN)

### Classification
| Variant | Pathology | Key Features |
|---------|-----------|--------------|
| **AIDP** (Acute Inflammatory Demyelinating Polyneuropathy) | Demyelinating | Most common; ascending weakness, sensory symptoms |
| **AMAN** (Acute Motor Axonal Neuropathy) | Axonal | C. jejuni, Asia; worse prognosis |
| **AMSAN** (Acute Motor and Sensory Axonal Neuropathy) | Axonal | Severe, poor prognosis |
| **MFS** (Miller Fisher Syndrome) | Demyelinating | Ataxia, areflexia, ophthalmoplegia (anti-GQ1b) |
| **Bickerstaff brainstem encephalitis** | CNS + PNS | Altered consciousness, ataxia, hyperreflexia, ophthalmoplegia (anti-GQ1b) |
| **Pure sensory / pandysautonomia** | Rare | Selective fibre involvement |
| **Pharyngeal-cervical-brachial** | Rare | Bulbar + upper limbs |

### Clinical Features
- **Antecedent infection 1-3 weeks prior** (URTI, gastroenteritis)
- **Symmetric ascending weakness** starting in legs (proximal + distal)
- **Areflexia / hyporeflexia** (universal, early)
- **Mild sensory symptoms** (paraesthesia, often subjective more than objective)
- **Pain** (back, limb) — common, often severe
- **Cranial nerve involvement:** Facial (50%), bulbar (oropharyngeal weakness), ophthalmoplegia (MFS)
- **Autonomic dysfunction** (BP lability, ileus, urinary retention, brady/tachycardia)
- **Respiratory failure** (20-30%) — **leading cause of mortality**

### Diagnostic Criteria (NINDS / Brighton)
| Level | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **1 (Required)** | Progressive bilateral weakness, ↓or absent reflexes, monophasic, 4 weeks plateau |
| **Supportive** | CSF albuminocytological dissociation, NCS abnormalities (demyelinating/axonal), antecedent infection |
| **Exclusion** | HIV, lymphoma, lead poisoning, vasculitis, alternative diagnosis |

### Severity — Hughes / GBS Disability Scale
| Grade | Function |
|-------|----------|
| 0 | Normal |
| 1 | Able to run |
| 2 | Unable to run, walks independently |
| 3 | Needs stick/walking aid |
| 4 | Bed/chair-bound |
| 5 | Ventilated |
| 6 | Dead |

## 4. Investigations — GBS

### First-Line
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| **LP** | ↑ Protein (often >1 g/L), normal cells (albuminocytological dissociation) — may be normal in first week |
| **NCS** | Demyelinating: ↓ MCV, ↑ DML, prolonged/absent F-waves, conduction block, temporal dispersion |
| | Axonal: ↓ CMAP/SNAP amplitude, normal velocity |
| **Bloods** | FBC, U&E, LFT, Ca2+, Mg2+, PO4-, CK (↑in AMAN/myositis), HIV (HIV-associated GBS), serology (CMV, EBV, hepatitis E) |
| **Stool culture** | Campylobacter jejuni |
| **Anti-ganglioside antibodies** | Anti-GM1 (AMAN), Anti-GQ1b (MFS, Bickerstaff) |

### Imaging
- **MRI spine** (if atypical — exclude cord lesion)
- **MRI brachial/lumbar plexus** (enhancement of nerve roots in GBS)
- **CXR** (baseline, exclude aspiration, diaphragmatic weakness)

### Respiratory Assessment
- **FVC (forced vital capacity)** — most useful
- **NIF (negative inspiratory force)** — sniff test
- **PCF (peak cough flow)**
- **ABG** — late indicator; don't wait for hypoxia/hypercapnia

## 5. Management — GBS

### General
- **Admit to neurology with close monitoring** (HDU/ICU if respiratory concerns)
- **VTE prophylaxis** (LMWH)
- **Pain management** (neuropathic — gabapentin, amitriptyline, opiates)
- **Bowel/bladder care**
- **Autonomic monitoring** (continuous ECG, BP)
- **Nutrition** (NG if bulbar)
- **Physiotherapy** (passive, DVT prophylaxis)

### Respiratory Monitoring — 20/30/40 Rule
| Parameter | Threshold | Action |
|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **FVC** | <20 mL/kg or <40% predicted | ICU, consider intubation |
| **NIF** | <30 cmH2O (less negative) | ICU |
| **PCF** | <270 L/min | Inadequate cough |
| **Bulbar failure** | Severe | NG feeding, consider intubation |

### Specific Treatment — Immunomodulation
| Treatment | Regimen | Indication |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **IVIG** | 0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days (total 2 g/kg) | First-line (easier than PLEX) |
| **Plasma exchange (PLEX)** | 5 exchanges over 1-2 weeks | Alternative first-line |
| **Steroids** | Not beneficial in GBS (alone) | — |

> **Do NOT combine** IVIG + PLEX routinely — no added benefit.
> **Do NOT delay** — start within 2 weeks of onset.

### Supportive
- **Intubation + ventilation** if respiratory failure (FVC <20 mL/kg, bulbar failure)
- **Tracheostomy** if prolonged ventilation (>2 weeks)
- **Autonomic:** Treat bradycardia (atropine, pacing), hypertension (labetalol), hypotension (fluids, vasopressors)
- **Rehabilitation:** Multidisciplinary

### Complications
| Complication | Management |
|--------------|-----------|
| **Respiratory failure** | Intubation, ventilation |
| **Aspiration** | NG, head elevation |
| **DVT/PE** | LMWH prophylaxis |
| **Pressure ulcers** | 2-hourly turning |
| **Constipation/ileus** | Laxatives, NG |
| **Autonomic storms** | ICU monitoring |
| **Persistent disability** | Rehabilitation, vocational support |
| **SIADH** | Fluid restriction |
| **Hyponatraemia** | Treat cause |

### Prognosis
- 80% recover (over weeks to months); 20% have persistent disability
- Mortality: 5-10% (respiratory failure, autonomic, PE)
- **Poor prognostic factors:** AMAN, advanced age, rapid progression, need for ventilation, axonal NCS pattern

## 6. Other Acute Flaccid Paralysis Causes

### Transverse Myelitis
- **Acute/subacute** weakness + sensory level + bladder/bowel
- **MRI spine** (cord swelling, T2 hyperintensity, enhancement)
- **CSF:** Pleocytosis, ↑ protein
- **Treatment:** IV methylprednisolone 1 g × 3-5 days; PLEX if severe/refractory

### Acute Flaccid Myelitis (Enterovirus D68)
- **Children/young adults**, post-respiratory illness
- MRI: longitudinal cord lesion (anterior horn)
- No proven treatment; supportive

### Myasthenic Crisis
- See Neurological Emergencies note
- FVC <20, bulbar failure → intubation + PLEX (preferred) or IVIG

### Botulism
- **Descending paralysis** (cranial → limbs)
- **Pupils fixed, dilated**, dry mouth, ileus
- **Wound botulism** (IVDU), infant (honey), foodborne (home-preserved)
- **Treatment:** Antitoxin (trivalent), supportive (may need ventilation weeks-months)

### Tick Paralysis
- **Ascending paralysis** in child with tick (often scalp)
- **Removal** → rapid recovery
- Mimics GBS

### Periodic Paralysis
- **Hypokalaemic:** Provoked by rest after exercise, high-carb meal; thyroid disease screen
- **Hyperkalaemic:** Shorter episodes, provoked by fasting, exercise
- Treatment: K+ correction (cautious), prevention (acetazolamide, dichlorphenamide)

### Spinal Cord Compression
- **MRI urgent**
- IV dexamethasone 10 mg STAT then 4 mg 6H
- Neurosurgical decompression within 24h

## 7. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| **GBS** | Ascending, areflexic, post-infectious, LP shows albuminocytological dissociation |
| **AIDP** | Most common; demyelinating; F-wave prolonged early |
| **AMAN** | Axonal; C. jejuni; Asia; worse prognosis |
| **MFS** | Triad: ataxia, areflexia, ophthalmoplegia (anti-GQ1b) |
| **20/30/40 rule** | FVC <20 mL/kg, NIF <30, FVC <40% predicted → ICU |
| **Treatment** | IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day ×5 OR PLEX ×5 — within 2 weeks |
| **Steroids** | Not used in GBS |
| **Botulism** | Descending paralysis, fixed dilated pupils, antitoxin |
| **Myasthenic crisis** | Bulbar + respiratory failure; PLEX preferred |
| **Transverse myelitis** | Sensory level, MRI cord, IV MP |
| **Cord compression** | MRI urgent, IV dexamethasone, neurosurgery |
| **Autonomic in GBS** | Labile BP/HR — ICU monitoring |

---

## 8. Special Situations

| Situation | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Pregnancy** | GBS rare in pregnancy; if severe, IVIG safe |
| **Children** | Campylobacter common; supportive care |
| **Elderly** | Worse prognosis, comorbidities |
| **HIV** | Exclude HIV-associated GBS (CIDP-like, subacute) |
| **Anticoagulated** | IVIG (↑ thrombosis risk); caution with PLEX (line, citrate) |
| **Campylobacter** | May cause severe axonal form |

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** What is the classic CSF finding in GBS?
   **A:** Albuminocytological dissociation = ↑protein with normal cell count (after first week).
2. **Q:** Indications for ICU admission in GBS?
   **A:** FVC <20 mL/kg, NIF <30, FVC <40% predicted, bulbar failure, autonomic dysfunction, severe weakness.
3. **Q:** Treatment options in GBS?
   **A:** IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day ×5 days OR plasma exchange ×5 exchanges — within 2 weeks.
4. **Q:** Miller Fisher syndrome triad?
   **A:** Ataxia, areflexia, ophthalmoplegia (anti-GQ1b antibody).
5. **Q:** Botulism features?
   **A:** Descending paralysis, fixed dilated pupils, autonomic dysfunction (dry mouth, ileus), often wound or infant source.

## Common Confusions

| Confusion | Clarification |
|-----------|---------------|
| GBS = myasthenia | GBS = peripheral nerve (areflexic); MG = NMJ (reflexes preserved) |
| Steroids in GBS | NOT effective; do not use alone |
| Botulism vs GBS | Botulism = descending; GBS = ascending |
| Normal LP rules out GBS | LP can be normal in first week; repeat if suspicion |
| Polio is gone | Still endemic in Afghanistan, Pakistan; vaccine-derived cases |

---

## Mnemonics

1. **ASCENDING GBS, DESCENDING BOTULISM** — patterns
2. **AIDP = DEMYELINATE, AMAN = AXONAL** — AIDP block/dispersion, AMAN amplitude
3. **20/30/40** — FVC <20, NIF <30, <40% predicted
4. **IVIG OR PLEX** — first-line for GBS
5. **ALBUMINOCYTOLOGICAL DISSOCIATION** — GBS CSF
6. **MFS = 3 A's** — Ataxia, Areflexia, Anti-GQ1b

## SBA Questions (10)

1. **Scenario:** A 30-year-old 2 weeks after gastroenteritis now with ascending weakness, areflexia, FVC 25 mL/kg. Diagnosis and management?
   **A:** GBS — IVIG 0.4 g/kg/day ×5; close respiratory monitoring (FVC >20, monitor closely)
   **B:** Multiple sclerosis — Steroids
   **C:** Stroke — Aspirin
   **D:** GBS — Steroids alone
   **Ans:** A — GBS, IVIG first-line.

2. **Scenario:** A 35-year-old with ataxia, areflexia, ophthalmoplegia. Anti-GQ1b positive. Diagnosis?
   **A:** GBS (AIDP) **B:** MFS (Miller Fisher syndrome) **C:** Myasthenia gravis **D:** Botulism
   **Ans:** B

3. **Scenario:** GBS patient, FVC drops to 15 mL/kg, NIF 20 cmH2O, unable to clear secretions. Action?
   **A:** Discharge **B:** Elective intubation in controlled setting before emergency **C:** Tracheostomy **D:** Observation
   **Ans:** B — Elective intubation (controlled) before respiratory arrest.

4. **Scenario:** A 50-year-old with GBS, IVIG started, now BP 200/110, HR 130 alternating with bradycardia 40. Cause?
   **A:** Anxiety **B:** Autonomic dysfunction (GBS dysautonomia) — ICU monitoring **C:** Sepsis **D:** DVT
   **Ans:** B — GBS dysautonomia is common; careful management.

5. **Scenario:** A 6-month-old with descending paralysis, constipation, poor feeding, fixed dilated pupils. Honey exposure?
   **A:** Spinal muscular atrophy **B:** Infant botulism — human-derived botulinum antitoxin (BIG-IV or BabyBIG) **C:** GBS **D:** MG
   **Ans:** B — Infant botulism; treat with antitoxin.

6. **Scenario:** Patient with acute flaccid paralysis, sensation normal, no respiratory failure. MRI spine normal. LP shows normal protein. NCS normal on day 3. Diagnosis?
   **A:** Confirmed GBS **B:** Repeat LP/NCS at day 7-14; clinical diagnosis of GBS supported by course **C:** MG **D:** Not GBS
   **Ans:** B — Early NCS and LP may be normal; repeat if suspicion remains.

7. **Scenario:** Transverse myelitis (MRI confirmed). First treatment?
   **A:** IVIG **B:** IV methylprednisolone 1 g/day ×3-5 days **C:** Aspirin **D:** Plasma exchange
   **Ans:** B — IV MP first-line for TM.

8. **Scenario:** Spinal cord compression from metastasis at T6, neurological deficit. First step?
   **A:** Aspirin **B:** IV dexamethasone 10 mg STAT, then 4 mg 6H; urgent MRI; neurosurgery consult **C:** Antibiotics **D:** IVIG
   **Ans:** B — IV dexamethasone + urgent surgical decompression.

9. **Scenario:** A 25-year-old with acute quadriparesis, normal sensation, absent reflexes, autonomic lability. LP: protein 2.5 g/L, cells normal. NCS: prolonged F-waves. Diagnosis?
   **A:** GBS (AIDP) **B:** Polymyositis **C:** Transverse myelitis **D:** MG
   **Ans:** A — AIDP classic (albuminocytological dissociation, prolonged F-waves).

10. **Scenario:** Hypokalaemic periodic paralysis triggered by rest after exercise. Best prevention?
    **A:** Propranolol **B:** Acetazolamide (carbonic anhydrase inhibitor) **C:** Insulin **D:** Thyroxine
    **Ans:** B — Acetazolamide reduces attack frequency.
