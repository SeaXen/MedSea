# Autonomic Function Testing

> [!tip] **Autonomic dysfunction = a wide spectrum:** from localized (Horner's, sweating) to generalized (MSA, PAF, GBS dysautonomia). Testing reveals sympathetic (adrenergic) vs parasympathetic (cardiovagal) vs sudomotor involvement.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] Describe anatomy of sympathetic and parasympathetic systems
- [ ] Choose appropriate bedside and laboratory tests
- [ ] Interpret heart rate variability, Valsalva ratio, tilt table, sudomotor tests
- [ ] Differentiate causes of orthostatic intolerance
- [ ] Recognise autonomic dysfunction in GBS, MSA, PAF, diabetes

---

## 1. Definition / Classification

### Definition
**Autonomic nervous system (ANS):** Regulates involuntary functions — HR, BP, sweating, GI motility, bladder, pupils. Two divisions:
- **Sympathetic (thoracolumbar):** "Fight or flight"
- **Parasympathetic (craniosacral):** "Rest and digest"

### Classification of Autonomic Disorders
| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Central** | MSA, Parkinson's (later), PAF, spinal cord injury |
| **Peripheral** | Diabetic autonomic neuropathy, GBS dysautonomia, amyloid, GBS |
| **Pure Autonomic Failure (PAF)** | Isolated autonomic failure, no CNS signs |
| **Drug-induced** | Anticholinergics, alpha-blockers, opioids |
| **Localized** | Horner's (sympathetic), Adie's (parasympathetic) |
| **Reflex / Functional** | Vasovagal syncope, situational syncope |
| **Dysautonomia syndromes** | POTS (postural tachycardia), IST (inappropriate sinus tachycardia) |

---

## 2. Anatomy — Quick Recap

### Sympathetic
- Lateral horn T1-L2 → paravertebral chain → target organs
- **Preganglionic:** ACh; **Postganglionic:** Noradrenaline (except sweat glands = ACh)

### Parasympathetic
- CN III, VII, IX, X + S2-S4
- **Preganglionic & Postganglionic:** ACh (muscarinic)

### Key Reflexes
| Reflex | Afferent | Centre | Efferent |
|--------|----------|--------|----------|
| **Baroreflex** | CN IX, X | NTS (medulla) | Vagus (HR), sympathetic (BP) |
| **Valsalva** | Aortic/carotid baroreceptors | Medulla | Sympathetic vasoconstriction |
| **Cough/micturition** | Visceral afferents | Brainstem/spinal | Efferent autonomic |

## 3. Clinical History & Bedside Examination

### Symptoms
| Domain | Symptoms |
|--------|----------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Orthostatic dizziness/syncope, palpitations, postprandial hypotension |
| **Sudomotor** | Hyperhidrosis, anhidrosis, heat intolerance |
| **GI** | Early satiety, nausea, bloating, constipation, diarrhoea (diabetic) |
| **GU** | Bladder urgency/frequency/retention, erectile dysfunction |
| **Pupillary** | Light sensitivity, blurred near vision |
| **Sleep / Respiratory** | Stridor (MSA), sleep apnoea |

### Bedside Tests
- **Lying-to-standing BP:** Measure BP supine ×5 min → stand 1 & 3 min. Drop ≥20/10 = orthostatic hypotension
- **HR change with standing:** ↑ 11-29 bpm normal at 15th beat; ↑ ≥30 bpm = POTS
- **Heart rate response to Valsalva:** Valsalva ratio = longest RR / shortest RR; normal >1.21
- **Pupillary responses:** Light, accommodation, Horner's, Adie's
- **Sweating:** Inspect, palpate, starch-iodine test (minor's starch)
- **Carotid sinus massage** (avoid if carotid disease)

## 4. Laboratory Tests

### Heart Rate Variability (HRV) — Cardiovagal
| Test | Method | Normal | Abnormal |
|------|--------|--------|----------|
| **Resting HRV** | RR interval variation during quiet breathing | High | ↓ in vagal dysfunction |
| **Deep breathing (R-R interval)** | Deep breath ×1 min, measure max-min RR | >15 bpm HR change (>20 years) | ↓ = cardiovagal failure |
| **Valsalva ratio** | 15 sec forced expiration against 40 mmHg | >1.21 | ↓ in cardiovagal failure |
| **Head-up tilt (HUT)** | 60-70° head-up ×30-45 min | No/limited symptoms | Orthostatic hypotension, syncope, POTS |
| **Heart rate response to standing** | 30:15 ratio (RR 30th / 15th) | >1.04 | ↓ in cardiovagal dysfunction |

### Blood Pressure Variability — Adrenergic
| Test | Method | Normal | Abnormal |
|------|--------|--------|----------|
| **BP response to standing** | Supine → stand | Drop <20/10 mmHg | ≥20/10 = orthostatic hypotension |
| **BP response to Valsalva** | Phases I-IV | Phase II (early): ↓BP, recovers; Phase IV: overshoot | ↓ recovery in adrenergic failure |
| **BP response to sustained handgrip** | 30% max ×3 min | ↑ DBP >10 mmHg | Failure = sympathetic dysfunction |
| **Cold pressor** | Hand in ice water ×1-2 min | ↑ BP | Failure = sympathetic dysfunction |

### Sudomotor Function
| Test | Method | What it Shows |
|------|--------|---------------|
| **Thermoregulatory sweat test (TST)** | Heat chamber, indicator powder | Anhidrosis patterns (central, peripheral, focal) |
| **Quantitative sudomotor axon reflex test (QSART)** | Local ACh iontophoresis, sweat measured | Postganglionic sudomotor function |
| **Sympathetic skin response (SSR)** | Sudomotor via electrical stimulation | Sympathetic sudomotor integrity |
| **Silastic imprint / QDIRT** | Sweat droplet quantification | Regional sweat output |

### Pharmacological Tests
| Test | What it Shows |
|------|---------------|
| **Noradrenaline supine + standing** | PAF (low resting, no rise), MSA (normal resting, no rise on standing) |
| **Vasoactive intestinal peptide (VIP), renin, aldosterone** | Research |
| **Tyramine / noradrenaline challenge** | Research |

## 5. Head-Up Tilt (HUT) Test

### Indications
- **Recurrent unexplained syncope** (after cardiac causes excluded)
- **Suspected reflex (vasovagal) syncope**
- **POTS evaluation**
- **Chronic orthostatic intolerance**

### Protocol
- Supine ≥5 min, then **60-70° head-up** for 20-45 min
- BP, HR continuously
- Optional **pharmacological provocation** (GTN/SL, isoprenaline, edrophonium)

### Patterns
| Pattern | BP | HR | Diagnosis |
|---------|----|----|-----------|
| **Normal** | Mild ↓ or stable | ↑ 5-30 bpm | No orthostatic intolerance |
| **Orthostatic hypotension** | ↓ ≥20/10 mmHg | No/minimal ↑ | Sympathetic failure |
| **POTS** | Stable or mild ↓ | ↑ ≥30 bpm (or ≥40 in teens) | Postural tachycardia |
| **Cardioinhibitory syncope (vasovagal)** | ↓ BP + bradycardia/asystole | ↓ | Reflex syncope |
| **Vasodepressor syncope** | ↓ BP | Stable/↑ | Reflex syncope |
| **MSA / PAF** | Sustained OH | No/minimal ↑ | Central / peripheral failure |

## 6. POTS (Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome)

### Diagnostic Criteria
- HR ↑ ≥30 bpm within 10 min of standing (≥40 in adolescents) without orthostatic hypotension
- Symptoms: palpitations, light-headedness, fatigue, exercise intolerance, brain fog
- Duration ≥3 months
- Exclusion of other causes (anaemia, dehydration, hyperthyroidism, drugs)

### Subtypes
| Subtype | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Neuropathic** | Small fibre neuropathy in lower limbs |
| **Hyperadrenergic** | ↑ plasma noradrenaline on standing (>600 pg/mL) |
| **Hypovolaemic** | ↓ blood volume |
| **Autoimmune** | Post-viral, autoantibodies (adrenergic, muscarinic) |

### Management
- Non-pharmacological: fluids (2-3 L), salt (10-12 g), compression stockings, exercise (graded)
- Pharmacological: **Beta-blocker** (propranolol, ivabradine), **fludrocortisone**, **midodrine**, **clonidine**, **pyridostigmine**

## 7. MSA (Multiple System Atrophy) vs PAF

### MSA
- **Central** autonomic failure (preganglionic) + cerebellar (MSA-C) or parkinsonian (MSA-P)
- **Supine plasma NA:** Often NORMAL or ↑ (central)
- **Severe bladder dysfunction**, erectile dysfunction
- **Stridor** (vocal cord abductor paralysis)
- **Falls, rapid progression**
- Levodopa-poorly responsive parkinsonism

### PAF (Pure Autonomic Failure)
- **Peripheral** autonomic failure only
- **Supine plasma NA:** LOW
- Less severe bladder symptoms
- Slow progression, better prognosis
- No CNS signs

### Distinguishing Test
- **Plasma noradrenaline supine:** Low in PAF; normal/high in MSA
- **Cardiac MIBG scan:** ↓ uptake in PAF (cardiac sympathetic denervation); preserved in MSA

---

## 8. Diabetic Autonomic Neuropathy

### Tests
- **HRV (deep breathing, Valsalva):** Cardiovagal — earliest
- **BP response to standing:** Sympathetic — later
- **QTc prolongation** (ECG)
- **Gustatory sweating** (facial sweating after food)
- **Gastric emptying study** (diabetic gastroparesis)
- **Urodynamics** (bladder)
- **Cardiac MIBG:** Reduced uptake

### Clinical Significance
- ↑ Mortality (5-year ~50% if symptomatic autonomic neuropathy)
- ↑ Perioperative risk (haemodynamic instability)
- Foot ulceration risk (sweat loss → dry skin → fissures)

---

## 9. Autonomic Dysfunction in Other Conditions

| Condition | Pattern |
|-----------|---------|
| **GBS** | Labile BP/HR (sympathetic storms); consider ICU monitoring |
| **Spinal cord injury (above T6)** | Autonomic dysreflexia (paroxysmal hypertension) |
| **Parkinson's disease** | Cardiac sympathetic denervation (MIBG ↓), OH (medications also contribute) |
| **Amyloid** | Severe orthostatic hypotension + peripheral neuropathy |
| **Botulism** | Autonomic failure (anticholinergic) |
| **Spinal cord lesion above T6** | Autonomic dysreflexia |

## 10. Management

### Orthostatic Hypotension
| Strategy | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Non-pharm** | Fluids (2-3 L), salt ↑, head-of-bed ↑ 10-30°, slow position changes, compression stockings |
| **Midodrine** | α1-agonist, 2.5-10 mg TDS; avoid supine (supine hypertension) |
| **Fludrocortisone** | Mineralocorticoid, 0.1-0.3 mg/day; monitor K+ |
| **Droxidopa** | Noradrenaline precursor, FDA-approved |
| **Avoid** | Heat, alcohol, large meals (carbohydrate load), dehydration |

### Supine Hypertension (in OH)
- Head-up tilt at night
- Short-acting antihypertensives (e.g., captopril, losartan, GTN patch)

### Bladder
| Problem | Treatment |
|---------|-----------|
| **Urinary retention** | Intermittent self-catheterisation |
| **Detrusor overactivity** | Anticholinergics (oxybutynin), mirabegron |
| **Nocturia** | Desmopressin (caution Na+ ↓) |

### Erectile Dysfunction
- PDE5 inhibitors (caution with nitrates), vacuum devices

### Hyperhidrosis
- Topical antiperspirants, botulinum toxin, iontophoresis, anticholinergics (glycopyrrolate)

## 11. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| **Cardiovagal tests** | HRV, deep breathing, Valsalva ratio, 30:15 ratio |
| **Adrenergic tests** | BP standing, BP Valsalva phase IV, sustained handgrip, cold pressor |
| **Sudomotor** | TST, QSART, SSR |
| **OH** | Drop ≥20/10 mmHg within 3 min standing |
| **POTS** | HR ↑ ≥30 bpm on standing, no OH |
| **Valsalva ratio** | Normal >1.21 |
| **Tilt test** | 60-70° ×20-45 min for syncope |
| **MSA vs PAF** | NA supine LOW in PAF, NORMAL/HIGH in MSA; MIBG ↓ PAF, preserved MSA |
| **GBS dysautonomia** | ICU monitoring for BP/HR lability |
| **Autonomic dysreflexia** | Spinal cord injury above T6 |
| **Diabetic autonomic** | Earliest abnormality = ↓ HRV; ↑ mortality |

## 12. Special Situations

| Situation | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Pregnancy** | Tilt table contraindicated; physiological changes complicate interpretation |
| **Children** | POTS common; lower BP/HR thresholds |
| **Elderly** | Many drugs worsen OH (alpha-blockers, diuretics) |
| **Athletes** | Resting bradycardia, ↑ HRV — normal |
| **Cardiac disease** | Tilt less informative; atrial pacing, arrhythmias confound |
| **Anti-cholinergic burden** | Common cause of autonomic symptoms in elderly |

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** Define orthostatic hypotension.
   **A:** Sustained drop in systolic BP ≥20 mmHg or diastolic ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing.
2. **Q:** What is the Valsalva ratio and what is normal?
   **A:** Longest RR / shortest RR during Valsalva; normal >1.21.
3. **Q:** How do you distinguish MSA from PAF biochemically?
   **A:** Plasma noradrenaline supine — LOW in PAF; NORMAL/HIGH in MSA.
4. **Q:** Define POTS.
   **A:** HR ↑ ≥30 bpm on standing without orthostatic hypotension, with chronic symptoms.
5. **Q:** What is autonomic dysreflexia?
   **A:** Paroxysmal hypertension in spinal cord injury above T6, triggered by noxious stimulus below level (full bladder, bowel, pressure).

---

## Common Confusions

| Confusion | Clarification |
|-----------|---------------|
| OH = dehydration | Many causes (neurogenic, drug, cardiac) |
| POTS = anxiety | Real syndrome with measurable haemodynamics |
| MSA = Parkinson's | Autonomic failure, cerebellar, falls, stridor — different |
| GBS dysautonomia = pain | Affects BP/HR/HRV — needs ICU monitoring |

---

## Mnemonics

1. **20/10 RULE for OH** — ↓SBP ≥20 / ↓DBP ≥10 within 3 min standing
2. **30 BEATS for POTS** — HR ↑ ≥30 bpm (≥40 in teens)
3. **NA LOW PAF, NA HIGH MSA** — supine noradrenaline distinguishes
4. **T6 = DYSREFLEXIA** — spinal cord lesions above T6

## SBA Questions (10)

1. **Scenario:** A 70-year-old man with Parkinson's, falls, dizziness on standing. BP supine 150/85, standing at 1 min 95/60. Symptoms worst after meals. Diagnosis?
   **A:** MSA-P **B:** Parkinson's with neurogenic OH **C:** Cardiac failure **D:** PAF
   **Ans:** B — Parkinson's often has OH from sympathetic denervation and drugs. MSA considered if severe/progressive.

2. **Scenario:** A 25-year-old woman with chronic fatigue, palpitations on standing. HR supine 75, standing 10 min HR 120, BP stable. Diagnosis?
   **A:** POTS **B:** MSA **C:** Vasovagal syncope **D:** Anaemia
   **Ans:** A — HR ↑ ≥30 bpm without OH = POTS.

3. **Scenario:** A 60-year-old with severe OH, bladder dysfunction, stridor, cerebellar ataxia. Plasma NA supine elevated. Likely diagnosis?
   **A:** PAF **B:** MSA **C:** Parkinson's **D:** Diabetic autonomic neuropathy
   **Ans:** B — MSA: NA supine normal/high (central), with cerebellar + autonomic features.

4. **Scenario:** A 50-year-old with severe OH, no CNS signs, low supine plasma NA. Likely diagnosis?
   **A:** PAF **B:** MSA **C:** ALS **D:** GBS
   **Ans:** A — PAF: peripheral autonomic failure only, low NA, no CNS features.

5. **Scenario:** Diabetic patient with resting tachycardia, exercise intolerance, orthostatic hypotension, gastroparesis. Earliest autonomic abnormality?
   **A:** ↓ HRV (deep breathing) **B:** Orthostatic hypotension **C:** Anhidrosis **D:** Erectile dysfunction
   **Ans:** A — Cardiovagal dysfunction earliest.

6. **Scenario:** Spinal cord injury patient (T4) develops sudden severe hypertension during bladder catheterisation. Diagnosis?
   **A:** Septic shock **B:** Autonomic dysreflexia **C:** POTS **D:** Pulmonary embolism
   **Ans:** B — T6-level trigger (bladder distension) → autonomic dysreflexia. Sit up, empty bladder.

7. **Scenario:** Recurrent unexplained syncope. ECG, echo normal. Next investigation?
   **A:** EEG **B:** Tilt table test **C:** MRI brain **D:** Lumbar puncture
   **Ans:** B — Tilt test for suspected reflex syncope.

8. **Scenario:** POTS patient. Best initial non-pharmacological intervention?
   **A:** Beta-blockers **B:** Increased fluids (2-3 L), salt, compression, exercise **C:** Pacemaker **D:** Vasodilators
   **Ans:** B — Non-pharmacological first.

9. **Scenario:** Patient on midodrine for OH. Side effect to monitor?
   **A:** Hypotension **B:** Supine hypertension **C:** Bradycardia **D:** Diarrhoea
   **Ans:** B — Midodrine causes supine hypertension; avoid lying flat for 4h post-dose.

10. **Scenario:** A 30-year-old with GBS, ventilated. Sudden BP 200/110, HR 130, then BP 70/40, HR 50. Best action?
    **A:** Vasopressors alone **B:** ICU monitoring, treat labile BP/HR (sedation, labetalol, fluids) **C:** Discharge **D:** Sedation only
    **Ans:** B — GBS dysautonomia = ICU monitoring + treat lability carefully.
