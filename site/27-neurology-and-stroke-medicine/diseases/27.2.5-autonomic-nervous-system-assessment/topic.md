# Autonomic Nervous System Assessment

> [!tip] **Autonomic dysfunction ranges from local (Horner, Adie) to generalised (MSA, PAF, GBS).** Recognising patterns (sympathetic vs parasympathetic, central vs peripheral) and tests (HRV, BP response, sudomotor, biochemical) is essential.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] Describe anatomy and physiology of sympathetic and parasympathetic systems
- [ ] Recognise major autonomic syndromes (MSA, PAF, POTS, dysautonomia in GBS/DM)
- [ ] Localise lesion (central vs peripheral, preganglionic vs postganglionic)
- [ ] Interpret autonomic testing (HRV, BP, sudomotor)
- [ ] Differentiate central vs peripheral causes of autonomic failure
- [ ] Manage orthostatic hypotension and autonomic dysfunction

## 1. Anatomy & Physiology

### Sympathetic (Thoracolumbar)
- **Preganglionic:** Lateral horn T1-L2 → exit in white rami communicantes
- **Sympathetic chain:** Paravertebral (cervical to sacral)
- **Postganglionic:** Long; targets sweat glands, blood vessels, viscera
- **Neurotransmitter:** Preganglionic = ACh; Postganglionic = Noradrenaline (except sweat glands = ACh)

### Parasympathetic (Craniosacral)
- **Cranial:** CN III (pupil, accommodation), CN VII (lacrimal, salivary), CN IX (parotid), CN X (heart, GI, lungs)
- **Sacral:** S2-S4 (bladder, distal colon, rectum, genitals)
- **Neurotransmitter:** Preganglionic and postganglionic = ACh (muscarinic)

### Key Functions
| System | Functions |
|--------|-----------|
| **Sympathetic** | ↑ HR, contractility; bronchodilation; ↓ GI motility; ↑ sweating; pupil dilation; ejaculation |
| **Parasympathetic** | ↓ HR; bronchoconstriction; ↑ GI motility; ↑ secretions; pupil constriction; erection |

## 2. Major Autonomic Syndromes

### Multiple System Atrophy (MSA)
- **Sporadic** adult-onset (>30 years) neurodegenerative disorder with autonomic failure + parkinsonism (MSA-P) or cerebellar ataxia (MSA-C)
- **Autonomic:** Orthostatic hypotension, urinary dysfunction (urgency, retention), erectile dysfunction, stridor
- **Pathology:** α-synuclein inclusions in glia (glial cytoplasmic inclusions)
- **Subtypes:** MSA-P (parkinsonian, 80% levodopa-poorly responsive); MSA-C (cerebellar, predominant ataxia)
- **Differential from Parkinson's:** Severe autonomic failure, falls, stridor, cerebellar features, poor levodopa response
- **MRI:** Hot cross bun sign (pons), putaminal atrophy
- **Treatment:** Fludrocortisone, midodrine, oxybutynin/tolterodine (bladder), CPAP/tracheostomy (stridor)

### Pure Autonomic Failure (PAF)
- **Peripheral autonomic failure only** (no CNS signs)
- **Progressive** orthostatic hypotension, bladder symptoms, erectile dysfunction
- **Plasma NA supine: LOW** (peripheral); MIBG ↓ (cardiac sympathetic denervation)
- **Better prognosis** than MSA; conversion to MSA rare
- **Treatment:** Midodrine, fludrocortisone, hydration

### Parkinson's Disease with Autonomic Dysfunction
- **Cardiac sympathetic denervation** (MIBG ↓ early — distinguishes PD from MSA)
- **OH** (medications, autonomic)
- **GI:** Gastroparesis, constipation (severe in late disease)
- **Bladder:** Urgency, frequency (PSP, MSA more severe)

### Diabetic Autonomic Neuropathy
- **Cardiovascular** (HRV ↓, OH, ↑QTc, exercise intolerance, ↑ mortality)
- **GI:** Gastroparesis (early satiety), constipation/diarrhoea
- **GU:** Erectile dysfunction, retrograde ejaculation, bladder dysfunction
- **Sudomotor:** Anhidrosis (foot), gustatory sweating
- **Treatment:** Intensive glycaemic control, symptomatic

### Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (POTS)
- **HR ↑ ≥30 bpm within 10 min** standing (≥40 in adolescents)
- **No orthostatic hypotension**; chronic symptoms (>3 months)
- **Female predominance** (5:1); young adult onset
- **Subtypes:** Neuropathic, hyperadrenergic, hypovolaemic, autoimmune
- **Treatment:** Fluids (2-3 L), salt ↑, compression stockings, exercise (graded), β-blocker (propranolol), ivabradine, fludrocortisone, midodrine

### Reflex Syncope (Vasovagal)
- **Trigger:** Emotion, pain, prolonged standing, sight of blood, micturition, cough
- **Prodrome:** Nausea, sweating, lightheadedness, "greying out"
- **BP ↓ + HR ↓** (cardioinhibitory) or BP ↓ alone (vasodepressor)
- **Tilt test positive**
- **Treatment:** Trigger avoidance, hydration, salt, counter-manoeuvres (leg crossing, hand grip); midodrine, fludrocortisone; pacemaker (cardioinhibitory)

### Autonomic Dysreflexia
- **Spinal cord injury above T6** (major splanchnic outflow)
- **Trigger:** Bladder distension, bowel impaction, pressure sores, ingrown toenail
- **Symptoms:** Sudden severe hypertension (BP can rise >40 mmHg), headache, flushing/sweating above lesion, bradycardia (reflex), piloerection
- **Treatment:** Sit up, remove trigger (catheterise, evacuate bowel), GTN, nifedipine

### Familial Dysautonomia (Riley-Day)
- **AR** (IKBKAP / ELP1 mutation); Ashkenazi Jewish
- **Absent tears, ↓ taste, ↓ pain sensation**, labile BP, hyperhidrosis, **sural nerve biopsy** (large-fibre loss)
- **Treatment:** Supportive; fludrocortisone for OH crises

### Autoimmune Autonomic Ganglionopathy (AAG)
- **Antibodies against ganglionic ACh receptors** (α3)
- **Subacute severe pan-autonomic failure**
- **Treatment:** IVIG, plasma exchange, immunosuppression

### Paraneoplastic Autonomic Neuropathy
- **Anti-Hu** (small cell lung cancer), anti-CRMP5
- **Subacute onset**, severe autonomic failure

## 3. Localised Autonomic Disorders

### Horner's Syndrome
- **Sympathetic lesion**: ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis (forehead if preganglionic)
- **Causes:** Carotid/vertebral dissection, Pancoast tumour, brainstem stroke, syringomyelia, cluster headache
- **Wallenberg (lateral medullary):** Ipsilateral Horner's
- **Carotid dissection:** Painful Horner's (medical emergency — stroke risk)
- **Pupil:** Cocaine test (no dilation in Horner); hydroxyamphetamine (distinguishes preganglionic vs postganglionic)

### Adie's Pupil (Holmes-Adie)
- **Postganglionic parasympathetic lesion** (ciliary ganglion)
- **Dilated pupil**, poor/absent light response, slow constriction to accommodation ("light-near dissociation")
- **Often associated with absent ankle/knee reflexes** (Adie's syndrome)
- **Treatment:** None usually needed; pilocarpine for cosmesis

### Argyll-Robertson Pupil
- **Neurosyphilis** — tertiary
- **Small, irregular pupil, light-near dissociation**
- **Accommodates but doesn't react to light**

### Charcot-Marie-Tooth with Pupil
- **ROSALIE (CMT + iritis)** or AD CMT with abnormal pupils

## 4. History Taking in Autonomic Disease

### Symptom Domains
| Domain | Symptoms |
|--------|----------|
| **Cardiovascular** | Light-headedness, syncope, palpitations, chest pain |
| **Sudomotor** | Hyperhidrosis, anhidrosis, heat intolerance |
| **GI** | Dysphagia, early satiety, nausea, bloating, constipation, diarrhoea |
| **GU** | Frequency, urgency, retention, incontinence, erectile dysfunction |
| **Ocular** | Dry eyes, blurred near vision, light sensitivity |
| **Pupillary** | Anisocoria, Horner features |
| **Other** | Sleep disturbance, stridor (MSA) |

### Triggers / Pattern
- **Time of day** (morning OH worse after prolonged supine)
- **Postprandial** (worsens OH — splanchnic pooling)
- **Heat, alcohol** (vasodilation → OH)
- **Standing** (orthostatic)

## 5. Examination

### Bedside
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| **BP supine / standing** | OH = ↓ SBP ≥20 / ↓ DBP ≥10 within 3 min |
| **HR change on standing** | ↑ 11-29 normal; ≥30 = POTS |
| **30:15 ratio** | RR at 30th beat / 15th beat >1.04 normal |
| **Valsalva manoeuvre** | Valsalva ratio >1.21 normal |
| **Pupils** | Size, symmetry, light, accommodation |
| **Sweating** | Inspection, palpate |
| **Skin** | Colour, trophic changes |

### Cardiovascular Reflex Tests
| Test | Tests | Normal |
|------|-------|--------|
| **HR response to deep breathing** | Cardiovagal | >15 bpm HR change |
| **Valsalva ratio** | Cardiovagal | >1.21 |
| **HR response to standing (30:15)** | Cardiovagal | >1.04 |
| **BP response to standing** | Adrenergic | <20/10 mmHg drop |
| **BP response to Valsalva (phase IV)** | Adrenergic | BP overshoot |
| **BP response to sustained handgrip** | Adrenergic | ↑ DBP >10 mmHg |
| **Cold pressor** | Adrenergic | BP rise |

### Sudomotor
| Test | Tests |
|------|-------|
| **Thermoregulatory sweat test (TST)** | Central + peripheral sudomotor |
| **QSART** | Postganglionic sudomotor |
| **Sympathetic skin response (SSR)** | Sympathetic sudomotor |

## 6. Investigations

### First-Line
- **FBC** (anaemia — fatigue, OH)
- **U&E, glucose, HbA1c** (DM)
- **TFT** (hyperthyroid — tachy; hypothyroid — fatigue)
- **ESR, CRP**
- **Vitamin B12, folate**
- **Autoimmune screen** (ANA, ENA, ANCA)
- **HIV, syphilis** (relevant to autonomic neuropathy)

### Specialised
- **Tilt table test** — orthostatic intolerance, syncope
- **24-hour ambulatory BP** (nocturnal hypertension / OH)
- **Cardiac MIBG scan** — sympathetic denervation (PD, PAF; preserved in MSA)
- **Plasma noradrenaline supine + standing** — central (MSA: N/H) vs peripheral (PAF: L) vs healthy (R)
- **CSF** (autoimmune encephalitis, NMO)
- **MRI brain / spine** (cord lesion, MSA features)
- **Gastric emptying study** (gastroparesis)
- **Urodynamics** (bladder)
- **Small fibre biopsy** (POTS, SFN)

## 7. Distinguishing Central vs Peripheral Autonomic Failure

| Feature | Central (MSA) | Peripheral (PAF, DM, amyloid) |
|---------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| **Supine plasma NA** | Normal/high | Low |
| **HR response to standing** | Preserved | Reduced |
| **Cardiac MIBG** | Preserved | Reduced (denervation) |
| **CNS signs** | Parkinsonism, cerebellar | Absent |
| **Skin biopsy (noradrenergic fibres)** | Preserved | Reduced |
| **Progression** | Faster, falls, stridor | Slower |

## 8. Management of Orthostatic Hypotension

### Non-Pharmacological (FIRST-LINE)
- **Education:** Avoid triggers (heat, alcohol, large meals, dehydration)
- **Fluids:** 2-3 L/day
- **Salt:** 10-12 g/day (if not hypertensive)
- **Head-of-bed elevation:** 10-30° (reduces supine hypertension)
- **Compression:** Stockings (abdominal binder if severe)
- **Slow position changes**
- **Counter-manoeuvres:** Leg crossing, squatting, hand grip

### Pharmacological
| Drug | Class | Dose | Notes |
|------|-------|------|-------|
| **Midodrine** | α1 agonist | 2.5-10 mg TDS | **Supine hypertension**; avoid within 4h of lying |
| **Fludrocortisone** | Mineralocorticoid | 0.1-0.3 mg/day | Monitor K+, BP, weight; oedema |
| **Droxidopa** | NA prodrug | 100-600 mg TDS | FDA-approved |
| **Pyridostigmine** | AChE inhibitor | 60 mg TDS | Enhances ganglionic transmission |
| **Atomoxetine** | NE reuptake inhibitor | 18 mg BD | For central OH |
| **Octreotide** | Somatostatin analogue | SC | Postprandial OH |

### Supine Hypertension
- Short-acting antihypertensives: captopril, losartan, GTN patch (nocturnal)

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|

## 10. Special Situations

| Situation | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Pregnancy** | OH common (physiological); avoid midodrine |
| **Children** | POTS, familial dysautonomia (Ashkenazi) |
| **Elderly** | Polypharmacy worsens OH (alpha-blockers, diuretics) |
| **Diabetes** | Earliest autonomic sign = ↓ HRV |
| **Spinal cord injury** | Autonomic dysreflexia above T6 |
| **Surgery** | Anticipate haemodynamic instability in autonomic failure |

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** How do you differentiate MSA from PAF biochemically?
   **A:** Supine plasma noradrenaline — LOW in PAF (peripheral), NORMAL/HIGH in MSA (central). Cardiac MIBG ↓ in PAF; preserved in MSA.
2. **Q:** Define OH and its management.
   **A:** ↓ SBP ≥20 / ↓ DBP ≥10 within 3 min standing. Non-pharm: fluids, salt, head-up, compression. Drugs: midodrine, fludrocortisone, droxidopa.
3. **Q:** POTS criteria?
   **A:** HR ↑ ≥30 bpm (≥40 adolescents) within 10 min standing, no OH, symptoms ≥3 months.
4. **Q:** Horner's syndrome features and most important cause?
   **A:** Ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis. **Carotid dissection** (painful) — stroke risk.
5. **Q:** Autonomic dysreflexia — what level of lesion and triggers?
   **A:** SCI above T6; triggers below lesion (bladder, bowel, pressure, ingrown toenail).

## Common Confusions

| Confusion | Clarification |
|-----------|---------------|
| MSA = Parkinson's | MSA = atypical parkinsonism + autonomic; poor levodopa response |
| PAF = MSA | PAF = peripheral only; better prognosis |
| OH = dehydration | Many causes (neurogenic, drug, cardiac) |
| MIBG ↓ = PD | Also ↓ in PAF; preserved in MSA |
| Argyll-Robertson = Adie's | AR = small (syphilis); Adie's = dilated |

---

## Mnemonics

1. **MSA = MULTIPLE SYSTEM = multiple problems** — autonomic, parkinsonism, cerebellar
2. **PAF = PERIPHERAL = pure** — no CNS signs
3. **POTS = POSTURAL TACHYCARDIA** — HR up, no OH
4. **HORNER = PTOSIS, MIOSIS, ANHIDROSIS** — sympathetic lesion
5. **ADIE = DILATED PUPIL, LIGHT-NEAR SPLIT** — parasympathetic
6. **AR = ARGYLL ROBERTSON = SYPHILIS** — small pupil, light-near dissociation
7. **AUTONOMIC DYSREFLEXIA = T6 TRIGGER** — SCI above T6

## MCQs (10)

1. **Q:** Plasma noradrenaline supine in PAF is:
   **A:** High **B:** Normal **C:** Low **D:** Variable
   **Ans:** C — Low (peripheral failure).

2. **Q:** MSA is differentiated from PAF by:
   **A:** Higher age of onset **B:** CNS involvement (parkinsonism/cerebellar) and normal/high supine NA **C:** Earlier onset **D:** Sensory loss
   **Ans:** B

3. **Q:** Cardiac MIBG scan in MSA is:
   **A:** Decreased **B:** Preserved **C:** Variable **D:** Always abnormal
   **Ans:** B — Preserved in MSA (central); decreased in PAF/PD.

4. **Q:** POTS diagnostic HR criterion:
   **A:** HR ↑ ≥10 bpm on standing **B:** HR ↑ ≥30 bpm (≥40 in teens) **C:** HR ↑ ≥50 bpm **D:** Stable
   **Ans:** B

5. **Q:** Horner's syndrome triad:
   **A:** Miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis **B:** Mydriasis, ptosis, anhidrosis **C:** Miosis, exophthalmos, sweating **D:** Mydriasis, exophthalmos, no sweating
   **Ans:** A

6. **Q:** Argyll-Robertson pupil is associated with:
   **A:** Myasthenia gravis **B:** Neurosyphilis **C:** Parkinson's **D:** Multiple sclerosis
   **Ans:** B

7. **Q:** Autonomic dysreflexia occurs in spinal cord injury above:
   **A:** T1 **B:** T6 **C:** T12 **D:** L1
   **Ans:** B

8. **Q:** First-line drug for orthostatic hypotension:
   **A:** Midodrine or fludrocortisone **B:** Beta-blocker **C:** ACE inhibitor **D:** Calcium channel blocker
   **Ans:** A

9. **Q:** Adie's pupil is caused by:
   **A:** Preganglionic parasympathetic lesion **B:** Postganglionic parasympathetic lesion (ciliary ganglion) **C:** Sympathetic lesion **D:** Cortical lesion
   **Ans:** B

10. **Q:** Diabetic autonomic neuropathy earliest test:
    **A:** OH measurement **B:** HRV (deep breathing) **C:** MIBG **D:** Tilt test
    **Ans:** B — Cardiovagal dysfunction earliest.

## SBA Questions (10)

1. **Scenario:** A 60-year-old with parkinsonism, severe orthostatic hypotension, urinary incontinence, erectile dysfunction, falls. No levodopa response. Diagnosis?
   **A:** Parkinson's disease **B:** MSA-P **C:** Progressive supranuclear palsy **D:** Vascular parkinsonism
   **Ans:** B — MSA-P (parkinsonian + autonomic failure + poor levodopa).

2. **Scenario:** A 50-year-old with severe orthostatic hypotension only, no CNS signs. Plasma NA supine LOW. Cardiac MIBG ↓. Diagnosis?
   **A:** MSA **B:** PAF **C:** Parkinson's **D:** Diabetic autonomic neuropathy
   **Ans:** B — PAF (peripheral autonomic failure only).

3. **Scenario:** A 25-year-old woman with chronic fatigue, palpitations on standing, HR ↑ from 75 to 120, BP stable. Diagnosis?
   **A:** Vasovagal syncope **B:** POTS **C:** MSA **D:** Anxiety
   **Ans:** B — HR ↑30, no OH.

4. **Scenario:** Patient with T4 spinal cord injury develops sudden severe hypertension (220/120) during bladder catheterisation. Best action?
   **A:** Continue procedure **B:** Sit up, treat trigger (bladder), GTN/nifedipine if needed **C:** IV fluids **D:** Antibiotics
   **Ans:** B — Autonomic dysreflexia — remove trigger.

5. **Scenario:** A 30-year-old with painful Horner's syndrome. Next investigation?
   **A:** MRI brain **B:** CTA/MRA neck (carotid dissection) **C:** EEG **D:** Lumbar puncture
   **Ans:** B — Painful Horner = carotid dissection (stroke risk).

6. **Scenario:** A 60-year-old diabetic with resting tachycardia, exercise intolerance, orthostatic hypotension, gastroparesis. Earliest autonomic abnormality?
   **A:** OH **B:** ↓ HRV (deep breathing) **C:** Anhidrosis **D:** Constipation
   **Ans:** B — Cardiovagal dysfunction earliest.

7. **Scenario:** Patient with GBS, ventilated. Labile BP 90/50 to 200/110, alternating brady/tachycardia. Management?
   **A:** Discharge **B:** ICU monitoring, treat lability (labetalol, fluids, atropine, vasopressors) **C:** Permanent pacemaker **D:** Aspirin
   **Ans:** B — GBS dysautonomia is critical; ICU care.

8. **Scenario:** MSA patient with supine hypertension. Treatment at night?
   **A:** Midodrine **B:** Head-up tilt, short-acting antihypertensive (GTN patch, captopril) **C:** Aspirin **D:** Caffeine
   **Ans:** B — Supine hypertension management.

9. **Scenario:** Argyll-Robertson pupil (small, irregular, light-near dissociation) in 65-year-old with progressive cognitive decline. Diagnosis?
   **A:** Alzheimer's **B:** Neurosyphilis (tertiary) **C:** Parkinson's **D:** Multiple sclerosis
   **Ans:** B — Tertiary syphilis.

10. **Scenario:** POTS patient — first-line non-pharmacological treatment?
    **A:** Beta-blocker **B:** Fluids (2-3 L), salt (10-12 g), compression, exercise **C:** Pacemaker **D:** Vasodilators
    **Ans:** B — Lifestyle first; pharmacotherapy if inadequate.
