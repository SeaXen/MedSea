# Acute Visual Loss

> [!tip] **Acute visual loss = emergency.** Differentiate **monocular (eye/orbit/optic nerve)** from **binocular (retrochiasmal/cortex)**. Time-critical causes: GCA, retinal artery occlusion, retinal detachment, endophthalmitis.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] Classify acute visual loss (monocular vs binocular, transient vs persistent, painful vs painless)
- [ ] Diagnose retinal causes (AION, CRAO, BRVO, detachment)
- [ ] Recognise and treat giant cell arteritis (urgent steroids)
- [ ] Differentiate optic neuritis from other optic neuropathies
- [ ] Investigate appropriately (ESR/CRP, OCT, FFA, MRI, LP)

---

## 1. Definition / Classification

### Definition
**Sudden loss of vision** (partial or complete) in one or both eyes, with or without pain. The **time of onset, pattern, associated symptoms, and examination findings** guide urgent management.

### Classification
| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Monocular, transient** | Amaurosis fugax (retinal TIA), GCA, papilloedema (transient visual obscurations), Uhthoff's phenomenon |
| **Monocular, persistent** | Retinal artery/vein occlusion, retinal detachment, optic neuritis, AION, vitreous haemorrhage, endophthalmitis |
| **Binocular, sudden** | Pituitary apoplexy, occipital stroke, bilateral optic nerve lesion, posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES) |
| **Progressive over hours-days** | GCA, optic neuritis, retinal detachment |

## 2. Anatomical Localisation

| Site | Pattern |
|------|---------|
| **Cornea / anterior chamber** | Halo, painful, photophobia (keratitis, glaucoma) |
| **Lens (cataract)** | Gradual, painless, glare |
| **Vitreous** | Floaters, "cobwebs" |
| **Retina** | Curtain, flashing lights, sectoral or altitudinal |
| **Optic nerve** | Loss of colour vision, RAPD, central scotoma |
| **Chiasm** | Bitemporal hemianopia |
| **Optic tract** | Contralateral homonymous hemianopia |
| **Occipital cortex** | Homonymous hemianopia, often with macular sparing |

## 3. Critical Causes — Don't Miss

### Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA) / Temporal Arteritis
- **Sudden, painless, often transient or persistent monocular vision loss** (amaurosis fugax → AION → blindness)
- **Jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, headache, polymyalgia rheumatica**
- **ESR ↑↑, CRP ↑** (CRP more sensitive)
- **Treat with prednisolone 40-60 mg OD (no visual loss) or IV methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg ×3 days (visual loss) IMMEDIATELY** — before biopsy
- **Temporal artery biopsy** within 1-2 weeks (steroids don't prevent diagnosis if done within 2 weeks)

### Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)
- **Sudden, painless, severe monocular vision loss** (count fingers or worse)
- **Cherry-red spot** at macula, pale retina, "box-car" segmentation of arteries
- **RAPD present**
- **Ocular stroke equivalent** — urgent stroke workup (carotid Doppler, echo, ECG)
- **Treatment (limited evidence):** Ocular massage, anterior chamber paracentesis, IV acetazolamide, IV TPA (within 6-8h in selected centres)

### Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO)
- Sectoral field loss; similar to CRAO but smaller area
- Same workup as CRAO

### Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)
- **Sudden, painless** loss of vision; **blood and thunder** fundus (dilated tortuous veins, haemorrhages, cotton-wool spots)
- Risk factors: HTN, DM, glaucoma, hypercoagulable states
- Treat underlying cause; intravitreal anti-VEGF for macular oedema

### Anterior Ischaemic Optic Neuropathy (AION)
- **Arteritic (A-AION):** GCA — sudden severe loss
- **Non-arteritic (NA-AION):** Small vessel disease (HTN, DM, sleep apnoea, disc-at-risk) — altitudinal field loss
- **Disc swelling, often pale, RAPD present**

### Retinal Detachment
- **Flashes (photopsia), floaters, "curtain coming down"**
- **PVD → tear → detachment** (rhegmatogenous)
- **Treatment:** Laser (tear) or surgery (detachment) — URGENT (within days)
- Risk factors: myopia, trauma, prior surgery

### Vitreous Haemorrhage
- **Sudden painless vision loss with floaters, "red haze"**
- Causes: diabetic retinopathy, tear/detachment, vein occlusion
- **B-scan ultrasound** to assess retina

### Optic Neuritis
- **Subacute (over days), painful eye movement (90%), ↓ colour vision, RAPD, central scotoma**
- Young female, demyelinating (MS, NMO, MOG)
- **MRI** for demyelinating lesions; **aquaporin-4 (NMO), MOG antibodies**

### Pituitary Apoplexy
- **Sudden severe headache, bitemporal hemianopia, ophthalmoplegia, ↓consciousness**
- **MRI pituitary (haemorrhage)**
- **Emergency** — IV hydrocortisone, neurosurgery

### Occipital Stroke
- **Sudden binocular homonymous hemianopia**, often with macular sparing
- **Stroke workup** (CT/MRI, CTA, echo)

## 4. Clinical Assessment

### History
| Question | Importance |
|----------|------------|
| **Onset (sudden, gradual, transient)** | Vascular vs inflammatory |
| **Monocular vs binocular** | Localisation |
| **Painful vs painless** | Optic neuritis (painful eye movement), GCA (headache), retinal detachment (deep pain rare) |
| **Pattern (curtain, altitudinal, central scotoma, hemianopia)** | Anatomical site |
| **Associated symptoms** | Jaw claudication (GCA), flashes (detachment), diplopia (apoplexy, aneurysm), headache |
| **PMH** | HTN, DM, AF, vasculitis, MS, sarcoid |
| **Drugs** | Amiodarone, ethambutol, hydroxychloroquine (toxic optic neuropathy) |
| **Social** | Smoking, alcohol (tobacco-alcohol amblyopia), sexual (syphilis), IVDU (HIV, endocarditis) |

### Examination
- **Visual acuity (Snellen)** — each eye separately
- **Pupils:** RAPD (relative afferent pupillary defect)
- **Colour vision (Ishihara)** — early loss in optic neuropathy
- **Visual fields (confrontation + formal perimetry)**
- **Fundoscopy** (dilated if possible — caveat in GCA suspicion)
- **Anterior segment** (cornea, AC)
- **Eye movements** (pain on movement = optic neuritis; ophthalmoplegia = aneurysm, apoplexy)
- **Temporal artery** (tenderness, reduced pulse)

### RAPD (Marcus Gunn Pupil)
- **Swinging flashlight test:** Affected eye dilates (paradoxical) when light swings to it
- Indicates **optic nerve or retinal disease** (unilateral/bilateral asymmetric)
- **Absent in:** media opacity (cataract), macular disease, cortical blindness

## 5. Investigations

### First-Line (Urgent)
| Test | Indication |
|------|-----------|
| **ESR + CRP** | **GCA** (urgent — don't delay treatment) |
| **Blood glucose** | Hyperglycaemia (osmotic lens swelling), diabetes |
| **FBC, U&E, LFT** | General |
| **Viral/immune** | ANA, ENA (vasculitis), HIV, syphilis, Lyme |
| **Carotid Doppler** | CRAO, BRAO, amaurosis fugax |
| **ECG, echo** | AF, valvular disease (CRAO source) |
| **OCT (optical coherence tomography)** | Macular oedema, ONH swelling, MS activity |
| **FFA (fundus fluorescein angiography)** | Vascular occlusions, vasculitis |

### Neuroimaging
| Modality | Indication |
|----------|-----------|
| **MRI brain + orbits + fat-suppression** | Optic neuritis (demyelination), chiasmal lesion, pituitary, stroke |
| **CT head** | Acute stroke / haemorrhage (suspected occipital cortex lesion) |
| **CTA / MRA** | Carotid dissection, aneurysm |

### Specialised
| Test | Indication |
|------|-----------|
| **Visual evoked potentials (VEP)** | Subclinical optic neuritis (delayed P100) |
| **LP (CSF)** | Optic neuritis (OCB), infection, IIH |
| **Temporal artery biopsy** | GCA confirmation |
| **B-scan ocular ultrasound** | Retinal detachment, vitreous haemorrhage |

## 6. Specific Diagnoses — Details

### GCA
- **ESR ↑↑ (often >100), CRP ↑, thrombocytosis, anaemia of chronic disease**
- **Temporal artery biopsy** (≥2 cm length; skip lesions — if negative, consider contralateral biopsy)
- **Treatment (NICE / EULAR):**
  - **No visual symptoms:** Prednisolone 40-60 mg PO OD
  - **Visual symptoms:** IV methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg ×3 days → oral prednisolone
  - **Taper slowly** over 12-18 months
  - **Adjunctive tocilizumab** (steroid-sparing, IL-6 antagonist)
- **Complications:** Aortic aneurysm, stroke, MI (large-vessel vasculitis)

### CRAO
- **"Stroke of the eye"** — 4.5h window for IV TPA (selected cases)
- Acute interventions: ocular massage, anterior chamber paracentesis, IV acetazolamide, hyperbaric O2 (limited evidence)
- **Stroke workup:** Carotid Doppler, echo, ECG, lipid profile
- **Visual prognosis poor** if retinal ischaemia established

### Optic Neuritis
- **MRI brain + orbit (STIR/fat-suppressed):** Lesions typical of MS
- **CSF:** OCB (MS)
- **Anti-AQP4 (NMO):** Longitudinally extensive optic nerve + LETM
- **Anti-MOG (MOGAD):** Bilateral, optic disc swelling
- **Treatment:** IV methylprednisolone 1g/day ×3 days (faster recovery, no long-term visual benefit)

### Pituitary Apoplexy
- **MRI:** Haemorrhagic pituitary enlargement
- **Treatment:** IV hydrocortisone 100 mg STAT (before endocrine workup if severe); surgery if severe visual loss / altered consciousness

### Vitreous Haemorrhage
- **B-scan ultrasound** if fundus view obscured
- **Identify retinal tear / detachment** (referral to retinal surgeon)
- **DM retinopathy:** Laser / anti-VEGF

## 7. Red Flags

| Red Flag | Cause |
|----------|-------|
| **Sudden painless monocular loss + jaw claudication + headache** | **GCA** — start steroids immediately |
| **Sudden painless severe loss + cherry-red spot** | **CRAO** — stroke workup |
| **Flashes + floaters + curtain** | **Retinal detachment** — urgent referral |
| **Bitemporal hemianopia + headache** | **Pituitary apoplexy** — emergency |
| **Painful eye movement + ↓colour vision** | **Optic neuritis** — MRI |
| **Homonymous hemianopia** | **Occipital stroke** |
| **Painful red eye + haloes + ↓vision** | **Acute angle-closure glaucoma** |
| **Sudden onset in diabetic / hypertensive** | Vascular event |

---

## 8. Management — Time-Critical

| Time | Action |
|------|--------|
| **Immediate** | ABCs, visual acuity, RAPD, fundoscopy |
| **Within minutes** | GCA suspected → IV methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg |
| **Within hours** | CRAO → consider ocular massage, paracentesis, IV TPA (per local protocol) |
| **Within 24h** | Pituitary apoplexy → IV hydrocortisone, MRI, neurosurgery |
| **Within 24-48h** | Retinal detachment → vitreoretinal surgery referral |
| **Within days** | Optic neuritis → MRI, IV methylprednisolone if severe |
| **Within 1-2 weeks** | Temporal artery biopsy (steroids won't affect yield) |

---

## 9. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| **GCA** | ESR/CRP urgent; IV MP if visual loss; TAB within 2 weeks |
| **CRAO** | Cherry-red spot; "stroke of eye"; IV TPA per protocol |
| **Optic neuritis** | Painful eye movement; ↓colour vision; MRI for demyelination |
| **NMO vs MS** | Anti-AQP4 (NMO); LETM, severe vision loss; MOG = bilateral |
| **CRVO** | Blood and thunder fundus; anti-VEGF for oedema |
| **Retinal detachment** | Curtain, flashes, floaters; urgent surgery |
| **Pituitary apoplexy** | Bitemporal hemianopia, headache, ↓consciousness; IV hydrocortisone |
| **RAPD** | Optic nerve or severe retinal disease; absent in media opacity / cortical |
| **Visual fields** | Altitudinal = AION; central scotoma = optic neuritis; hemianopia = chiasmal/post-chiasmal |
| **Sudden painless** | Vascular (CRAO, AION), GCA |

## 10. Special Situations

| Situation | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Pregnancy** | GCA rare; consider hypercoagulable (CRVO); avoid warfarin in 1st trimester |
| **Children** | Optic neuritis consider MOG; trauma common |
| **Elderly** | GCA, NA-AION, retinal detachment |
| **Diabetic** | CRVO, vitreous haemorrhage, diabetic maculopathy |
| **Anticoagulated** | Vitreous haemorrhage, intracranial haemorrhage |
| **HIV** | CMV retinitis (floaters, scotomas — yellow-white lesions) |

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** Cherry-red spot at macula — diagnosis?
   **A:** Central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO).
2. **Q:** Sudden painless vision loss + jaw claudication + headache — first step?
   **A:** Start high-dose steroids (prednisolone 40-60 mg or IV methylprednisolone if visual symptoms); ESR/CRP; temporal artery biopsy.
3. **Q:** RAPD suggests lesion where?
   **A:** Optic nerve or severe retinal disease; not cortical or media.
4. **Q:** Sudden bitemporal hemianopia + headache. Diagnosis?
   **A:** Pituitary apoplexy.
5. **Q:** Pain on eye movement + ↓colour vision in young woman. Diagnosis?
   **A:** Optic neuritis (demyelinating — MS, NMO, MOG).

---

## Common Confusions

| Confusion | Clarification |
|-----------|---------------|
| All optic neuritis = MS | Could be NMO (anti-AQP4) or MOGAD (anti-MOG) |
| GCA + negative biopsy | Skip lesions — consider contralateral biopsy |
| CRAO treat with thrombolysis | Limited evidence; "stroke of eye" — within 4.5h |
| RAPD in cortical blindness | NO — RAPD absent (post-chiasmal lesion) |
| Vitreous haemorrhage vs retinal detachment | B-scan ultrasound |

---

## Mnemonics

1. **GCA = JAW CLAUDICATION + ↑ESR** — start steroids STAT
2. **CRAO = CHERRY-RED SPOT** — ocular stroke
3. **OPTIC NEURITIS = PAIN ON MOVEMENT + ↓COLOUR** — MRI
4. **CURTAIN DOWN = DETACHMENT** — urgent surgery
5. **BITEMPORAL = PITUITARY** — apoplexy if sudden

## MCQs (10)

1. **Q:** Cherry-red spot at the macula indicates:
   **A:** Retinal detachment **B:** Central retinal artery occlusion **C:** Retinal vein occlusion **D:** Optic neuritis
   **Ans:** B

2. **Q:** GCA first-line treatment if vision is threatened:
   **A:** Aspirin **B:** IV methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg ×3 days **C:** Methotrexate **D:** Acetazolamide
   **Ans:** B

3. **Q:** Pain on eye movement suggests:
   **A:** Retinal detachment **B:** Optic neuritis **C:** CRAO **D:** CRVO
   **Ans:** B

4. **Q:** RAPD is absent in which lesion?
   **A:** Optic neuritis **B:** Retinal detachment **C:** Cortical blindness **D:** Severe CRAO
   **Ans:** C — RAPD requires intact afferent path; cortical lesion doesn't affect afferent limb.

5. **Q:** Pituitary apoplexy first treatment:
   **A:** Aspirin **B:** IV hydrocortisone 100 mg STAT **C:** IV MP **D:** Antibiotics
   **Ans:** B

6. **Q:** Blood and thunder fundus describes:
   **A:** CRAO **B:** CRVO **C:** Retinal detachment **D:** Vitreous haemorrhage
   **Ans:** B

7. **Q:** In CRAO, the classic time window for IV TPA consideration is:
   **A:** 30 min **B:** 1.5h **C:** 4.5h **D:** 24h
   **Ans:** C — Same as stroke (selected centres).

8. **Q:** Most common cause of CRAO is:
   **A:** Atherosclerosis (carotid embolus) **B:** Giant cell arteritis **C:** Hypercoagulable **D:** Trauma
   **Ans:** A — Carotid atherosclerosis (embolus).

9. **Q:** Altitudinal visual field defect is classic for:
   **A:** Optic neuritis **B:** AION **C:** CRVO **D:** Pituitary adenoma
   **Ans:** B — AION (non-arteritic, disc at risk).

10. **Q:** Most appropriate initial test for suspected GCA:
    **A:** MRI brain **B:** ESR + CRP **C:** Temporal artery biopsy **D:** Carotid Doppler
    **Ans:** B — Treatment should NOT wait for biopsy.

## SBA Questions (10)

1. **Scenario:** An 80-year-old woman with new headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness, ESR 95. Sudden loss of vision in left eye. Best immediate action?
   **A:** IV methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg ×3 days, then oral taper; ESR/CRP, then temporal artery biopsy
   **B:** Aspirin **C:** Acetazolamide **D:** Reassurance
   **Ans:** A — Treat GCA aggressively to protect contralateral eye.

2. **Scenario:** A 25-year-old with painful vision loss in right eye, RAPD, ↓colour vision. MRI shows periventricular, juxtacortical, and cord lesions. Diagnosis?
   **A:** NMO **B:** MS (optic neuritis) **C:** Leber's **D:** CRVO
   **Ans:** B — Optic neuritis in MS.

3. **Scenario:** A 35-year-old woman with sudden severe headache, bitemporal hemianopia, ophthalmoplegia, ↓consciousness. MRI shows pituitary haemorrhage. First treatment?
   **A:** Aspirin **B:** IV hydrocortisone 100 mg STAT **C:** IV MP **D:** Antibiotics
   **Ans:** B — Pituitary apoplexy: cortisol replacement first.

4. **Scenario:** A 65-year-old diabetic with sudden painless loss of vision, "blood and thunder" fundus. Diagnosis?
   **A:** CRAO **B:** CRVO **C:** Detachment **D:** Vitreous haemorrhage
   **Ans:** B — CRVO (dilated veins, haemorrhages).

5. **Scenario:** Patient with flashes of light, floaters, "curtain coming down." Next step?
   **A:** Topical anaesthetic **B:** Urgent ophthalmology referral (retinal detachment suspected) **C:** MRI **D:** LP
   **Ans:** B — Retinal detachment is an ophthalmological emergency.

6. **Scenario:** A 50-year-old woman with painless vision loss in right eye, RAPD, optic disc swelling, ESR 110, CRP 60. Diagnosis?
   **A:** NA-AION **B:** A-AION (arteritic, GCA) **C:** Optic neuritis **D:** CRVO
   **Ans:** B — Arteritic AION (GCA); high ESR/CRP, start IV MP.

7. **Scenario:** Young man with painless sequential bilateral optic neuropathy. mtDNA test positive for G11778A. Diagnosis?
   **A:** MS **B:** LHON (Leber's) **C:** NMO **D:** Syphilis
   **Ans:** B — LHON typically young males with painless sequential visual loss.

8. **Scenario:** A 60-year-old with sudden vision loss in left eye, RAPD, "cherry-red" spot, pale retina. Stroke workup shows 90% carotid stenosis. Best management?
   **A:** Anti-VEGF **B:** Carotid endarterectomy (symptomatic stenosis) **C:** Aspirin only **D:** Reassurance
   **Ans:** B — Symptomatic carotid stenosis → CEA within 2 weeks.

9. **Scenario:** Patient on ethambutol for TB develops progressive painless vision loss with ↓colour vision. Diagnosis?
   **A:** Optic neuritis **B:** Toxic optic neuropathy (ethambutol) **C:** GCA **D:** NMO
   **Ans:** B — Stop ethambutol, refer ophthalmology.

10. **Scenario:** A 70-year-old with sudden painless monocular vision loss. ESR 30 (mildly ↑), CRP 25 (↑), no headache, no jaw claudication. Best next step?
    **A:** Start IV MP and temporal artery biopsy
    **B:** IV MP only if symptoms suggest GCA (high ESR alone insufficient; consider differentials)
    **C:** Aspirin
    **D:** Reassure
    **Ans:** B — Mild ESR/CRP elevation without GCA features; investigate differential (NA-AION, retinal causes).
