> [!important]
> **Hymenoptera stings: Bee (Apidae) = barbed stinger, venom sac → melittin, phospholipase A2, hyaluronidase. Wasp (Vespidae) = smooth stinger, multiple stings, venom = phospholipase A1, antigen 5, mastoparan. Ant (Formicidae): fire ant (*Solenopsis*) = piperidine alkaloids (solenopsin). Anaphylaxis = IgE-mediated, rapid onset. IM adrenaline 0.5mg (1:1000) FIRST-LINE. Biphasic reactions 4–12h. Large local reactions = antihistamines, steroids, not anaphylaxis. Immunotherapy (VIT) for recurrent anaphylaxis.**

## 2. Definitions & Key Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Hymenoptera** | Order: bees, wasps, ants — modified ovipositor = stinger |
| **Anaphylaxis** | Acute IgE-mediated hypersensitivity — rapid onset, multisystem |
| **Large local reaction (LLR)** | >10 cm swelling, peaks 24–48h, resolves 5–10 days — NOT anaphylaxis |
| **Biphasic anaphylaxis** | Recurrence 4–12h (up to 72h) after initial resolution |
| **Venom Immunotherapy (VIT)** | Subcutaneous escalating doses → 95–98% protection for 3–5 years |
| **Melittin** | Bee venom major peptide — membrane lysis, pain, mast cell degranulation |
| **Phospholipase A1/A2** | Major allergens (Api m 1, Ves v 1) — membrane degradation |
| **Antigen 5 (Ag5)** | Wasp/ant major allergen — pathogenesis unclear |
| **Solenopsin** | Fire ant alkaloid venom — pustule formation, necrosis |

## 3. Core Content

### Section 1: Hymenoptera Biology & Venom Composition

#### Bee (*Apis mellifera* — Honeybee)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Stinger** | **Barbed** — remains embedded with venom sac; continues pumping |
| **Stings** | Single (stinger tears away → bee dies) |
| **Venom yield** | ~50–140 µg dry weight |
| **Major allergens** | **Api m 1** (phospholipase A2), **Api m 2** (hyaluronidase), **Api m 3** (melittin), **Api m 10** (icarapin) |
| **Key components** | Melittin (50% dry wt) — membrane lysis, pain; PLA2 — membrane degradation; hyaluronidase — spread; mast cell degranulating peptide |
| **Distribution** | Worldwide (domesticated + feral) |

#### Wasp (*Vespula, Polistes, Dolichovespula* — Yellowjackets, Paper Wasps, Hornets)

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Stinger** | **Smooth** — no barbs; can sting repeatedly |
| **Stings** | Multiple |
| **Venom yield** | ~1–17 µg dry weight (less than bee) |
| **Major allergens** | **Ves v 1** (phospholipase A1), **Ves v 5** (antigen 5), **Ves v 2** (hyaluronidase) |
| **Key components** | Phospholipase A1, antigen 5 (Ag5), mastoparan (mast cell degranulation), kinins, serotonin |
| **Distribution** | Worldwide; *Vespula* (ground-nesting) most aggressive |

#### Ant (Formicidae)

| Species | Venom | Clinical |
|---------|-------|----------|
| **Fire ant (*Solenopsis invicta/richteri*)** | **Piperidine alkaloids (solenopsin)** — cytotoxic, necrotic | Immediate burning → **sterile pustule at 24h** (diagnostic); anaphylaxis risk |
| **Jack jumper ant (*Myrmecia pilosula* — Australia)** | Protein venom (Myr p 1, Myr p 2) — similar to wasp | **Major cause of anaphylaxis in Tasmania/SE Australia** |
| **Bulldog ant (*Myrmecia* spp.)** | Protein venom | Severe anaphylaxis |
| **Harvester ant (*Pogonomyrmex* — USA)** | Protein venom | Painful, local reaction, rare anaphylaxis |

## 4. Clinical Presentation

### Anaphylaxis (IgE-Mediated)

#### Diagnostic Criteria (WAO / Resuscitation Council UK)
**Acute onset (minutes–hours) + at least ONE of:**
1. **Skin/mucosal** — urticaria, angioedema, flushing, itching
2. **Respiratory** — wheeze, stridor, dyspnoea, hypoxia, hoarseness
3. **Circulatory** — hypotension, tachycardia, syncope, shock
4. **Gastrointestinal** — vomiting, abdominal pain, incontinence (supportive)

| Feature | Timing |
|---------|--------|
| **Onset** | Minutes (typically < 30 min) |
| **Peak** | 5–30 min |
| **Biphasic** | 4–12h (up to 72h) after resolution |
| **Late** | Serum sickness-like (days) — rare |

#### Clinical Features by System

| System | Features |
|--------|----------|
| **Skin** | Generalised urticaria, angioedema (lips, tongue, eyelids), flushing, pruritus |
| **Respiratory** | Laryngeal oedema (stridor, hoarseness), bronchospasm (wheeze), dyspnoea, hypoxia |
| **Cardiovascular** | Hypotension (distributive shock), tachycardia (reflex or direct), arrhythmia, syncope |
| **GI** | Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, diarrhoea |
| **Neurological** | Anxiety, sense of doom, confusion, loss of consciousness |

### Large Local Reaction (LLR) — NOT Anaphylaxis

| Feature | LLR | Anaphylaxis |
|---------|-----|-------------|
| **Swelling** | **>10 cm**, crosses joint, peaks 24–48h | May have urticaria/angioedema (transient) |
| **Systemic** | **None** — no respiratory/circulatory compromise | **Yes** — respiratory, CVS, GI |
| **Onset** | Delayed (hours) | Immediate (minutes) |
| **Duration** | 5–10 days | Acute (hours), biphasic possible |
| **Tryptase** | Normal | Elevated (peak 1–2h, baseline 24h) |
| **Management** | Antihistamines, oral steroids, cold compress | **IM adrenaline 0.5mg FIRST** |

### Fire Ant (*Solenopsis*) Envenomation

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Bite + sting** | Mandibles grip → pivot → multiple stings in circle |
| **Venom** | 95% piperidine alkaloids (solenopsin) — cytotoxic |
| **Immediate** | Intense burning ("fire"), wheal-and-flare |
| **Characteristic** | **Sterile pustule at 24h** (cloudy fluid, not pus) |
| **Allergic** | Local reaction common; systemic anaphylaxis in ~1% |
| **Secondary** | Infection if pustule ruptured; scarring |

### Jack Jumper Ant (*Myrmecia pilosula*) — Australia

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Region** | Tasmania, SE Australia (Victoria, NSW, ACT) |
| **Significance** | **Major cause of ant anaphylaxis** — 2–3% of population sensitised |
| **Venom** | Protein (Myr p 1, Myr p 2) — cross-reacts with wasp venom |
| **VIT available** | Yes — jack jumper ant VIT (Tasmania) |
| **Anaphylaxis rate** | ~3% per sting in sensitised |

## 5. Diagnostic Approach

### Bedside / Point-of-Care

| Test | Indication | Interpretation |
|------|------------|----------------|
| **History** | All | Timing, insect ID (bee vs wasp vs ant), previous reactions, atopy |
| **Stinger check** | Bee | Barbed stinger + venom sac in skin → remove by scraping |
| **Tryptase** | Suspected anaphylaxis | **Peak 1–2h**, baseline 24h; elevated = mast cell activation |
| **Serum IgE** | VIT workup | Specific IgE to bee/wasp/ant venom |
| **Skin prick** | VIT workup | Positive at 0.1–1 µg/mL; correlate with clinical history |
| **Basophil activation test (BAT)** | Refractory/ambiguous | Functional assay — emerging |

### Laboratory Investigations

| Test | Indication | Expected Findings |
|------|------------|-------------------|
| **Tryptase** | Anaphylaxis confirmation | > 11.4 µg/L or > 2 + 1.2× baseline |
| **FBC** | Post-anaphylaxis | Eosinophilia (late), leukocytosis |
| **U&E, creatinine** | Severe hypotension | AKI from hypoperfusion |
| **ABG** | Respiratory compromise | Respiratory acidosis, hypoxia |
| **ECG** | Cardiovascular | Sinus tachy, ischaemia (rare) |
| **Mast cell tryptase (baseline)** | Recurrent anaphylaxis | > 20 µg/L suggests mastocytosis |

## 6. Management

### Immediate / Pre-hospital (Anaphylaxis)

| Step | Action | Dose/Detail |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **1. Recognise** | Acute + skin/mucosal + (respiratory OR circulatory) | Call for help |
| **2. IM Adrenaline** | **FIRST-LINE, IMMEDIATELY** | **0.5 mg (1:1000) IM anterolateral thigh** (vastus lateralis) |
| **3. Position** | Supine ± legs raised (if hypotensive) | Left lateral if pregnant |
| **4. Airway/O₂** | High-flow O₂ via non-rebreather | Intubate early if stridor/hoarseness |
| **5. IV Access** | 2 large-bore cannulae | 500–1000 mL crystalloid bolus (NS/Hartmann's) |
| **6. Repeat Adrenaline** | If no improvement | IM 0.5 mg q5–15 min PRN |
| **7. Adjuncts** | After adrenaline + fluids | Chlorphenamine 10 mg IV, Hydrocortisone 200 mg IV |
| **8. Nebulised Salbutamol** | Bronchospasm | 5 mg neb PRN |
| **9. Refractory Shock** | Adrenaline infusion | 1 mg in 100 mL NS at 1–10 mL/min (titrate) |
| **10. Glucagon** | β-blocker patients | 1–5 mg IV bolus, then 1–5 mg/h infusion |

### Bee Stinger Removal

| Correct | Incorrect |
|---------|-----------|
| **Scrape** (credit card, fingernail, knife edge) | Pull/squeeze with forceps/tweezers |
| Remove intact venoms sac | Squeezes venom sac → more venom injected |
| Lateral scraping motion | Digging, pinching |

### Post-Acute / Observation

| Parameter | Duration |
|-----------|----------|
| **Observation** | **6–12 hours** minimum (biphasic risk) — longer if severe/late presentation |
| **Discharge criteria** | Symptom-free ≥ 4h off treatment, oral fluids tolerated, written action plan, EpiPen if indicated |
| **Prednisolone** | 30–50 mg PO daily × 3 days (may reduce biphasic, controversial) |
| **Antihistamines** | Non-sedating (cetirizine/loratadine) × 3–5 days |
| **Follow-up** | Allergy referral for VIT assessment |

### Large Local Reaction (LLR) Management

| Intervention | Dose |
|--------------|------|
| **Oral antihistamine** | Cetirizine 10 mg daily / loratadine |
| **Oral corticosteroid** | Prednisolone 30–50 mg daily × 3–5 days |
| **Cold compress** | 15 min q4–6h |
| **Elevation** | Affected limb |
| **Antibiotics** | **NOT** indicated (sterile inflammation) |

## 7. Venom Immunotherapy (VIT)

### Indications (Must Meet **BOTH**)

| Criterion | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **1. Clinical** | **Recurrent systemic anaphylaxis** to Hymenoptera sting (≥2 episodes) OR **1 episode + high risk** (high exposure, mastocytosis, high baseline tryptase) |
| **2. Immunological** | **Positive skin test** AND/OR **specific IgE** to relevant venom |
| **NOT indicated for:** | Large local reactions only, single mild systemic reaction (cutaneous only), positive test without clinical history |

### Protocol (Standard)

| Phase | Duration | Dose Escalation |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| **Build-up** | 12–16 weeks (conventional) / days–weeks (rush/ultra-rush) | Weekly/biweekly → maintenance dose |
| **Maintenance** | **3–5 years** | 100 µg venom (∼1–2 stings equivalent) every 4–8 weeks |
| **Discontinuation** | After 3–5 years if low risk | Shared decision — some continue >5y |

### Efficacy & Safety

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Protection rate** | **95–98%** against systemic reaction |
| **Duration** | **3–5 years** after stop — waning immunity |
| **Systemic reaction rate** | 5–15% of injections (mostly mild) |
| **Fatalities** | Extremely rare (< 1: million injections) |
| **Contraindications** | Uncontrolled asthma, active autoimmune, β-blockers (relative), pregnancy (continue if already on; don't start) |

### Adjunct: Omalizumab (Anti-IgE)

| Role | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Indication** | Recurrent systemic reactions during VIT build-up; high baseline tryptase; mastocytosis |
| **Dose** | 150–300 mg SC q2–4wks per IgE/weight |
| **Effect** | Reduces systemic reactions during build-up, allows faster up-titration |
| **Not** | Replacement for VIT |

## 8. Special Populations

### Mastocytosis

| Feature | Management |
|---------|------------|
| **Risk** | **Increased severity** of anaphylaxis; may lack skin signs |
| **Screening** | Baseline tryptase > 20 µg/L → bone marrow biopsy |
| **VIT** | **Indicated** for any anaphylaxis; higher maintenance dose (200 µg); longer duration |
| **Omalizumab** | Strongly recommended adjunct |
| **EpiPen** | Carry **2 autoinjectors** |

### Children

| Feature | Management |
|---------|------------|
| **LLR** | Common; NOT anaphylaxis; antihistamines + steroids |
| **Anaphylaxis** | Same IM adrenaline dose by weight (0.01 mg/kg max 0.5 mg) |
| **VIT** | Indicated if recurrent anaphylaxis; paediatric allergist |

### Pregnancy

| Feature | Management |
|---------|------------|
| **Anaphylaxis** | IM adrenaline 0.5 mg — **safe**, maternal benefit > fetal risk |
| **VIT** | **Continue** if already on maintenance; **do not initiate** build-up |
| **Omalizumab** | Category B — continue if needed |

## 9. Complications

| Complication | Recognition | Management |
|--------------|-------------|------------|
| **Biphasic anaphylaxis** | Recurrence 4–12h (up to 72h) | Prolonged observation (12–24h if severe); prednisolone × 3d |
| **Refractory shock** | β-blocker, ACEi, severe | Adrenaline infusion, glucagon, vasopressin, IV fluids |
| **Cardiac arrest** | Severe anaphylaxis, Kounis syndrome (allergic MI) | ALS + adrenaline 1 mg IV q3–5min; coronary angiogram if STEMI |
| **Airway obstruction** | Laryngeal oedema | Early intubation; cricothyroidotomy if failed |
| **Kounis syndrome** | Allergic ACS — STEMI/NSTEMI | Troponin, ECG; manage as ACS + anaphylaxis |
| **Mastocytosis unmasked** | Severe anaphylaxis, high tryptase | Screen tryptase; bone marrow if > 20 µg/L |

## 10. Disposition Criteria

| Admit (ICU/HDU) | Observe (ED/Ward 6–12h) | Discharge |
|------------------|--------------------------|-----------|
| **Severe anaphylaxis** — refractory shock, intubation, biphasic | **Moderate anaphylaxis** — responded to 1–2 IM adrenaline | **Mild/LLR** — antihistamine ± steroids, action plan |
| **β-blocker/ACEi** — refractory risk | **First anaphylaxis** — need allergy referral | **EpiPen prescribed** + allergy follow-up |
| **Mastocytosis** | **Paediatric** — lower threshold | **Written action plan** + allergy referral |
| **Comorbidities** (cardiac, respiratory) | **High exposure** (beekeeper) | **VIT assessment** referral |

## 11. High-Yield FCPS/MRCP Points

> [!important]
> - **Anaphylaxis = IM adrenaline 0.5mg (1:1000) anterolateral thigh FIRST-LINE** — not antihistamine, not steroid, not IV adrenaline
> - **Bee stinger = SCRAPE** — barbed stinger leaves venom sac; squeezing injects more venom
> - **Large local reaction (LLR) ≠ anaphylaxis** — >10cm swelling at 24–48h, **NO respiratory/circulatory compromise**
> - **VIT indication = recurrent anaphylaxis** (not LLR) + positive skin test/IgE; 95–98% protection, 3–5 years
> - **Biphasic anaphylaxis = 4–12h** after resolution; observe ≥6–12h; prednisolone × 3d may help
> - **Fire ant** = multiple stings + **sterile pustule at 24h** (piperidine alkaloids)
> - **Jack jumper ant (Australia)** = major anaphylaxis cause; protein venom; VIT available
> - **Mastocytosis** = screen tryptase if severe/unexplained anaphylaxis; VIT + omalizumab
> - **Tryptase timing** = peak 1–2h post-reaction, baseline at 24h
> - **β-blocker + anaphylaxis** = refractory; **glucagon** 1–5 mg IV

## 12. Common Confusions / Exam Traps

| Trap | Correction |
|------|------------|
| **Antihistamine = first-line for anaphylaxis** | **NO** — IM adrenaline is FIRST-LINE; antihistamine adjunct only |
| **Steroid = first-line for anaphylaxis** | **NO** — delayed (4–6h); adjunct only |
| **IV adrenaline better than IM** | **NO** — IM safer, faster absorption; IV only for arrest/refractory shock |
| **LLR = mild anaphylaxis** | **NO** — LLR is local only; NO systemic features |
| **Bee stinger = pull out with tweezers** | **NO** — SCRAPE; squeezing injects venom sac |
| **VIT for LLR** | **NO** — only for recurrent anaphylaxis |
| **Single anaphylaxis = needs VIT** | **Usually NO** — need recurrent OR high-risk features |
| **Fire ant = single sting** | **NO** — pivots, multiple stings in circle |
| **All ants = protein venom** | **NO** — fire ant = alkaloid (solenopsin); jack jumper = protein |
| **Tryptase normal = not anaphylaxis** | **NO** — can be normal; clinical diagnosis |

## 24. Viva Questions (10)

**Q1: What is the first-line treatment for anaphylaxis and why is IM preferred over IV?**
A: **IM adrenaline 0.5mg (1:1000) anterolateral thigh.** IM is preferred because: safer (lower risk of arrhythmia, hypertensive crisis), easier administration (no IV access needed), reliable absorption (vastus lateralis), and easier to titrate than IV bolus. IV adrenaline reserved for cardiac arrest or refractory shock with cardiac monitoring.

**Q2: How do you differentiate a large local reaction (LLR) from anaphylaxis?**
A: LLR = >10 cm swelling peaking at 24–48h, **NO respiratory or circulatory compromise**, tryptase normal. Anaphylaxis = acute onset + skin/mucosal features + (respiratory OR circulatory compromise), tryptase elevated (peak 1–2h). LLR managed with antihistamines + oral steroids; anaphylaxis requires IM adrenaline.

**Q3: What is the correct way to remove a honeybee stinger and why?**
A: **Scrape off** (credit card, fingernail, knife edge) — **DO NOT squeeze or pull with forceps**. The barbed stinger remains attached to the venom sac; squeezing injects the remaining venom. Scraping removes the stinger intact with the sac.

**Q4: What are the indications for venom immunotherapy (VIT)?**
A: **Recurrent systemic anaphylaxis** (≥2 episodes) OR 1 episode with high-risk features (high exposure, mastocytosis, elevated baseline tryptase) **PLUS** positive skin test and/or specific IgE to relevant venom. **NOT for LLR, not for single mild cutaneous reaction.**

**Q5: What is the VIT protocol and efficacy?**
A: Build-up 12–16 weeks (conventional) to maintenance dose 100 µg venom (∼1–2 sting equivalents), then maintenance every 4–8 weeks for **3–5 years**. Efficacy **95–98%** protection against systemic reactions. Waning immunity after stopping.

**Q6: What is biphasic anaphylaxis and how is it managed?**
A: Recurrence of anaphylaxis symptoms **4–12h (up to 72h)** after initial resolution. Occurs in 1–20% of cases. Management: observe minimum **6–12h** in ED (longer if severe), consider prednisolone 30–50 mg × 3 days (controversial), written action plan, EpiPen on discharge.

**Q7: Describe fire ant envenomation and its management.**
A: **Solenopsis** — pivots on mandibles, **multiple stings in circular pattern**. Venom = **piperidine alkaloids (solenopsin)**. Clinical: immediate burning, wheal-and-flare → **sterile pustule at 24h** (diagnostic). Management: antihistamines, topical steroids, keep pustules intact (do not incise), monitor for anaphylaxis. Secondary infection if ruptured.

**Q8: What is the significance of jack jumper ant (*Myrmecia pilosula*) in Australia?**
A: **Major cause of ant anaphylaxis in Tasmania/SE Australia** — 2–3% of population sensitised. Protein venom (Myr p 1, Myr p 2) cross-reacts with wasp venom. **VIT available** (Tasmania). Anaphylaxis rate ~3% per sting in sensitised.

**Q9: How does mastocytosis affect anaphylaxis risk and management?**
A: Mastocytosis = clonal mast cell disorder → **increased risk of severe anaphylaxis**, may present without skin signs. Screen with **baseline tryptase > 20 µg/L** → bone marrow biopsy. VIT indicated for any anaphylaxis (higher maintenance dose 200 µg, longer duration). **Omalizumab strongly recommended** as adjunct. Carry 2 EpiPens.

**Q10: Why is glucagon used for anaphylaxis in patients on β-blockers?**
A: β-blockers blunt the response to adrenaline (β2-mediated bronchodilation, vasodilation, chronotropy). **Glucagon** bypasses β-receptors → increases cAMP directly → positive inotropy, chronotropy, bronchodilation. Dose: 1–5 mg IV bolus, then 1–5 mg/h infusion. Also consider atropine for bradycardia, vasopressin for refractory shock.
