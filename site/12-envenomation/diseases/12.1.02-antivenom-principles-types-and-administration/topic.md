> [!important]
> **Antivenom = specific antidote for envenomation. Antibody fragments bind venom → neutralisation, enhanced clearance. Types: Whole IgG, F(ab')₂ (pepsin), Fab (papain). Monospecific vs polyspecific (polyvalent). Dose by venom load (vials), not patient weight. IV infusion in saline. Early administration is key. Skin testing NOT recommended. Premedication not routine.**

## 2. Definition & Background

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Definition** | Antivenom = immunoglobulin preparation (whole IgG or fragments) from hyperimmunised animals, capable of neutralising specific venom toxins |
| **Source animals** | Horses (equine) most common; sheep (ovine), rabbits, camelids (camelid nanobodies — newer) |
| **Production** | Animal hyperimmunised with sublethal venom doses → plasma harvested → IgG purified → fragmented if required |
| **First developed** | 1895–96 (Calmette, Phisalix) — anti-cobra serum |
| **Global supply** | Polyvalent products from Australia (CSL), India (VINS, Bharat Serums), South Africa (SAIMR), Mexico (Birmex), Brazil (Butantan) |

---

## 3. Mechanism of Action

| Step | Detail |
|---|---|
| **1. Binding** | Antibody variable region binds venom epitope (toxin, enzyme) |
| **2. Neutralisation** | Prevents venom binding to cellular/tissue targets (receptors, basement membrane, clotting factors) |
| **3. Clearance** | Fc receptor–mediated phagocytosis by RES (for IgG/F(ab')₂); renal clearance for Fab |
| **4. Redistribution** | Antibody-venom complexes dissociate slowly, releasing active venom (theoretical concern) |
| **5. Aggregate formation** | Large immune complexes cleared by spleen/liver |

**Limitation:** Antivenom **cannot reverse** established tissue damage (necrosis, established paralysis) — only neutralises circulating venom. Therefore **early** administration is critical.

---

## 4. Types of Antivenom

### By Molecular Composition

| Type | Production | Molecular Weight | Half-life | Reactions | Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Whole IgG** | Intact IgG from animal plasma | 150 kDa | Long (3–7 d) | More common (Fc-mediated complement activation) | Older products, some Indian/EQUIP antivenoms |
| **F(ab')₂** | Pepsin digestion of IgG (removes Fc) | 100 kDa | Intermediate (1–3 d) | Fewer reactions | **Most common** (CSL Australia, SAIMR, BIOCLON) |
| **Fab** | Papain digestion of IgG (removes Fc + 1 Fab) | 50 kDa | Short (4–12 h) | Fewer reactions but may need repeat dosing | Digitalis antivenom, *Crotalus* Fab (CroFab — US) |

### By Specificity

| Type | Definition | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Monospecific (monovalent)** | Antibodies against single species | Definite ID, common in Australia (Taipan AV, Brown AV) |
| **Polyvalent (polyspecific)** | Antibodies against multiple species (often 4–6) | Unidentified snake, multiple species in region (India ASV, SAIMR) |

### By Region / Product

| Region | Common Product | Specificity | Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Australia** | CSL Polyvalent (Taipan, Brown, Tiger, Black, Death Adder) | Polyvalent | F(ab')₂ (sheep) |
| **India** | Bharat Serums / VINS Polyvalent (Indian Big Four: Cobra, Krait, Russell's Viper, Saw-scaled Viper) | Polyvalent | Whole IgG (equine) |
| **South Africa** | SAIMR Polyvalent (Banded Cobra, Snouted Cobra, Cape Cobra, Black Mamba, Puff Adder, Gaboon Viper, Rinkhals) | Polyvalent | F(ab')₂ (equine) |
| **USA** | CroFab (Crotalidae Polyvalent Immune Fab — North American pit vipers) | Polyvalent | Fab (ovine) |
| **Mexico/Central America** | Antivipmyn / Coralmyn | Polyvalent | F(ab')₂ (equine) |
| **Brazil** | Butantan / BIOCLON (Bothrops, Crotalus, Lachesis, Micrurus) | Polyvalent (Bothrops, Crotalus) | F(ab')₂ (equine) |

## 5. Pharmacokinetics

| Parameter | Whole IgG | F(ab')₂ | Fab |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MW (kDa)** | 150 | 100 | 50 |
| **Vd** | Plasma + interstitial | Plasma + interstitial (smaller) | Plasma only (small) |
| **t½α (distribution)** | 1–4 h | 0.5–2 h | 0.5 h |
| **t½β (elimination)** | 60–180 h | 30–90 h | 12–20 h |
| **Clearance** | RES (FcRn recycling) | Renal + RES | Renal (filtered) |
| **Tissue penetration** | Limited (large) | Better | Best (small) |
| **Redistribution** | Slow | Faster | Fastest |

## 6. Indications for Antivenom

### Absolute Indications (AV Required)

| Indication | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Systemic neurotoxicity** | Ptosis, ophthalmoplegia, bulbar palsy, respiratory failure (elapid) |
| **Coagulopathy (VICC)** | INR > 1.5, fibrinogen < 1 g/L, platelets < 50, 20WBCT positive (viperid) |
| **Severe systemic signs** | Hypotension, shock, arrhythmia, pulmonary oedema |
| **Severe local progression** | Swelling > half limb in 24 h, necrosis, compartment syndrome |
| **Rhabdomyolysis** | Sea snake, myotoxic viperids — CK > 5000, myoglobinuria, AKI |
| **Acute kidney injury** | Creatinine rise, oliguria |
| **Cardiovascular collapse** | Scorpion, marine, anaphylaxis |

### Relative Indications (Consider AV)

| Indication | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mild systemic** | Nausea, vomiting, mild pain, no coagulopathy |
| **Local swelling progressive** | < half limb, no systemic |
| **Possible envenomation** | Unidentified snake, high suspicion |

### NOT Indications (Observe Only)

| Scenario | Action |
|---|---|
| **Dry bite** | Fang marks, no local/systemic signs, normal 20WBCT — observe 12–24 h |
| **Local pain/swelling only** | Mild, not progressive — analgesia, observe |
| **Non-venomous bite** | Identify — no AV needed |

## 7. Dosing Principles

| Principle | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Dose by venom load** | Based on number of vials to neutralise AVERAGE venom yield from one bite |
| **NOT by patient weight** | Same dose for adult and child (children have worse envenomation per kg but same venom load) |
| **Initial dose** | Usually 1–2 vials (some products 4–10) to neutralise average envenomation |
| **Repeat dose** | If no clinical improvement or coagulopathy not corrected at 6 h |
| **Maximum dose** | No strict maximum; titrate to clinical response |
| **Adjunctive therapy** | Supportive care, not a substitute |

### Examples of Initial Dosing

| Product | Initial Dose | Repeat Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| **CSL Australian Polyvalent** | 1–2 vials (3 vials for severe) | Repeat if symptoms persist at 1–2 h |
| **Indian Polyvalent ASV** | 8–10 vials (severe), 5 vials (moderate) | Repeat if 20WBCT +ve at 6 h |
| **SAIMR Polyvalent** | 10 vials (severe), 5 vials (moderate) | Repeat if bleeding/coagulopathy persists |
| **CroFab** | 4–6 vials (initial), 2 vials (repeat) | Repeat at 6, 12, 18 h if needed |
| **Antivipmyn** | 2–5 vials (initial) | Repeat based on clinical response |

## 8. Administration Protocol

### Pre-Infusion

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1. Confirm indication** | WHO grade / SSS / clinical signs |
| **2. Obtain consent** | Explain risks (anaphylaxis 5–20%, serum sickness) |
| **3. IV access** | Two large-bore (≥18G) |
| **4. Resuscitation equipment** | Adrenaline, antihistamine, hydrocortisone, oxygen, suction, ambu-bag |
| **5. Baseline labs** | Coagulation, FBC, U&E, CK, urinalysis, ECG, 20WBCT |
| **6. Skin test** | **NOT recommended** (poor predictive value, can sensitise, delay treatment) |
| **7. Premedication** | **Not routine**; consider SC adrenaline 0.25 mg in high-risk (atopic) patients |
| **8. Fluid pre-load** | 500 mL crystalloid in adults; reduces hypotension |

### Infusion

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1. Dilution** | Reconstitute in 10–50 mL saline; further dilute in 100–250 mL saline |
| **2. Initial rate** | Start SLOW — 1 mL/min for 15 min (observe for reaction) |
| **3. If tolerated** | Increase to full infusion over 1 h total |
| **4. Total time** | 30–60 min for most products; some 1–2 h |
| **5. Observation** | Continuous during infusion; q15min vitals |
| **6. IM alternative** | Only if no IV access — slower absorption, lower peak, more tissue reaction |

### Post-Infusion

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1. Monitor** | q15min × 1 h, q30min × 4 h, q1h × 24 h |
| **2. Repeat labs** | 20WBCT at 1 h, 6 h, 12 h, 24 h; coagulation 6-hourly |
| **3. Clinical response** | Coagulopathy reversal, neuro improvement, vital stability |
| **4. Repeat dose** | If no improvement at 1–6 h |
| **5. Watch for late reaction** | Serum sickness at 5–14 d (prednisolone if severe) |
| **6. Documentation** | Product, batch, dose, time, reactions |

## 9. Special Populations

| Population | Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Pregnancy** | AV indicated if envenomation; benefits outweigh risk; use F(ab')₂ if available |
| **Children** | Same dose as adult (by venom load); careful fluid balance; close monitoring |
| **Elderly** | Higher anaphylaxis risk; comorbidities; start slower |
| **Atopic / multiple allergy** | Higher reaction risk; consider SC adrenaline prophylaxis |
| **β-blocker use** | Blunts anaphylaxis response; consider glucagon |
| **ACE inhibitor use** | Higher angioedema risk with reaction |
| **Renal impairment** | Fab preparations cleared renally — may accumulate |

## 12. Viva Questions (10)

**Q1: What is the mechanism of action of antivenom?**
A: Antibody (IgG or fragment) binds venom epitope → neutralises circulating venom by preventing target binding → antibody-venom complex cleared by RES (Fc receptor mediated) for IgG/F(ab')₂, or by renal filtration for Fab.

**Q2: Compare whole IgG, F(ab')₂, and Fab antivenoms.**
A: Whole IgG = intact, 150 kDa, long t½ (60–180 h), more reactions (Fc-mediated complement); F(ab')₂ = pepsin-digested, 100 kDa, intermediate t½ (30–90 h), fewer reactions; Fab = papain-digested, 50 kDa, short t½ (12–20 h), fewest reactions but may need repeat dosing.

**Q3: What is the difference between monospecific and polyspecific antivenom?**
A: Monospecific = antibodies against a single species (used when snake is identified, e.g., Australian taipan AV). Polyspecific/polyvalent = antibodies against multiple species (4–6), used when snake is unidentified or multiple species in region (Indian ASV, SAIMR).

**Q4: How is the dose of antivenom determined?**
A: By VENOM LOAD (number of vials to neutralise the average venom yield from one bite), not by patient weight. Adults and children receive the same dose because venom load is the same; children are more vulnerable per kg.

**Q5: What is the preferred route and rate of antivenom administration?**
A: IV infusion in saline, starting slowly (1 mL/min for 15 min), then increased to complete in 30–60 min. IM is a last resort if no IV access (slower absorption, lower peak).

**Q6: Why is skin testing no longer recommended before antivenom?**
A: Poor predictive value for anaphylaxis (both false + and −); can sensitise the patient; delays life-saving treatment. Direct infusion with resuscitation readiness is safer.

**Q7: When should antivenom be repeated?**
A: If there is no clinical improvement at 1–6 h, or if coagulopathy (INR, fibrinogen, 20WBCT) is not corrected at 6 h post-infusion. Repeat with the same or higher dose.

**Q8: What are the limits of antivenom?**
A: Cannot reverse established tissue damage, necrosis, or established paralysis. Only neutralises circulating (free) venom. Cannot prevent ongoing venom release from depot at bite site. Antivenom does not cross BBB well — neurotoxins already in CSF may continue to act.

**Q9: Should antivenom be given in pregnancy?**
A: Yes, if indicated for envenomation. Maternal benefit outweighs fetal risk. F(ab')₂ preferred (less Fc-mediated placental transfer). Early AV reduces risk of fetal demise from maternal shock or coagulopathy.

**Q10: What is the role of premedication before antivenom?**
A: Not routine. Adrenaline (0.25 mg SC) may be considered in high-risk (atopic, previous AV) patients. Antihistamines and steroids are NOT effective at preventing anaphylaxis. Resuscitation readiness is more important.

## 13. Confusions & Mnemonics

| Confusion | Clarification |
|---|---|
| Antivenom dose by weight | NO — by venom load (vials) |
| Skin test mandatory | NO — not recommended; poor PPV, delays |
| Antivenom reverses necrosis | NO — only neutralises circulating venom |
| One dose cures | NO — may need repeat at 6 h if coagulopathy persists |
| AV = vaccine | NO — passive immunisation, short-term only |
| Premedication always needed | NO — resuscitation readiness > routine premed |
| All snakebites need AV | NO — only systemic or severe local |
| IM AV as effective as IV | NO — IV is faster, higher peak, preferred |
| Children need less AV | NO — same as adult (by venom load) |
| AV prevents all reactions | NO — early (5–20%) and late (5–14 d serum sickness) reactions still occur |

**Mnemonics:**
- **AV types**: **I**ntact (IgG) = **I**mmunogenic; **F(ab')₂** = **F**ragment; **Fab** = **F**astest clearance
- **Dose by**: **V**enom, **N**ot **W**eight = **VNVW**
- **Route**: **I**V **N**ot **I**M (preferred) = **IVNIM**
- **Indications**: **N**euro, **C**oag, **C**VS, **R**enal, **S**evere local = **NCCRS**
- **Skin test**: **N**ever **N**ow = **NN**
- **Premed**: **N**ot **R**outine = **NR**
- **Repeat dose**: If **N**o improvement at **6 h** = **N6**
