# Intra-abdominal Infections (Secondary Peritonitis, Intra-abdominal Abscess)

Related: [[Sepsis and Septic Shock]], [[Clostridioides difficile Infection]], [[Acute Pancreatitis]], [[Liver Abscess]], [[Antimicrobial Stewardship]]

> [!important]
> **Intra-abdominal infection (IAI) = peritoneal inflammation ± abscess from GI tract microbes.** **Primary = spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP); Secondary = GI perforation/visceral rupture; Tertiary = recurrent/persistent after adequate source control.** **Source control = DEFINITIVE treatment (surgery/drainage); antibiotics = ADJUNCT.** **Empirical: cover Gram-neg + anaerobes (Enterococcus situationally).** **Duration: 4 days post-source-control if adequate (STOP-IT trial).** **MDR risk: healthcare-associated, prior abx, immunosuppression.**

## Learning Objectives
- Classify IAI: primary (SBP), secondary (perforation), tertiary (recurrent)
- Diagnose using clinical, laboratory, imaging criteria
- Apply source control principles (surgery, percutaneous drainage)
- Select empirical antibiotics by acquisition (community vs healthcare) and MDR risk
- Determine treatment duration (4 days post-source-control)
- Manage special scenarios: SBP, tertiary peritonitis, pancreatic necrosis infection, liver abscess
- Recognise when antifungal coverage needed

## Classification
| Type | Definition | Typical Pathogens |
|------|------------|-------------------|
| **Primary (SBP)** | Ascites fluid infection **without** surgically treatable source (cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome) | *E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, *Streptococcus* spp., *Enterococcus* (usually monomicrobial) |
| **Secondary** | **Perforation/rupture** of GI tract (appendicitis, diverticulitis, peptic ulcer, ischaemic bowel, trauma, anastomotic leak) | **Polymicrobial**: Gram-neg (*E. coli*, *Klebsiella*) + **Anaerobes** (*Bacteroides fragilis*) ± Enterococcus |
| **Tertiary** | **Recurrent/persistent** infection ≥48h after adequate source control + antibiotics | **MDR**: *Pseudomonas*, *Acinetobacter*, *Enterococcus* (VRE), *Candida*, *Enterobacteriaceae* (ESBL/CRE) |

> [!key]
> **Source control (surgery/drainage) = PRIMARY treatment.** **Antibiotics alone FAIL without source control.** **Tertiary peritonitis = failure of source control or host factors.**

## Diagnosis
### Clinical
| Feature | Secondary Peritonitis | SBP | Tertiary Peritonitis |
|---------|----------------------|-----|---------------------|
| **Pain** | Severe, localised → generalised | Mild, diffuse | Persistent/recurrent |
| **Fever** | High, rigors | Low-grade or absent | Persistent/recurrent |
| **Signs** | Rigid abdomen, guarding, absent bowel sounds | Shifting dullness, mild tenderness | Variable, may be masked |
| **Sepsis** | Common | Variable | Common |

### Laboratory
| Test | Findings |
|------|----------|
| **WCC** | Elevated (or low in severe sepsis) |
| **CRP / PCT** | Markedly elevated |
| **Lactate** | ↑ if hypoperfusion/sepsis |
| **Ascitic fluid (SBP)** | **PMN ≥250 cells/mm³** (diagnostic); culture (blood culture bottles at bedside) |
| **Blood cultures** | Positive 30–50% (secondary); 20–30% (SBP) |

### Imaging
| Modality | Role |
|----------|------|
| **CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast** | **Gold standard** — free air, fluid collections, abscess, thickened bowel, source identification |
| **Ultrasound** | Bedside, ascites, gallbladder, pelvic collections |
| **X-ray (erect chest/abdomen)** | Free air (perforation) — quick but less sensitive |

## Source Control — The DEFINITIVE Treatment
| Source | Intervention |
|--------|--------------|
| **Perforated peptic ulcer** | Laparoscopic/primary repair + omental patch |
| **Appendicitis** | Appendicectomy (+/- drainage if abscess) |
| **Diverticulitis (perforated)** | Resection (Hartmann's) or laparoscopic lavage (selected) |
| **Anastomotic leak** | Re-laparotomy, diversion, drainage |
| **Ischaemic bowel** | Resection of necrotic segment |
| **Intra-abdominal abscess** | **Percutaneous drainage (CT/US-guided)** ± surgery |
| **Infected pancreatic necrosis** | **Step-up approach**: percutaneous drainage → endoscopic necrosectomy → minimally invasive surgery |

> [!critical]
> **Source control within 6–12h of diagnosis.** **Antibiotics WITHOUT source control = treatment failure.** **Damage control surgery for unstable: quick source control, temporary abdomen closure, ICU resuscitation, planned re-laparotomy.**

## Empirical Antibiotic Therapy
### Community-Acquired Secondary Peritonitis (Low MDR Risk)
| Regimen | Doses | Spectrum |
|---------|-------|----------|
| **Piperacillin-tazobactam** | 4.5g IV 6h | **Gram-neg + Anaerobes + Enterococcus (some)** — **1st line** |
| **Ceftriaxone 2g IV 12h + Metronidazole 500mg IV 8h** | Alternative | Gram-neg + Anaerobes (NO Enterococcus) |
| **Meropenem 1g IV 8h** | Alternative (if β-lactam allergy not anaphylaxis) | Broad Gram-neg + Anaerobes |
| **Cefepime 2g IV 8h + Metronidazole 500mg IV 8h** | Alternative | Gram-neg (Pseudomonas) + Anaerobes |
| **Ertapenem 1g IV 24h** | Alternative (if NO Pseudomonas risk) | Gram-neg + Anaerobes |

### Healthcare-Associated / High MDR Risk (Prior abx, hospitalisation, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis)
| Regimen | Doses | Spectrum |
|---------|-------|----------|
| **Meropenem 1g IV 8h** | **Preferred** | **Gram-neg (Pseudomonas, ESBL) + Anaerobes** |
| **Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV 6h** | Alternative (if local Pseudomonas susceptibility) | Gram-neg + Anaerobes + Enterococcus |
| **Cefepime 2g IV 8h + Metronidazole 500mg IV 8h** | Alternative | Pseudomonas + Anaerobes |
| **+ Vancomycin 15–20mg/kg IV 6h** | If **Enterococcus** risk (prosthetic material, prior Enterococcus, severe sepsis) | VRE: Linezolid/Daptomycin |
| **+ Fluconazole 400mg IV/PO OD** | If **Candida** risk (recent abx, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric leak) | Candida: Echinocandin if fluconazole-R or unstable |

> [!tip]
> **Enterococcus coverage: NOT routine for community secondary peritonitis.** **Add if: prosthetic material, immunosuppression, severe sepsis, valvular disease, prior Enterococcus, tertiary peritonitis.** **Candida coverage: add if risk factors (recent broad abx, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric/oesophageal leak, prolonged ICU).**

### SBP (Primary Peritonitis)
| Regimen | Dose | Duration |
|---------|------|----------|
| **Cefotaxime 2g IV 8h** (or Ceftriaxone 2g IV 24h) | **1st line** | **5 days** (extend if slow response) |
| **Alternative** | Piperacillin-tazobactam, Meropenem | |
| **Albumin infusion** | **1.5g/kg day 1 + 1g/kg day 3** | Prevents renal dysfunction (↓ mortality) |

### Intra-abdominal Abscess (Drained)
| Scenario | Duration |
|----------|----------|
| **Adequate source control (drainage + antibiotics)** | **4 days post-source-control** (STOP-IT trial) |
| **Inadequate source control / ongoing sepsis** | Extend until resolution |

## Treatment Duration — STOP-IT Trial Evidence
| Scenario | Duration |
|----------|----------|
| **Adequate source control achieved** | **4 days FIXED** after source control (non-inferior to 8–10 days) |
| **Source control NOT achievable / inadequate** | Continue until clinical resolution + source control achieved |
| **SBP** | 5 days (extend if slow response) |
| **Infected pancreatic necrosis** | Individualised (usually 2–4 weeks after drainage) |

> [!key]
> **STOP-IT trial (2015): 4 days post-source-control = non-inferior to longer courses.** **Adequate source control = all purulent material removed, no ongoing leak, drains output <200mL/day enteric content.**

## Special Scenarios
### Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)
- **Diagnosis:** Ascitic fluid PMN ≥250/mm³ (send in blood culture bottles at bedside)
- **Empirical:** Cefotaxime 2g IV 8h ×5d (or Ceftriaxone 2g IV 24h)
- **Albumin:** 1.5g/kg day 1 + 1g/kg day 3 (prevents HRS, ↓ mortality)
- **Prophylaxis:** **Secondary prophylaxis** after 1st episode (Norfloxacin 400mg PO daily or TMP-SMX) — **until transplant or resolution of ascites**
- **Primary prophylaxis:** In cirrhosis with **ascitic protein <1.5g/dL + Child-Pugh ≥9 or variceal bleed** (Norfloxacin 400mg daily)

### Tertiary Peritonitis
- **Definition:** Recurrent/persistent peritonitis ≥48h after adequate source control + antibiotics
- **Pathogens:** MDR Gram-neg (*Pseudomonas*, *Acinetobacter*, ESBL/CRE), *Enterococcus* (VRE), *Candida*
- **Management:** Source control re-assessment (CT, re-laparotomy), broad antibiotics (Meropenem ± Vancomycin ± Fluconazole/Echinocandin), immunomodulation, nutrition

### Infected Pancreatic Necrosis
- **Step-up approach (PANTER trial):** 
  1. **Percutaneous drainage** (first)
  2. **Endoscopic transluminal necrosectomy** (if drainage fails)
  3. **Minimally invasive surgery (VARD)** → **Open necrosectomy** (last)
- **Antibiotics:** Meropenem (penetrates necrosis) ± anti-MRSA ± antifungal
- **Timing:** Delay intervention ≥4 weeks if possible (walled-off necrosis)

### Pyogenic Liver Abscess
| Aetiology | Pathogens | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Biliary** (most common) | *E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, *Enterococcus*, *Bacteroides* | **Percutaneous drainage + antibiotics** |
| **Portal venous** (diverticulitis, appendicitis) | Polymicrobial, anaerobes prominent | Drainage + antibiotics |
| **Haematogenous** | *S. aureus*, *Streptococcus*, *Candida* | Antibiotics ± drainage |
| **Cryptogenic** | *Klebsiella* (K1/K2 hypervirulent), *E. coli* | Drainage + antibiotics |

**Empirical:** Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g 6h OR Ceftriaxone 2g 24h + Metronidazole 500mg 8h
**Duration:** 2–4 weeks (IV→oral after drainage + clinical improvement)
**Klebsiella liver abscess:** Metastatic infection risk (endophthalmitis, meningitis, brain abscess) — ophthalmology review

## Antifungal Coverage Indications
| Indication | Preferred Agent |
|------------|-----------------|
| **Candida isolated from intra-abdominal culture** | **Echinocandin (Caspofungin/Micafungin/Anidulafungin)** if unstable/fluconazole-R; Fluconazole if stable/susceptible |
| **High-risk clinical scenario (no culture yet):** recent broad abx, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric/oesophageal leak, prolonged ICU | **Empirical echinocandin** (preferred over fluconazole for critically ill) |
| **Candida in pancreatic necrosis** | **Echinocandin** |

> [!tip]
> **Echinocandin preferred for empirical/definitive therapy in critically ill (fungicidal, no drug interactions, broad Candida coverage).** **Fluconazole if stable, susceptible, no recent azole exposure.**

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- **IAI classification: primary (SBP), secondary (perforation), tertiary (recurrent post-source-control)**
- **Source control = DEFINITIVE treatment (surgery/drainage); antibiotics = adjunct**
- **Community secondary peritonitis: Pip-tazo 4.5g 6h (1st line) covers Gram-neg + anaerobes + some Enterococcus**
- **Healthcare-associated/high MDR risk: Meropenem 1g 8h (preferred) ± Vancomycin ± Fluconazole/Echinocandin**
- **Enterococcus coverage: NOT routine; add if prosthetic material, severe sepsis, immunosuppression, prior Enterococcus**
- **Candida coverage: add if recent broad abx, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric leak**
- **Duration: 4 days FIXED post-adequate source control (STOP-IT trial)**
- **SBP: Ascitic PMN ≥250 → Cefotaxime 5d + Albumin 1.5g/kg D1 + 1g/kg D3**
- **SBP prophylaxis: secondary (after 1st episode) norfloxacin/TMP-SMX; primary if ascitic protein <1.5 + Child-Pugh ≥9**
- **Infected pancreatic necrosis: step-up (drainage → endoscopic → surgery); Meropenem (penetrates necrosis)**
- **Liver abscess: drainage + antibiotics 2–4w; Klebsiella → metastatic screening (eye, brain)**

## Common Viva Questions
1. **What is the difference between primary, secondary, and tertiary peritonitis?** Primary = SBP (no surgical source); Secondary = GI perforation; Tertiary = recurrent after adequate source control.
2. **What is the most important treatment for secondary peritonitis?** Source control (surgery/drainage) — antibiotics alone fail.
3. **Empirical antibiotics for community-acquired secondary peritonitis?** Pip-tazo 4.5g 6h (covers Gram-neg, anaerobes, some Enterococcus).
4. **When do you add Enterococcus coverage?** Prosthetic material, severe sepsis, immunosuppression, valvular disease, prior Enterococcus, tertiary peritonitis.
5. **When do you add antifungal coverage?** Recent broad abx, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric/oesophageal leak, prolonged ICU.
6. **What is the recommended duration after adequate source control?** 4 days fixed (STOP-IT trial).
7. **SBP diagnosis?** Ascitic fluid PMN ≥250/mm³.
8. **SBP treatment?** Cefotaxime 2g IV 8h ×5d + Albumin 1.5g/kg D1 + 1g/kg D3.
9. **Infected pancreatic necrosis management?** Step-up: percutaneous drainage → endoscopic necrosectomy → minimally invasive surgery.
10. **Klebsiella liver abscess complication?** Metastatic infection (endophthalmitis, meningitis, brain abscess) — ophthalmology review.

## Common Confusions / Exam Traps
| Confusion | Clarification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Antibiotics alone for secondary peritonitis | **Source control MANDATORY; antibiotics adjunct only** |
| Enterococcus coverage for all secondary peritonitis | **NOT routine; only if risk factors present** |
| 10–14 days antibiotics for all IAI | **4 days post-source-control if adequate (STOP-IT)** |
| SBP = secondary peritonitis | **SBP = primary peritonitis (no surgical source); ascites PMN ≥250** |
| Albumin not needed in SBP | **Albumin 1.5g/kg D1 + 1g/kg D3 prevents HRS, ↓ mortality** |
| Fluconazole for all Candida | **Echinocandin preferred for critically ill/empirical; fluconazole if stable/susceptible** |
| Meropenem doesn't cover anaerobes | **Meropenem covers anaerobes EXCELLENTLY (including B. fragilis)** |
| Tertiary peritonitis = just longer antibiotics | **Tertiary = reassess source control + MDR pathogens + host factors** |
| Pancreatic necrosis = immediate surgery | **Step-up approach: delay ≥4w, drainage first, surgery last** |
| Liver abscess = antibiotics alone | **Drainage + antibiotics (except very small <3cm)** |

## Summary
**Intra-abdominal infections (IAI)** = peritoneal inflammation ± abscess from GI tract microbes. **Classification:** **Primary (SBP)** = ascites infection without surgical source (cirrhosis); **Secondary** = GI perforation/rupture (appendicitis, diverticulitis, perforated ulcer, ischaemic bowel, anastomotic leak, trauma); **Tertiary** = recurrent/persistent infection ≥48h after adequate source control + antibiotics. **Diagnosis:** clinical (pain, fever, peritonism), labs (WCC, CRP, PCT, lactate), ascitic fluid **PMN ≥250/mm³ (SBP diagnostic)**, imaging **CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast (gold standard)**. **Source control = DEFINITIVE treatment** (surgery: perforation, appendicectomy, resection; drainage: percutaneous abscess, step-up pancreatic necrosis); antibiotics = adjunct. **Empirical antibiotics:** **Community secondary peritonitis:** Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV 6h (covers Gram-neg, anaerobes, some Enterococcus); alternatives: Ceftriaxone+Metronidazole, Meropenem, Ertapenem (no Pseudomonas). **Healthcare-associated/high MDR risk/tertiary:** Meropenem 1g IV 8h (preferred) ± Vancomycin (Enterococcus risk) ± Echinocandin (Candida risk). **Enterococcus coverage add:** prosthetic material, severe sepsis, immunosuppression, valvular disease, prior Enterococcus, tertiary peritonitis. **Candida coverage add:** recent broad antibiotics, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric/oesophageal leak, prolonged ICU. **Duration:** **4 days FIXED post-adequate source control** (STOP-IT trial). **SBP:** Cefotaxime 2g IV 8h (or Ceftriaxone 2g IV 24h) ×5 days + **Albumin 1.5g/kg day 1 + 1g/kg day 3** (prevents HRS, ↓ mortality). **Prophylaxis:** secondary after 1st SBP (norfloxacin/TMP-SMX); primary if ascitic protein <1.5g/dL + Child-Pugh ≥9 or variceal bleed. **Infected pancreatic necrosis:** Step-up approach (PANTER) — percutaneous drainage → endoscopic transluminal necrosectomy → minimally invasive surgery; Meropenem (pancreatic penetration). **Pyogenic liver abscess:** Drainage + antibiotics 2–4 weeks; Klebsiella (K1/K2) → metastatic screening (endophthalmitis, meningitis, brain abscess).

## Answer Key with Explanations
### MCQs
1. **B** — SBP = primary peritonitis = ascites infection without surgically treatable source (cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome).
2. **B** — Source control (surgery/drainage) is definitive; antibiotics alone fail without it.
3. **B** — Pip-tazo 4.5g 6h covers Gram-neg + anaerobes + some Enterococcus; 1st line for community secondary peritonitis.
4. **B** — STOP-IT trial: 4 days fixed post-adequate source control non-inferior to longer courses.
5. **B** — SBP diagnostic criterion: ascitic fluid PMN ≥250/mm³.
6. **B** — Albumin 1.5g/kg D1 + 1g/kg D3 prevents HRS and reduces mortality in SBP.
7. **B** — Enterococcus coverage NOT routine; only with risk factors (prosthetic, severe sepsis, immunocompromised, valvular, prior Enterococcus, tertiary).

9. **B** — Hypervirulent Klebsiella (K1/K2) liver abscess: metastatic infection risk (endophthalmitis, meningitis, brain abscess).
