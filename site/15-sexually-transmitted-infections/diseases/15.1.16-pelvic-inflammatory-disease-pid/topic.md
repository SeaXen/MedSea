> [!important]
> **PID = ascending infection** from cervix/vagina → uterus, tubes, ovaries, peritoneum. **Empirical treatment mandatory** — delay causes infertility, ectopic pregnancy, chronic pain. **Low threshold** for treatment: "better to overtreat than undertreat."

## 1. Learning Objectives
- Apply clinical diagnostic criteria (CDC minimal vs enhanced)
- Initiate empirical treatment immediately upon suspicion
- Differentiate PID from mimics (appendicitis, ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion)
- Manage severe PID (hospitalisation, IV antibiotics, surgical complications)
- Implement partner management and follow-up

## 2. Definition and Pathogenesis
- **PID** = inflammation of upper female genital tract (endometrium, fallopian tubes, ovaries, pelvic peritoneum)
- **Ascending infection**: Cervix → Endometrium → Fallopian tubes → Peritoneum
- **Aetiology**: Polymicrobial — sexually transmitted (GC, CT, M. genitalium) + endogenous (anaerobes, G. vaginalis, Enterobacteriaceae, Strep)
- **Risk factors**: Young age, multiple partners, new partner, IUD (first 3 weeks), prior PID, STI history, douching

## 3. Diagnostic Criteria (CDC / BASHH)

### Minimal Criteria (Treat if ANY present + lower genital tract inflammation)
| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Cervical motion tenderness** (chandelier sign) | Pain on moving cervix |
| **Uterine tenderness** | Adnexal/uterine tenderness on bimanual exam |
| **Adnexal tenderness** | Ovarian/tubal tenderness |

> **PLUS**: Evidence of lower genital tract inflammation (cervical mucopus, +ve NAAT GC/CT, raised WBC, ESR/CRP)

### Enhanced Criteria (Increase Specificity)
| Criterion | Threshold |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Fever** | >38.3°C |
| **Abnormal cervical/vaginal discharge** | Mucopus, NAAT +ve |
| **Raised inflammatory markers** | ESR >30, CRP >20, WBC >10 |
| **Imaging** | Transvaginal US: thickened tubes >6mm, fluid in cul-de-sac, tubo-ovarian complex |
| **Laparoscopy** | Purulent material, friable tubes, adhesions (gold standard) |

> **Clinical judgment paramount** — "Low threshold for treatment" as delay = infertility/ectopic

## 4. Differential Diagnosis
| Condition | Key Differentiators |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Acute appendicitis** | RLQ pain, migration from periumbilical, no cervical motion tenderness, no discharge |
| **Ectopic pregnancy** | +ve pregnancy test, vaginal bleeding, adnexal mass, pain lateralised |
| **Ovarian torsion** | Sudden severe unilateral pain, nausea/vomiting, no fever/discharge |
| **Endometriosis** | Chronic cyclic pain, dysmenorrhoea, dyspareunia, no acute fever |
| **Ovarian cyst rupture** | Sudden pain, often mid-cycle, no cervical tenderness |
| **Diverticulitis** | LLQ pain, bowel symptoms, no cervical tenderness |
| **IUD complication** | Actinomycosis, perforation; recent insertion? |

## 5. Empirical Treatment (Immediate — Do Not Delay for Results)

### Outpatient (Mild–Moderate PID)
| Regimen | Dose | Duration |
|---------|------|----------|
| **1st-line (BASHH/CDC)** | **Ceftriaxone 500mg IM single dose** + **Doxycycline 100mg BD PO** + **Metronidazole 400mg BD PO** | 14 days |
| **Alternative (if cephalosporin allergy)** | **Gentamicin 240mg IM single dose** + **Metronidazole 400mg BD PO** + **Doxycycline 100mg BD PO** | 14 days |
| **Alternative (if metronidazole intolerant)** | Ceftriaxone 500mg IM + Doxycycline 100mg BD (no metronidazole if anaerobic cover not needed) | 14 days |

> **Doxycycline 14d** = critical for C. trachomatis + M. genitalium + anaerobes

### Inpatient (Severe PID / Tubo-Ovarian Abscess / Pregnancy / Failed Outpatient)
| Regimen | Dose | Duration |
|---------|------|----------|
| **IV 1st-line** | **Ceftriaxone 2g IV q12h** + **Doxycycline 100mg BD PO/IV** + **Metronidazole 500mg IV q8h** | 24h after clinical improvement → switch to outpatient regimen to complete 14d |
| **Alternative** | **Clindamycin 900mg IV q8h + Gentamicin 2mg/kg IV q8h** (or 5mg/kg q24h) | 24h after improvement → switch |

> **Switch criteria**: Afebrile 24h, improving pain, tolerating oral → complete 14d total

## 6. Specific Clinical Scenarios

### Tubo-Ovarian Abscess (TOA)
- **Diagnosis**: Ultrasound (complex adnexal mass, thick walls, septations) / CT / MRI
- **Management**: **IV antibiotics + drainage** (image-guided percutaneous) if >5cm or failing antibiotics
- **Surgery**: Laparoscopic salpingo-oophorectomy if ruptured / failing medical management
- **Duration**: 14 days IV + possible extended course

### Fitz-Hugh-Curtis Syndrome (Perihepatitis)
- **Features**: RUQ pain, pleural rub, "violin string" adhesions on laparoscopy
- **Pathogens**: GC, CT
- **Treatment**: Standard PID regimen

### PID in Pregnancy
- **Rare** (cervical mucus plug protective)
- **High risk**: Preterm labour, sepsis, fetal loss
- **Antibiotics**: Ceftriaxone + Azithromycin (avoid doxycycline/metronidazole in 1st trimester)
- **Admit**: IV therapy, obstetric review

### IUD and PID
- **Risk**: Highest in first 3 weeks post-insertion (Actinomyces, GC, CT)
- **Management**: **Do NOT routinely remove IUD** if mild PID; treat antibiotics, remove if no improvement 48–72h
- **Actinomyces**: Penicillin G IV × 2w → Amoxicillin PO × 6–12m

## 7. Partner Management
- **All partners within 6 months** (GC/CT) should be notified, tested, treated empirically
- **Expedited Partner Therapy (EPT)**: Provide medication for partners without clinical assessment
- **Abstain** until both partners completed treatment + 7 days
- **Test of cure**: Not routine; if symptoms persist → repeat NAAT, consider reinfection/resistance

## 8. Complications and Sequelae
| Complication | Frequency (Untreated) |
|--------------|----------------------|
| **Infertility** (tubal factor) | 10–15% after 1 episode; ↑ with recurrences |
| **Ectopic pregnancy** | 6–10x risk; tubal damage |
| **Chronic pelvic pain** | 18–30% |
| **Tubo-ovarian abscess** | 5–15% of hospitalised PID |
| **Fitz-Hugh-Curtis** | ~5–10% |
| **Sepsis / peritonitis** | Rare with prompt treatment |

## 9. Follow-Up
| Timepoint | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| **48–72h** | Clinical review (outpatient); should be improving |
| **2 weeks** | End of treatment review; symptom resolution |
| **4–6 weeks** | Test of cure (NAAT GC/CT) if symptoms persist; reinfection screen |
| **6 months** | Reinfection screen (high recurrence risk) |

## 10. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- **PID = clinical diagnosis** — low threshold for treatment
- **Minimal criteria**: Cervical motion/uterine/adnexal tenderness + inflammation
- **Empirical Rx**: Ceftriaxone IM + Doxycycline 14d + Metronidazole 14d
- **Inpatient**: IV Ceftriaxone + Doxycycline + Metronidazole
- **Doxycycline 14d essential** (CT, M. genitalium, anaerobes)
- **Don't remove IUD routinely** — treat first, remove if no improvement 48–72h
- **TOA**: IV antibiotics + percutaneous drainage if >5cm
- **Partner notification 6 months**; EPT encouraged
- **Sequelae**: Infertility, ectopic, chronic pain ↑ with each episode

## 12. Common Confusions / Exam Traps
- **Doxycycline 14 days** — not 7d (misses CT, M. genitalium)
- **Metronidazole** — covers anaerobes (often overlooked)
- **IUD removal** — NOT routinely; only if no improvement 48–72h
- **Severe PID** = IV therapy; TOA = drainage >5cm
- **Fitz-Hugh-Curtis** = perihepatitis (RUQ pain) + PID
- **Pregnancy** = Ceftriaxone + Azithromycin (avoid doxy/metro)
- **Test of cure** not routine — reinfection common
