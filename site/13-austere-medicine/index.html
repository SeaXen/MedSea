▸ 13.1.01 Air Pollution Respiratory Health
[!info] Davidson Ch 9 Alignment: Environmental Medicine → Air Pollution & Respiratory Health
FCPS/MRCP Focus: Criteria pollutants, sources, health effects (respiratory, cardiovascular, neurological), AQI/WHO guidelines, vulnerable groups, prevention strategies

1. 🎯 Learning Objectives

  • [ ] Identify Criteria Air Pollutants: PM2.5, PM10, O3, NO2, SO2, CO, Lead
  • [ ] Apply Source Apportionment: Traffic, industry, power generation, biomass burning, agriculture
  • [ ] Recognise Health Effects: Respiratory (asthma, COPD, lung cancer), Cardiovascular (MI, stroke, HTN), Neurological, Developmental, Cancer
  • [ ] Apply Air Quality Indices: AQI, WHO Guidelines (2021), NAAQS, Air Quality Bands
  • [ ] Identify Vulnerable Groups: Children, elderly, pregnant, pre-existing cardiopulmonary disease, low-income
  • [ ] Apply Prevention & Control: Source control, urban planning, clean energy, personal protection, policy

10. ❓ Viva Questions

  1. What are the six criteria air pollutants and which is considered most harmful?
  • PM2.5, PM10, O3, NO2, SO2, CO, Pb; PM2.5 most harmful (systemic effects, deep lung penetration).
  1. What are the WHO 2021 Air Quality Guideline values for PM2.5 and NO2 annual means?
  • PM2.5: 5 µg/m³, NO2: 10 µg/m³.
  1. Which pollutant is the best proxy for traffic-related air pollution?
  • NO2 (and Black Carbon).
  1. What are the main health effects of PM2.5?
  • Respiratory (asthma, COPD, lung cancer), Cardiovascular (MI, stroke, HF), Cognitive decline, Diabetes, Low birth weight, Mortality.
  1. How does Ozone form and what are its health effects?
  • Photochemical reaction (NOx + VOCs + sunlight); Respiratory: airway inflammation, reduced lung function, asthma/COPD exacerbation, premature mortality.
  1. What are the WHO 2021 AQG values for PM2.5 and NO2 and how do they differ from 2005?
  • PM2.5: 5 µg/m³ (was 10); NO2: 10 µg/m³ (was 40); Stricter based on new evidence.
  1. Which groups are most vulnerable to air pollution?
  • Children (lung development), Elderly, Pregnant women, Pre-existing cardiopulmonary disease, Low-income communities.
  1. What are the main sources of PM2.5 in urban areas?
  • Traffic (exhaust, non-exhaust), Industry, Residential heating (biomass/coal), Secondary formation (NOx, SO2, NH3).
  1. What is the difference between PM10 and PM2.5 health effects?
  • PM10: Upper airways, coarse; PM2.5: Deep lung, systemic translocation, broader effects.
  1. What are the main interventions to reduce air pollution at source?
  • Transport (EV, public transit, LEZ, fuel standards), Industry (BAT, scrubbers, fuel switch), Residential (clean cooking, insulation), Agriculture (NH3 reduction), Urban planning (compact, green, active transport).
  1. How does air pollution affect children's lung development?
  • Reduced lung function growth, increased asthma incidence, PM2.5, NO2, TRAP implicated.
  1. What is the Air Quality Index and how is it used?
  • Standardised index (0-500) based on worst pollutant sub-index; Categories: Good to Hazardous; Guides public advisories.
  1. What is the health impact of indoor air pollution from biomass fuel?
  • 3.2M deaths/year (WHO); Women/children in LMIC; ARI, COPD, Lung Cancer, Cataract, LBW.

11. ❓ Viva Questions

  1. What are the clinical features of acute organophosphate poisoning?
  • SLUDGE: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI cramps, Emesis; Miosis, Bradycardia, Bronchorrhoea, Muscle Fasciculations, Weakness, Respiratory Failure.
  1. What is the antidote for organophosphate poisoning and when should it be given?
  • Atropine 2mg IV (Repeat q5-10min until dry secretions/HR>80); Pralidoxime 1-2g IV (Within 24-48h).
  1. What are the health effects of chronic arsenic exposure?
  • Skin lesions (Hyperkeratosis, Hyperpigmentation), Cancer (Skin, Lung, Bladder), CVD, Diabetes, Neurotoxicity.
  1. Why is cadmium particularly toxic to the kidneys?
  • Accumulates in Proximal Tubules → Tubular Proteinuria (β2-Microglobulin), Fanconi Syndrome, Osteomalacia (Itai-Itai Disease).
  1. What is the mechanism of benzene-induced leukaemia?
  • Metabolites (Benzene Oxide, Quinones) → DNA Damage, Chromosomal Aberrations (Aneuploidy, Translocations) → AML.
  1. What is the significance of dioxins (TCDD) and how are they measured?
  • Most Toxic Synthetic Chemical, Carcinogen Group 1, Measured as TEQ (Toxic Equivalency) relative to TCDD.
  1. What are PFAS and why are they called "Forever Chemicals"?
  • Per/Polyfluoroalkyl Substances, C-F Bond Extremely Stable → Persistent, Bioaccumulative, Mobile → Drinking Water Major Exposure.
  1. How is radiation risk assessed for radon exposure?
  • Radon = #2 Lung Cancer Cause, Indoor Accumulation, Test Homes in High-Risk Areas, Mitigation (Sub-Slab Depressurisation).
  1. What are the key biomarkers for lead exposure?
  • Blood Lead Level (BLL) = Recent Exposure, Zinc Protoporphyrin (ZPP) = Chronic Effect, Bone Lead (XRF) = Cumulative Burden (Research).
  1. What is the management of acute arsenic poisoning?
  • Chelation: DMPS/DMSA (IV/PO), Supportive (Fluids, Electrolytes, Cardiac Monitoring), Source Removal.
  1. What is the significance of biomonitoring in environmental health?
  • Measures Internal Dose (Integrates All Routes), Reflects Biologically Effective Dose, Guides Risk Assessment & Public Health Action.

  • Heat-related illness (exhaustion, stroke), Extreme weather injuries/deaths (floods, cyclones, wildfires, droughts), UV radiation (skin cancer, cataracts).

  • Temperature ↑ → Faster pathogen replication in vectors, longer transmission seasons, geographic range expansion (latitude/altitude).

  • PM2.5/CO/VOCs from smoke → Respiratory/CV exacerbation, Premature mortality, Mental health impacts, Displacement.
  1. How does climate change affect food security and nutrition?
  • ↓ Crop yields (heat/drought/floods), ↓ Nutrient density (Protein, Zn, Fe) due to CO2, Fisheries decline → Malnutrition, Micronutrient Deficiencies.

  • PTSD/Depression/Anxiety post-disasters, Eco-anxiety/Solastalgia, Heat → Aggression/Suicide, Displacement Trauma.

  • Children, Elderly, Pregnant Women, Chronic Disease, Low-Income, Coastal/Island/Arctic/Urban Poor, Outdoor Workers, Indigenous.
  1. What are the health co-benefits of climate mitigation?

11. ❓ Viva Questions

  1. What are the phases of the disaster management cycle?
  • Mitigation, Preparedness, Response, Recovery
  1. What is Crush Syndrome and how is it managed?
  • Crush Injury >4-6h → Rhabdomyolysis → Hyperkalaemia, AKI → IV Fluids, Bicarbonate Alkalinisation, Dialysis if Severe.
  1. What are the health priorities in the first 72 hours after a major earthquake?
  • Search & Rescue, Triage, Trauma Care, Crush Syndrome Management, WASH, Disease Surveillance, Mental Health.
  1. How do you manage a Chlorine Gas exposure?
  • Remove from Exposure, Decontaminate, Supportive Respiratory Care (O2, Bronchodilators, Steroids if ARDS), Monitor for Delayed Pulmonary Oedema.
  1. What is the immediate treatment for Cyanide poisoning?
  • Hydroxocobalamin 5g IV (Preferred) OR Nitrite-Thiosulfate Kit (Amyl Nitrite Inhalation → Sodium Nitrite IV → Sodium Thiosulfate IV).
  1. What is the recommended water quantity per person per day in emergencies?
  • 15 Litres/person/day (Minimum 7.5 L Survival).
  1. What is the role of Potassium Iodide (KI) in radiation emergencies?
  • Blocks Thyroid Uptake of Radioactive I-131, Dose: 130mg Adult/Child>10y, 65mg 3-10y, 32mg 1m-3y, 16mg <1m, Give Before/Shortly After Exposure.
  1. How do you manage Crush Syndrome?
  • Aggressive IV Hydration (NS/RL), Urine Alkalinisation (Bicarbonate), Monitor K+/Ca2+/CK, Early Dialysis if AKI/Oliguria/Hyperkalaemia.
  1. What are the early warning system components?
  • Risk Knowledge, Monitoring/Warning, Dissemination/Communication, Response Capability.
  1. What vaccines are priority in humanitarian emergencies?
  • Measles (6m-15y), Cholera (OCV), Meningitis (MenA/ACWY), Hepatitis A, Tetanus.
  1. How do you triage in a mass casualty event?
  • START (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) or SALT (Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport) — Red (Immediate), Yellow (Delayed), Green (Minor), Black (Expectant/Deceased).
  1. What is the minimum water quantity per person per day in emergencies?
  • 15 Litres/person/day (Sphere Standard), Minimum Survival 7.5 Litres.
  1. What is the role of Mental Health in disaster response?
  • Psychological First Aid (PFA) Immediately, Community-Based MHPSS, Integrate into PHC, Screen for PTSD/Depression/Anxiety, Long-term Follow-up.

12. ❓ Viva Questions

  1. What are the four steps of risk assessment?
  • Hazard Identification, Dose-Response Assessment, Exposure Assessment, Risk Characterization
  1. What is the difference between NOAEL and LOAEL?
  • NOAEL = Highest dose with NO adverse effect; LOAEL = Lowest dose WITH adverse effect
  1. What are the four main exposure pathways?
  • Inhalation, Ingestion, Dermal, Transplacental
  1. Which populations are most vulnerable to environmental hazards?
  • Children (developing organs), Pregnant women (placental transfer), Elderly (reduced reserve), Occupational groups, Low-income communities
  1. What is the purpose of a Health Impact Assessment (HIA)?
  • Predict health effects of policies/projects/plans → Inform decision-making, mitigate harms, enhance benefits, promote equity
  1. What is the role of biomonitoring in environmental health?
  • Measures internal dose (blood, urine, hair) → Integrates all exposure routes, reflects biologically effective dose
  1. What is the difference between RfD and CSF?
  • RfD = Non-cancer threshold (mg/kg/day); CSF = Cancer slope factor (risk per mg/kg/day)
  1. What is the WHO framework for risk assessment?
  • Hazard ID → Dose-Response → Exposure Assessment → Risk Characterization
  1. What are the main exposure pathways for lead?
  • Ingestion (paint, dust, soil, water), Inhalation (dust, fumes), Transplacental (pregnancy)
  1. What is the role of HIA in urban planning?
  • Assess health impacts of transport, housing, energy, land use policies → Integrate health into urban design
  1. How does climate change act as a risk multiplier in environmental health?
  • Amplifies existing risks: heat, vector-borne disease, water/food security, air quality, displacement, mental health |

14. ❓ Viva Questions

  1. What is the hierarchy of controls in occupational health?
  • Eliminate → Substitute → Engineering → Administrative → PPE (Most to Least Effective)
  1. What are the radiographic features of Silicosis?
  • Small Rounded Opacities (Simple), Progressive Massive Fibrosis (PMF), Eggshell Calcification of Hilar Nodes.
  1. How do you differentiate Occupational Asthma from Work-Exacerbated Asthma?
  • OA = New Asthma Caused by Work (Latent Period, Sensitiser/Irritant); WEA = Pre-existing Asthma Worsened by Work.
  1. What is the classic radiographic finding in Asbestosis?
  • Pleural Plaques (Calcified, Diaphragm), Basal Interstitial Fibrosis, Honeycombing (Late).
  1. What is the diagnostic criteria for Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome (HAVS)?
  • Vibration White Finger (Raynaud's) + Sensorineural Symptoms (Numbness/Tingling), Stockholm Workshop Scale, Exposure History to Vibrating Tools.
  1. What is the characteristic audiometric finding in Noise-Induced Hearing Loss?
  • Bilateral Symmetric Sensorineural Hearing Loss with Notch at 4-6 kHz.
  1. What is the relationship between Asbestos and Mesothelioma?
  • Asbestos = Only Known Cause of Mesothelioma (Signature Cancer), All Types Carcinogenic, Latency 20-40 Years.
  1. What is the management of Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome?
  • Stop Exposure, Warmth, Smoking Cessation, Nifedipine (If Severe), Avoid Cold/Vibration.
  1. What are the common causes of Occupational Contact Dermatitis?
  • Allergic: Nickel, Chromate (Cement), Epoxy, Rubber Accelerators, Formaldehyde; Irritant: Wet Work, Solvents, Cutting Oils, Acids/Alkalis.
  1. What are the criteria for diagnosing Occupational Asthma?
  • Work-Related Symptoms + Objective Evidence (PEF Variability >20%, Specific IgE, Specific Inhalation Challenge).
  1. What are the health surveillance requirements for Lead workers?
  • Blood Lead Level 3-6 Monthly, Action Level >20 µg/dL, Suspension >50 µg/dL, ZPP Monitoring.
  1. What is the difference between Occupational Asthma and RADS?
  • OA = Latent Period, Sensitiser; RADS = Single High Exposure → Immediate Onset, No Latency, Persistent Asthma.
  1. What are the prescribed diseases for Workers' Compensation (UK)?
  • Mesothelioma, Asbestosis, Silicosis, CWP, Byssinosis, Occupational Asthma, NIHL, HAVS, WRULDs, Contact Dermatitis, Occupational Cancers, Infectious Diseases (Lab/Animal Workers).
