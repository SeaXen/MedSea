# Operative vaginal delivery

---

## Definition

Operative vaginal delivery (OVD) — ventouse (vacuum) or forceps — is used to expedite delivery in the second stage of labour for fetal or maternal indications. Choice of instrument depends on clinical circumstances, operator experience, and station. Successful OVD avoids caesarean section and its associated morbidity.

## Core Clinical Content

### Incidence
10-15% of vaginal deliveries; varies by hospital and population

### Indications
Fetal distress (CTG abnormalities); maternal exhaustion; prolonged second stage; medical conditions (cardiac, neurological — avoid Valsalva); after epidural

### Prerequisites
Fully dilated; ruptured membranes; engaged head (station +2 or below); cephalic presentation; known fetal position; adequate pelvis; empty bladder; experienced operator; maternal consent

### Outlet OVD
Head at perineum; scalp visible; easy delivery; low risk

### Low OVD
Leading point at +2 or below; rotation up to 45° acceptable

### Mid-cavity OVD
Head engaged but above +2; higher risk; rarely used; senior operator

### Ventouse
Vacuum cup; silastic or metal Kiwi; less maternal trauma; higher neonatal cephalhaematoma; less effective for rotation; avoid <34 weeks (cephalic) and face/breech

### Forceps
Wrigley's (outlet), Neville-Barnes (low), Kielland (rotation); more maternal trauma but faster delivery; safer for preterm and face presentations

### Complications maternal
Perineal trauma (3rd/4th degree); PPH; vaginal/cervical lacerations; urinary retention; fistula (rare); pelvic floor dysfunction

### Complications neonatal
Cephalhaematoma; caput; retinal haemorrhage; facial nerve palsy (forceps); shoulder dystocia; subgaleal haemorrhage (ventouse) — emergency
