# Rhesus isoimmunisation and anti-D

---

## Definition

Rhesus (Rh) isoimmunisation occurs when a Rh-negative mother is exposed to Rh-positive fetal red cells (often at delivery), producing anti-D IgG antibodies that can cross the placenta and cause haemolytic disease of the fetus and newborn (HDFN) in subsequent pregnancies. Anti-D prophylaxis at 28-30 weeks and post-delivery prevents sensitisation.

## Core Clinical Content

### Pathophysiology
Rh- mother sensitised to Rh+ fetal red cells → IgG anti-D → crosses placenta → HDFN in subsequent pregnancies

### At-risk pregnancies
Any Rh- mother with Rh+ (or unknown) fetus; sensitising events: delivery, miscarriage, ectopic, CVS, amniocentesis, APH, trauma, ECV

### Sensitising events
Anti-D 250-500 IU IM within 72 h of event; Kleihauer test to quantify fetomaternal haemorrhage and adjust dose

### Routine prophylaxis
Anti-D 500 IU IM at 28 weeks (UK); 1500 IU IM at 28 and 34 weeks (US)

### Post-delivery
Anti-D 500 IU IM within 72 h of delivery of Rh+ baby; Kleihauer to confirm adequate dose

### HDFN spectrum
Mild anaemia → hydrops fetalis (severe); kernicterus in neonate; intrauterine transfusion

### Monitoring affected pregnancy
Maternal anti-D titres; middle cerebral artery Doppler (peak systolic velocity); serial USS; amniocentesis if needed

### Treatment severe HDFN
Intrauterine transfusion; early delivery; exchange transfusion; IVIG neonatal

### Already sensitised
No prophylaxis helps; monitor titres; consider plasmapheresis; MDT; consider IVIG; delivery timing critical

### Future
Prophylaxis effective; rare failures due to silent sensitisation or inadequate dose
