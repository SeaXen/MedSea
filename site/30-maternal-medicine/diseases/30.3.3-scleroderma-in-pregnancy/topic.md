# Scleroderma in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Scleroderma (systemic sclerosis) in pregnancy is uncommon as disease often affects older women, but it does occur. Limited cutaneous disease has better outcomes; diffuse cutaneous disease with renal crisis risk, pulmonary hypertension, and severe organ involvement carries high maternal mortality. Multidisciplinary care is essential.

## Core Clinical Content

### Epidemiology
F:M 3-4:1; usually perimenopausal; F childbearing years have worse disease

### Types
Limited cutaneous (CREST — old definition; better prognosis); diffuse cutaneous (rapid skin, internal organ); systemic sclerosis overlap

### Effect of pregnancy
Disease often stable; renal crisis risk (especially diffuse + abrupt BP rise); cardiopulmonary complications dangerous

### Maternal risks
Renal crisis (scleroderma renal crisis — malignant HTN, AKI, microangiopathic haemolysis — 5-10% mortality); PAH crisis; PPH (poor wound healing; uterine vessels affected)

### Fetal risks
IUGR; preterm; miscarriage; stillbirth; small for gestational age; placental insufficiency

### Preconception
Stable disease ≥3-5 years; echo (PAH, LV); PFTs; renal function; switch MMF to azathioprine; avoid teratogens

### Monitoring
Monthly BP, U&E, urine; growth scans; umbilical artery Doppler; echo 2nd/3rd trimester; review symptoms

### Treatment
Continue nifedipine; ACE inhibitors for renal crisis (despite pregnancy; life-saving); proton pump inhibitors; iloprost for severe Raynaud's; steroids

### Renal crisis management
Captopril or enalapril IV (off-label but life-saving); control BP urgently; ITU; avoid NSAIDs

### Delivery and postpartum
Vaginal preferred; assisted second stage; avoid ergometrine; PPH risk; breastfeeding considerations
