# Adrenal disease in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Adrenal disease in pregnancy is rare but includes Cushing's syndrome (often ACTH-independent, e.g. adrenal adenoma), primary hyperaldosteronism, Addison's disease, and congenital adrenal hyperplasia. Physiological cortisol rise makes diagnosis challenging; biochemical workup and imaging are key.

## Core Clinical Content

### Physiology
CBG rises; total cortisol rises 2-3x; free cortisol rises; ACTH normal to mildly elevated

### Cushing's syndrome
Rare; usually adrenal adenoma; features overlap with normal pregnancy (HTN, striae, GDM)

### Diagnosis
24-h urinary free cortisol (cut-off 3x upper limit); late-night salivary cortisol; MRI without gadolinium

### Management Cushing's
Surgery (adrenalectomy) in 2nd trimester if feasible; medical — metyrapone preferred

### Addison's disease
Primary adrenal insufficiency; glucocorticoid + mineralocorticoid replacement; labour dose 100 mg hydrocortisone IV

### Addisonian crisis
Vomiting, hypotension, hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia; IV hydrocortisone 100 mg stat

### Congenital adrenal hyperplasia
21-hydroxylase deficiency; dexamethasone crosses placenta; consider prenatal treatment for affected female fetus

### Primary hyperaldosteronism
Rare; features overlap with normal pregnancy; treat with labetalol; surgical if Conn's adenoma

### Phaeochromocytoma
Rare but dangerous; screen if refractory HTN or paroxysmal symptoms; α-blockade then β; deliver with MDT

### Postpartum
Review replacement; may need dose reduction if pre-pregnancy dose high
