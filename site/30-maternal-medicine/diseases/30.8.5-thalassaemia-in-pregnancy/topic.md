# Thalassaemia in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Thalassaemia in pregnancy — alpha and beta — are inherited disorders of haemoglobin synthesis common in Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, South Asian, and South-East Asian populations. Maternal complications include anaemia, transfusion requirements, iron overload, and thromboembolism; severe fetal forms (α-thalassaemia major, β-thalassaemia major) cause hydrops fetalis or lifelong transfusion dependence.

## Core Clinical Content

### Definition
Inherited disorders of globin chain synthesis; α (4 genes) and β (2 genes)

### α-thalassaemia
Silent carrier (1 gene); trait (2); HbH (3) — moderate anaemia; Hb Barts (4) — hydrops fetalis, lethal

### β-thalassaemia
Trait (carrier) — mild anaemia; intermedia — moderate; major — transfusion dependent; chelation

### Screening
MCV <80; ferritin normal; Hb electrophoresis/HPLC; partner testing if MCV low; cascade screening in families

### Preconception
Genetic counselling; partner testing; PGD if both carriers; optimise transfusion/chelation

### Antenatal care
MDT (haematologist, obstetrician); serial Hb; transfuse if <80 g/L or symptomatic; folic acid; iron if deficient (cautious); monitor chelation (deferoxamine safe; deferasirox avoid)

### Fetal monitoring
Anomaly scan; MCA Doppler for fetal anaemia; consideration of intrauterine transfusion if hydrops

### Risks maternal
Anaemia; transfusion; thromboembolism; alloimmunisation; iron overload; cardiac complications

### Risks fetal/neonatal
IUGR; preterm birth; hydrops (α-major); congenital anomalies if transfused in 1st trimester

### Delivery
Vaginal preferred; assisted second stage if cardiomyopathy; avoid ergometrine; active management

### Postpartum
VTE prophylaxis; contraception (avoid COC if iron overload/high thrombosis risk); transfusion plan
