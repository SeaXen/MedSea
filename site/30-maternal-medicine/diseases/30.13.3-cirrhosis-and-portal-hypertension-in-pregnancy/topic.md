# Cirrhosis and portal hypertension in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Cirrhosis and portal hypertension in pregnancy are uncommon as most cirrhotic women are beyond reproductive age or have amenorrhoea. However, with improved care, more women with compensated cirrhosis are becoming pregnant. Pregnancy carries significant risks of variceal bleeding, hepatic decompensation, and maternal mortality.

## Core Clinical Content

### Preconception
Optimise; screen for varices; beta-blocker; eradication of varices; counsel on risks; consider MELD score

### Maternal risks
Variceal bleeding (5-30%); hepatic decompensation (ascites, encephalopathy, jaundice); postpartum haemorrhage; mortality 1-2% (compensated) up to 50% (decompensated)

### Fetal risks
Miscarriage; preterm; low birth weight; stillbirth; congenital anomalies if on teratogens

### Preconception screening
Upper GI endoscopy (varices); ultrasound (spleen, ascites, liver nodularity); MELD score; viral load if viral cause

### Antenatal care
MDT (hepatologist, obstetrician, anaesthetist); endoscopic surveillance; LMWH if portal vein thrombosis or thrombocytopenia; avoid NSAIDs

### Variceal bleeding
Most common in 2nd trimester; band ligation preferred; terlipressin (use cautiously); transfusion; rescue TIPSS if refractory

### Beta-blockers
Propranolol or carvedilol for variceal prophylaxis; safe in pregnancy

### Drugs to avoid
Statins (teratogenic); ACEI/ARB; methotrexate; some herbal products; high-dose vitamins A and E

### Delivery
Vaginal preferred; assisted second stage (avoid Valsalva); avoid ergometrine; C-section for obstetric indication (haemorrhage risk)

### Postpartum
Monitor for decompensation; VTE prophylaxis; breastfeeding compatible with most liver medications
