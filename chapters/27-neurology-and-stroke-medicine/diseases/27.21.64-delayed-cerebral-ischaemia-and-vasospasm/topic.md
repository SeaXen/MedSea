# Delayed Cerebral Ischaemia and Vasospasm after SAH

## Learning Objectives
- Define DCI and distinguish from angiographic vasospasm.
- Identify the typical time course and clinical features.
- Apply nimodipine prophylaxis correctly.
- Recognise and treat symptomatic DCI early.
- Discuss the role of advanced monitoring (TCD, CTA, CT perfusion).

## Definition
- **Delayed cerebral ischaemia (DCI)** = new neurological deficit or cerebral infarction occurring days 4–14 after SAH, not attributable to other causes (rebleed, hydrocephalus, seizure, metabolic).
- **Angiographic vasospasm** = narrowing of intracranial arteries visible on CTA/DSA — common but not synonymous with DCI (some vasospasm is asymptomatic; some DCI occurs without severe vasospasm).
- Modern understanding: DCI is multifactorial — macrovasospasm, microcirculatory dysfunction, microthrombosis, cortical spreading depolarisation, neuroinflammation.

## Time Course
- **Onset**: typically days 4–14.
- **Peak incidence**: day 7.
- Most patients at risk until day 21; risk then declines.
- Severe bleed (modified Fisher 3–4) and poor clinical grade increase risk.

## Pathophysiology
- Blood breakdown products (oxyhaemoglobin, endothelin-1) trigger smooth-muscle contraction in vessel walls.
- Microcirculatory dysfunction and microthrombosis contribute.
- Cortical spreading depolarisation may be a final common pathway.

## Clinical Features
- New focal neurological deficit (e.g., hemiparesis, aphasia, hemianopia).
- Decreased level of consciousness.
- New fever is a warning sign.
- Often fluctuating deficits.
- **Pitfall**: not all neurological deterioration in SAH is DCI — exclude rebleed, hydrocephalus, hyponatraemia, seizure, infection.

## Monitoring
- **Transcranial Doppler (TCD)** — daily, look for mean MCA velocity >120 cm/s (moderate spasm) or >200 cm/s (severe).
- **CTA / CT perfusion** — for non-invasive monitoring; can show delayed perfusion.
- **DSA** — gold standard; allows intra-arterial treatment.

## Prophylaxis
- **Nimodipine 60 mg PO 4-hourly for 21 days** — the only proven pharmacological prophylaxis for DCI.
- Euvolaemia (isotonic IV fluids).
- Statin therapy (e.g., simvastatin 40 mg) — evidence mixed but commonly used.
- Magnesium sulphate — not supported by recent trials (MASH-2).

## Treatment of Symptomatic DCI
1. **Euvolaemia + induced hypertension** — IV crystalloid; target SBP 160–200 mmHg with noradrenaline/phenylephrine (only after aneurysm is secured).
2. **Endovascular intervention**:
   - **Intra-arterial nimodipine or verapamil** (chemical angioplasty).
   - **Balloon angioplasty** for accessible proximal vessels.
3. **Refractory cases**: therapeutic hypothermia, milrinone, ICU support — no clear evidence.

## Triple-H Therapy: Caveats
- Historical: **Hypervolaemia, Hypertension, Haemodilution**.
- Modern: **Euvolaemia + induced hypertension** is the standard. Hypervolaemia and haemodilution have not improved outcomes and carry risks (pulmonary oedema, ARDS).
- Avoid aggressive haemodilution.

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- "Nimodipine dose" → **60 mg 4-hourly for 21 days**.
- "Peak DCI" → **Day 7**.
- "First-line treatment of symptomatic DCI" → **Euvolaemia + induced hypertension** (only after aneurysm securing).
- "Triple-H current status" → **Euvolaemia + hypertension only**; hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out.

## Viva Questions
**Q1. Define DCI and distinguish from vasospasm.**
> DCI = new neurological deficit or cerebral infarction days 4-14 post-SAH, not from other causes. Angiographic vasospasm = arterial narrowing on imaging — common but not synonymous with DCI.

**Q2. Why nimodipine in SAH if not for vasodilation?**
> Nimodipine reduces DCI by neuroprotective and microcirculatory effects, not primarily by vasodilation. Improves outcomes even when angiographic vasospasm is unchanged.

**Q3. First-line treatment of symptomatic DCI?**
> Euvolaemia + induced hypertension (target SBP 160-200 mmHg with noradrenaline/phenylephrine) — only AFTER aneurysm is secured.

**Q4. Why was 'Triple-H' therapy modernised?**
> Hypervolaemia and haemodilution have not improved outcomes and carry risks (pulmonary oedema, ARDS). Euvolaemia + HTN is the current standard.

**Q5. MASH-2 trial — implication for magnesium?**
> Magnesium sulphate does not improve outcomes in SAH despite promising early trials. Not recommended as prophylaxis.

## Confusions & Mnemonics
- **Delayed cerebral ischaemia and vasospasm** — see key points above
- **'ACoA → bitemporal hemianopia'** — Anterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses the optic chiasm from above
- **'PCoA → pupil-involving third'** — Posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III
- **'Triple-H = Euvolaemia + HTN only'** — Hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out
- **'CSW → salt + fluid; SIADH → fluid restrict'** — opposite treatments
- **'Day 7 = DCI peak'** — Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks day 7
