## Definition

**Clinical features and presentation of SAH** describe the spectrum of acute neurological symptoms in subarachnoid haemorrhage. The hallmark is sudden 'thunderclap' headache (peak in seconds) often with meningismus, vomiting, photophobia, ± loss of consciousness, ± focal deficits (e.g., PCoA → pupil-involving 3rd nerve palsy).


# Clinical Features and Presentation of SAH

## Learning Objectives
- Recognise the spectrum of clinical presentation in SAH.
- Apply severity grading scales (Hunt & Hess, WFNS).
- Distinguish sentinel headache from migraine or thunderclap headache of other causes.
- Identify high-risk features requiring urgent intervention.

## Cardinal Presentation: Thunderclap Headache
- **Definition**: sudden-onset headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds to minutes.
- **"Worst headache of my life"** — most common self-description.
- Often described as "exploding", "thunderclap", "like being hit on the head".
- Frequently associated with **vomiting, photophobia, neck stiffness** (meningeal irritation from blood).
- Loss of consciousness in ~50 % (transient in many, sustained in severe bleeds).

## Sentinel ("Warning") Headache
- A minor leak preceding the major SAH in 30–50 % of patients, typically 6–20 days earlier.
- Often misinterpreted as migraine, "sick headache", or neck strain.
- Key exam point: failure to recognise a sentinel headache is a **common cause of preventable death**.
- Any new sudden severe headache — even if it resolves — needs CT/LP to exclude SAH.

## Other Presenting Features
- **Nausea and vomiting** — common, due to raised ICP and meningeal irritation.
- **Photophobia** — meningeal irritation.
- **Neck stiffness** — develops hours after bleed; not present immediately.
- **Focal neurological deficits** — from mass effect of aneurysm (e.g., PCoA → third nerve palsy).
- **Seizures** — 5–10 % at onset.
- **Fever** — common in first 24–48 h, low-grade, due to blood-induced inflammation.

## Fundoscopy
- **Terson syndrome** = vitreous haemorrhage seen on fundus examination, due to sudden raised ICP transmitted to retinal veins.
- Indicates severe SAH; associated with worse prognosis.
- May be detected even when the patient is unconscious.

## Cardiovascular
- **Takotsubo cardiomyopathy** ("broken heart syndrome") — apical ballooning in 5–10 % of SAH.
- Troponin may be elevated; ECG may show ST/T changes mimicking MI.
- Cause: catecholamine surge.

## Severity Grading — Davidson 24e Perspective
### Hunt & Hess Scale
| Grade | Features |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Asymptomatic or mild headache, slight nuchal rigidity |
| 2 | Moderate-severe headache, nuchal rigidity, no deficit (except possibly cranial nerve palsy) |
| 3 | Drowsy, confused, mild focal deficit |
| 4 | Stupor, moderate-severe deficit |
| 5 | Coma, decerebrate posturing |

### WFNS Scale
| Grade | GCS | Focal deficit |
|-------|-----|---------------|
| 1 | 15 | Absent |
| 2 | 14–13 | Absent |
| 3 | 14–13 | Present |
| 4 | 12–7 | Present or absent |
| 5 | 6–3 | Present or absent |

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- Hunt & Hess 1–2 / WFNS 1–2: good candidate for early aneurysm securing.
- Hunt & Hess 4–5 / WFNS 4–5: high mortality; treat aggressively but consider goals of care.
- Grade 3 is the threshold where aggressive intervention is most debated.

## Differential Diagnosis of Thunderclap Headache
- SAH (most important to exclude).
- Reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome (RCVS).
- Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis.
- Pituitary apoplexy.
- Migraine (but usually not truly thunderclap).
- Cluster headache (often considered, but cluster has autonomic features and is not truly "thunderclap").
- Hypertensive emergency.
- Posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES).

## Viva Questions
**Q1. Define thunderclap headache.**
> Sudden-onset headache reaching maximum intensity within seconds to minutes. Classically 'worst headache of life'.

**Q2. What is a sentinel headache?**
> A minor SAH leak preceding the major bleed in 30-50% of patients, typically 6-20 days earlier. Often misdiagnosed as migraine or 'sick headache'.

**Q3. Differential diagnosis of thunderclap headache.**
> SAH, RCVS, cerebral venous sinus thrombosis, pituitary apoplexy, migraine, cluster headache, hypertensive emergency, PRES.

**Q4. What is Terson syndrome?**
> Vitreous haemorrhage on fundoscopy from sudden raised ICP transmitted to retinal veins. Indicates severe SAH and worse prognosis.

**Q5. Takotsubo cardiomyopathy in SAH — features?**
> Apical ballooning, mimics MI (chest pain, ST changes, troponin rise). Due to catecholamine surge. Self-resolves over days to weeks.

## Confusions & Mnemonics
- **Clinical features and presentation of SAH** — see key points above
- **'ACoA → bitemporal hemianopia'** — Anterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses the optic chiasm from above
- **'PCoA → pupil-involving third'** — Posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III
- **'Triple-H = Euvolaemia + HTN only'** — Hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out
- **'CSW → salt + fluid; SIADH → fluid restrict'** — opposite treatments
- **'Day 7 = DCI peak'** — Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks day 7
