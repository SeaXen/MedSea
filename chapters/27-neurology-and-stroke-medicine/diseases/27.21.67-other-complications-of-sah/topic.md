## Definition

**Other complications of SAH** include rebleeding (highest risk 24 h), hyponatraemia (CSW vs SIADH), seizures (5-10%), Takotsubo cardiomyopathy (apical ballooning from catecholamine surge), Terson syndrome (vitreous haemorrhage from raised ICP), and long-term cognitive/mood impairment.


# Other Complications of SAH

## Learning Objectives
- Recognise and manage rebleeding.
- Differentiate cerebral salt wasting (CSW) from SIADH.
- Identify Takotsubo cardiomyopathy and its implications.
- Recognise Terson syndrome and other systemic complications.
- Plan VTE prophylaxis safely post-securing.

## 1. Rebleeding
- **Risk**: highest in first 24 h (~4 %), then ~1.5 %/day for 4 weeks; falls to near zero after securing.
- **Mortality**: 70 % if rebleeds.
- **Prevention**:
  - Definitive: **secure the aneurysm within 72 h**.
  - Supportive: bed rest, analgesia, antiemetics, laxatives, BP control, avoid Valsalva.
  - Anti-fibrinolytics (e.g., tranexamic acid) вҖ” short course pre-securing may reduce early rebleed but does not improve outcome; controversial.

## 2. Hyponatraemia
- **Common** (30вҖ“50 %); daily U&Es mandatory.
- **Cerebral salt wasting (CSW)**:
  - Hypovolaemic hyponatraemia.
  - Mechanism: natriuresis + diuresis from brain-derived natriuretic peptides.
  - Treatment: **isotonic saline + salt Вұ fludrocortisone**.
  - **DO NOT fluid restrict** (worsens hypovolaemia and risk of infarction).
- **SIADH**:
  - Euvolaemic hyponatraemia.
  - Mechanism: inappropriate ADH release.
  - Treatment: **fluid restriction**.
- **Distinguishing**:
  - CSW: high urine output, signs of hypovolaemia (tachycardia, hypotension, decreased CVP, weight loss).
  - SIADH: low/normal urine output, euvolaemia.

## 3. Seizures
- **Incidence**: 5вҖ“10 % (early); late seizures <10 %.
- **Risk factors**: intraparenchymal extension, MCA aneurysm, severe bleed, infarct.
- **Management**:
  - Levetiracetam 500вҖ“1000 mg BD for 1вҖ“2 weeks; longer if late seizures.
  - Phenytoin is also effective but more interactions.
- **Prophylaxis**: controversial; some centres give a short course.

## 4. Cardiac Complications
- **Takotsubo cardiomyopathy** (apical ballooning syndrome) вҖ” 5вҖ“10 %.
  - Mimics MI: chest pain, ST changes, troponin rise.
  - LV apical akinesis with basal hyperkinesis.
  - Self-resolves over days to weeks; supportive care.
  - Important: avoid inotropes that worsen apical ballooning; consider beta-blockade.
- **ECG changes** in 50вҖ“100 %:
  - ST elevation/depression.
  - T-wave inversion (especially deep, "cerebral T waves").
  - Prolonged QT.
  - Arrhythmias: AF, VT, VF, sinus bradycardia, sinus tachycardia.
- **Troponin** may be elevated in 20вҖ“30 %; not specific.

## 5. Terson Syndrome
- **Vitreous haemorrhage** seen on fundoscopy.
- Mechanism: sudden raised ICP вҶ’ retinal vein rupture.
- Indicates severe SAH (poor prognosis).
- Often detected when patient is unconscious.
- Resolves spontaneously in most; vitrectomy if persistent.

## 6. Respiratory Complications
- **Neurogenic pulmonary oedema** вҖ” 5вҖ“10 %; catecholamine surge вҶ’ pulmonary capillary leak.
- **Aspiration pneumonia** вҖ” risk in comatose or dysphagic patients.
- **ARDS** вҖ” particularly after SAH with sepsis or massive transfusion.
- **PE/DVT** вҖ” risk in immobile patients; LMWH only after aneurysm is secured.

## 7. Endocrine and Metabolic
- **Hyperglycaemia** вҖ” common; target glucose 6вҖ“11 mmol/L.
- **Hypomagnesaemia** вҖ” contributes to vasospasm; replace.
- **Hypokalaemia** вҖ” common; replace.
- **Fever** вҖ” common non-infectious fever in first 72 h; consider paracetamol and surface cooling; rule out infection (VAP, UTI, line infection).

## 8. Long-Term Sequelae
- **Cognitive impairment** вҖ” 30вҖ“50 %; memory, executive function, processing speed.
- **Mood disorders** вҖ” depression (40 %), anxiety (30 %), PTSD.
- **Fatigue** вҖ” common, often persistent.
- **Chronic headache** вҖ” 30 %.
- **Epilepsy** вҖ” late seizures in 5вҖ“10 %.

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- "Cerebral T waves" вҶ’ deep, symmetric T-wave inversions in SAH (cerebrogenic pseudocardiac injury).
- "Terson syndrome" вҶ’ vitreous haemorrhage from raised ICP.
- "CSW vs SIADH" вҶ’ CSW = hypovolaemic (treat with salt + fluid); SIADH = euvolaemic (fluid restrict).
- "Highest rebleed risk" вҶ’ first 24 h.

## Clinical Features of SAH Complications

The complications of SAH and their clinical features:

- **Rebleeding**: sudden deterioration in GCS, new severe headache, often within 24 h of initial bleed
- **Hyponatraemia** (CSW vs SIADH): see [[Acute medical management of SAH]] for distinction
- **Seizures**: 5-10% (early), focal or generalised
- **Takotsubo cardiomyopathy**: chest pain, ST changes on ECG, troponin rise, apical ballooning on echo
- **Terson syndrome**: vitreous haemorrhage on fundoscopy
- **Delayed cerebral ischaemia (DCI)**: see [[Delayed cerebral ischaemia and vasospasm]]
- **Long-term cognitive/mood impairment**: memory, executive function, depression, fatigue

## Clinical Investigation and Monitoring

- **Daily U&Es** (Na+ monitoring for CSW/SIADH)
- **ECG telemetry** for at least 24 h
- **Troponin** if Takotsubo suspected
- **Neuropsychology review** for long-term sequelae

## Viva Questions
**Q1. When is the highest risk of rebleeding?**
> First 24 hours (~4% risk). Risk then falls but is significant until the aneurysm is secured.

**Q2. How do you differentiate CSW from SIADH?**
> CSW: hypovolaemic вҖ” high urine output, вҶ“ CVP, weight loss, signs of hypovolaemia (tachycardia, hypotension). Treat with isotonic saline + salt Вұ fludrocortisone. SIADH: euvolaemic вҖ” low urine output, concentrated urine. Treat with fluid restriction. DO NOT fluid-restrict CSW (worsens hypovolaemia and risk of infarction).

**Q3. Cerebral T waves in SAH вҖ” what is the mechanism?**
> Deep symmetric T-wave inversions due to catecholamine surge. May be associated with troponin rise and Takotsubo cardiomyopathy.

**Q4. Terson syndrome вҖ” what is it and what does it signify?**
> Vitreous haemorrhage on fundoscopy from sudden raised ICP transmitted to retinal veins. Indicates severe SAH and worse prognosis. Often resolves spontaneously; vitrectomy if persistent.

**Q5. Long-term complications of SAH.**
> Cognitive impairment (30-50%, especially memory, executive function, processing speed), mood disorders (depression 40%, anxiety 30%, PTSD), fatigue, chronic headache (30%), late seizures (5-10%).
