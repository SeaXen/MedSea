# Hydrocephalus after SAH

## Learning Objectives
- Differentiate acute vs chronic hydrocephalus post-SAH.
- Recognise clinical features of raised ICP and deteriorating GCS.
- Apply external ventricular drain (EVD) for acute hydrocephalus.
- Plan timing of permanent CSF diversion (VP shunt) for chronic hydrocephalus.

## Definition
- **Hydrocephalus post-SAH** = accumulation of CSF in the ventricular system due to disruption of CSF flow or absorption.
- Affects 20–30 % of SAH patients.

## Two Patterns

### Acute Obstructive Hydrocephalus
- **Onset**: within minutes to 24 h of SAH.
- **Mechanism**: intraventricular blood obstructs CSF flow (e.g., at the aqueduct, fourth ventricular outflow).
- **Incidence**: 15–20 % of SAH patients.
- **Clinical features**: rapid deterioration in GCS, pupillary changes, Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respiration).
- **Diagnosis**: CT shows dilated temporal horns and lateral/third ventricles with blood in the ventricles.
- **Management**:
  - **External ventricular drain (EVD)** — emergency CSF diversion; placed at the bedside under ICP monitoring.
  - Aim to drain CSF to keep ICP <20 cmH₂O.
  - EVD can be clamped intermittently to assess for shunt dependence.

### Chronic Communicating Hydrocephalus
- **Onset**: typically 2–4 weeks after SAH (may develop months later).
- **Mechanism**: arachnoid granulation fibrosis → impaired CSF absorption.
- **Clinical features**: **Hakim's triad** of normal pressure hydrocephalus (gait disturbance, urinary incontinence, cognitive impairment) — but most post-SAH chronic hydrocephalus is obstructive-like with raised pressure and deteriorating consciousness.
- **Diagnosis**: CT/MRI shows ventricular enlargement out of proportion to sulcal atrophy.
- **Management**:
  - **VP shunt** (ventriculoperitoneal) — definitive treatment.
  - **Lumboperitoneal shunt** — alternative if no mass effect.
  - **Programmable valves** allow non-invasive pressure adjustment.
  - **Endoscopic third ventriculostomy (ETV)** — used in selected cases.

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- "Acute hydrocephalus post-SAH" → **EVD**.
- "Chronic hydrocephalus post-SAH" → **VP shunt**.
- "Hakim's triad" → gait apraxia, urinary incontinence, dementia (NPH).
- "How long to wait before considering VP shunt?" → Typically 2–4 weeks; trials of clamping the EVD help determine shunt dependence.

## Complications of EVD
- Infection (ventriculitis) — 5–15 %.
- Haemorrhage at insertion.
- Catheter misplacement.
- CSF overdrainage (subdural haematoma).
- Daily CSF sampling for cell count, glucose, protein, culture.

## Complications of VP Shunt
- Infection (~5 %).
- Shunt obstruction (most common cause of shunt failure).
- Overdrainage → subdural haematoma.
- Underdrainage → persistent hydrocephalus.

## Clinical Features of Hydrocephalus after SAH

Hydrocephalus after SAH presents with rapid neurological deterioration:

- **Acute obstructive (within 24 h)**: rapid GCS drop, pupillary changes, Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respiration). CT shows dilated ventricles with intraventricular blood.
- **Chronic communicating (2-4 weeks)**: Hakim's triad (gait apraxia, urinary incontinence, cognitive decline) — though post-SAH chronic hydrocephalus is more often obstructive-like with raised pressure and deteriorating consciousness rather than classic NPH.
- **Headache, vomiting, papilloedema** (signs of raised ICP)

## Clinical Investigation and Management

**Acute**: bedside external ventricular drain (EVD) under ICP monitoring; target ICP < 20 cmH2O.
**Chronic**: VP shunt (ventriculoperitoneal) with programmable valve.

See: [[Investigation and diagnosis of SAH]], [[Acute medical management of SAH]].

## Viva Questions
**Q1. How does acute post-SAH hydrocephalus present?**
> Rapid deterioration in GCS, pupillary changes, Cushing's triad (HTN, bradycardia, irregular respiration). CT shows dilated ventricles with blood in the ventricles.

**Q2. Management of acute obstructive hydrocephalus post-SAH?**
> Emergency external ventricular drain (EVD) at the bedside under ICP monitoring. Aim to keep ICP < 20 cmH2O.

**Q3. When is VP shunt indicated?**
> For chronic communicating hydrocephalus (typically 2-4 weeks after SAH). EVD can be clamped intermittently to assess shunt dependence before definitive shunt placement.

**Q4. EVD complications?**
> Infection (ventriculitis 5-15%), haemorrhage at insertion, catheter misplacement, CSF overdrainage (subdural haematoma). Daily CSF sampling for cell count, glucose, protein, culture.

**Q5. Hakim's triad of NPH.**
> Gait apraxia (magnetic gait), urinary incontinence, dementia ('wet, wobbly, wacky'). However, post-SAH chronic hydrocephalus is often obstructive-like with raised pressure rather than NPH.

## Confusions & Mnemonics
- **Hydrocephalus after SAH** — see key points above
- **'ACoA → bitemporal hemianopia'** — Anterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses the optic chiasm from above
- **'PCoA → pupil-involving third'** — Posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III
- **'Triple-H = Euvolaemia + HTN only'** — Hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out
- **'CSW → salt + fluid; SIADH → fluid restrict'** — opposite treatments
- **'Day 7 = DCI peak'** — Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks day 7
