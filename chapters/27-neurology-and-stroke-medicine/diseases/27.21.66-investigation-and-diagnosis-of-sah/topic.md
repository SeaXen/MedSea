## Definition

**Investigation and diagnosis of SAH** follows the standard pathway: non-contrast CT (within 6 h, sensitivity ~98-100%), lumbar puncture at 12 h if CT negative (xanthochromia), CTA to identify aneurysm, DSA as gold standard, and Modified Fisher scale to predict vasospasm risk.


# Investigation and Diagnosis of SAH

## Learning Objectives
- Apply the standard CT → LP → CTA → DSA pathway.
- Interpret xanthochromia vs blood-stained CSF.
- Identify the role of the modified Fisher scale.
- Recognise when to repeat imaging.

## Step 1: Non-Contrast CT Head
- **Sensitivity**: ~98–100 % if performed within 6 h of ictus.
- Falls to ~85 % at 24 h, ~50 % at 7 days, near zero by 2–3 weeks.
- Look for hyperdensity in **basal cisterns, Sylvian fissures, interhemispheric fissure, sulci**.
- Centring: blood centred anterior to brainstem suggests perimesencephalic pattern.
- Use **modified Fisher scale** (or original Fisher) to grade blood load — predicts vasospasm risk.

### Modified Fisher Scale
| Grade | CT finding | Symptomatic vasospasm risk |
|-------|------------|----------------------------|
| 0 | No SAH, no IVH | 0 % |
| 1 | Thin SAH, no IVH | ~6 % |
| 2 | Thin SAH with IVH | ~16 % |
| 3 | Thick SAH, no IVH | ~33 % |
| 4 | Thick SAH with IVH | ~40 % |

## Step 2: Lumbar Puncture (if CT negative)
- **Indication**: CT negative but clinical suspicion remains high.
- **Timing**: 12 h after ictus (so red cells have time to lyse and form oxyhaemoglobin/bilirubin).
- **Findings**:
  - **Xanthochromia** = yellow discoloration of CSF, due to bilirubin from red cell breakdown.
  - **Opening pressure** often raised.
  - **RBC count**: high, but no decrement from tube 1 to tube 4 (cf. traumatic tap).
  - **Spectrophotometry** (if available) is the gold standard for xanthochromia.
- **Pitfall**: a traumatic tap can give a false positive RBC count. Xanthochromia takes ~12 h to develop, so a tap too early is unhelpful.

## Step 3: CTA (CT Angiography)
- Identifies aneurysms ≥3 mm with sensitivity >95 %.
- Should be performed urgently once SAH is confirmed.
- Provides 3D anatomy for treatment planning (clipping vs coiling).
- Negative CTA + classic perimesencephalic pattern → proceed to DSA to definitively exclude aneurysm.
- Negative CTA + non-perimesencephalic pattern → still proceed to DSA (some aneurysms are missed on CTA).

## Step 4: DSA (Digital Subtraction Angiography)
- **Gold standard** for aneurysm detection.
- Detects aneurysms <3 mm and small AVMs that CTA may miss.
- Performed urgently once SAH is confirmed.
- May need to be repeated at 1–2 weeks if initial study is negative (occasionally vasospasm obscures an aneurysm).

## Other Investigations
- **MRI/MRA** — useful as adjunct; FLAIR can show SAH when CT is negative in subacute phase (>7 days).
- **ECG** — look for ST/T wave changes, prolonged QT, Takotsubo pattern.
- **Troponin** — elevated in 20–30 %; indicates severity but not specific.
- **U&Es** — daily, to detect hyponatraemia (CSW vs SIADH).
- **FBC, coagulation screen, blood glucose, group & save** — baseline.
- **Pregnancy test** in women of childbearing age (radiation safety).
- **ECG telemetry** for at least 24 h (arrhythmia risk).

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- The "classical exam question" is: patient with sudden severe headache, normal CT, what next? → **LP looking for xanthochromia**, performed 12 h after ictus.
- Why does xanthochromia take 12 h? → Bilirubin is produced by in-vivo breakdown of red cells (not traumatic tap).
- Why are U&Es checked daily? → **Cerebral salt wasting** (CSW) and SIADH are both common.

## Clinical Features Prompting SAH Investigation

SAH is investigated when there is clinical suspicion based on:

- **Sudden 'thunderclap' headache** (peak in seconds) — the cardinal trigger
- **Meningismus** with sudden severe headache
- **± Loss of consciousness** (~50%)
- **± Focal neurological deficit** (e.g., painful 3rd nerve palsy)
- **Sentinel headache** (warning leak) — 30-50% of SAH patients
- **Terson syndrome** (vitreous haemorrhage) on fundoscopy

The investigation pathway is: **non-contrast CT** → if negative, **LP at 12 h** (xanthochromia) → **CTA** → **DSA** if needed. See: [[Clinical features and presentation of SAH]].

## Viva Questions
**Q1. Investigation pathway for suspected SAH with negative CT?**
> LP at 12 hours looking for xanthochromia. If positive → CTA → DSA. If still high suspicion and CT/LP negative → repeat CTA or DSA.

**Q2. Why does xanthochromia take 12 hours to develop?**
> Bilirubin is produced by in-vivo breakdown of red cells (oxyhaemoglobin → bilirubin). A tap too early (< 12 h) is unhelpful. Spectrophotometry is the gold standard for xanthochromia.

**Q3. Modified Fisher scale and its clinical use.**
> Quantifies blood load on CT. 0 = no SAH/IVH; 1 = thin SAH; 2 = thin SAH + IVH; 3 = thick SAH; 4 = thick SAH + IVH. Grade 3-4 have high risk of symptomatic vasospasm (~33-40%).

**Q4. When is DSA indicated even after negative CTA?**
> When CT shows classic perimesencephalic pattern (PNSAH) to definitively exclude aneurysm, OR when CT shows non-perimesencephalic SAH but CTA negative (to exclude small aneurysm < 3 mm).

**Q5. ECG changes in SAH and their significance.**
> ST elevation/depression, T-wave inversions (deep, 'cerebral T waves'), prolonged QT, arrhythmias. Troponin may be elevated (20-30%) but is non-specific. Indicates severity and risk of Takotsubo.

## Confusions & Mnemonics
- **Investigation and diagnosis of SAH** — see key points above
- **'ACoA → bitemporal hemianopia'** — Anterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses the optic chiasm from above
- **'PCoA → pupil-involving third'** — Posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III
- **'Triple-H = Euvolaemia + HTN only'** — Hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out
- **'CSW → salt + fluid; SIADH → fluid restrict'** — opposite treatments
- **'Day 7 = DCI peak'** — Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks day 7
