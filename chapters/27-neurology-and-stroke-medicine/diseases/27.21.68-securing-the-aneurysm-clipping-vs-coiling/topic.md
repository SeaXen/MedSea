## Definition

**Securing the aneurysm** is the definitive treatment of aneurysmal SAH — excluding the aneurysm from the circulation to prevent rebleeding. Two main techniques: endovascular coiling (preferred for posterior circulation, narrow neck) and surgical clipping (preferred for MCA bifurcation, wide neck, mass effect). Decision is MDT-led (ISAT 2002 supports coiling when both feasible).


# Securing the Aneurysm: Clipping vs Coiling

## Learning Objectives
- Describe endovascular coiling and surgical clipping.
- Apply the ISAT evidence to choose between modalities.
- Identify the time window for securing the aneurysm.
- List complications of each technique.

## Why "Securing" Is the First Step
- Rebleeding carries ~70 % mortality and is most likely in the first 24 h.
- Once the aneurysm is secured (excluded from circulation), rebleeding risk approaches zero.
- All other management is supportive until this is achieved.

## Timing
- **Aim for securing within 72 h** of the ictus (early securing).
- Delays beyond 72 h are associated with higher rebleeding risk and worse DCI outcomes.
- Patient must be haemodynamically stable (euvolaemia, BP controlled) before intervention.

## Two Main Techniques

### 1. Endovascular Coiling
- **How**: A catheter is advanced from the femoral artery through the aorta, into the carotid/vertebral, and into the aneurysm. Platinum coils are deployed into the aneurysm sac to induce thrombosis and exclude the aneurysm from circulation.
- **Indications**:
  - Posterior circulation aneurysms (basilar tip, vertebral).
  - Narrow neck.
  - Patients who are poor surgical candidates (elderly, comorbidities).
  - Patient preference.
- **Contraindications/cautions**:
  - Very wide neck (>4 mm or neck-to-dome ratio >1:2) — risk of coil herniation.
  - Large aneurysm with branch vessels arising from the dome.
  - Inability to access the aneurysm (tortuous vessels, severe atherosclerosis).
- **Complications**:
  - Thromboembolism (3–5 %).
  - Intra-procedural rupture (1–2 %).
  - Coil compaction or migration (long-term).
  - Aneurysm recurrence (10–20 % at 1 year → needs surveillance MRA/CTA).

### 2. Surgical Clipping
- **How**: A craniotomy is performed; the aneurysm neck is identified under microscopic vision and a titanium clip is applied across the neck.
- **Indications**:
  - **MCA bifurcation** aneurysms (where surgical access is excellent and branching vessels can be preserved).
  - Large or giant aneurysms.
  - Wide neck or branching from dome.
  - Failed coiling or coil compaction.
  - Mass effect (e.g., PCoA aneurysm with third nerve palsy).
- **Complications**:
  - Surgical trauma to brain (~3–5 %).
  - Vessel occlusion (1–3 %).
  - Infection, CSF leak, cranial nerve injury.
  - Residual aneurysm (rare with experienced surgeon).

## Evidence — ISAT Trial (2002, Lancet)
- **International Subarachnoid Aneurysm Trial** — RCT of coiling vs clipping in 2 143 patients with SAH where both techniques were deemed feasible.
- **Result**: 1-year dependency and death lower with coiling (23.7 % vs 30.6 %, ARR 6.9 %).
- **Caveat**: MCA aneurysms were under-represented in ISAT. Most neurovascular centres still prefer **clipping for MCA aneurysms**.
- **Long-term follow-up**: small increased rebleeding risk with coiling (1 per 901 patient-years) but offset by better functional outcome.

## Choosing Between Coiling and Clipping — MDT Decision
- Discussed at a **multidisciplinary team meeting** (MDT) involving interventional neuroradiologist, vascular neurosurgeon, and intensivist.
- Factors: aneurysm location, size, neck morphology, patient age, comorbidities, patient preference.
- Stent-assisted coiling and flow diversion (e.g., Pipeline embolisation device) for complex/uncoilable aneurysms.

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- "Which is better — coiling or clipping?" → **Coiling is generally preferred for posterior circulation and narrow-neck aneurysms**; **clipping for MCA bifurcation**.
- "What is the ISAT trial?" → RCT showing coiling has better 1-year outcome when both feasible.
- "When to secure the aneurysm?" → **Within 72 h**.

## Adjunct Techniques
- **Stent-assisted coiling** — for wide-neck aneurysms; needs dual antiplatelet therapy.
- **Flow diversion (Pipeline)** — for large/giant aneurysms; needs dual antiplatelet therapy.
- **WEB (Woven EndoBridge)** — for bifurcation aneurysms.
- **Bypass surgery** — for complex aneurysms where direct clipping is not possible.

## Post-Securing Surveillance
- **CTA or MRA** at 6 months, 1 year, then yearly for 5 years.
- Aneurysm recurrence or "compaction" may need retreatment.

## Clinical Features Driving Securing Decision

The clinical features that influence the choice of coiling vs clipping include:

- **Aneurysm location**: posterior circulation (basilar tip, vertebral) → coiling preferred; MCA bifurcation → clipping preferred
- **Aneurysm morphology**: wide neck, large size, branching from dome → clipping preferred
- **Patient factors**: age, comorbidities, surgical candidacy
- **Mass effect**: e.g., PCoA aneurysm with 3rd nerve palsy → may prefer clipping for decompression
- **MDT decision**: interventional neuroradiologist + vascular neurosurgeon + intensivist

See: [[Aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage]] for anatomy, [[Clinical features and presentation of SAH]] for clinical presentation.

## Viva Questions
**Q1. Coiling vs clipping — which is preferred?**
> Coiling is generally preferred for posterior circulation aneurysms (basilar tip, vertebral) and narrow-neck aneurysms. Clipping is preferred for MCA bifurcation aneurysms. Decision is MDT-led.

**Q2. Why is coiling preferred for posterior circulation?**
> Difficult surgical access; ISAT supports endovascular approach for posterior circulation; coiling has lower procedural morbidity.

**Q3. ISAT trial (2002) — main finding.**
> International Subarachnoid Aneurysm Trial — RCT of coiling vs clipping in 2,143 patients with SAH where both techniques were deemed feasible. 1-year dependency and death lower with coiling (23.7% vs 30.6%).

**Q4. Why is clipping preferred for MCA bifurcation?**
> MCA aneurysms have excellent surgical access; branching vessels can be preserved; ISAT under-represented MCA aneurysms; lower recurrence rate with clipping.

**Q5. Complications of coiling.**
> Thromboembolism (3-5%), intra-procedural rupture (1-2%), coil compaction or migration (long-term), aneurysm recurrence (10-20% at 1 year → needs surveillance MRA/CTA).

## Confusions & Mnemonics
- **Securing the aneurysm (clipping vs coiling)** — see key points above
- **'ACoA → bitemporal hemianopia'** — Anterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses the optic chiasm from above
- **'PCoA → pupil-involving third'** — Posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III
- **'Triple-H = Euvolaemia + HTN only'** — Hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out
- **'CSW → salt + fluid; SIADH → fluid restrict'** — opposite treatments
- **'Day 7 = DCI peak'** — Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks day 7
