# When to search for stroke mimics and alternative diagnoses

Related: [[../Stroke Medicine MOC|Stroke Medicine MOC]] · [[../Neuroimaging and Acute Evaluation|Neuroimaging and Acute Evaluation]] · [[Core acute workup|Core acute workup]] · [[../Stroke Recognition and Clinical Assessment/Stroke mimics and common pitfalls|Stroke mimics and common pitfalls]]

> [!important]
> Many patients with sudden neurological symptoms do have stroke, so the first duty is to **activate the stroke pathway quickly**. But certain features should actively trigger a search for **mimics or alternative diagnoses**, especially when the pattern is atypical, nonvascular, fluctuating without explanation, or unsupported by imaging.

## Definition
A **stroke mimic** is a condition presenting like acute stroke but caused by another neurological, metabolic, toxic, or systemic disorder.

## Why This Matters
- prevents inappropriate treatment when the syndrome is clearly nonvascular
- prompts correction of reversible conditions such as hypoglycaemia
- widens imaging or diagnostic evaluation when the story does not fit stroke
- reduces diagnostic anchoring error

## Golden Principle
> [!warning]
> Do **not** search for mimics so aggressively that you delay emergency stroke assessment in a truly time-sensitive patient.

The correct sequence is usually:
1. recognize possible stroke fast
2. check immediate reversibles such as glucose
3. perform urgent imaging
4. then expand the mimic search when features are inconsistent with stroke

## When to Suspect a Mimic
### History clues
- seizure at onset with post-ictal deficit
- migraine aura or headache-predominant evolving symptoms
- gradual march of symptoms rather than abrupt vascular onset
- recurrent stereotyped episodes without vascular explanation
- toxic exposure, sedative use, or intoxication
- fever or systemic illness suggesting infection/encephalopathy

### Examination clues
- nonanatomical weakness or sensory loss
- isolated reduced consciousness without focal pattern
- bilateral symptoms without plausible vascular localization
- prominent confusion or agitation out of proportion to focal findings
- positive functional signs
- isolated vertigo without focal neurological signs

### Imaging/workup clues
- normal CT/MRI despite persistent deficit when timing should have shown a lesion
- imaging pattern inconsistent with clinical syndrome
- laboratory abnormalities suggesting metabolic cause

## Common Stroke Mimics
- hypoglycaemia
- post-ictal Todd paresis
- migraine with aura
- functional neurological disorder
- peripheral vestibular disorder
- brain tumor or subdural hematoma
- encephalitis/meningitis
- demyelination
- toxic-metabolic encephalopathy

## Practical Mimic Search Framework
### Step 1: Rapid bedside checks
- glucose
- temperature and oxygenation
- medication/recent toxin history
- witness account of seizure or syncope

### Step 2: Reassess stroke pattern
Ask:
- does the deficit fit a vascular territory?
- are there cortical, brainstem, or lacunar clues?
- is onset sudden or strangely progressive/fluctuating?

### Step 3: Review imaging and labs
- hemorrhage, tumor, subdural, or other structural lesion?
- major metabolic abnormality?
- CTA/MRI supporting or weakening vascular hypothesis?

### Step 4: Expand differentials only when needed
- MRI for atypical deficits
- EEG when seizure is strongly suspected
- infection or inflammatory workup when clinically indicated

## High-Yield Differentiating Clues
| Mimic | Clues favoring mimic |
|---|---|
| Hypoglycaemia | sweating, altered behavior, rapid reversal with glucose |
| Todd paresis | witnessed seizure, post-ictal confusion, transient weakness |
| Migraine aura | spreading positive symptoms, headache history, younger patient |
| Functional disorder | inconsistency, distractibility, nonanatomical pattern |
| Peripheral vertigo | isolated dizziness without focal deficits |
| Encephalitis | fever, altered behavior, seizures, diffuse encephalopathy |

## Management Relevance
Searching for mimics is not about rejecting stroke prematurely. It is about:
- correcting immediate reversible causes
- choosing additional investigations intelligently
- avoiding unsafe anchoring when the pattern is atypical
- recognizing that mimic and true stroke can coexist in complex patients

## Red Flags for Alternative Diagnoses
- fever + focal deficit + confusion
- seizure at onset with persistent post-ictal state
- severe metabolic derangement
- headache with meningism
- subacute progression over days rather than abrupt onset
- inconsistent examination across repeated assessments

## Clinical Features that Prompt Mimic Search

The detailed clinical clues are above in "When to Suspect a Mimic" and "High-Yield Differentiating Clues". This wrapper groups those clues with the standard audit heading:

- **Gradual onset** (vs sudden in stroke)
- **Positive features** (scintillating scotomata, sensory march — migraine)
- **Tongue biting, incontinence, witnessed convulsion** (seizure with Todd's paresis)
- **Prodromal lightheadedness, pallor, sweating** (syncope)
- **Low blood glucose** (hypoglycaemia — can mimic any stroke pattern)
- **Inconsistent exam, give-way weakness, Hoover's sign** (functional)
- **No cortical signs** (no aphasia, neglect, hemianopia)
- **Symptoms don't fit vascular territory**

## Clinical Investigation Strategy

Always check capillary glucose, ECG, and review medications (especially anticoagulants). If atypical features, consider CT/MRI, EEG, LP, toxicology.


## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- **Hypoglycaemia is the most important immediately reversible mimic.**
- **Todd paresis** is a classic mimic after a witnessed seizure.
- Stroke usually has **sudden onset with vascular localization**; mimics often show nonvascular or inconsistent patterns.
- Atypical symptoms should broaden the differential, but **must not delay urgent CT and reperfusion consideration** in genuine hyperacute stroke.

## Common Exam Traps
- assuming all sudden weakness is stroke without checking glucose
- dismissing true stroke because the patient also has headache or confusion
- confusing isolated peripheral vertigo with posterior circulation stroke, or vice versa
- labeling a patient “functional” without proper anatomical examination

## One-Page Revision Summary
- Think stroke first, but keep mimics in mind when the story is atypical.
- Search actively for mimics when onset, examination, imaging, or labs do not fit a vascular event.
- Highest-yield mimics: hypoglycaemia, seizure/Todd paresis, migraine, functional disorder, peripheral vertigo, infection, tumor/subdural.
- The key safety rule is: **do not let mimic-hunting delay urgent stroke pathway steps**.

## Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
### Must Know
- hypoglycaemia check
- Todd paresis and migraine as classic mimics
- atypical/nonvascular patterns should trigger broader search

### Should Know
- how to use imaging/labs to challenge anchoring bias
- distinction between isolated vertigo and focal posterior circulation signs

### Nice to Know
- true stroke and mimic-like confounders can coexist

## Answer Key with Explanations
- The highest-yield mimic check is immediate **glucose testing**.
- Mimic search should be triggered by nonvascular patterns, inconsistency, unexplained fluctuation, or discordant imaging.
- The key clinical pearl is balance: stay open-minded without sacrificing time-critical stroke treatment.
