# Aneurysmal Subarachnoid Haemorrhage

## Learning Objectives
- Describe the pathology of berry (saccular) aneurysms.
- Identify the common sites of aneurysm formation.
- List conditions associated with berry aneurysm formation.
- Recognise the time course of rebleeding and vasospasm.

## Definition
- **Aneurysmal SAH** = spontaneous SAH due to rupture of a saccular ("berry") intracranial aneurysm.
- Accounts for ~85 % of spontaneous SAH.

## Pathology
- Saccular aneurysms arise at arterial bifurcations where the muscular media is congenitally deficient.
- Wall consists only of intima and adventitia — prone to rupture when diameter >5–7 mm.
- Larger aneurysms (>7 mm) and those with irregular morphology have higher rupture risk.

## Common Sites (in order of frequency)
- **Anterior communicating artery (ACoA)** — most common site; ~30–35 %.
- **Posterior communicating artery (PCoA)** junction with ICA — ~25–30 %; classically produces a painful third-nerve palsy.
- **Middle cerebral artery (MCA) bifurcation** — ~20 %.
- **Basilar tip / posterior circulation** — ~5–10 %.
- Multiple aneurysms in 20–30 % of patients.

## Associated Conditions
- **ADPKD** (autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease) — 5–10 % of patients with SAH.
- **Ehlers–Danlos syndrome type IV** (vascular type).
- **Marfan syndrome**, **coarctation of the aorta**, **fibromuscular dysplasia**.
- Family history of SAH (≥2 first-degree relatives).
- Smoking, hypertension, chronic alcohol excess.

## Clinical Clues by Site
- **ACoA aneurysm**: bitemporal hemianopia from chiasmal compression, frontal lobe signs, abulia.
- **PCoA aneurysm**: painful third-nerve palsy (pupil-involving, "surgical" third) → fixed dilated pupil + ptosis + down-and-out eye.
- **MCA bifurcation**: contralateral face/arm weakness, expressive aphasia (dominant).
- **Basilar tip**: oculomotor palsy, locked-in syndrome, decreased consciousness.

## Time Course of Complications
- **Rebleeding**: peak 0–24 h (~4 % risk), then ~1.5 %/day for 4 weeks. Highest single risk factor for mortality.
- **Hydrocephalus**: acute (within 24 h, due to blood in ventricles) or chronic (after 2 weeks, due to arachnoid granulation fibrosis).
- **Delayed cerebral ischaemia (DCI)**: days 4–14, peaks day 7. Historically called "vasospasm" but mechanism involves microthrombosis, cortical spreading depolarisations, and macrovasospasm.
- **Seizures**: 5–10 %, more common with intraparenchymal extension.
- **Hyponatraemia**: from cerebral salt wasting (CSW) or SIADH — common, daily U&Es mandatory.

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- Test: which artery is the most common site of berry aneurysm? → **ACoA**.
- Test: pupil-involving third-nerve palsy → posterior communicating artery aneurysm.
- Test: prophylaxis against vasospasm → oral nimodipine 60 mg 4-hourly for 21 days.

## Clinical Features by Aneurysm Site

Aneurysmal SAH presents with sudden 'thunderclap' headache (covered in [[Clinical features and presentation of SAH]]). The clinical features that LOCALISE the aneurysm include:

- **ACoA aneurysm**: bitemporal hemianopia (chiasmal compression), frontal lobe signs (abulia, confusion)
- **PCoA aneurysm**: painful pupil-involving 3rd nerve palsy ('surgical third' — pupil fixed/dilated, eye down-and-out, ptosis)
- **MCA bifurcation**: contralateral face/arm weakness, expressive aphasia (dominant)
- **Basilar tip**: oculomotor palsy, locked-in syndrome, decreased consciousness
- **Vertebral / PICA**: lateral medullary syndrome (Wallenberg) — see [[Lateral medullary syndrome]]

Multiple aneurysms are found in 20-30% of SAH patients.


## Clinical Investigation and Management Summary

The detailed investigation and management pathways for aneurysmal SAH are covered in the dedicated sub-files. This wrapper provides the cross-reference for the standard audit section.

### Investigation Pathway
- **Non-contrast CT** (within 6 h, sensitivity ~98-100%) — first investigation
- **CTA** immediately after SAH confirmed — identifies aneurysm
- **DSA** if CTA negative (gold standard)
- **Modified Fisher scale** — predicts symptomatic vasospasm risk
- See: [[Investigation and diagnosis of SAH]]

### Management Principles
- **Secure aneurysm within 72 h** (coiling or clipping, MDT decision)
- **Nimodipine 60 mg PO 4-hourly × 21 days** (DCI prophylaxis)
- **BP control** (SBP < 160 pre-securing, liberalise post-securing)
- **Isotonic IV fluids** (avoid hypotonic)
- See: [[Acute medical management of SAH]], [[Securing the aneurysm (clipping vs coiling)]]

### Time Course of Complications (key to exam)
- **Rebleeding**: peak 0-24 h
- **Hydrocephalus**: acute (< 24 h) or chronic (2-4 weeks)
- **DCI**: peak day 7 (range 4-14)
- See: [[Delayed cerebral ischaemia and vasospasm]], [[Hydrocephalus after SAH]], [[Other complications of SAH]]

## Viva Questions
**Q1. Most common site of berry aneurysm?**
> Anterior communicating artery (ACoA) — ~30-35%

**Q2. Classic clinical sign of PCoA aneurysm?**
> Painful pupil-involving third nerve palsy (CN III compression at the PCoA-ICA junction)

**Q3. Why is coiling preferred for posterior circulation aneurysms?**
> Difficult surgical access; ISAT supports endovascular approach for posterior circulation; coiling has lower procedural morbidity

**Q4. Why is clipping preferred for MCA bifurcation?**
> MCA aneurysms have excellent surgical access; branches can be preserved; ISAT under-represented MCA aneurysms

**Q5. Risk factors for berry aneurysm formation?**
> Hypertension, smoking, alcohol, family history (≥2 first-degree relatives), ADPKD, Ehlers-Danlos type IV, Marfan, coarctation of aorta, fibromuscular dysplasia

## Confusions & Mnemonics
- **Aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage** — see key points above
- **'ACoA → bitemporal hemianopia'** — Anterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses the optic chiasm from above
- **'PCoA → pupil-involving third'** — Posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III
- **'Triple-H = Euvolaemia + HTN only'** — Hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out
- **'CSW → salt + fluid; SIADH → fluid restrict'** — opposite treatments
- **'Day 7 = DCI peak'** — Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks day 7
