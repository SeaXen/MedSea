# Anterior vs posterior circulation stroke clues

Related: [[../Stroke Medicine MOC|Stroke Medicine MOC]] · [[../Stroke Recognition and Clinical Assessment|Stroke Recognition and Clinical Assessment]] · [[Localization and vascular territory clues|Localization and vascular territory clues]] · [[Sudden focal neurological deficit recognition]]

> [!important]
> Early bedside localization helps clinicians recognize stroke quickly, anticipate airway and disability risks, and select urgent vascular imaging or reperfusion pathways.

## Learning Objectives
- Distinguish anterior from posterior circulation stroke at the bedside.
- Link key symptoms to likely vascular territory.
- Recognize why posterior circulation stroke is often missed.
- Use localization clues to guide urgency and imaging strategy.

## Definition
**Anterior circulation stroke** involves territories supplied mainly by the **internal carotid system**: ophthalmic artery, anterior cerebral artery (ACA), middle cerebral artery (MCA), and their branches.

**Posterior circulation stroke** involves the **vertebrobasilar system**: vertebral arteries, basilar artery, posterior cerebral arteries (PCA), and cerebellar branches.

## Relevant Anatomy
## Anterior circulation
- Internal carotid artery
- ACA
- MCA
- lenticulostriate perforators

Main structures affected:
- frontal, parietal, lateral temporal cortex
- internal capsule
- basal ganglia
- retina/optic pathways in some cases

## Posterior circulation
- vertebral arteries
- basilar artery
- PCA
- PICA, AICA, SCA

Main structures affected:
- brainstem
- cerebellum
- thalamus
- occipital lobes
- medial temporal/visual pathways

## Why the distinction matters
- anterior circulation strokes often produce obvious unilateral cortical deficits
- posterior circulation strokes may present with **dizziness, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, ataxia, crossed signs, reduced consciousness**, and may be under-recognized
- basilar artery occlusion can be catastrophic but initially subtle

## Pathophysiology
Symptoms depend on:
- which artery is occluded
- size of infarct
- cortical vs perforator involvement
- collateral circulation
- involvement of long tracts, cranial nerve nuclei, cerebellum, thalamus, or visual cortex

## Anterior Circulation Stroke Clues
### Typical cortical signs
- aphasia (dominant hemisphere)
- neglect/inattention (usually nondominant hemisphere)
- gaze deviation toward lesion
- visual field defect, especially homonymous hemianopia
- cortical sensory loss
- apraxia

### Typical motor/sensory pattern
- contralateral face and arm weakness often greater than leg in MCA stroke
- contralateral sensory loss
- leg-predominant weakness in ACA stroke

### Ocular clues
- amaurosis fugax or monocular visual loss may suggest carotid/retinal ischemia
- gaze preference in large hemispheric stroke

## Posterior Circulation Stroke Clues
### Brainstem/cerebellar clues
- vertigo or acute dizziness with focal neurological signs
- diplopia
- dysarthria
- dysphagia
- nystagmus
- ataxia
- limb incoordination
- hiccups in medullary syndromes
- crossed findings: ipsilateral cranial nerve signs with contralateral limb weakness/sensory loss

### Occipital/thalamic clues
- isolated homonymous hemianopia may occur in PCA stroke
- cortical blindness
- memory disturbance in some PCA syndromes
- altered consciousness if thalamic or basilar involvement

## Classical Clinical Pattern Comparison
| Feature | Anterior circulation | Posterior circulation |
|---|---|---|
| Language deficit | Common in dominant hemisphere stroke | Uncommon except complex higher dysfunction |
| Neglect | Common in nondominant cortical stroke | Uncommon |
| Face-arm weakness | Common | Variable |
| Leg-predominant weakness | ACA pattern | Less typical |
| Vertigo | Uncommon as dominant feature | Common |
| Diplopia | Uncommon | Suggestive |
| Dysphagia/dysarthria | Can occur, but less localizing | Common in brainstem stroke |
| Ataxia | Less prominent | Common |
| Reduced consciousness | Large hemispheric stroke or edema | Basilar/thalamic/brainstem involvement |
| Visual syndrome | Field defect with cortical signs | Occipital visual symptoms may dominate |

## Bedside Localization Approach
1. Confirm this is an acute focal syndrome.
2. Ask if there are **cortical signs**: aphasia, neglect, gaze deviation.
3. Look for **brainstem/cerebellar signs**: diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, nystagmus, ataxia.
4. Assess pattern of weakness:
   - face-arm > leg -> MCA more likely
   - leg > face-arm -> ACA possible
   - pure long-tract signs without cortical deficits -> consider lacunar/posterior pathways depending on exam
5. Check visual symptoms:
   - monocular loss -> carotid/retinal territory
   - hemianopia with aphasia/neglect -> anterior cortical
   - isolated hemianopia -> PCA possible

## Important Posterior Circulation Warning Signs
- acute vertigo with new focal neurological signs
- new diplopia plus ataxia
- dysphagia with brainstem signs
- fluctuating reduced consciousness
- quadriparesis or evolving locked-in picture
- severe occipital headache/neck pain with vertebral dissection context

> [!warning]
> Posterior circulation stroke is frequently underdiagnosed because symptoms may be mislabeled as peripheral vertigo, intoxication, GI illness, or nonspecific dizziness.

## Investigations
### Immediate imaging
- non-contrast CT head to exclude haemorrhage
- CTA / MRA when large-vessel occlusion or posterior circulation stroke suspected
- MRI diffusion-weighted imaging may help when CT is initially nondiagnostic, especially in posterior fossa

### Additional tests
- ECG, glucose, basic labs
- vascular imaging of carotids for anterior circulation disease when relevant
- cardiac workup when embolic pattern suspected

## Diagnosis
Localization remains clinical, but confirmation comes from neuroimaging.

A likely **anterior circulation stroke** is suggested by:
- unilateral weakness/sensory loss + aphasia/neglect/gaze deviation/cortical signs

A likely **posterior circulation stroke** is suggested by:
- vertigo/diplopia/dysarthria/dysphagia/ataxia/cranial nerve signs/crossed findings/occipital visual syndromes

## Differential Diagnosis
### Anterior circulation mimics
- seizure with Todd paresis
- hypoglycaemia
- migraine aura
- functional neurological disorder

### Posterior circulation mimics
- peripheral vestibular disease
- intoxication
- demyelination
- Wernicke encephalopathy
- brainstem encephalitis

## Management Relevance
Localization helps determine:
- urgency of vessel imaging
- suspicion of large-vessel occlusion
- airway risk from bulbar dysfunction
- need for dysphagia precautions
- need to watch for cerebellar edema or brainstem compression

### Practical consequences
- posterior circulation stroke with bulbar signs -> NPO until screened, close airway monitoring
- suspected basilar occlusion -> urgent reperfusion pathway consideration
- large anterior circulation syndrome -> edema monitoring and reperfusion pathway assessment

## Complications
### Anterior circulation
- malignant MCA edema
- aphasia-related disability
- neglect and falls
- seizures in cortical infarction

### Posterior circulation
- aspiration due to dysphagia
- respiratory failure with bulbar involvement
- obstructive hydrocephalus/cerebellar swelling
- locked-in syndrome in basilar occlusion

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- Aphasia and neglect strongly suggest **anterior cortical** stroke.
- Vertigo alone is not enough, but vertigo with **diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, limb ataxia, or focal weakness** should trigger concern for posterior circulation stroke.
- Brainstem lesions give **crossed signs**.
- PCA stroke may present mainly with visual deficits.
- Posterior fossa stroke may be less obvious on early CT than supratentorial stroke.

## Common Exam Traps
- Calling posterior circulation stroke “labyrinthitis” when focal signs are present.
- Forgetting that dysphagia/diplopia/localizing cranial nerve deficits point to brainstem disease.
- Assuming all hemiparesis is anterior circulation.
- Missing isolated visual cortex syndromes in PCA stroke.

## Mnemonic
**Posterior = 5 D's + 3 N's + A**
- Diplopia
- Dysarthria
- Dysphagia
- Dizziness
- Drop in consciousness
- Nystagmus
- Nausea/vomiting
- Numbness/negative sensory change
- Ataxia

## One-Page Revision Summary
- Anterior circulation = carotid system -> MCA/ACA cortical syndromes.
- Think **anterior** when there is aphasia, neglect, gaze deviation, face-arm weakness, cortical sensory loss.
- Think **posterior** when there is vertigo with focal signs, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, ataxia, crossed findings, visual cortex syndrome, or reduced consciousness.
- Posterior circulation stroke is easily missed and may need CTA/MRA even when CT looks unrevealing.
- Localization is clinically useful for airway planning, dysphagia precautions, and reperfusion decisions.

## Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
### Must Know
- cortical signs -> anterior
- brainstem/cerebellar signs -> posterior
- posterior circulation strokes are commonly missed

### Should Know
- ACA vs MCA patterns
- PCA visual syndromes
- crossed brainstem signs

### Nice to Know
- named brainstem/cerebellar arterial syndromes in detail

## Flashcards
- Q: Main arterial system in anterior circulation stroke?  
  A: Internal carotid system.
- Q: Two strong cortical signs of anterior stroke?  
  A: Aphasia and neglect.
- Q: Three posterior circulation clues?  
  A: Diplopia, dysphagia, ataxia.
- Q: What are crossed signs?  
  A: Ipsilateral cranial nerve deficits with contralateral long-tract findings.
- Q: Which posterior vessel syndrome may present with isolated visual loss/hemianopia?  
  A: PCA stroke.
- Q: Why can posterior circulation stroke be missed?  
  A: Symptoms mimic benign dizziness/vertigo and CT may be less revealing early.
- Q: Face-arm more than leg weakness suggests which territory?  
  A: MCA.
- Q: Leg-predominant weakness suggests which territory?  
  A: ACA.
