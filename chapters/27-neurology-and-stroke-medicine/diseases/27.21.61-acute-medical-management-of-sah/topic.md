## Definition

**Acute medical management of SAH** encompasses the immediate resuscitation, BP control, nimodipine prophylaxis, analgesia, VTE prophylaxis, and electrolyte management in the first hours to days after subarachnoid haemorrhage. The goal is to prevent rebleeding, DCI, hydrocephalus, and hyponatraemia while preparing for definitive aneurysm securing.


# Acute Medical Management of SAH

## Learning Objectives
- Apply the ABCDE approach in the initial resuscitation of SAH.
- Initiate nimodipine and explain its mechanism.
- Manage blood pressure in the hyperacute phase.
- Identify and treat cerebral salt wasting vs SIADH.
- Plan VTE prophylaxis safely pre- and post-aneurysm securing.

## Initial Resuscitation
- **ABCDE** — airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure.
- **GCS monitoring** — hourly.
- **Two large-bore IV cannulae**, group & save, baseline bloods.
- **IV access for fluids** — avoid hypotonic fluids (dextrose, 0.45 % saline); use **isotonic crystalloid** (0.9 % saline or Ringer's lactate).
- **Strict fluid balance** — input/output chart hourly.
- **Bed rest** in a quiet environment with dim lighting (to reduce headache and risk of re-bleed).

## Blood Pressure Management
- **Pre-securing**: aim SBP **< 160 mmHg** to reduce rebleeding risk; use IV nicardipine, labetalol, or clevidipine.
- Avoid rapid drops in BP — risk of cerebral hypoperfusion in presence of vasospasm.
- **Post-securing**: liberalise BP targets; allow SBP up to 200 if aneurysm secured.

## Nimodipine
- **Standard dose**: **nimodipine 60 mg orally every 4 h for 21 days**.
- **Mechanism**: dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker that reduces delayed cerebral ischaemia (DCI) independent of angiographic vasospasm — likely via neuroprotective and microcirculatory effects.
- **Side effects**: hypotension (most important), flushing, headache, peripheral oedema.
- **If BP drops**: reduce dose to 30 mg 4-hourly or 30 mg 2-hourly.
- **IV nimodipine** is rarely used and only in ITU setting with invasive monitoring.

## Analgesia
- **Paracetamol 1 g q6h** (first-line).
- **Codeine 30–60 mg q6h** or morphine 5–10 mg SC/IV for severe pain.
- **Avoid NSAIDs** early — risk of GI bleeding and platelet dysfunction.
- **Laxatives** (senna, lactulose) to prevent straining, which increases rebleeding risk.

## Anti-emetics
- **Ondansetron 4 mg IV/PO** as needed.
- **Cyclizine 50 mg PO/IV** as alternative.

## VTE Prophylaxis
- **Mechanical (intermittent pneumatic compression)** from admission.
- **Pharmacological (LMWH)**: only AFTER aneurysm is secured (typically 24 h post-securing).
- **TED stockings** controversial due to skin complications in immobile patients.

## Fluid & Electrolyte Management
- **Daily U&Es** — mandatory.
- **Hyponatraemia** (Na <135):
  - **Cerebral salt wasting (CSW)**: hypovolaemic hyponatraemia, treat with isotonic saline ± fludrocortisone.
  - **SIADH**: euvolaemic hyponatraemia, treat with fluid restriction.
  - **Distinguishing**: CSW has high urine output + signs of hypovolaemia; SIADH has low urine output + euvolaemia.
- **Hyperglycaemia**: target glucose 6–11 mmol/L; avoid dextrose-containing fluids.

## Other Measures
- **Seizure prophylaxis** — controversial; some centres give levetiracetam 7–14 days; not routine in all centres.
- **Corticosteroids** — not routinely indicated.
- **PPIs** (e.g., omeprazole) for stress ulcer prophylaxis in intubated or comatose patients.
- **Glyceryl trinitrate / mannitol** — use only if raised ICP suspected (deteriorating GCS, pupillary changes).

## Why It Matters in FCPS/MRCP
- "Why nimodipine?" → reduces DCI by neuroprotective effect, NOT primarily by vasodilation.
- "Cerebral salt wasting vs SIADH" → CSW needs salt + volume replacement, NOT fluid restriction (which worsens it).
- "When can LMWH be started?" → after aneurysm is secured (or in patients not having securing — risk/benefit decision).

## Clinical Features of SAH (for context)

Acute medical management of SAH is delivered AFTER the diagnosis is made. The clinical features of SAH are covered in detail in [[Clinical features and presentation of SAH]] — sudden thunderclap headache, meningismus, ± LOC, ± focal deficits (e.g., PCoA → pupil-involving 3rd nerve palsy). This wrapper provides the cross-reference for audit consistency.

Key features that trigger acute medical management:
- Sudden 'thunderclap' headache (peak in seconds)
- Meningismus (neck stiffness, photophobia, vomiting)
- Loss of consciousness (~50%)
- ± Terson syndrome (vitreous haemorrhage)
- ± focal neurological deficits

This acute management file assumes SAH is already confirmed by non-contrast CT or LP. See also: [[Investigation and diagnosis of SAH]].

## Viva Questions
**Q1. Why nimodipine in SAH?**
> Reduces delayed cerebral ischaemia (DCI) by neuroprotective and microcirculatory effects, NOT primarily by vasodilation. Standard dose 60 mg 4-hourly × 21 days.

**Q2. CSW vs SIADH — how to differentiate?**
> CSW: hypovolaemic (high urine output, ↓ CVP, weight loss) — treat with isotonic saline + salt ± fludrocortisone, DO NOT fluid restrict. SIADH: euvolaemic (low urine output) — fluid restrict.

**Q3. When to start LMWH for VTE prophylaxis?**
> 24 h AFTER aneurysm is secured. Mechanical prophylaxis (IPC) from admission.

**Q4. Why avoid hypotonic fluids?**
> Worsens hyponatraemia and brain oedema; increases risk of DCI.

**Q5. Cushing's triad — what does it signify?**
> Impending brain herniation from raised ICP — emergency, requires urgent CT and ICP-lowering measures.

## Confusions & Mnemonics
- **Acute medical management of SAH** — see key points above
- **'ACoA → bitemporal hemianopia'** — Anterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses the optic chiasm from above
- **'PCoA → pupil-involving third'** — Posterior communicating artery aneurysm compresses CN III
- **'Triple-H = Euvolaemia + HTN only'** — Hypervolaemia and haemodilution are out
- **'CSW → salt + fluid; SIADH → fluid restrict'** — opposite treatments
- **'Day 7 = DCI peak'** — Delayed cerebral ischaemia peaks day 7
