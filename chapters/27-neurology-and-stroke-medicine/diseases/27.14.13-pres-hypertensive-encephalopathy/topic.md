# Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome (PRES) & Hypertensive Encephalopathy

> **High-Yield Definition:** PRES: reversible subcortical vasogenic oedema, predominantly parieto-occipital, triggered by hypertension, eclampsia, immunosuppressants, cytotoxic drugs. Hypertensive encephalopathy: severe hypertension causing encephalopathy with end-organ damage.

## PRES (Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome)

### Definition
- Reversible subcortical vasogenic oedema, predominantly parieto-occipital
- Triggered by: hypertension, eclampsia/preeclampsia, immunosuppressive drugs (cyclosporine, tacrolimus, sirolimus), cytotoxic drugs (cytarabine, gemcitabine, methotrexate, vincristine, cisplatin), autoimmune disease (SLE, vasculitis), renal failure, hypomagnesaemia, HIV
- Often reversible with treatment of underlying cause

### Epidemiology
- All ages, F>M
- Associated with: HTN, eclampsia (20% of eclamptic women), renal failure, immunosuppressive therapy, autoimmune

### Pathophysiology
- Failure of cerebral autoregulation due to severe hypertension
- Endothelial dysfunction, blood-brain barrier breakdown
- Vasogenic oedema (vs cytotoxic in ischaemia)
- Predominantly posterior (vertebrobasilar territory has less sympathetic innervation)

### Clinical Features
- **Headache** (most common, severe)
- **Visual disturbances**: cortical blindness, hemianopia, neglect
- **Altered consciousness**: confusion, lethargy, stupor, coma
- **Seizures** (often, generalised, focal, status)
- **Visual symptoms** (often dramatic): homonymous hemianopia, cortical blindness, Anton syndrome
- Focal neurological deficits (uncommon)
- May overlap with hypertensive encephalopathy

### Investigations
- **MRI brain**: vasogenic oedema, FLAIR hyperintensity, predominantly parieto-occipital, may involve frontal, cerebellar, brainstem
- CT may show hypodensity
- **CTA/MRA** (exclude arterial occlusion, vasospasm, RCVS)
- **Blood pressure** (often severely elevated, but may be normal if triggered by drugs)
- Renal function (CKD may be trigger)
- **Exclude**: posterior circulation stroke, venous thrombosis, RCVS, encephalitis, metabolic

### Management
- **Treat underlying cause**:
  - **Hypertension**: controlled reduction (avoid rapid drops - 25% in first hour, then gradual)
  - **Eclampsia**: delivery, magnesium sulphate, BP control
  - **Immunosuppressants/cytotoxics**: stop or reduce
  - **Autoimmune**: immunosuppression
  - **Renal failure**: dialysis
- **BP control**:
  - Labetalol, nicardipine, GTN, sodium nitroprusside
  - Target: MAP -25% in first hour, then <160/100 over 24 hours
  - Avoid rapid correction (watershed ischaemia)
- **Seizure control**: levetiracetam (preferred, no interactions)
- **Oedema**: mannitol, hypertonic saline (if severe)
- **Multidisciplinary**: neurology, intensive care, nephrology, obstetrics (eclampsia), pharmacology
- **Reversibility**: usually complete within days-weeks with treatment
- **Monitoring**: BP, neurological, MRI, renal, drug levels

## Hypertensive Encephalopathy

### Definition
- Acute neurological syndrome from severe hypertension (usually >180/120) exceeding cerebral autoregulation
- Causes encephalopathy with other end-organ damage (kidneys, eyes, heart)
- Reversible with prompt BP control

### Pathophysiology
- Failure of cerebral autoregulation (chronic hypertension shifts upper limit right)
- Acute severe hypertension → forced vasodilation → BBB disruption → vasogenic oedema
- End-organ damage: hypertensive retinopathy, nephropathy, encephalopathy, heart failure, aortic dissection

### Clinical Features
- **Headache** (severe)
- **Visual disturbance**: papilloedema, haemorrhages, exudates, cotton wool spots, papilloedema
- **Altered consciousness**: confusion, lethargy, stupor, coma
- **Seizures** (generalised)
- **Focal deficits** (uncommon)
- Nausea, vomiting
- Visual (blurred, loss)
- End-organ: chest pain (MI, dissection), dyspnoea (pulmonary oedema), oliguria (AKI)

### Investigations
- **BP measurement** (both arms)
- ECG, cardiac enzymes (MI, LVH, strain)
- Chest X-ray (aortic dissection, pulmonary oedema)
- **Fundoscopy** (papilloedema, haemorrhages, exudates)
- **Renal function** (AKI, CKD), urinalysis (proteinuria, haematuria)
- **CT/MRI brain**: vasogenic oedema (PRES), exclude haemorrhage, ischaemia
- Consider: aortic dissection (CT aortogram), renal artery stenosis (renin, imaging), phaeochromocytoma (catecholamines), Cushing's, primary hyperaldosteronism

### Management
- **EMERGENCY**: lower BP carefully
- **Goal**: reduce MAP by 20-25% in first hour, then gradually to 160/100 over next 24-48 hours, then to target <130/80 over weeks
- **Avoid**: rapid correction (watershed ischaemia, blindness)
- **IV agents**:
  - **Labetalol** (alpha + beta-blocker): 10-20mg bolus, infusion 1-2mg/min
  - **Nicardipine** (CCB): infusion 5-15mg/h
  - **Esmolol** (short-acting beta-blocker): bolus + infusion
  - **GTN** (nitrate): 5-200μg/min
  - **Sodium nitroprusside** (arterial + venous): 0.25-10μg/kg/min (limit cyanide toxicity)
  - **Fenoldopam** (D1 agonist)
- **Oral agents** (after IV stabilisation): ACEi, ARB, CCB, beta-blocker, thiazide
- **Specific**:
  - **Eclampsia**: delivery, magnesium sulphate 4g IV loading, then 1-2g/h
  - **Phaeochromocytoma**: phentolamine (alpha-blockade first), then beta-blocker, then surgery
  - **Aortic dissection**: beta-blocker first (esmolol, labetalol), then vasodilator (need to reduce shear stress), urgent surgery
  - **Pulmonary oedema**: oxygen, diuretics, vasodilators
  - **Renal failure**: ACEi/ARB cautiously, may need dialysis
- **Multidisciplinary**: emergency, ICU, neurology, cardiology, nephrology, ophthalmology, obstetrics (eclampsia), vascular surgery
- **Monitor**: BP (continuous initially), ECG, urine output, neurological, vision, renal, cardiac

## Red Flags
- Severe hypertension (BP >180/120) with end-organ damage
- Eclampsia, preeclampsia
- Immunosuppressant/cytotoxic drug exposure
- Stroke mimic (often misdiagnosed)
- PRES may progress to seizure, status, coma, death if untreated
- Hypertensive emergency can be fatal (MI, dissection, pulmonary oedema, AKI)
- Reversible if treated promptly
- Avoid rapid BP reduction (watershed ischaemia)

## Prognosis
- **PRES**: usually reversible within days-weeks with treatment
  - 70-90% complete recovery
  - 10-30% residual deficits (cognitive, visual)
  - Recurrence with re-exposure to trigger
- **Hypertensive encephalopathy**:
  - Reversible with prompt BP control
  - Outcome depends on end-organ damage
  - Cardiovascular, renal, CNS most important
  - 5-year survival: depends on BP control, end-organ damage
- Multidisciplinary care essential
- Long-term: BP control, monitor end-organ, lifestyle, avoid triggers
