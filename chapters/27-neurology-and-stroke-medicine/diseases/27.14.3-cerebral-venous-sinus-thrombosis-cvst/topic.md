# Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis (CVST)

> **High-Yield Definition:** CVST: thrombosis of intracranial venous sinuses (superior sagittal, transverse, sigmoid, cavernous, straight) and/or cortical veins. Causes venous infarction (often with haemorrhage), raised ICP. Risk: OCP, pregnancy, dehydration, thrombophilia, infection.

## Definition
- Thrombosis of intracranial dural venous sinuses and/or cortical veins
- Causes venous congestion, raised ICP, venous infarction (often haemorrhagic), seizures

## Epidemiology
- Incidence: 1-2/100,000/year (Western), higher in developing countries
- F:M 3:1
- Younger than arterial stroke (mean 30-40y, but all ages)
- Pregnancy/postpartum: peak risk

## Aetiology — Virchow's triad
- **Venous stasis**: dehydration, immobilisation, heart failure, obesity, mechanical (oral contraceptives)
- **Endothelial injury**: infection (sinusitis, otitis, mastoiditis - especially cavernous), trauma, surgery, inflammation (Behçet's, SLE, IBD)
- **Hypercoagulability**:
  - Pregnancy, postpartum (especially 3rd trimester - 6 weeks postpartum, hypercoagulable state)
  - Oral contraceptives (OCP)
  - Genetic thrombophilia: Factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, protein C/S deficiency, antithrombin deficiency
  - Antiphospholipid syndrome
  - Malignancy (paraneoplastic, occult)
  - Nephrotic syndrome
  - Dehydration (gastroenteritis, D&V, hot climates)
  - Infection (sepsis, meningitis, mastoiditis - otogenic)
  - Inflammatory (Behçet's, SLE, sarcoid, IBD)
  - Drugs (OCP, tamoxifen, hormone replacement, testosterone, EPO)
  - COVID-19 (vaccine-induced thrombotic thrombocytopenia - VITT, rare)

## Clinical Features
- **Headache** (most common, 90%, progressive over days, often severe, thunderclap-like if haemorrhagic, often mimics migraine, raised ICP)
- Seizures (40%, focal, secondary generalised, often multiple, status epilepticus more common than arterial stroke)
- Focal neurological deficit (variable, depends on location):
  - Superior sagittal sinus: bilateral motor, seizures
  - Transverse/sigmoid: temporal lobe (aphasia if dominant), seizures
  - **Cavernous sinus**: CN III, IV, V1, V2, VI palsy (ophthalmoplegia, V1/V2 sensory loss), proptosis, chemosis, often bilateral (septic), fever
  - Cortical vein: focal deficit, seizures
- **Raised ICP**: papilloedema, vomiting, visual loss (chronic)
- Encephalopathy, coma (severe, bilateral, posterior fossa)
- Septic CVST: fever, sepsis, signs of source (sinus, ear, mastoid, face)

## Investigations — EMERGENCY
- **CT head** (often normal early, may show hyperdense sinus - 'cord sign', 'dense triangle sign')
- **CT venography (CTV)**: filling defect in sinus
- **MR venography (MRV)**: gold standard, loss of flow void
- **MRI brain + DWI**: venous infarction (often bilateral, parasagittal, not respecting arterial territory, often haemorrhagic - on SWI/GRE)
- **D-dimer** (often elevated, but can be normal - sensitive but not specific)
- **Bloods**: FBC, coagulation, ESR, CRP, thrombophilia screen (Factor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, protein C/S, antithrombin, antiphospholipid, homocysteine), autoimmune (ANA, ENA, anti-dsDNA, antiphospholipid), complement, pregnancy test
- Exclude: infection (LP if safe, not if raised ICP), tumour, AVM

## Management — EMERGENCY
- **Anticoagulation** (KEY TREATMENT, even with haemorrhagic infarction):
  - **Heparin** (LMWH preferred over UFH in most cases - ISCVT trial)
  - Enoxaparin 1mg/kg BD (or 1.5mg/kg OD)
  - Despite haemorrhagic infarction (controversial but evidence supports benefit)
  - **Transition to warfarin** (target INR 2-3) or DOAC (rivaroxaban, apixaban - ongoing trials, increasingly used) for 3-12 months
  - **Duration**: 3-6 months if provoked, 6-12 months if unprovoked, lifelong if recurrent or severe thrombophilia
- **Seizure management**: levetiracetam (preferred, no interactions with anticoagulants), load if active seizure
- **Raised ICP**: head elevation, mannitol, hypertonic saline, acetazolamide, therapeutic LP (after anticoagulation established), EVD, surgical decompression
- **Treat underlying**:
  - Infection: antibiotics (cavernous sinus - IV ceftriaxone + metronidazole, surgical drainage)
  - Dehydration: rehydrate
  - OCP: stop
  - Pregnancy: LMWH throughout pregnancy and postpartum
  - Thrombophilia: long-term anticoagulation
- **Surgical**: thrombectomy (controversial, severe cases), EVD for hydrocephalus, decompressive craniectomy (malignant oedema)
- **Avoid**: OCP, dehydration, smoking
- Multidisciplinary: neurology, neurosurgery, haematology, ICU, neuroradiology, OT, PT, SLT, dietitian, palliative
- Monitor: neurological, MRI/MRV (3-6 months), coagulation, thrombophilia, pregnancy, haemorrhagic complications

## Red Flags
- EMERGENCY: rapid deterioration, status epilepticus, raised ICP, herniation, haemorrhagic infarction, coma, cavernous sinus (infection - sepsis, bilateral), cortical vein (focal deficit), pregnancy/postpartum, anticoagulation reversal if bleeding, hydrocephalus

## Prognosis
- Mortality 5-15% (lower than arterial stroke)
- 80% make good recovery
- Predictors: coma, deep CVST, posterior fossa, malignancy, infection
- Recurrence: 2-4% (higher with thrombophilia)
- Epilepsy: 10-15% (more common with haemorrhagic infarction, motor deficit)
- Chronic headache: 10-20%
- Visual loss (chronic papilloedema): rare with treatment
- Multidisciplinary care essential
- Most recover with prompt anticoagulation
- Family screening if hereditary thrombophilia
