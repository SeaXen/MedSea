# Syncope

> **High-Yield Definition:** Syncope: transient loss of consciousness (TLOC) due to global cerebral hypoperfusion, with rapid onset, short duration, spontaneous recovery. Reflex (neurally-mediated), cardiac (cardiovascular), orthostatic, or neurological (rare).

## Definition
- **TLOC**: transient loss of consciousness
- Syncope: TLOC due to global cerebral hypoperfusion
- Non-syncope TLOC: epilepsy, psychogenic pseudosyncope, vertebrobasilar TIA, concussion, intoxication

## Classification (ESC 2018)
- **Reflex (neurally-mediated) syncope** (most common):
  - Vasovagal: emotional (fear, pain, sight of blood), orthostatic
  - Situational: cough, micturition, defecation, swallowing, post-exercise
  - Carotid sinus: massage, tight collar, head turning
  - Non-classical (atypical forms)
- **Cardiac syncope** (highest mortality):
  - Arrhythmogenic: bradyarrhythmia (sinus node dysfunction, AV block), tachyarrhythmia (VT, SVT, AF with rapid ventricular response)
  - Structural: aortic stenosis, HCM, mitral stenosis, tamponade, PE, aortic dissection, myocardial ischaemia/infarction
- **Orthostatic hypotension syncope**:
  - Initial orthostatic hypotension (within 15 sec)
  - Classic orthostatic hypotension (>20/10 mmHg, within 3 min)
  - Delayed orthostatic hypotension (>3 min)
  - Drug-induced (antihypertensives, diuretics, vasodilators, antidepressants)
  - Volume depletion (dehydration, haemorrhage)
  - Autonomic failure (primary - MSA, PAF; secondary - diabetic, amyloid, alcohol)
- **Neurological syncope** (rare):
  - Vertebrobasilar TIA
  - Subclavian steal
  - Seizure (ictal asystole, post-ictal)
  - Increased ICP (cough syncope, colloid cyst)

## Epidemiology
- Common: 6% incidence per year
- Lifetime prevalence: 35% (especially young women - vasovagal)
- Cardiac syncope: higher mortality (10-20% per year if untreated)
- Reflex syncope: benign prognosis
- Orthostatic: variable

## Clinical Features
- **Prodrome** (reflex): lightheadedness, nausea, warmth, diaphoresis, tunnel vision, palpitations
- **Onset**: rapid (seconds), may be sudden (cardiac)
- **During**: unresponsive, hypotonic, may have brief tonic-clonic movements (convulsive syncope - 5-20%), incontinence rare (vs seizure), tongue biting rare
- **Recovery**: rapid (seconds-minutes), often with fatigue, nausea, diaphoresis (post-syncope syndrome)
- **No post-ictal confusion** (vs seizure)
- **Circumstances**: prolonged standing (vasovagal), coughing, micturition, exertion (cardiac), emotion, pain, head turning (carotid sinus)
- **Family history**: SCD (HCM, LQT, Brugada), arrhythmia
- **Medications**: antihypertensives, antiarrhythmics, QT-prolonging, diuretics, vasodilators, antidepressants, opioids

## Investigations
- **History**: critical, often diagnostic
- **Examination**:
  - **Vital signs**: BP both arms, HR, lying and standing (orthostatic)
  - Cardiac: murmurs, JVP, carotid bruits, apex beat
  - Neurological: focal signs (rare, concerning for neurological cause)
- **ECG**: 12-lead, ambulatory monitoring (Holter 24-48h, event recorder, implantable loop recorder for infrequent), exercise ECG
- **Echocardiogram**: structural heart disease, EF, valve disease, HCM
- **Tilt table test**: suspected reflex syncope, orthostatic, unexplained recurrent syncope (passive 20-45 min, +/- GTN, +/- clomipramine provocation)
- **Carotid sinus massage**: >50y with suspected reflex syncope (with ECG monitoring, exclude carotid stenosis/bruits, contraindicated in TIA/stroke/CAD within 3 months)
- **Bloods**: FBC (anaemia), glucose, electrolytes, troponin (if cardiac), D-dimer (if PE), BNP/NT-proBNP
- **Neurological**: rarely indicated unless focal signs or seizure (EEG, MRI, video-EEG monitoring)
- **Implantable loop recorder**: recurrent unexplained syncope, low-frequency

## Management

### Reflex (Vasovagal) Syncope
- **Education, reassurance** (benign prognosis)
- **Avoid triggers**: prolonged standing, dehydration, alcohol, hot environments
- **Physical counter-manoeuvres**: leg crossing, hand grip, squatting (at prodrome)
- **Hydration**: 2-3L/day, salt
- **Compression stockings** (class II-III)
- **Tilt training** (recurrent, refractory)
- **Pharmacological**: midodrine 2.5-10mg TDS, fludrocortisone 0.1-0.3mg/day, beta-blockers (atenolol, propranolol - limited evidence), SSRIs (paroxetine, fluoxetine)
- **Pacemaker**: rare, refractory reflex syncope with prolonged asystole (cardioinhibitory)

### Cardiac Syncope
- **Treat underlying**:
  - Arrhythmia: antiarrhythmic, pacemaker (bradyarrhythmia, AV block), ICD (VT, ischaemia), ablation (SVT, VT)
  - Structural: aortic valve replacement (severe AS), aortic dissection repair, PE treatment, ischaemia management
- **Driving restrictions**: must follow DVLA/local guidelines
- Multidisciplinary: cardiology, EP, cardiac surgery

### Orthostatic Syncope
- **Stop/reduce offending drugs**: antihypertensives, diuretics, vasodilators, alpha-blockers
- **Hydration, salt**: 2-3L/day, salt tablets
- **Compression stockings**: class II/III (waist-high)
- **Counter-manoeuvres**: stand slowly, leg crossing
- **Elevate head of bed**
- **Pharmacological**: midodrine 2.5-10mg TDS, fludrocortisone 0.1-0.3mg/day
- **Treat underlying**: autonomic failure (MSA, PAF), diabetes

### Neurological Syncope
- Treat underlying (vertebral artery stenosis, increased ICP)

## Red Flags (Cardiac Syncope — High Risk)
- Exertional syncope (HCM, AS, ischaemia)
- Syncope with chest pain (MI, dissection, PE)
- Syncope with dyspnoea (PE, tamponade, AS)
- Syncope with palpitations (arrhythmia)
- Sudden cardiac death family history
- Syncope with abnormal ECG (ischaemia, arrhythmia, conduction)
- Syncope in supine (cardiac, not reflex)
- Syncope with severe headache (SAH, dissection)
- Syncope with focal neurological deficit (stroke, TIA)
- Older patient with cardiac disease
- Severe injury during syncope
- No prodrome (sudden)

## Investigations for Unexplained Syncope
- Tilt table test (positive: vasovagal pattern)
- Implantable loop recorder (recurrent, infrequent)
- Electrophysiology study (cardiac arrhythmia suspected)
- Exercise stress test (exertional)
- CT/MRI brain (focal neurology, suspected seizure)
- EEG (suspected seizure)

## Driving Regulations (DVLA UK example)
- **Single episode, benign cause**: 1 month off
- **Recurrent, reflex**: 4 weeks off, provided no underlying cardiac
- **Cardiac**: 1 month off after treatment, cardiac opinion
- **Unexplained single**: 6 months off
- **Unexplained recurrent**: 12 months off
- **Pacemaker**: 1 week off (if no other risk)
- ICD: 6 months off (after appropriate intervention)

## Multidisciplinary
- Cardiology (syncope unit, EP)
- Neurology (reflex, autonomic)
- Internal medicine
- Geriatrics (elderly, falls)
- Endocrinology (diabetes, adrenal)
- Pharmacy (medication review)
- OT, PT (falls, rehabilitation)

## Prognosis
- **Reflex**: benign, low mortality, high recurrence, injuries
- **Cardiac**: high mortality (10-20% per year if untreated), depends on underlying, treat improves prognosis
- **Orthostatic**: depends on cause, treat improves
- **Unexplained**: 1-year mortality 5-10%, recurrence common
- Injuries: 5-15% (fracture, head injury, laceration)
- Quality of life: impaired (anxiety, falls, restrictions)
- Multidisciplinary essential
