# Dystonia

Related: [[Movement Disorders Hub]], [[Hyperkinetic Movement Disorders Hub]], [[Wilsons Disease]], [[Drug-Induced Parkinsonism]], [[Chorea]], [[Parkinsons Disease]]

> [!tip] **Dystonia = sustained or intermittent muscle contractions causing abnormal, often repetitive, movements, postures, or both.** Classified by **distribution, age of onset, aetiology**. "Twisted" is the clinical hallmark.

## Learning Objectives
- [ ] Define dystonia and recognise clinical features
- [ ] Classify by distribution (focal, segmental, generalised, hemidystonia) and aetiology
- [ ] Identify common forms: cervical dystonia, blepharospasm, writer's cramp, oromandibular
- [ ] Recognise "geste antagoniste" (sensory trick)
- [ ] Treat with botulinum toxin (focal), anticholinergics, baclofen, deep brain stimulation (GPi)

## 1. Definition / Epidemiology / Classification

**Definition (MDS 2013):** Movement disorder characterised by **sustained or intermittent muscle contractions** causing abnormal, often repetitive, movements, postures, or both. Movements are typically **patterned, twisting, and may be tremulous**. Dystonia is often initiated or worsened by voluntary action and associated with overflow muscle activation.

**Epidemiology:**
- **Prevalence:** 16-30/100,000 (all forms); focal dystonias most common
- **Age of onset:** Variable; focal (adults), generalised (childhood)
- **Sex:** Female predominance in cervical and cranial dystonias

**Classification (Axis I-IV):**
- **Axis I — Clinical features:**
  - Age of onset (infancy, childhood, adolescence, adult)
  - Body distribution (focal, segmental, generalised, hemidystonia)
  - Temporal pattern (persistent, action-specific, diurnal, paroxysmal)
  - Associated features (isolated, combined, plus myoclonus, parkinsonism, etc.)
- **Axis II — Aetiology:**
  - **Inherited** (DYT genes, mtDNA)
  - **Acquired** (lesion, drug)
  - **Idiopathic**

**Distribution:**

| Type | Definition | Examples |
|------|------------|----------|
| **Focal** | Single body region | Blepharospasm, cervical dystonia, writer's cramp, oromandibular |
| **Segmental** | Two or more adjacent regions | Cranial + cervical; bibrachial |
| **Multifocal** | Two or more non-adjacent regions | Cranial + upper limb |
| **Generalised** | Both legs ± trunk ± other regions | Early-onset DYT1 |
| **Hemidystonia** | One side of body | Stroke, lesion, CBD |

## 2. Aetiology / Pathophysiology

**Mechanism:**
- **Basal ganglia dysfunction** — loss of inhibition in sensorimotor circuits
- Decreased GABAergic inhibition
- Abnormal sensory-motor integration (cerebellar)
- Excess plasticity in motor cortex
- Reduced surround inhibition
- Hyperactivity in putamen, GPi, thalamus

**Genetics (DYT genes):**
- **DYT1 (TOR1A):** Childhood-onset generalised, AD, GAG deletion in torsin A; Ashkenazi Jewish founder effect
- **DYT5 (GCH1):** Dopa-responsive dystonia (Segawa disease), AD; diurnal fluctuation
- **DYT6 (THAP1):** Adolescent-onset mixed, AD
- **DYT11 (SGCE):** Myoclonus-dystonia, AD
- **DYT12 (ATP1A3):** Rapid-onset dystonia-parkinsonism
- **DYT16 (PRKRA):** Young-onset dystonia-parkinsonism

**Acquired causes:**
- **Perinatal injury** (CP with dystonia)
- **Stroke** (basal ganglia, thalamus)
- **Tumour, demyelination**
- **Drugs:** Antipsychotics (acute dystonia, tardive), metoclopramide, levodopa, anticonvulsants
- **Toxins:** CO, manganese

**"Pseudo-dystonias"** (not true dystonia): Sandifer's (reflux), stiff person syndrome, myotonia, tenosynovitis

## 3. Clinical Features

**Cardinal features:**
- **Patterned, twisting movements/postures** (the "twist")
- **Worsened by action** (task-specific or voluntary)
- **Sensory tricks (geste antagoniste):** Touching the affected area reduces dystonia (highly characteristic)
- **Overflow:** Spread to adjacent muscles
- **Mirror movements:** Dystonia on unaffected side when affected side moves
- **Tremor:** Often coexists (dystonic tremor)
- **Pain:** Common, especially cervical
- **Inconsistent over time** (variable)

**Examination:**
- Observe at rest, with posture, with action
- Watch for sensory tricks (touch, pinch, hand on chin)
- Exclude weakness, sensory loss, pyramidal/cerebellar signs

**Common forms:**

| Form | Features |
|------|----------|
| **Cervical dystonia (torticollis)** | Neck rotation (rotation, laterocollis, retrocollis, anterocollis); pain, head tremor |
| **Blepharospasm** | Involuntary eye closure; spasms; can be severe; ↓ with talking, singing |
| **Oromandibular** | Jaw clenching, opening, deviation; ↓ opening dystonia; lingual dystonia |
| **Writer's cramp** | Task-specific dystonia on writing; abnormal grip |
| **Laryngeal (spasmodic dysphonia)** | Adductor (strained) or abductor (whispering) |
| **Limb dystonia** | Focal, often distal; task-specific |

## 4. Diagnostic Approach

**Clinical diagnosis:** Patterned, twisting movements, sensory tricks, overflow, action-induced

**Workup:**

| Step | Tests |
|------|-------|
| **History** | Onset, progression, diurnal variation, drug history, family history |
| **Examination** | Distribution, sensory tricks, dystonic tremor, neurological exam |
| **First-line investigations** | MRI brain, copper studies, bloods |
| **Second-line** | Genetic testing (DYT1, 5, 6, 11), DAT-SPECT, LP |

**Genetics indicated:** Childhood/adolescent onset, generalised, family history

## 5. Investigations

| Test | Purpose | Findings |
|------|---------|----------|
| **MRI brain** | Rule out structural cause | Normal in primary; basal ganglia/thalamic lesions in acquired |
| **Copper studies (Cp, 24h urine Cu, KF)** | Exclude Wilson's | Low ceruloplasmin, ↑urine Cu, KF rings |
| **Slit lamp** | Wilson's | Kayser-Fleischer rings |
| **Genetic panel (DYT genes)** | Childhood onset, family history | TOR1A, GCH1, THAP1, SGCE |
| **DAT-SPECT** | If parkinsonism features | Differentiates PD |
| **Amino acids, organic acids, lactate** | If metabolic | Mitochondrial, organic acidaemia |
| **EEG** | If paroxysmal dystonia | Differentiates from seizure |
| **Trial of levodopa** | Suspected DYT5 (Segawa) | Marked response to low-dose levodopa |

**Trial of levodopa:** All childhood-onset dystonia should have a trial of **levodopa 100-300 mg/day for ≥3 months** to exclude DYT5 (Segawa disease) — curative.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Tics** | Suppressible, premonitory urge, stereotyped |
| **Chorea** | Continuous, flowing, non-stereotyped |
| **Athetosis** | Slow, writhing (often with CP) |
| **Myoclonus** | Brief, shock-like, <100 ms |
| **Tremor** | Rhythmic, oscillatory |
| **Stiff person syndrome** | Rigid, painful, anti-GAD; paraspinal |
| **Tetanus** | Trismus, risus, opisthotonus, history of wound |
| **Psychogenic (functional)** | Variable, distractible, incongruous |
| **Spasticity** | Velocity-dependent increase in tone, pyramidal pattern |
| **Stereotypy** | Repetitive, fixed, often in autism, learning disability |
| **Sandifer's syndrome** | Reflux-related abnormal posturing; GI symptoms |

## 7. Management

**Principles:** Treat underlying cause; botulinum toxin for focal; oral agents for generalised; surgery (DBS) for refractory.

**1. Botulinum toxin (focal dystonia — first-line):**
- **Indications:** Cervical dystonia, blepharospasm, oromandibular, laryngeal, focal limb
- **Type A:** Botox, Dysport, Xeomin
- **Type B:** NeuroBloc (rimabotulinumtoxinB) for type A resistance
- **Onset:** 3-7 days; duration 3-4 months
- **Repeat:** 3-monthly minimum to avoid antibody formation

**2. Oral medications (generalised/segmental):**

| Drug | Dose | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| **Trihexyphenidyl** | 1-30 mg/day | First-line oral; anticholinergic side effects (esp. elderly) |
| **Levodopa** | 100-1000 mg/day | Trial in all childhood-onset (Segawa, dopa-responsive) |
| **Baclofen** | 30-120 mg/day | GABA-B; intrathecal for severe |
| **Clonazepam** | 0.5-6 mg/day | Sedation, dependence |
| **Tetrabenazine** | 25-200 mg/day | VMAT inhibitor; depression risk |
| **Diazepam** | 5-30 mg/day | Adjunct |
| **Carbamazepine** | 200-1200 mg/day | For paroxysmal |

**3. Surgical (refractory generalised):**
- **Deep brain stimulation (DBS) of GPi (globus pallidus internus)** — most effective for DYT1, severe generalised
- **Thalamotomy** (radiofrequency, FUS) — older option
- **DBS is first-line surgical**

**4. Rehabilitation:**
- PT, OT, sensory trick devices
- Voice/speech therapy for laryngeal
- Support groups

**5. Treatment-specific forms:**
- **DYT5 (Segawa):** Low-dose levodopa (often 100-300 mg/day) — dramatic response
- **Cervical dystonia:** Botulinum toxin + sensory tricks
- **Blepharospasm:** Botulinum toxin
- **Writer's cramp:** Botulinum toxin + writing aids
- **Drug-induced:** Stop drug; anticholinergics; tetrabenazine for tardive

## 8. Drug Considerations
- **Trihexyphenidyl:** Anticholinergic; dry mouth, urinary retention, confusion, glaucoma; avoid elderly
- **Tetrabenazine:** Depression risk, parkinsonism, sedation; contraindicated in untreated depression
- **Levodopa:** Nausea, orthostasis, dyskinesia (in dopa-responsive)
- **Antipsychotics:** AVOID — worsen dystonia
- **Metoclopramide:** AVOID — acute dystonia/tardive risk

## 9. Procedures
- **Botulinum toxin injection:** EMG or ultrasound-guided
- **DBS of GPi:** Refractory generalised dystonia
- **Sensory trick device:** Customised

## 10. Complications
- **Disability:** Pain, contractures, postural deformities
- **Cervical dystonia:** Cervical myelopathy (rare), bone degeneration
- **Blepharospasm:** Functional blindness
- **Drug-induced:** Tardive dystonia (may be permanent)
- **Botulinum side effects:** Ptosis, dysphagia, weakness, dry mouth

## 11. Red Flags
- Sudden onset (consider stroke, drugs)
- Hemidystonia (consider structural lesion)
- Young patient + dystonia (Wilson's, DYT genes, Segawa)
- Status dystonicus (medical emergency, treat as SE)

## 12. Prognosis
- **Focal adult:** Stable, slowly progressive, treatable
- **Generalised childhood:** Often progressive; DYT1 may plateau in adulthood
- **Dopa-responsive (DYT5):** Excellent with levodopa
- **Acquired:** Depends on lesion
- **DBS response:** 50-80% improvement in generalised

## 13. Topic Correlation
- [[Wilsons Disease]] — must exclude in young
- [[Chorea]] — differential
- [[Parkinsons Disease]] — drug-induced dystonia
- [[Drug-Induced Parkinsonism]] — tardive
- [[Hyperkinetic Movement Disorders Hub]] — classification

## 14. Special Situations
- **Pregnancy:** Avoid botulinum (limited data); trihexyphenidyl cautious; levodopa safe
- **Paediatric:** Trial of levodopa; genetic testing; DBS
- **Elderly:** Anticholinergics worsen cognition; botulinum preferred
- **Status dystonicus:** Emergency — intubation, IV sedation, ICU
- **Driving:** Report; may need assessment
- **Driving/DVLA:** Depends on disability

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary
- **Definition:** Sustained/intermittent muscle contractions → patterned twisting movements
- **Hallmark:** Sensory trick (geste antagoniste)
- **Distribution:** Focal, segmental, generalised, hemidystonia
- **Most common focal:** Cervical dystonia, blepharospasm, writer's cramp
- **Focal treatment:** Botulinum toxin (first-line)
- **Generalised treatment:** Anticholinergics, baclofen, DBS of GPi
- **Childhood-onset:** Always trial levodopa (Segawa) and check copper (Wilson's)
- **Genetics:** DYT1 (TOR1A, AD, generalised), DYT5 (GCH1, dopa-responsive)

## Viva Questions
1. Define dystonia and give cardinal features — Sustained contractions, patterned, twisting, sensory tricks
2. What is geste antagoniste? — Sensory trick that reduces dystonia (highly characteristic)
3. First-line treatment for focal dystonia — Botulinum toxin
4. What is DYT1? — Childhood-onset generalised dystonia; TOR1A gene; AD; GAG deletion
5. DYT5 (Segawa disease) — Dopa-responsive dystonia; GCH1; dramatic levodopa response
6. Why trial levodopa in all childhood dystonia? — To exclude dopa-responsive dystonia
7. DBS target for generalised dystonia — Globus pallidus internus (GPi)

## Common Confusions
| Confusion | Clarification |
|-----------|---------------|
| Dystonia vs spasticity | Dystonia: patterned, sensory trick; spasticity: velocity-dependent, pyramidal |
| Dystonia vs chorea | Dystonia: sustained, patterned; chorea: flowing, continuous |
| Dystonia vs tics | Tics: suppressible, premonitory urge, stereotyped |
| Dystonia vs athetosis | Athetosis: slow, writhing (in CP); dystonia: twisted, patterned |
| Acute dystonic reaction vs tardive dystonia | Acute: hours-days, drug-related; tardive: months-years, often persistent |
| Writer's cramp vs hand dystonia | Writer's cramp: task-specific; hand dystonia: present in other actions |

## Mnemonics
- **Dystonia** = **T**wisted, **T**ense, **T**rick (**TTT**)
- **Focal forms** = **C**ervical, **B**lepharospasm, **W**riter's cramp, **O**romandibular (**CBWO**)
- **DYT genes** = **1** = childhood generalised; **5** = dopa-responsive; **6** = adolescent; **11** = myoclonus-dystonia
- **Treatment ladder** = **B**otulinum (focal) → **A**nticholinergics → **B**aclofen → **D**BS (generalised)
