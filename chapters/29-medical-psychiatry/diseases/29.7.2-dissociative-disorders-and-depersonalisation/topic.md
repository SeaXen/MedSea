# Dissociative disorders and depersonalisation

---

## Definition

Dissociative disorders are a group of conditions involving disruption of consciousness, memory, identity, emotion, perception, body representation, motor control, or behaviour. Common presentations include dissociative amnesia, fugue, depersonalisation/derealisation, and DID.

## Core Clinical Content

### Dissociative disorders (F44)
Dissociative amnesia, fugue, stupor, trance, motor, convulsions, anaesthesia

### Dissociative amnesia
Inability to recall important autobiographical information; not due to medical condition

### Dissociative fugue
Dissociative amnesia + purposeful travel; new identity may be assumed

### Depersonalisation/derealisation
Feeling of unreality of self (depersonalisation) or surroundings (derealisation)

### DPD (depersonalisation disorder)
Persistent or recurrent; significant distress; not due to substance/medical

### Dissociative identity disorder (DID)
Formerly multiple personality disorder; ≥2 distinct personality states

### DID controversy
Disputed validity; some consider iatrogenic; high comorbidity with childhood trauma

### Dissociation association
Often with childhood trauma, borderline personality, PTSD

### Differential
PTSD, complex PTSD, factitious, malingering, neurological (TLE, post-ictal)

### Treatment
Trauma-focused therapy; phase-oriented (stabilisation, processing, integration); DBT skills
