# ECT (electroconvulsive therapy) and other biological treatments

---

## Definition

Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) is a safe and highly effective treatment for severe psychiatric conditions, especially treatment-resistant depression, with electrical current inducing a generalised seizure under anaesthesia. Other biological treatments include TMS, ketamine, and esketamine.

## Core Clinical Content

### ECT mechanism
Induced generalised seizure under anaesthesia + muscle relaxant

### ECT indications
Severe/treatment-resistant depression, catatonia, psychotic depression, NMS, severe mania, pregnancy (preferred)

### ECT efficacy
60-80% response in severe depression; rapid effect (1-2 weeks)

### ECT course
Usually 6-12 sessions; 2-3 per week

### ECT side effects
Memory impairment (retrograde/anterograde), headache, nausea, anaesthesia risks

### ECT contraindications
No absolute contraindications; relative: ↑ ICP, recent MI/stroke, pregnancy (modified ECT safe)

### ECT in pregnancy
Safe; preferred for severe depression; avoid hyperventilation; protect airway

### Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)
Non-invasive; for treatment-resistant depression; rTMS effective

### Esketamine (Spravato)
NMDA antagonist; intranasal; for treatment-resistant depression (with SSRI)

### Ketamine
IV; rapid antidepressant effect (hours); potential abuse; research/limited use
