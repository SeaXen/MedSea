Hypovolaemic Shock
1 Learning Objectives
  • Identify causes of hypovolaemic shock
  • Differentiate haemorrhagic vs non-haemorrhagic hypovolaemia
  • Apply ATLS principles of fluid resuscitation
  • Recognise when blood products are needed

3 Causes

Haemorrhagic

  • Trauma (external + internal)
  • GI bleed, ruptured AAA, ectopic pregnancy

Non-haemorrhagic

  • Severe dehydration (diarrhoea, vomiting, burns, DKA, diabetes insipidus)
4 Clinical Features
  • Cool, clammy skin, delayed capillary refill
  • Tachycardia, narrow pulse pressure
  • Flat neck veins, low JVP
  • Oliguria, confusion
5 Management
  • Stop the bleeding (pressure, tourniquet, surgery, endoscopy)
  • Two large-bore IV cannulae
  • Crystalloid resuscitation (balanced solutions)
  • Blood products early if Class III/IV or ongoing bleeding (1:1:1 ratio)
  • Permissive hypotension (SBP 80–90 mmHg) in penetrating trauma until bleeding controlled
  • Monitor lactate, base deficit, urine output
6 Red Flags
  • Class III/IV shock
  • Ongoing bleeding
  • Coagulopathy / trauma-induced coagulopathy
7 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Tachycardia may be absent in elderly or beta-blocked patients
  • Hypotension is a late sign
  • Give blood early in haemorrhagic shock — crystalloids alone worsen coagulopathy
8 Common Viva Questions
  1. Classify hypovolaemic shock by blood loss.
  2. What is permissive hypotension and when is it used?
  3. When would you activate massive transfusion protocol?
9 One-Page Revision Summary

Hypovolaemia = low preload Stop bleeding + large-bore IV access + balanced crystalloids Early blood in significant haemorrhage Monitor lactate and urine output

10 Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
Must Know
  • ATLS classification I–IV by blood loss (pulse, BP, UO, mental status)
  • Haemorrhagic vs non-haemorrhagic causes
  • Stop bleeding first, then 2 large-bore IV + balanced crystalloids
  • Early blood products (1:1:1) for Class III/IV or ongoing bleed
  • Permissive hypotension SBP 80–90 in penetrating trauma
Should Know
  • Lactate, base deficit, UO as resuscitation targets
  • Trauma-induced coagulopathy (crystalloids dilute clotting factors)
  • Tranexamic acid within 3 hours of trauma
Nice to Know
  • Damage control resuscitation vs surgery
  • Cell salvage, REBOA
  • Permissive hypotension contraindicated in TBI
