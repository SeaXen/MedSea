ACUTE MEDICINE AND CRITICAL ILLNESS
Acute Medicine in Pregnancy
1 Learning Objectives
  • Understand key physiological changes in pregnancy affecting acute care
  • Adapt ABCDE assessment for the pregnant patient
  • Recognise pregnancy-specific causes of acute deterioration
  • Apply modified management algorithms (e.g. cardiac arrest, sepsis)
2 Key Physiological Changes
SystemChangeClinical Implication
--------------------------------------
**Cardiovascular**CO ↑ 30–50%, HR ↑ 10–20 bpm, BP ↓ (mid-pregnancy)Tolerates haemorrhage better but decompensates suddenly; supine hypotension
**Respiratory**Minute ventilation ↑ 40%, PaCO₂ ↓ to ~4 kPa, functional residual capacity ↓Rapid desaturation on apnoea; "normal" PaCO₂ = impending respiratory failure
**Renal**GFR ↑ 50%, creatinine ↓"Normal" creatinine may indicate AKI
**Haematological**Dilutional anaemia, hypercoagulable state (VTE risk ↑)Higher threshold for transfusion; VTE prophylaxis essential
**GI**Delayed gastric emptying, refluxAspiration risk — always assume full stomach
3 Modified ABCDE in Pregnancy
  • A: Airway — difficult intubation (oedema, breast enlargement); nasal intubation preferred; smaller ETT (6.5–7.0)
  • B: Breathing — target SpO₂ ≥94%; left lateral tilt (15°) or manual uterine displacement from 20 weeks
  • C: Circulation — IV access above diaphragm; fluid resuscitate aggressively; left lateral tilt improves venous return
  • D: Disability — consider eclampsia, intracranial haemorrhage
  • E: Exposure — assess fundal height, fetal heart rate (if >22 weeks)
4 Pregnancy-Specific Acute Conditions
ConditionKey FeaturesManagement
--------------------------------------
**Eclampsia**Seizure + hypertension + proteinuria (usually >20 weeks)**Magnesium sulphate** 4g IV load → 1g/hr; control BP (labetalol/nifedipine); delivery definitive
**HELLP**Haemolysis, Elevated LFTs, Low PlateletsUrgent delivery if >34 weeks; magnesium, BP control, platelets if <20 or bleeding
**Amniotic Fluid Embolism**Sudden collapse, hypoxia, coagulopathy, cardiac arrestSupportive — ICU, massive transfusion, consider ECMO
**Septic Abortion**Fever, bleeding, pelvic pain, septic shock**Antibiotics within 1 hour** + surgical evacuation
**Pulmonary Embolism**Leading cause of maternal deathCT-PA if stable; anticoagulate (LMWH); consider thrombolysis if massive
5 Shock in Pregnancy
  • Supine hypotension syndrome — always left lateral tilt
  • Haemorrhage (antepartum/postpartum) — massive transfusion protocol early
  • Sepsis — physiological changes mask signs (normal HR/BP may be shock)
6 Cardiac Arrest in Pregnancy (>20 weeks)
  1. Standard ALS + left lateral tilt (manual uterine displacement)
  2. Early intubation (experienced operator, smaller tube)
  3. Peri-mortem C-section if no ROSC at 4 minutes — aim for delivery by 5 minutes (improves maternal venous return + fetal survival)
  4. Continue ALS after delivery

8 Red Flags
  • "Normal" PaCO₂ in pregnant patient = respiratory fatigue
  • Sudden collapse post-delivery = amniotic fluid embolism / massive PE
  • Seizure in pregnancy = eclampsia until proven otherwise
9 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Left lateral tilt (15°) or manual uterine displacement from 20 weeks — improves venous return by 20–30%
  • "Normal" BP in pregnancy may be hypotension (pre-preg BP higher)
  • Peri-mortem C-section at 4 min if no ROSC
  • Magnesium sulphate = first-line for eclampsia seizure prophylaxis/treatment
  • LMWH for VTE prophylaxis in all admitted pregnant patients (unless contraindicated)

13 One-Page Revision Summary

Pregnancy = altered physiology

  • Left lateral tilt ≥20 weeks

  • Eclampsia → MgSO₄ + delivery
  • Cardiac arrest → peri-mortem C-section at 4 min
  • Safe antibiotics: penicillins, cephalosporins, metronidazole

17 Flashcards
  • Q: Peri-mortem C-section timing

A: Start at 4 min, deliver by 5 min if no ROSC

  • Q: Left lateral tilt gestation

A: ≥20 weeks

  • Q: Eclampsia first-line

A: MgSO₄ 4g IV load → 1g/hr

  • Q: "Normal" PaCO₂ in pregnancy

A: ~4.0 kPa — if higher, respiratory fatigue

  • Q: Safe antibiotics in pregnancy

A: Penicillins, cephalosporins, metronidazole, erythromycin, clindamycin

  • Q: Antihypertensives in pregnancy

A: Labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa — AVOID ACEi/ARBs

  • Q: VTE prophylaxis in pregnancy

A: LMWH (not warfarin)

  • Q: Magnesium sulphate monitoring

A: Patellar reflex, respiratory rate, urine output, serum Mg²⁺

18 Answer Key with Explanations

MCQ 1: C — 20 weeks (uterus at umbilicus, compresses IVC supine). MCQ 2: B — Target SpO₂ ≥94% (fetal oxygenation). MCQ 3: C — MgSO₄ is first-line for eclampsia. MCQ 4: B — 4 minutes (aim delivery by 5 min). MCQ 5: C — Co-amoxiclav safe; others teratogenic/toxic. MCQ 6: A — PaCO₂ ~4 kPa in pregnancy (↑ minute ventilation). MCQ 7: C — Thromboembolism (leading direct cause per MBRRACE). MCQ 8: C — Labetalol safe; ACEi/ARBs teratogenic. MCQ 9: B — Dilutional (plasma volume ↑ 50%, RBC mass ↑ 25%). MCQ 10: B — MgSO₄ 4g IV over 5–10 min, then 1g/hr infusion.

SBA 1: B — MgSO₄ + BP control + delivery (definitive). SBA 2: A — Peri-mortem C-section at 4 min if no ROSC. SBA 3: B — Sepsis in pregnancy = aggressive fluids + broad-spectrum antibiotics + source control (gynae). SBA 4: B — LMWH only safe anticoagulant in pregnancy. SBA 5: B — Sudden collapse in labour = AFE (hypoxia + coagulopathy + collapse). SBA 6: B — <150/100 typical target in severe pre-eclampsia. SBA 7: B — Non-heparin alternatives for HIT in pregnancy (danaparoid/fondaparinux/argatroban). SBA 8: C — Ramipril (ACEi) contraindicated. SBA 9: B — Physiological dilutional anaemia. SBA 10: C — Smaller ETT (6.5–7.0) due to airway oedema.
