Obstructive Shock
1 Learning Objectives
  • Recognise the three main causes of obstructive shock
  • Use bedside ultrasound for rapid diagnosis
  • Know the specific interventions for each cause

4 Management

Massive PE

  • Thrombolysis (alteplase) if unstable
  • Consider ECMO or surgical embolectomy

Cardiac Tamponade

  • Urgent pericardiocentesis
  • Fluid bolus while preparing

Tension Pneumothorax

  • Immediate needle decompression (2nd ICS mid-clavicular line)
  • Followed by chest drain
5 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis — do not wait for CXR
  • Tamponade shows pulsus paradoxus and electrical alternans
  • Massive PE often has clear lungs despite severe shock

7 One-Page Revision Summary

Obstructive = mechanical blockage PE → thrombolysis Tamponade → pericardiocentesis Tension PTX → needle decompression (clinical diagnosis)

8 Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
Must Know
  • Three causes: massive PE, cardiac tamponade, tension pneumothorax
  • All cause raised JVP (obstructed venous return)
  • Tension PTX = clinical diagnosis (don't wait for CXR)
  • Immediate interventions: PE=thrombolysis, Tamponade=pericardiocentesis, Tension PTX=needle decompression
Should Know
  • Beck's triad (tamponade): hypotension, raised JVP, muffled heart sounds
  • Pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg in tamponade (also severe asthma, PE)
  • RV strain on echo = massive PE
  • Electrical alternans on ECG = tamponade (large effusion)
Nice to Know
  • Surgical pericardial window vs pericardiocentesis
  • VA-ECMO for massive PE
  • REBOA not for obstructive shock
