ACUTE MEDICINE AND CRITICAL ILLNESS
Acute Medicine in the Elderly
1 Learning Objectives
  • Recognise atypical presentations of acute illness in older adults
  • Apply Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) principles
  • Use frailty scores (CFS) to guide escalation decisions
  • Adjust investigations and management for physiological changes
  • Avoid common prescribing pitfalls (polypharmacy, anticholinergics)
2 Key Physiological Changes with Ageing
SystemChangeClinical Implication
--------------------------------------
**Cardiovascular**↓ Compliance, ↓ β-receptor response, ↑ afterloadBlunted tachycardia in shock; higher baseline BP; prone to orthostatic hypotension
**Respiratory**↓ Chest wall compliance, ↓ FRC, ↓ cough reflexRapid desaturation; silent aspiration; "normal" RR may be high for age
**Renal**↓ GFR ~1 mL/min/yr after 40, ↓ concentrating abilityDrug accumulation; dehydration risk; "normal" creatinine may = CKD
**Neurological**↓ Brain volume, ↓ neurotransmittersDelirium risk; atypical stroke presentation; altered drug sensitivity
**Thermoregulation**↓ Shivering, ↓ vasoconstrictionHypothermia in sepsis; absent fever in infection
**Immune**ImmunosenescenceAtypical infection presentation; poor vaccine response

4 Frailty Assessment (Clinical Frailty Scale — CFS)
ScoreDescription1-Year Mortality
--------------------------------------
1–3Fit / Well / Managing well<5%
4Vulnerable / Slow~10%
5Mildly frail~20%
6Moderately frail~40%
7Severely frail~60%
8Very severely frail~80%
9Terminally ill~90%

CFS ≥5 = frail — guides ceiling of care discussions, ICU admission thresholds, and rehabilitation potential.

5 Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) Domains
  1. Medical — diagnoses, medications, polypharmacy review
  2. Functional — ADLs, IADLs, mobility, falls risk
  3. Cognitive — MMSE/MoCA, delirium screening (4AT)
  4. Psychological — mood (GDS-15), anxiety
  5. Social — support network, housing, carer burden
  6. Environmental — home safety, aids/adaptations
6 Delirium (Acute Confusional State)
  • 4AT: Alertness, AMT4, Attention, Acute change
  • Hypoactive (lethargy) more common than hyperactive in elderly — missed!
  • PINCH ME causes: Pain, Infection, Nutrition, Constipation, Hydration, Medication, Environment
  • Management: Treat cause + non-pharmacological (reorientation, sleep hygiene) + haloperidol/risperidone if severe distress
7 Falls Assessment
  • Definition: Unintentional rest on ground/floor
  • Multifactorial: gait/balance, medications (↓ psychotropics), vision, footwear, environment, postural hypotension
  • After fall: Lie/sit/stand BP, medication review, bone health (vit D, bisphosphonate if osteoporotic)
8 Polypharmacy & Prescribing (STOPP/START Criteria)

Common Inappropriate Prescriptions in Elderly (STOPP)

  • Long-acting benzodiazepines (falls, delirium)
  • Anticholinergics (confusion, constipation, urinary retention)
  • NSAIDs (GI bleed, AKI, HF exacerbation)
  • Duplicate drug classes
  • Drugs without indication

START — omissions (e.g. vit D, bisphosphonate in osteoporosis, ACEi in HF, anticoagulation in AF)

9 Fluid Resuscitation in Elderly
  • Smaller boluses (250 mL) with frequent reassessment
  • Higher risk of pulmonary oedema (↓ cardiac compliance)
  • Target urine output ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr but accept lower in frail
  • Monitor JVP, lung bases, NT-proBNP
10 Red Flags
  • "Off legs" / "not themselves" = occult sepsis
  • New confusion = delirium until proven otherwise
  • Fall = marker of acute illness (not just mechanical)
  • Hypothermia in sepsis = poor prognosis
11 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Frailty (CFS) > chronological age for decision-making
  • "Normal" vital signs ≠ well — blunted responses
  • Delirium = medical emergency — screen with 4AT
  • Polypharmacy review at every admission (STOPP/START)
  • CGA improves outcomes — MDT approach

13 Common Confusions / Exam Traps
  • Attributing new confusion to "dementia progression" without excluding delirium
  • Assuming "no fever" = no infection
  • Giving fluid boluses without reassessment (flash pulmonary oedema)
  • Prescribing anticholinergics/benzodiazepines for "agitation" — worsens delirium
14 Mnemonics
  • 4AT — Alertness, AMT4, Attention, Acute change
  • PINCH ME — Pain, Infection, Nutrition, Constipation, Hydration, Medication, Environment
  • CGA — Medical, Functional, Cognitive, Psychological, Social, Environmental

20 Answer Key with Explanations

MCQ 1: C — CFS 6 (moderately frail) ~40% 1-year mortality. MCQ 2: C — New confusion is the hallmark of sepsis in elderly. MCQ 3: A — 4AT = Alertness, AMT4, Attention, Acute change. MCQ 4: C — Hypertension not in PINCH ME. MCQ 5: B — Falls + delirium risk. MCQ 6: B — 250 mL boluses with reassessment. MCQ 7: B — Hypoactive more common, often missed. MCQ 8: D — Echo not routine after fall. MCQ 9: B — Low muscle mass → low creatinine production. MCQ 10: C — CFS 7 = severely frail.

SBA 1: B — Atypical sepsis (confusion, no fever, HR not tachycardic enough for age) = admit for IV antibiotics. SBA 2: B — Amitriptyline (anticholinergic), diazepam (long-acting benzo), ibuprofen (NSAID) all STOPP criteria. SBA 3: B — Multifactorial delirium post-op (opioids + dehydration + environment = PINCH ME). SBA 4: B — Valid ADRT applies; comfort care. SBA 5: B — Orthostatic hypotension from polypharmacy; reduce diuretic/beta-blocker, serial BP. SBA 6: B — Haloperidol low-dose first-line for delirium distress; avoid benzos. SBA 7: C — CGA = Medical, Functional, Cognitive, Psychological, Social, Environmental. SBA 8: C — CFS guides but doesn't mandate; discuss outcomes, consider ward/HDU ceiling. SBA 9: B — Amitriptyline (TCA) highly anticholinergic. SBA 10: B — Constipation/faecal impaction common cause of overflow incontinence + confusion.
