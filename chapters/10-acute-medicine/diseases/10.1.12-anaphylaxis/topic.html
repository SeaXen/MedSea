ACUTE MEDICINE AND CRITICAL ILLNESS
Anaphylaxis
1 Learning Objectives
  • Recognise anaphylaxis clinically
  • Deliver immediate adrenaline
  • Know when to give second doses and adjuncts
  • Plan safe discharge and follow-up
2 Definition & Triggers

Rapid onset of:

  • Airway compromise (swelling, stridor)
  • Breathing difficulty (wheeze, hypoxia)
  • Circulation problems (hypotension, shock)
  • Skin/mucosal changes (urticaria, flushing) — may be absent

Common triggers: foods, drugs (penicillins, NSAIDs), insect stings, latex, contrast.

3 Immediate Management (Resuscitation Council)
  1. Remove trigger if possible
  2. Adrenaline 0.5 mg IM (anterolateral thigh) — repeat every 5 min if no improvement
  3. High-flow oxygen
  4. IV access + fluid challenge if hypotensive
  5. Antihistamine (chlorphenamine 10 mg IV) + hydrocortisone 200 mg IV (after adrenaline)
4 Red Flags
  • Stridor or airway swelling
  • Hypotension + wheeze
  • Previous severe reaction or asthma
5 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Adrenaline first, every time — antihistamines and steroids are adjuncts only
  • IM route is preferred over IV in most cases
  • Observe for at least 6–12 hours after resolution (biphasic reaction risk)
6 Common Viva Questions
  1. What is the first-line drug in anaphylaxis and the dose/route?
  2. When would you give a second dose of adrenaline?
  3. What is the role of steroids and antihistamines?
7 One-Page Revision Summary

Adrenaline 0.5 mg IM immediately Repeat every 5 min if needed Oxygen + fluids + call for help Observe ≥6 hours, prescribe adrenaline auto-injector on discharge

8 Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
Must Know
  • Adrenaline 0.5 mg IM anterolateral thigh = FIRST-LINE (repeat q5min)
  • IM preferred over IV (safer, faster absorption)
  • Antihistamines/steroids = ADJUNCTS only (after adrenaline)
  • Observe 6–12 hours (biphasic reaction risk)
  • Discharge with adrenaline auto-injector + allergy clinic referral
Should Know
  • Criteria: Airway/Breathing/Circulation/Skin involvement (rapid onset)
  • IV adrenaline only if IM failed + cardiovascular collapse (with monitoring)
  • Fluid resuscitation for hypotensive patients
  • Common triggers: foods, drugs, insect stings, latex, contrast
Nice to Know
  • Mast cell tryptase (timing: 1–2h post-reaction, repeat at 24h)
  • Idiopathic anaphylaxis
  • Alpha-gal syndrome (delayed red meat allergy)
