ACUTE MEDICINE AND CRITICAL ILLNESS
Sepsis and Septic Shock
1 Learning Objectives
  • Apply the current Sepsis-3 definition
  • Recognise sepsis early using qSOFA / clinical suspicion
  • Deliver the Hour-1 Bundle
  • Understand the pathophysiology of septic shock
  • Manage fluid, vasopressors, and source control
2 Definition (Sepsis-3, 2016)
  • Sepsis: Life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection (SOFA score increase ≥2)
  • Septic shock: Sepsis with persisting hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg and lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate volume resuscitation
3 qSOFA (quick SOFA) – Bedside Screening

≥2 of the following:

  • Respiratory rate ≥22/min
  • Altered mentation (GCS <15)
  • Systolic BP ≤100 mmHg
4 Pathophysiology
  • PAMPs + DAMPs → massive cytokine release
  • Vasodilatation + capillary leak + myocardial depression
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction + cytopathic hypoxia
  • Coagulopathy and immunosuppression (CARS)
5 Clinical Features
  • Fever or hypothermia
  • Tachycardia, tachypnoea
  • Warm then cold peripheries
  • Altered mental status
  • Oliguria, jaundice, ileus
  • Petechiae / purpura (meningococcal / DIC)
6 Hour-1 Bundle (Surviving Sepsis)
  1. Measure lactate
  2. Obtain blood cultures before antibiotics
  3. Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics
  4. Begin rapid fluid resuscitation (30 mL/kg crystalloid if hypotension or lactate ≥4)
7 Management
  • Fluids: 30 mL/kg crystalloid in first 3 hours (balanced solutions preferred)
  • Vasopressors: Noradrenaline first-line (target MAP ≥65 mmHg)
  • Source control: Within 6–12 hours (surgical drainage, removal of infected devices)
  • Antibiotics: Within 1 hour — broad spectrum, de-escalate later
  • Steroids: Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day if vasopressor-dependent
  • Blood products: Hb target 70 g/L (unless active bleeding or ischaemia)
8 Red Flags / Emergencies
  • Lactate ≥4 mmol/L
  • Refractory hypotension
  • DIC, ARDS, AKI
  • Meningococcal sepsis (rapid purpura fulminans)
9 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Sepsis can present with normal temperature (especially elderly/immunocompromised)
  • Lactate clearance is a key resuscitation target
  • Early source control is as important as antibiotics
  • Do not delay antibiotics for imaging or cultures if patient is unstable

12 Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
Must Know
  • Sepsis-3 definitions (infection + SOFA ≥2; septic shock = vasopressor + lactate >2 after fluids)
  • qSOFA ≥2 (RR≥22, GCS<15, SBP≤100) for screening
  • Hour-1 Bundle: lactate, blood cultures, broad-spectrum antibiotics, 30 mL/kg crystalloid, vasopressors
  • Noradrenaline first-line vasopressor (MAP ≥65)
  • Source control within 6–12h (drain abscess, remove line, surgery)
Should Know
  • Lactate clearance ≥10–20% per 2h as resuscitation target
  • Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day if vasopressor-dependent
  • Hb target 70 g/L (restrictive transfusion)
  • Sepsis can present with normothermia/hypothermia (esp. elderly)
Nice to Know
  • Procalcitonin for antibiotic stewardship
  • Dynamic fluid responsiveness (PLR, SVV)
  • ECMO in refractory septic shock
  • Long-term outcomes: PICS, cognitive/physical impairment

15 Flashcards
  • Q: Sepsis-3 sepsis definition

A: Infection + SOFA increase ≥2

  • Q: Sepsis-3 septic shock

A: Vasopressor for MAP ≥65 + lactate >2 AFTER 30 mL/kg crystalloid

  • Q: Hour-1 Bundle

A: Lactate, Cultures, Antibiotics, 30 mL/kg Fluid, Vasopressors

  • Q: qSOFA

A: RR≥22, GCS<15, SBP≤100 (≥2 = positive)

  • Q: First-line vasopressor

A: Noradrenaline (start peripherally early)

  • Q: Vasopressin dose

A: 0.01–0.04 U/min (add-on at NA ~0.25–0.5)

  • Q: Fluid responsiveness test

A: PLR + CO monitoring (gold); SVV/PPV if ventilated

  • Q: Hydrocortisone indication

A: Vasopressor-dependent septic shock — 200 mg/day

  • Q: Source control

A: Drainable foci ≤6h; necrotising fasciitis = immediate surgery

  • Q: Refractory septic shock

A: NA >0.5 + vasopressin + still hypotensive → adrenaline, hydrocortisone, consider ECMO
