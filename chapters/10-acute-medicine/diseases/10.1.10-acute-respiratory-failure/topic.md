> [!important]
> Acute respiratory failure = inability of the respiratory system to maintain adequate gas exchange (PaO₂ <8 kPa or PaCO₂ >6.7 kPa on room air).

## 1. Learning Objectives
- Classify respiratory failure into Type 1 and Type 2
- Recognise clinical signs of respiratory distress and fatigue
- Understand when to escalate to NIV or intubation
- Apply oxygen therapy safely

## 2. Classification
| Type | PaO₂ | PaCO₂ | A-a gradient | Examples |
|------|------|-------|--------------|----------|
| **Type 1 (Hypoxaemic)** | Low | Normal/low | ↑ | Pneumonia, PE, pulmonary oedema, ARDS |
| **Type 2 (Hypercapnic)** | Low | High | Normal | COPD exacerbation, neuromuscular disease, drug overdose |

## 3. Clinical Features of Impending Respiratory Arrest
- Use of accessory muscles, paradoxical breathing
- Unable to speak full sentences
- Respiratory rate >35 or <8
- GCS falling, agitation
- Rising PaCO₂ with acidosis

## 4. Management Principles
- **Oxygen**: Target SpO₂ 94–98% (88–92% in COPD with risk of hypercapnia)
- **Position**: Sitting upright
- **Treat cause**: Bronchodilators, diuretics, antibiotics, anticoagulation
- **NIV**: First-line in Type 2 failure (COPD, cardiogenic pulmonary oedema)
- **Intubation**: When NIV fails or patient is tiring / comatose

## 5. Red Flags
- pH <7.25 in Type 2 failure
- Rising PaCO₂ despite NIV
- Exhaustion, confusion, hypotension

## 6. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- Never give uncontrolled high-flow oxygen in COPD (risk of CO₂ retention)
- Rising respiratory rate is often the first sign of deterioration
- ABG is essential — SpO₂ alone misses hypercapnia

## 7. Common Viva Questions
1. Differentiate Type 1 and Type 2 respiratory failure.
2. What are the indications for NIV in acute respiratory failure?
3. When would you intubate a patient with respiratory failure?

## 8. One-Page Revision Summary
**Type 1**: Hypoxaemia + normal CO₂ (lung parenchymal disease)
**Type 2**: Hypoxaemia + hypercapnia (ventilatory failure)
Treat cause + controlled O₂ + NIV/intubation when needed.

## 9. Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
### Must Know
- Type 1 vs Type 2 classification (PaO₂, PaCO₂, A-a gradient)
- ABG essential — SpO₂ misses hypercapnia
- O₂ targets: 94–98% (88–92% in COPD)
- NIV first-line in Type 2 (COPD, cardiogenic oedema)
- Intubation when NIV fails or patient tiring

### Should Know
- Clinical signs of respiratory fatigue (accessory muscles, paradoxical breathing, unable to speak)
- pH <7.25 = intubation threshold in Type 2
- Controlled O₂ in COPD — never uncontrolled high-flow

### Nice to Know
- A-a gradient calculation and interpretation
- High-flow nasal oxygen (HFNO) role
- ECMO criteria in refractory failure

## 12. Flashcards
- Q: Type 1 failure
  A: Low PaO₂, normal/low PaCO₂, ↑ A-a gradient (parenchymal disease)
- Q: Type 2 failure
  A: Low PaO₂, HIGH PaCO₂, normal A-a gradient (ventilatory failure)
- Q: COPD O₂ target
  A: 88–92% (avoid CO₂ retention)
- Q: Non-COPD O₂ target
  A: 94–98%
- Q: NIV first-line for Type 2
  A: BiPAP for COPD; CPAP for cardiogenic oedema
- Q: Intubation threshold Type 2
  A: pH <7.25 despite NIV, exhaustion, confusion
- Q: ABG essential because
  A: SpO₂ misses hypercapnia
- Q: Silent chest in asthma
  A: Impending respiratory arrest — intubate
- Q: Rising RR
  A: Often FIRST sign of deterioration
