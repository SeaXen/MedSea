Invasive Mechanical Ventilation - Basics
1 Learning Objectives
  • Know indications for intubation and IMV
  • Understand basic ventilator modes and settings
  • Recognise common complications
  • Apply lung-protective ventilation principles
2 Indications for Intubation / IMV
  • Apnoea / respiratory arrest
  • GCS ≤8 (airway protection)
  • Refractory hypoxaemia (SpO₂ <90% on high-flow O₂)
  • Refractory hypercapnia / acidosis (pH <7.25)
  • Impending fatigue (rising RR, accessory muscle use)
  • Severe shock with high ventilatory demand
3 Basic Modes
ModeDescriptionUse
------------------------
**VCV** (Volume Control)Set tidal volume, RRStandard mode, guarantees minute volume
**PCV** (Pressure Control)Set inspiratory pressureBetter pressure limitation, ARDS
**PRVC / SIMV**Pressure-regulated volume / Synchronised IMVWeaning, transitional
4 Lung-Protective Ventilation (ARDSNet)
  • Tidal volume: 6 mL/kg predicted body weight
  • Plateau pressure: <30 cmH₂O
  • PEEP: titrated to FiO₂ (ARDSNet table)
  • FiO₂: target SpO₂ 88–95% (PaO₂ 8–12 kPa)
  • Permissive hypercapnia accepted (pH >7.20)
5 Key Settings
  • RR: 12–20 (adjust for pH/CO₂)
  • PEEP: 5–15 cmH₂O (higher in ARDS)
  • FiO₂: lowest to maintain target SpO₂
  • I:E ratio: 1:2 normally; inverse in severe ARDS
6 Complications
  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP)
  • Barotrauma / volutrauma
  • Haemodynamic compromise (reduced venous return)
  • Ventilator-induced lung injury (VILI)
7 Red Flags
  • Rising peak / plateau pressures
  • Sudden hypotension post-intubation
  • Auto-PEEP (dynamic hyperinflation)
8 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Low tidal volume (6 mL/kg) is the single most evidence-based intervention in ARDS
  • Always check for auto-PEEP in obstructive airways disease
  • Hypotension after intubation = usually hypovolaemia or reduced preload
