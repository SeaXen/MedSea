Malignant Hyperthermia Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome
1 Malignant Hyperthermia (MH)
Definition

Pharmacogenetic disorder of skeletal muscle calcium regulation triggered by volatile anaesthetics or suxamethonium. Uncontrolled Ca²⁺ release → hypermetabolism, rigidity, hyperthermia.

Triggers
  • All volatile agents (sevoflurane, isoflurane, desflurane, halothane)
  • Suxamethonium (depolarising muscle relaxant)
Clinical Features (Perioperative)
  • Masseter spasm / generalised rigidity (especially after suxamethonium)
  • Rising ETCO₂ (earliest sign)
  • Tachycardia, arrhythmias
  • Hyperthermia (can rise >1°C/5 min, >41°C)
  • Metabolic + respiratory acidosis
  • Rhabdomyolysis → myoglobinuria, hyperkalaemia
  • DIC, renal failure
Immediate Management
  1. STOP TRIGGERS — end volatile, stop suxamethonium
  2. Hyperventilate 100% O₂, high flows
  3. Dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV — repeat up to 10 mg/kg (specific antidote, ryanodine receptor antagonist)
  4. Cool actively — ice packs, cold IV fluids, lavage
  5. Treat hyperkalaemia (insulin/dextrose, calcium, bicarbonate)
  6. Monitor: core temp, ABG, CK, electrolytes, ECG, urine output
Red Flags
  • Unexplained rising ETCO₂ + rigidity in theatre
  • Family history of MH / anaesthetic deaths
MH Testing
  • Caffeine-halothane contracture test (muscle biopsy)
  • Genetic testing (RYR1, CACNA1S)
2 Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)
Definition

Idiosyncratic reaction to dopamine antagonists (antipsychotics, antiemetics). Dopamine blockade → hyperthermia, rigidity, autonomic instability.

Culprit Drugs
  • Typical antipsychotics: haloperidol, chlorpromazine, fluphenazine
  • Atypical antipsychotics: risperidone, olanzapine, clozapine
  • Antiemetics: metoclopramide, prochlorperazine
  • Onset: days to weeks after start / dose increase
Clinical Features (FEVER mnemonic)
  • Fever (≥38°C, often >40°C)
  • Encephalopathy (confusion, agitation, coma)
  • Vital sign instability (tachycardia, labile BP, diaphoresis)
  • Extreme rigidity (lead-pipe, "cogwheel")
  • Raised CK (often massive: >10,000)
Differential
  • MH (anaesthetic trigger)
  • Serotonin syndrome (clonus, hyperreflexia, serotonergic drugs)
  • Malignant catatonia
  • Sepsis
Diagnosis
  • Clinical (Hunter criteria for serotonin syndrome if confusion + clonus + serotonergic drug)
  • Exclude infection, metabolic, structural
  • CK massively elevated
Management
  1. STOP OFFENDING DRUG
  2. Supportive: cooling, hydration, electrolytes
  3. Dantrolene (also used in NMS, 1–2.5 mg/kg IV q6h)
  4. Bromocriptine 2.5 mg TDS (dopamine agonist)
  5. Amantadine (alternative dopamine agonist)
  6. Avoid dopamine antagonists

4 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • MH: rising ETCO₂ + rigidity + hyperthermia in theatre → dantrolene immediately
  • NMS: antipsychotic onset + fever + rigidity + high CK → stop drug + dantrolene + bromocriptine
  • Differentiate NMS from serotonin syndrome (clonus, hyperreflexia, diarrhoea)
  • Dantrolene works for both

7 Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
Must Know
  • MH: volatile/sux trigger → rising ETCO₂ + rigidity + hyperthermia → dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV
  • NMS: antipsychotic trigger → FEVER (Fever, Encephalopathy, Vital instability, Extreme rigidity, Raised CK) → stop drug + dantrolene + bromocriptine
  • Dantrolene works for both (ryanodine receptor antagonist)
  • Differentiate from serotonin syndrome (clonus, hyperreflexia, diarrhoea, serotonergic drugs)
Should Know
  • MH: dantrolene up to 10 mg/kg, active cooling, treat hyperkalaemia
  • NMS: bromocriptine 2.5 mg TDS, amantadine alternative
  • MH genetic: RYR1, CACNA1S; caffeine-halothane contracture test
  • NMS onset: days–weeks after start/dose increase
Nice to Know
  • Malignant catatonia differential
  • Prophylactic dantrolene in known MH susceptibility
  • Role of IVIG in severe NMS
  • RyR1 mutations also linked to exertional heat illness
