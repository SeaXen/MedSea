> [!important]
> Distributive shock = pathological vasodilatation with relative hypovolaemia. The most **common** form is septic shock.

## 1. Learning Objectives
- Recognise the haemodynamic profile of distributive shock
- Differentiate aetiologies: septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic, adrenal crisis
- Apply vasopressor strategy appropriately
- Identify and treat the underlying cause

## 2. Definition
Shock caused by widespread vasodilatation → reduced systemic vascular resistance (SVR) → relative hypovolaemia despite normal absolute volume.

## 3. Classification
| Type              | Mechanism                    | Key Feature                    |
|-------------------|------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Septic**        | Endotoxin + cytokine storm   | Warm peripheries, high CO      |
| **Anaphylactic**  | Mast cell degranulation      | Rapid onset, urticaria, angioedema |
| **Neurogenic**    | Loss of sympathetic tone     | Bradycardia + warm peripheries |
| **Adrenal crisis**| Cortisol deficiency          | Hypoglycaemia, hyponatraemia   |

## 4. Haemodynamic Profile
- **High** cardiac output (initially)
- **Low** SVR
- **Low** MAP
- **Warm**, bounding peripheries (until decompensation)

## 5. Management
- Fluids: large-volume crystalloids initially
- Vasopressors: **Noradrenaline** first-line (except neurogenic where phenylephrine may be used)
- **Treat cause**: antibiotics, adrenaline, steroids, hormone replacement

## 6. Red Flags
- Refractory hypotension on high-dose vasopressors
- Deteriorating mental status
- Lactate rising despite resuscitation

## 7. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- Neurogenic shock: **bradycardia** + hypotension + warm skin (differentiates from other shocks)
- Adrenal crisis: consider in any unexplained hypotension + hyponatraemia + hyperkalaemia
- Anaphylaxis needs **adrenaline first** — fluids and vasopressors are adjuncts

## 10. Must Know / Should Know / Nice to Know
### Must Know
- Definition and haemodynamic profile (high CO, low SVR)
- Four subtypes: septic, anaphylactic, neurogenic, adrenal crisis
- Noradrenaline first-line (except neurogenic)
- Neurogenic = bradycardia + hypotension

### Should Know
- Adrenal crisis: hypotension + hyponatraemia + hyperkalaemia
- Anaphylaxis: adrenaline IM first, fluids second
- Vasopressin as catecholamine-sparing add-on

### Nice to Know
- Detailed receptor mechanisms
- Angiotensin II for refractory vasoplegia
- ECMO in refractory distributive shock

## 14. Answer Key with Explanations
**MCQ 1**: C — Distributive shock = vasodilatation → warm peripheries.
**MCQ 2**: B — Neurogenic = loss of sympathetic tone.
**MCQ 3**: B — Noradrenaline first-line.
**MCQ 4**: C — Adrenaline IM first and most important.
**MCQ 5**: B — Bradycardia + hypotension (unopposed vagal tone).
**MCQ 6**: B — Cortisol deficiency → aldosterone deficiency → Na loss, K retention.
**MCQ 7**: C — High CO initially (compensatory).
**MCQ 8**: C — Low SVR (vasodilatation).
**MCQ 9**: B — Add-on to spare noradrenaline.
**MCQ 10**: B — Large-volume crystalloids initially.

**SBA 1**: C — Spinal injury + bradycardia + hypotension + warm skin = neurogenic.
**SBA 2**: A — Adrenal crisis = fluids + hydrocortisone 100mg IV immediate.
**SBA 3**: C — Adrenaline IM is first-line and life-saving.
**SBA 4**: B — Add vasopressin at NA ~0.25–0.5 to spare catecholamines.
**SBA 5**: C — Phenylephrine (pure α₁) preferred in neurogenic to avoid tachycardia.
**SBA 6**: C — Distributive = warm peripheries (initially).
**SBA 7**: C — Both steroid withdrawal and stress precipitate crisis.
**SBA 8**: B — IV adrenaline only if IM failed / cardiovascular collapse.
**SBA 9**: B — Chronic steroids + stress → relative adrenal insufficiency.
**SBA 10**: B — V₁ receptor agonist, non-catecholamine.
