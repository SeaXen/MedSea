Critical Care Monitoring
1 Learning Objectives
  • List essential monitoring in the critically ill
  • Interpret basic haemodynamic parameters
  • Know indications for invasive monitoring
  • Recognise monitoring limitations
2 Standard Monitoring (All ICU Patients)
  • ECG (continuous) — arrhythmia, ischaemia
  • SpO₂ (continuous) — oxygenation
  • NIBP / Arterial line (continuous BP) — beat-to-beat, ABG access
  • Temperature (continuous)
  • Urine output (hourly via catheter) — gold standard for renal perfusion
  • Fluid balance (strict daily)
3 Invasive Monitoring (Selected Patients)
ParameterIndicationMethod
-------------------------------
**CVP**Fluid responsiveness, RV functionCentral line
**Arterial BP**Beat-to-beat, vasoactive drugs, frequent ABGArterial line
**Cardiac Output**Unclear shock type, guided therapyPiCCO, LiDCO, echocardiography
**ScvO₂ / SvO₂**Tissue oxygen extractionCentral line / PA catheter
**ICP**Severe TBI, raised ICPEVD / bolt
4 Echocardiography (Bedside / Focused)
  • FICE / FATE — focused assessment in emergency
  • Assesses: LV/RV function, volume status, tamponade, PE, valve pathology
5 Haemodynamic Targets (General)
  • MAP ≥65 mmHg (individualise)
  • ScvO₂ ≥70% or SvO₂ ≥65%
  • Lactate clearance ≥10–20% per 2 hours
  • Urine output ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr
6 Red Flags
  • Rising lactate despite "normal" BP
  • Falling urine output
  • New arrhythmias
  • Rising ventilator pressures
7 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Urine output is the most sensitive real-time marker of renal perfusion
  • Arterial line is essential if on vasoactive drugs
  • CVP is a poor predictor of fluid responsiveness — use dynamic measures (SVV, PPV, PLR)
8 Common Viva Questions

  2. What is the target ScvO₂ in septic shock?
  3. Why is CVP a poor predictor of fluid responsiveness?
9 One-Page Revision Summary

Monitor: ECG, SpO₂, BP, Temp, Urine, Fluid balance Invasive: arterial line, CVP, CO if needed Targets: MAP ≥65, ScvO₂ ≥70, UO ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr Urine output = best real-time perfusion marker

14 Answer Key with Explanations

MCQ 1: B — Urine output hourly = most sensitive real-time. MCQ 2: B — Arterial line mandatory for vasoactives. MCQ 3: C — ScvO₂ ≥70% in septic shock. MCQ 4: B — CVP reflects pressure, not preload responsiveness. MCQ 5: C — PLR is dynamic (gold standard). MCQ 6: B — ≥10–20% per 2 hours. MCQ 7: B — SvO₂ 65–75%. MCQ 8: B — PiCCO = CO + EVLW + GEDV. MCQ 9: C — UO ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr. MCQ 10: A — FICE/FATE = focused assessment.

SBA 1: B — Rising lactate + low UO = inadequate perfusion despite "normal" MAP. SBA 2: B — Arterial line mandatory on vasoactives. SBA 3: A — SVV >13% = fluid responsive (if conditions met). SBA 4: B — Low ScvO₂ = inadequate DO₂ (anaemia, low CO, high VO₂). SBA 5: B — GCS ≤8 + abnormal CT = ICP monitor indication. SBA 6: B — Assess responsiveness first, don't blindly fluid load. SBA 7: B — PA catheter = PA pressure, PCWP, CO, SvO₂. SBA 8: A — NIRS = regional tissue oxygenation. SBA 9: B — High ScvO₂ + high lactate = cytopathic hypoxia (mitochondrial failure). SBA 10: D — Coronary angiography not an echo view. SBA 11: B — Neutral/slightly negative balance after resuscitation phase.
