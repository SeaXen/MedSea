Hymenoptera Stings (Bee, Wasp, Ant) and Anaphylaxis
1 Learning Objectives
  • Differentiate bee vs wasp vs ant venom composition
  • Recognise anaphylaxis vs large local reaction
  • Apply emergency management of anaphylaxis (IM adrenaline first-line)
  • Understand venom immunotherapy (VIT) indications
  • Manage fire ant and other ant stings
  • Apply to FCPS/MRCP vignettes
2 Definitions & Key Concepts
TermDefinition
------------------
**Hymenoptera**Order: bees, wasps, ants — modified ovipositor = stinger
**Anaphylaxis**Acute IgE-mediated hypersensitivity — rapid onset, multisystem
**Large local reaction (LLR)**>10 cm swelling, peaks 24–48h, resolves 5–10 days — NOT anaphylaxis
**Biphasic anaphylaxis**Recurrence 4–12h (up to 72h) after initial resolution
**Venom Immunotherapy (VIT)**Subcutaneous escalating doses → 95–98% protection for 3–5 years
**Melittin**Bee venom major peptide — membrane lysis, pain, mast cell degranulation
**Phospholipase A1/A2**Major allergens (Api m 1, Ves v 1) — membrane degradation
**Antigen 5 (Ag5)**Wasp/ant major allergen — pathogenesis unclear
**Solenopsin**Fire ant alkaloid venom — pustule formation, necrosis
3 Core Content
Section 1: Hymenoptera Biology & Venom Composition

#### Bee (Apis mellifera — Honeybee)

AspectDetail
----------------
**Stinger****Barbed** — remains embedded with venom sac; continues pumping
**Stings**Single (stinger tears away → bee dies)
**Venom yield**~50–140 µg dry weight
**Major allergens****Api m 1** (phospholipase A2), **Api m 2** (hyaluronidase), **Api m 3** (melittin), **Api m 10** (icarapin)
**Key components**Melittin (50% dry wt) — membrane lysis, pain; PLA2 — membrane degradation; hyaluronidase — spread; mast cell degranulating peptide
**Distribution**Worldwide (domesticated + feral)

#### Wasp (Vespula, Polistes, Dolichovespula — Yellowjackets, Paper Wasps, Hornets)

AspectDetail
----------------
**Stinger****Smooth** — no barbs; can sting repeatedly
**Stings**Multiple
**Venom yield**~1–17 µg dry weight (less than bee)
**Major allergens****Ves v 1** (phospholipase A1), **Ves v 5** (antigen 5), **Ves v 2** (hyaluronidase)
**Key components**Phospholipase A1, antigen 5 (Ag5), mastoparan (mast cell degranulation), kinins, serotonin
**Distribution**Worldwide; *Vespula* (ground-nesting) most aggressive

#### Ant (Formicidae)

SpeciesVenomClinical
--------------------------
**Fire ant (*Solenopsis invicta/richteri*)****Piperidine alkaloids (solenopsin)** — cytotoxic, necroticImmediate burning → **sterile pustule at 24h** (diagnostic); anaphylaxis risk
**Jack jumper ant (*Myrmecia pilosula* — Australia)**Protein venom (Myr p 1, Myr p 2) — similar to wasp**Major cause of anaphylaxis in Tasmania/SE Australia**
**Bulldog ant (*Myrmecia* spp.)**Protein venomSevere anaphylaxis
**Harvester ant (*Pogonomyrmex* — USA)**Protein venomPainful, local reaction, rare anaphylaxis
4 Clinical Presentation
Anaphylaxis (IgE-Mediated)

#### Diagnostic Criteria (WAO / Resuscitation Council UK) Acute onset (minutes–hours) + at least ONE of:

  1. Skin/mucosal — urticaria, angioedema, flushing, itching
  2. Respiratory — wheeze, stridor, dyspnoea, hypoxia, hoarseness
  3. Circulatory — hypotension, tachycardia, syncope, shock
  4. Gastrointestinal — vomiting, abdominal pain, incontinence (supportive)
FeatureTiming
-----------------
**Onset**Minutes (typically < 30 min)
**Peak**5–30 min
**Biphasic**4–12h (up to 72h) after resolution
**Late**Serum sickness-like (days) — rare

#### Clinical Features by System

SystemFeatures
------------------
**Skin**Generalised urticaria, angioedema (lips, tongue, eyelids), flushing, pruritus
**Respiratory**Laryngeal oedema (stridor, hoarseness), bronchospasm (wheeze), dyspnoea, hypoxia
**Cardiovascular**Hypotension (distributive shock), tachycardia (reflex or direct), arrhythmia, syncope
**GI**Nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, diarrhoea
**Neurological**Anxiety, sense of doom, confusion, loss of consciousness

Fire Ant (*Solenopsis*) Envenomation
FeatureDetail
-----------------
**Bite + sting**Mandibles grip → pivot → multiple stings in circle
**Venom**95% piperidine alkaloids (solenopsin) — cytotoxic
**Immediate**Intense burning ("fire"), wheal-and-flare
**Characteristic****Sterile pustule at 24h** (cloudy fluid, not pus)
**Allergic**Local reaction common; systemic anaphylaxis in ~1%
**Secondary**Infection if pustule ruptured; scarring
Jack Jumper Ant (*Myrmecia pilosula*) — Australia
FeatureDetail
-----------------
**Region**Tasmania, SE Australia (Victoria, NSW, ACT)
**Significance****Major cause of ant anaphylaxis** — 2–3% of population sensitised
**Venom**Protein (Myr p 1, Myr p 2) — cross-reacts with wasp venom
**VIT available**Yes — jack jumper ant VIT (Tasmania)
**Anaphylaxis rate**~3% per sting in sensitised

7 Venom Immunotherapy (VIT)
Indications (Must Meet **BOTH**)
CriterionDetail
-------------------
**1. Clinical****Recurrent systemic anaphylaxis** to Hymenoptera sting (≥2 episodes) OR **1 episode + high risk** (high exposure, mastocytosis, high baseline tryptase)
**2. Immunological****Positive skin test** AND/OR **specific IgE** to relevant venom
**NOT indicated for:**Large local reactions only, single mild systemic reaction (cutaneous only), positive test without clinical history
Protocol (Standard)
PhaseDurationDose Escalation
----------------------------------
**Build-up**12–16 weeks (conventional) / days–weeks (rush/ultra-rush)Weekly/biweekly → maintenance dose
**Maintenance****3–5 years**100 µg venom (∼1–2 stings equivalent) every 4–8 weeks
**Discontinuation**After 3–5 years if low riskShared decision — some continue >5y
Efficacy & Safety
MetricValue
---------------
**Protection rate****95–98%** against systemic reaction
**Duration****3–5 years** after stop — waning immunity
**Systemic reaction rate**5–15% of injections (mostly mild)
**Fatalities**Extremely rare (< 1: million injections)
**Contraindications**Uncontrolled asthma, active autoimmune, β-blockers (relative), pregnancy (continue if already on; don't start)
Adjunct: Omalizumab (Anti-IgE)
RoleDetail
--------------
**Indication**Recurrent systemic reactions during VIT build-up; high baseline tryptase; mastocytosis
**Dose**150–300 mg SC q2–4wks per IgE/weight
**Effect**Reduces systemic reactions during build-up, allows faster up-titration
**Not**Replacement for VIT

FeatureManagement

24 Viva Questions (10)

Q1: What is the first-line treatment for anaphylaxis and why is IM preferred over IV? A: IM adrenaline 0.5mg (1:1000) anterolateral thigh. IM is preferred because: safer (lower risk of arrhythmia, hypertensive crisis), easier administration (no IV access needed), reliable absorption (vastus lateralis), and easier to titrate than IV bolus. IV adrenaline reserved for cardiac arrest or refractory shock with cardiac monitoring.

Q2: How do you differentiate a large local reaction (LLR) from anaphylaxis? A: LLR = >10 cm swelling peaking at 24–48h, NO respiratory or circulatory compromise, tryptase normal. Anaphylaxis = acute onset + skin/mucosal features + (respiratory OR circulatory compromise), tryptase elevated (peak 1–2h). LLR managed with antihistamines + oral steroids; anaphylaxis requires IM adrenaline.

Q3: What is the correct way to remove a honeybee stinger and why? A: Scrape off (credit card, fingernail, knife edge) — DO NOT squeeze or pull with forceps. The barbed stinger remains attached to the venom sac; squeezing injects the remaining venom. Scraping removes the stinger intact with the sac.

Q4: What are the indications for venom immunotherapy (VIT)? A: Recurrent systemic anaphylaxis (≥2 episodes) OR 1 episode with high-risk features (high exposure, mastocytosis, elevated baseline tryptase) PLUS positive skin test and/or specific IgE to relevant venom. NOT for LLR, not for single mild cutaneous reaction.

Q5: What is the VIT protocol and efficacy? A: Build-up 12–16 weeks (conventional) to maintenance dose 100 µg venom (∼1–2 sting equivalents), then maintenance every 4–8 weeks for 3–5 years. Efficacy 95–98% protection against systemic reactions. Waning immunity after stopping.

Q6: What is biphasic anaphylaxis and how is it managed? A: Recurrence of anaphylaxis symptoms 4–12h (up to 72h) after initial resolution. Occurs in 1–20% of cases. Management: observe minimum 6–12h in ED (longer if severe), consider prednisolone 30–50 mg × 3 days (controversial), written action plan, EpiPen on discharge.

Q7: Describe fire ant envenomation and its management. A: Solenopsis — pivots on mandibles, multiple stings in circular pattern. Venom = piperidine alkaloids (solenopsin). Clinical: immediate burning, wheal-and-flare → sterile pustule at 24h (diagnostic). Management: antihistamines, topical steroids, keep pustules intact (do not incise), monitor for anaphylaxis. Secondary infection if ruptured.

**Q8: What is the significance of jack jumper ant (Myrmecia pilosula) in Australia? A: Major cause of ant anaphylaxis in Tasmania/SE Australia — 2–3% of population sensitised. Protein venom (Myr p 1, Myr p 2) cross-reacts with wasp venom. VIT available** (Tasmania). Anaphylaxis rate ~3% per sting in sensitised.

Q9: How does mastocytosis affect anaphylaxis risk and management? A: Mastocytosis = clonal mast cell disorder → increased risk of severe anaphylaxis, may present without skin signs. Screen with baseline tryptase > 20 µg/L → bone marrow biopsy. VIT indicated for any anaphylaxis (higher maintenance dose 200 µg, longer duration). Omalizumab strongly recommended as adjunct. Carry 2 EpiPens.

Q10: Why is glucagon used for anaphylaxis in patients on β-blockers? A: β-blockers blunt the response to adrenaline (β2-mediated bronchodilation, vasodilation, chronotropy). Glucagon bypasses β-receptors → increases cAMP directly → positive inotropy, chronotropy, bronchodilation. Dose: 1–5 mg IV bolus, then 1–5 mg/h infusion. Also consider atropine for bradycardia, vasopressin for refractory shock.
