# Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and dementia in the elderly

---

## Definition

Mild cognitive impairment (MCI) is a state of objectively measurable cognitive decline that is not severe enough to interfere significantly with daily activities. Dementia is an acquired, progressive cognitive decline severe enough to affect daily functioning. Both are common in older adults and require comprehensive assessment.

## Core Clinical Content

### MCI definition
Subjective + objective cognitive decline; preserved ADL; not dementia

### MCI prevalence
15-20% of ≥65; 5-15% progress to dementia per year

### MCI types
Amnestic (↑ risk Alzheimer's) vs non-amnestic (↑ risk other dementias)

### Reversible causes of cognitive impairment
DELIRIUM, Depression, Drugs, Deficiencies (B12, folate, thyroid), Normal pressure hydrocephalus

### Differential diagnosis
Dementia, delirium, depression, drugs, metabolic, neurological

### Investigation
Bloods (TFT, B12, folate, calcium), CT/MRI, MMSE/MoCA, neuropsychology

### Dementia types
Alzheimer's (60-70%), vascular (15-25%), Lewy body (10-15%), frontotemporal (5-10%)

### Mild cognitive impairment management
Risk factor modification (BP, lipids, exercise), cognitive training, monitor

### Prognosis
MCI: 5-15% progress/year; risk higher with APOE4, vascular risk factors

### Driving and dementia
DVLA must be notified; assess by specialist; usually cease driving
