# Theories and mechanisms of ageing

---

## Definition

Multiple theories of ageing have been proposed, broadly divided into programmed (genetic, evolutionary) and stochastic (damage) theories. Modern understanding integrates both, with hallmarks including genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, loss of proteostasis, deregulated nutrient sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, stem cell exhaustion, and altered intercellular communication.

## Core Clinical Content

### 12 hallmarks of ageing (López-Otín)
Genomic instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, loss of proteostasis, deregulated nutrient sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence, stem cell exhaustion, altered intercellular communication, disabled macroautophagy, chronic inflammation, dysbiosis

### Programmed theories
Evolutionary (antagonistic pleiotropy), neuroendocrine, immunological

### Antagonistic pleiotropy
Genes beneficial early (reproduction) are harmful late (ageing)

### Disposable soma theory
Limited resources for repair vs reproduction; ageing = accumulated damage

### Damage theories
Free radical/oxidative stress, DNA damage, error catastrophe, wear and tear

### Mitochondrial theory
Mitochondrial dysfunction → ↑ ROS → cellular damage; vicious cycle

### Hayflick limit
Cells can only divide ~50 times; related to telomere length

### mTOR pathway
Nutrient sensing; inhibited by caloric restriction; mTOR inhibitors (rapamycin) extend lifespan

### Sirtuins
NAD+-dependent deacetylases; activated by caloric restriction; SIRT1-7

### Senolytics
Drugs that clear senescent cells; potential anti-ageing therapy (in research)
