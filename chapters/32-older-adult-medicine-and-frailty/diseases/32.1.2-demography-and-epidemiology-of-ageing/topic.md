# Demography and epidemiology of ageing

---

## Definition

The demography of ageing describes the changing population structure with increasing numbers and proportions of older adults globally, driven by declining fertility, increased life expectancy, and population ageing. This has major implications for healthcare, social, and economic systems.

## Core Clinical Content

### Population ageing
Declining fertility + increased longevity = ↑ median age and proportion of older adults

### Demographic transition
Stage 5: low birth rate, low death rate, ageing population (developed countries)

### Life expectancy at 65
UK: ~20 more years (men 18, women 21); improving but compressing morbidity

### Healthy life expectancy
Years lived in good health; gap with total life expectancy = years with disability

### Old age groups
Young-old 65-74, old 75-84, oldest-old ≥85; very old ≥90 ('super-elderly')

### Compression of morbidity
Hypothesis: most disability compressed into final years if healthy behaviours

### Compression vs expansion
Compression: disability compressed; expansion: more years with disability

### Demographic dividend
Economic benefit from large working-age population before ageing

### Old-age dependency ratio
≥65 / 15-64; increasing rapidly in developed countries

### Implications
↑ chronic disease, dementia, frailty, healthcare costs; need for healthy ageing
