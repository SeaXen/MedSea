## 6. MCQs (Chapter-Specific)

### MCQ 1
**Q: An 82-year-old man is admitted after a fall. Examination shows confusion, urinary incontinence, and unsteady gait. Which is the most likely diagnosis?**
A) Dementia (Alzheimer's)
B) Delirium
C) Vascular dementia
D) Parkinson's disease
E) Subdural haematoma

**Answer: B — Delirium**
Acute onset, fluctuating course, inattention + altered consciousness. Common precipitants in elderly: infection, drugs, metabolic disturbance. Always rule out and treat underlying cause.

### MCQ 2
**Q: Which of the following is a reversible cause of dementia?**
A) Alzheimer's disease
B) Lewy body dementia
C) Normal pressure hydrocephalus
D) Frontotemporal dementia
E) Parkinson's dementia

**Answer: C — Normal pressure hydrocephalus**
Classic triad: dementia + gait disturbance ('magnetic gait') + urinary incontinence ('wet, wobbly, wacky'). Reversible with VP shunt.

### MCQ 3
**Q: A 78-year-old with hip fracture. Pre-operative assessment finds polypharmacy (9 medications). Which is the most appropriate approach?**
A) Continue all medications throughout perioperative period
B) Apply STOPP/START criteria for medication review; deprescribe where appropriate
C) Stop all medications 7 days pre-op
D) Add more medications for comorbidity
E) Switch all to oral liquids

**Answer: B — STOPP/START criteria + deprescribing**
STOPP/START (Screening Tool of Older Persons' Prescriptions/Screening Tool to Alert to Right Treatment) is the validated deprescribing tool. Reduces ADR risk and polypharmacy burden.

### MCQ 4
**Q: Regarding frailty assessment, which tool combines deficit accumulation with clinical judgement?**
A) Fried phenotype
B) Clinical Frailty Scale (Rockwood)
C) FRAIL scale
D) Edmonton Frail Scale
E) SPPB

**Answer: B — Clinical Frailty Scale (Rockwood)**
CFS (1-9) is the most widely used bedside frailty assessment in UK geriatric practice. Fried phenotype is the research gold standard.

### MCQ 5
**Q: An 80-year-old is found on the floor at home, dehydrated, with pressure ulcer grade 3 over sacrum. The family asks about treatment ceilings. Which is the most appropriate next step?**
A) Immediate surgery for pressure ulcer
B) Comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA) including discussion of goals of care, advance care planning
C) Resuscitation only
D) Transfer to long-term care
E) Palliative care only

**Answer: B — CGA + advance care planning**
CGA is the cornerstone of geriatric assessment for frail older adults. Combined with ACP, it aligns treatment with goals. Decisions should be patient-centred where possible.

### SBAs

**SBA 1:** An 85-year-old with advanced dementia, recurrent aspiration pneumonia, now NPO. Family wants 'everything done'. Which is the MOST appropriate clinical approach?
A) Insert PEG tube despite aspiration risk
B) Careful discussion of goals of care, advance care planning, comfort feeding approach, allow natural death
C) Transfer to ICU for intubation
D) Discontinue all care
E) Insert NG tube

**Answer: B** — PEG tubes do not reduce aspiration or improve survival in advanced dementia. ACP + comfort feeding + oral care is the evidence-based approach. Respect patient dignity.

**SBA 2:** A 78-year-old on warfarin, amiodarone, digoxin, furosemide, ramipril, metformin, atorvastatin, alendronate, omeprazole. Sodium 128, potassium 5.5. Which is the IMMEDIATE priority?
A) Stop alendronate
B) Stop omeprazole
C) Stop digoxin (consider toxicity with hyperkalaemia); review ACE-i, furosemide
D) Add calcium supplementation
E) Continue unchanged

**Answer: C** — Digoxin toxicity is potentiated by hyperkalaemia and renal impairment common in elderly. Review renin-angiotensin blockade and diuretic causing electrolyte disturbance.

**SBA 3:** A 90-year-old falls at home. No injuries but unable to get up. Lives alone. Which is the BEST comprehensive management plan?
A) Discharge home with no follow-up
B) Multifactorial falls assessment: home hazard review, medication review (antihypertensives, sedatives), vision check, bone health (DEXA + calcium/vit D), strength and balance programme (Otago), CGA referral
C) Bed rest for 2 weeks
D) Hip protectors only
E) Move to care home

**Answer: B** — Multifactorial intervention is the evidence-based approach. Each domain (hazards, drugs, vision, bone, exercise) reduces falls independently.

## 7. Flashcards (Anki-ready)

1. **Frailty Fried criteria**: ≥3 of: unintentional weight loss, exhaustion, weakness (grip), slow walking, low physical activity
2. **Clinical Frailty Scale**: Rockwood 1-9, bedside, validated
3. **CGA domains**: Medical, functional, social, cognitive, psychological, environmental
4. **STOPP/START**: Deprescribing tool for older adults
5. **Beers criteria**: List of PIMs (potentially inappropriate medications) in elderly
6. **Refeeding syndrome**: Hypophosphataemia, hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia; start low (5-10 kcal/kg/day), supplement thiamine
7. **Delirium vs dementia**: Acute vs chronic onset; fluctuating vs progressive; inattention hallmark of delirium
8. **Pressure ulcer grades**: 1 (non-blanching erythema) → 4 (full thickness tissue loss)
9. **NPH triad**: Wet, wobbly, wacky (incontinence, gait, dementia); treatable with VP shunt
10. **BPSD**: Behavioural and psychological symptoms of dementia; non-pharmacological first-line
11. **Otago programme**: Home-based strength and balance exercises for falls prevention
12. **Anticholinergic burden**: Cumulative effect increases delirium, falls, cognitive decline; review with ACB scale
13. **MUST score**: Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool
14. **Driving assessment**: DVLA notification for dementia, syncope, vision <6/12
15. **ACP**: Advance care planning — patient-led discussion of future care wishes
