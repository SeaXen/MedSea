# Pain management in the elderly

---

## Definition

Pain management in the elderly requires careful assessment, consideration of comorbidities, polypharmacy, and altered pharmacokinetics, with appropriate use of the WHO analgesic ladder and non-pharmacological approaches. Pain is often under-recognised and under-treated in older adults.

## Core Clinical Content

### Pain prevalence
25-50% of community elderly; 50-80% of nursing home; chronic pain common

### Pain types
Nociceptive (somatic, visceral), neuropathic, mixed; multiple causes common

### Pain assessment
Verbal rating, numeric rating, faces; in dementia: PAINAD, Abbey Pain Scale, Doloplus-2

### WHO analgesic ladder
Step 1: paracetamol ± adjuvant; Step 2: weak opioid (codeine, tramadol); Step 3: morphine

### Paracetamol
First-line; safe in elderly; max 4 g/day (3 g in frail, liver disease, malnutrition)

### NSAIDs
Use cautiously; ↑ GI bleeding, AKI, cardiovascular risk; shortest course possible

### Opioids in elderly
Start low, go slow; oral preferred; morphine first-line; laxative, antiemetic

### Neuropathic pain
Amitriptyline (cautious in elderly), gabapentin, pregabalin, duloxetine

### Non-pharmacological
Physiotherapy, TENS, acupuncture, CBT, social support

### Opioid side effects
Constipation, nausea, sedation, falls, respiratory depression; respiratory depression rare if titrated
