# Pharmacology and prescribing in the elderly

---

## Definition

Ageing causes pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic changes that alter drug handling and response. Prescribing in the elderly requires careful consideration of these changes, renal/hepatic function, comorbidities, polypharmacy, and patient preferences to optimise efficacy and minimise harm.

## Core Clinical Content

### Pharmacokinetics changes
↓ absorption (less effect), ↓ body water (↑ concentration water-soluble), ↑ fat (↑ retention lipophilic), ↓ lean mass

### Renal function decline
↓ GFR (~1 mL/min/year after 30); ↓ tubular function; ↑ half-life renally-cleared drugs

### Creatinine clearance estimation
Cockcroft-Gault (most accurate in elderly); eGFR overestimates in frail elderly

### Hepatic metabolism
↓ Phase I (oxidation, CYP450) more than Phase II (conjugation)

### Pharmacodynamic changes
↑ sensitivity to drugs (e.g., warfarin, opioids, benzodiazepines); ↓ homeostatic reserve

### Anticholinergic burden
Sum of anticholinergic effects of all medications; ↑ falls, confusion, constipation

### Beers criteria PIMs
Long-acting benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, antipsychotics in dementia

### START criteria
Under-prescribing: ACEi in CHF, bisphosphonates in osteoporosis, vitamin D in deficiency

### Number needed to treat (NNT)
↑ with age; may exceed life expectancy in very elderly

### Start low, go slow
Principle for prescribing in elderly; titrate gradually; review regularly
