# Biology and physiology of ageing

---

## Definition

Ageing is a progressive, universal, and irreversible biological process characterised by progressive loss of physiological function, increased vulnerability to disease, and ultimately death. It involves molecular, cellular, tissue, and systemic changes that accumulate over time.

## Core Clinical Content

### Ageing definition
Progressive, universal, irreversible decline in physiological function; ↑ vulnerability to disease

### Population trends
By 2050, 1 in 6 people globally will be ≥65; fastest growing is ≥80 ('oldest old')

### Theories of ageing
Stochastic (random damage) and programmed (genetically determined)

### Telomere shortening
Cellular ageing; telomeres shorten with each division; telomerase maintains (cancer risk)

### Free radical theory
Mitochondrial ROS damages DNA, proteins, lipids; antioxidants partly protective

### DNA damage theory
Accumulated mutations; ↓ DNA repair with age

### Somatic mutation theory
Accumulation of mutations drives ageing phenotypes

### Inflammageing
Chronic low-grade inflammation with age; ↑ IL-6, TNF-α, CRP

### Cellular senescence
Permanent cell cycle arrest; SASP (senescence-associated secretory phenotype)

### Caloric restriction
Most consistent intervention to extend lifespan (in animals)
