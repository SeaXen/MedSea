2 🧠 Core Concept: PK Changes in Liver Disease

```mermaid flowchart TD A[Cirrhosis / Liver Disease] --> B[Architectural Distortion<br>↓ Hepatocyte mass<br>Portosystemic shunting] B --> C1[↓ FIRST-PASS METABOLISM<br>↑ Bioavailability (oral)<br>High extraction drugs: propranolol, morphine, nitrates, verapamil] B --> C2[↓ HEPATIC CLEARANCE<br>↓ CYP450 (1A2, 2C9, 2C19, 3A4)<br>↓ Phase II (UGT, SULT, GST)<br>Low extraction drugs: warfarin, phenytoin, theophylline] B --> C3[↓ PROTEIN SYNTHESIS<br>↓ Albumin → ↑ Free fraction (acidic drugs: warfarin, phenytoin, valproate)<br>↓ α1-AGP → ↑ Free fraction (basic drugs: lidocaine, propranolol)] B --> C4[↑ VOLUME OF DISTRIBUTION<br>Ascites, oedema → ↑ Vd (hydrophilic drugs: aminoglycosides, digoxin)<br>↓ Albumin → ↑ Vd (protein-bound drugs)] B --> C5[RENAL IMPAIRMENT (HRS)<br>↓ GFR → ↓ Renal clearance<br>Combined hepatorenal dosing needed]

C1 --> D[CLINICAL EFFECT: ↑ Drug levels, ↑ toxicity risk] C2 --> D C3 --> D C4 --> D C5 --> D ```

3 ️⃣ Drug Dosing Principles in Hepatic Impairment
General Rules
PrincipleApplication
------------------------
**Avoid hepatotoxic drugs**Intrinsic (dose-dependent) + Idiosyncratic (unpredictable) — see Section 2
**Reduce dose for high extraction drugs**↓ First-pass → ↑ Bioavailability → **↓ Dose** (e.g., propranolol, morphine, verapamil, nitrates)
**Reduce dose for low extraction drugs with hepatic clearance**↓ CYP/UGT → ↓ Clearance → **↓ Dose or ↑ Interval** (e.g., warfarin, phenytoin, theophylline, diazepam)
**Prefer renal clearance drugs**If alternative exists with renal elimination (e.g., atenolol > propranolol; gabapentin > pregabalin? both renal)
**Monitor free drug levels**↓ Albumin → ↑ Free fraction → **Measure FREE level** (phenytoin, valproate, warfarin)
**TDM mandatory**Narrow therapeutic index drugs: warfarin, phenytoin, digoxin, lithium, immunosuppressants
**Start low, go slow**Lower initial dose; titrate to effect; monitor for toxicity
Dose Adjustment Categories (FDA/EMA)
CategoryActionExamples
----------------------------
**No adjustment needed**Renal clearance; minimal hepatic metabolismAtenolol, Gabapentin, Pregabalin, Digoxin (mostly renal), Lithium, Aminoglycosides, Vancomycin, Most antibiotics (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems)
**Reduce dose / Extend interval**Hepatic clearance; moderate impairment**Child-Pugh B**: ↓ 50% dose or ↑ interval 2x; **Child-Pugh C**: ↓ 75% or avoid
**Avoid / Contraindicated**Hepatotoxic; high extraction + narrow TI; lactic acidosis risk**NSAIDs, Metformin (Child-Pugh C), High-dose paracetamol >2g/day, Sedatives (benzos, Z-drugs), Certain antibiotics (isoniazid, rifampicin, flucloxacillin in susceptible)**

6 ️⃣ Drug-Induced Liver Injury (DILI): Patterns & Management
DILI Patterns
PatternBiochemistryExamplesManagement
---------------------------------------------
**Hepatocellular****ALT/AST ↑↑↑** (×10–100 ULN), Bilirubin ↑, ALP normal/mild ↑Paracetamol, Isoniazid, Valproate, Phenytoin, Carbamazepine, Statins (rare), Antiretrovirals**Stop drug immediately**; monitor LFTs; N-acetylcysteine for paracetamol
**Cholestatic****ALP ↑↑** (×2–5 ULN), ALT/AST mild ↑, Bilirubin ↑, GGT ↑↑Flucloxacillin, Co-amoxiclav, Macrolides, Rifampicin, Sulfonamides, Chlorpromazine, Azathioprine**Stop drug**; ursodeoxycholic acid for pruritus; monitor bilirubin (Hy's Law)

**Vascular**SOS (sinusoidal obstruction syndrome), Budd-Chiari, Nodular regenerative hyperplasiaCyclophosphamide, Azathioprine, Oxaliplatin, Oral contraceptives**Stop drug**; specialist management
Hy's Law (Predictor of Fatal DILI)
ALT/AST >3× ULN + Total Bilirubin >2× ULN + No ALP elevation (or <2× ULN) = ~10–50% mortality / transplant
  • Action: Stop drug immediately; urgent hepatology referral; monitor for liver failure

7 ️⃣ Special Situations
Paracetamol in Cirrhosis
  • Max dose: 2–3g/day (not 4g)
  • Avoid chronic use
  • Malnutrition/alcohol → ↓ glutathione → ↑ toxicity at lower doses
  • IV paracetamol — same limit; weight-based dosing (<50kg: 15mg/kg/dose)
Alcohol + Drugs in Liver Disease
  • Acute alcohol → inhibits CYP2E1, CYP3A4 → ↑ drug levels (metronidazole, warfarin, phenytoin)
  • Chronic alcohol → induces CYP2E1, CYP3A4, CYP1A2 → ↓ drug levels (paracetamol toxicity at lower doses, theophylline)
  • Disulfiram-like reaction: Metronidazole, TMP-SMX, Cephalosporins (cefotetan), Griseofulvin, Chloramphenicol + alcohol → flushing, tachycardia, hypotension
Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) / Transplant List
  • Child-Pugh C / MELD >15 — transplant assessment
  • Drugs to avoid pre-transplant: NSAIDs, nephrotoxins, hepatotoxins
  • Post-transplant: Immunosuppressant interactions (see [[Drug Interactions/High-risk drug combinations/Immunosuppressant interactions]])

9 🎤 Viva Questions (Expected Answers)
#QuestionExpected Answer
------------------------------
1How does cirrhosis affect drug pharmacokinetics?**1) ↓ First-pass metabolism** → ↑ bioavailability of high extraction drugs (propranolol, morphine). **2) ↓ Hepatic clearance** (CYP450, UGT) → ↓ clearance of low extraction drugs. **3) ↓ Albumin synthesis** → ↑ free fraction of protein-bound drugs (warfarin, phenytoin). **4) ↑ Vd** (ascites, oedema) → ↑ Vd of hydrophilic drugs. **5) Portosystemic shunting** → bypass liver.
2Child-Pugh B cirrhosis: general dosing approach?**Reduce dose ~50% or double interval** for hepatically cleared drugs. Avoid hepatotoxins. Prefer renal clearance alternatives. TDM for NTI drugs. Monitor free levels for highly protein-bound.
3Which beta-blocker is preferred in cirrhosis?**Atenolol** (renal clearance, no first-pass). Avoid propranolol (high extraction → ↑ bioavailability).
4Paracetamol max dose in cirrhosis?**2–3g/day** (not 4g). ↓ Glutathione stores → ↑ NAPQI toxicity risk.
5Why are NSAIDs contraindicated in cirrhosis?**1) GI bleed risk** (varices, gastropathy). **2) Renal impairment** → precipitates HRS (↓ prostaglandin-mediated renal perfusion). **3) Fluid retention** → worsens ascites.
6Warfarin in cirrhosis: why is INR unpredictable?**↓ Albumin** → ↑ free warfarin; **↓ CYP2C9** → ↓ clearance; **↓ Vitamin K-dependent factor synthesis** (liver synthetic dysfunction) → baseline INR elevated. **Result: INR does not reflect anticoagulation intensity.**
7DOAC choice in Child-Pugh B cirrhosis with AF?**Apixaban 2.5mg BD** (licensed for Child-Pugh B). Avoid rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban in Child-Pugh B/C.
8Metformin in cirrhosis: contraindicated in which Child-Pugh?**Child-Pugh B and C** (lactic acidosis risk — ↓ lactate clearance by liver). Child-Pugh A: use with caution, monitor lactate.
9Phenytoin monitoring in cirrhosis — what level?**FREE phenytoin level** (therapeutic 1–2 mg/L). Total level misleading due to ↓ albumin. Corrected formula unreliable.
10Valproate in liver disease — why avoid?**1) Hepatotoxicity** (microvesicular steatosis, mitochondrial toxicity). **2) ↑ Ammonia** → hyperammonaemic encephalopathy (↓ urea cycle). **3) ↓ Albumin** → ↑ free fraction.
11Flucloxacillin DILI pattern and timing?**Cholestatic**, delayed onset **2–4 weeks** (sometimes up to 8 weeks). Risk ↑ age >55, duration >2 weeks.
12Co-amoxiclav DILI — most common cause in UK?**Cholestatic**, 1–6 weeks. **Most common cause of DILI in UK**. Avoid if prior DILI.
13Preferred sedative in cirrhosis if essential?**Lorazepam or Oxazepam** — no active metabolites, glucuronidation (Phase II preserved longer), short half-life. Avoid diazepam, chlordiazepoxide, Z-drugs.
14Hy's Law criteria?**ALT/AST >3× ULN + Total Bilirubin >2× ULN + ALP <2× ULN** = high risk of fatal DILI/transplant (~10–50%).
10 🧩 Confusions & Mnemonics
ConfusionClarification
--------------------------
**"All statins contraindicated in liver disease"****FALSE.** Statins are **safe in stable chronic liver disease** (NAFLD, compensated cirrhosis). Monitor LFTs. Avoid in **acute liver failure / decompensated**. Rosuvastatin/pravastatin preferred (less CYP).
**"Metformin safe in Child-Pugh A"****Caution.** Child-Pugh A: may use with close lactate monitoring. Child-Pugh B/C: **contraindicated**. Lactic acidosis risk from ↓ hepatic lactate clearance.
**"Total phenytoin level OK in cirrhosis"****FALSE.** ↓ Albumin → ↑ free fraction. **Must measure FREE level.** Total level underestimates free drug.
**"All benzodiazepines same in liver disease"****Lorazepam/oxazepam/temazepam** = glucuronidation (Phase II) → relatively preserved. **Diazepam/chlordiazepoxide/clonazepam** = CYP3A4/2C19 (Phase I) → markedly impaired.
**"Child-Pugh and MELD interchangeable for dosing"****Child-Pugh** used for **drug dosing** (A/B/C categories). **MELD** used for **transplant prioritisation** (continuous score). Both reflect severity but Child-Pugh is standard in dosing guidelines.
**"Rifampicin safe in liver disease if monitor LFTs"**Rifampicin = **hepatotoxic + potent enzyme inducer** → worsens other drug levels. Avoid if possible in Child-Pugh C; use with extreme caution in B.
**"DOACs all contraindicated in cirrhosis"****Apixaban licensed for Child-Pugh B** (2.5mg BD). Rivaroxaban/dabigatran/edoxaban not recommended B/C. Warfarin = unpredictable but used (INR monitoring).

15 📌 Summary
  • PK in cirrhosis: ↓ first-pass (↑ bioavailability high extraction), ↓ CYP/UGT clearance, ↓ albumin (↑ free drug), ↑ Vd (ascites), shunting
  • Child-Pugh: A=mild (minor adj), B=moderate (↓50% dose/extend interval), C=severe (avoid hepatically cleared drugs)
  • Intrinsic hepatotoxins: Paracetamol >2g/day, methotrexate, INH, rifampicin, pyrazinamide, valproate, amiodarone
  • Idiosyncratic hepatotoxins: Flucloxacillin (cholestatic 2–4w), co-amoxiclav (cholestatic 1–6w, #1 UK), AEDs (DRESS/SJS/HLA), allopurinol (HLA-B*58:01), sulfonamides, azoles
  • Dosing: Prefer renal clearance (atenolol, levetiracetam, apixaban, insulin, gabapentin, penicillins); avoid NSAIDs, metformin (Child B/C), sedatives, valproate, high-dose paracetamol; free phenytoin level; TDM for NTI
  • Statins: Safe in stable CLD; monitor LFTs; rosuvastatin/pravastatin preferred
  • DILI: Hy's Law (ALT>3×ULN + Bil>2×ULN + ALP<2×ULN) = high mortality risk
16 ❓ MCQs (10)
  1. High extraction ratio drug — effect of cirrhosis on oral bioavailability?

A. Decreased B. Increased C. Unchanged D. Unpredictable Answer: B. ↓ First-pass metabolism → ↑ bioavailability (e.g., propranolol, morphine, verapamil, nitrates).

  1. Preferred beta-blocker in cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B)?

A. Propranolol B. Atenolol C. Metoprolol D. Carvedilol Answer: B. Atenolol = renal clearance, no first-pass. Propranolol high extraction → ↑ bioavailability.

  1. Maximum paracetamol dose in cirrhosis?

A. 4g/day B. 2–3g/day C. 1g/day D. Contraindicated Answer: B. ↓ Glutathione → ↑ NAPQI toxicity risk at lower doses.

  1. Which DOAC is licensed for use in Child-Pugh B cirrhosis?

A. Rivaroxaban B. Dabigatran C. Apixaban 2.5mg BD D. Edoxaban Answer: C. Apixaban 2.5mg BD is licensed for Child-Pugh B. Others not recommended.

  1. Metformin contraindicated in which Child-Pugh class?

A. A only B. B and C C. C only D. All classes Answer: B. Lactic acidosis risk from ↓ hepatic lactate clearance. Child-Pugh A: caution with monitoring.

  1. Phenytoin monitoring in cirrhosis — which level?

A. Total B. Free C. Both D. Neither Answer: B. ↓ Albumin → ↑ free fraction. Total level misleading. Free therapeutic range 1–2 mg/L.

  1. Valproate avoided in liver disease because:

A. Only hepatotoxicity B. Only ↑ ammonia C. Both hepatotoxicity AND ↑ ammonia (hyperammonaemic encephalopathy) + ↑ free fraction D. Renal toxicity Answer: C. Triple risk: mitochondrial hepatotoxicity, ↑ ammonia (↓ urea cycle), ↑ free fraction (↓ albumin).

  1. Hy's Law criteria for fatal DILI risk:

A. ALT>3x + Bil>2x + ALP>2x B. ALT>3x + Bil>2x + ALP<2x C. ALT>5x + Bil>3x D. ALP>3x + Bil>2x Answer: B. Hy's Law: ALT/AST >3× ULN + Total Bilirubin >2× ULN + ALP <2× ULN (no cholestasis).

  1. Flucloxacillin DILI pattern and typical onset:

A. Hepatocellular, 1–2 weeks B. Cholestatic, 2–4 weeks C. Mixed, 4–8 weeks D. Vascular, immediate Answer: B. Cholestatic pattern, delayed onset 2–4 weeks (delayed up to 8 weeks).

  1. Preferred sedative in cirrhosis if absolutely necessary?

A. Diazepam B. Zopiclone C. Lorazepam D. Clonazepam Answer: C. Lorazepam (or oxazepam) — glucuronidation (Phase II preserved), no active metabolites, short half-life.

17 📋 SBAs (10)
  1. 65M with Child-Pugh B cirrhosis (alcohol) needs rate control for AF. Current: propranolol 40mg TDS. Best switch?

A. Increase propranolol B. Switch to atenolol 25mg OD C. Add digoxin D. Switch to verapamil Answer: B. Propranolol high extraction → ↑ bioavailability in cirrhosis. Atenolol renal = preferred.

  1. 70F with cirrhosis on warfarin for mechanical mitral valve. INR 4.5 (target 2.5–3.5) on stable dose 5mg. No bleeding. Likely cause?

A. Drug interaction B. Cirrhosis: ↓ albumin (↑ free warfarin) + ↓ CYP2C9 + ↓ factor synthesis C. Dietary vitamin K D. Non-adherence Answer: B. Cirrhosis alters warfarin PK/PD unpredictably. ↓ albumin → ↑ free warfarin; ↓ CYP2C9 → ↓ clearance; ↓ factor synthesis → baseline INR elevated.

  1. Patient with Child-Pugh C cirrhosis needs analgesia for femoral fracture. Best opioid?

A. Morphine 10mg IV B. Transdermal buprenorphine 5μg/h C. Oral oxycodone 10mg D. Codeine 30mg Answer: B. Transdermal buprenorphine bypasses first-pass, less accumulation. Morphine/oxycodone high extraction → ↑ bioavailability. Codeine prodrug unpredictable.

  1. 60M with cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B) newly diagnosed T2DM. HbA1c 58. First-line?

A. Metformin 500mg BD B. Gliclazide 80mg BD C. Linagliptin 5mg OD (or SGLT2i/GLP-1 RA) D. Pioglitazone 15mg OD Answer: C. Metformin CI Child-Pugh B. SU hypoglycaemia risk. Pioglitazone fluid retention. DPP-4/SGLT2i/GLP-1 preferred.

  1. Patient on phenytoin 300mg OD for epilepsy develops cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B). Total phenytoin 12 mg/L (therapeutic 10–20). Albumin 22 g/L. Action?

A. Increase dose B. No dose change; measure FREE phenytoin level C. Decrease dose D. Switch to levetiracetam Answer: B. Hypoalbuminaemia → ↑ free fraction. Corrected total = 12/(0.2×2.2+0.1) = 22 mg/L (toxic). MUST measure FREE level.

  1. Antibiotic for intra-abdominal infection in Child-Pugh C cirrhosis. Best choice?

A. Co-amoxiclav B. Piperacillin-tazobactam (renal adjust) C. Clarithromycin D. Rifampicin Answer: B. Penicillins/cephalosporins/carbapenems = renal clearance, safe in liver disease. Co-amoxiclav/DILI risk. Macrolides/rifampicin hepatotoxic.

  1. Statins in compensated cirrhosis (Child-Pugh A) — correct statement?

A. Contraindicated B. Safe; monitor LFTs; rosuvastatin/pravastatin preferred C. Only simvastatin allowed D. Must stop if ALT >ULN Answer: B. Statins safe in stable CLD. Monitor LFTs. Rosuvastatin/pravastatin minimal CYP. Not contraindicated.

  1. Hy's Law indicates:

A. Drug-induced cholestasis B. High risk of fatal DILI / need for transplant C. Hepatocellular recovery D. Safe to continue drug Answer: B. ALT>3x + Bil>2x + ALP<2x = 10–50% mortality/transplant risk.

  1. Patient with cirrhosis on spironolactone 100mg develops hepatic encephalopathy. Precipitant?

A. Spironolactone B. Hypokalaemia from diuretic → ↑ renal ammonia production C. Spironolactone hepatotoxicity D. Hyperkalaemia Answer: B. Diuretics → hypokalaemia → ↑ renal ammoniagenesis → encephalopathy. Spironolactone itself not direct cause.

  1. Rifampicin in Child-Pugh B cirrhosis with TB. Dosing?

A. Standard dose B. Standard dose but MONITOR LFTs closely; avoid if Child-Pugh C C. Halve dose D. Contraindicated Answer: B. Rifampicin hepatotoxic + enzyme inducer. Use standard dose with LFT monitoring in Child-Pugh B. Avoid Child-Pugh C.
