# LV Aneurysm & Thrombus

## 1. Definition
**LV aneurysm**: transmural scar → thin, dyskinetic bulge (true aneurysm) — typically apical, anterior. Develops weeks to months after STEMI. **LV thrombus** often forms within the aneurysm (stasis).

## 2. Key Features
- **True aneurysm**: wall contains all layers (scar); mouth = neck; **rarely ruptures**
- **Pseudoaneurysm** (false): contained rupture, neck smaller than cavity; **high rupture risk**; needs urgent surgery
- **Aneurysm complications**: HF, mural thrombus → **embolisation** (stroke, peripheral), arrhythmia (VT), angina (ischaemia in residual viable myocardium)
- **ECG**: persistent ST↑ in infarct territory
- **Echo**: dyskinetic, thinned segment; thrombus = echogenic mass adjacent to aneurysm
- **CMR**: gold standard for tissue characterisation, thrombus detection
- **LV thrombus**: risk highest in anterior STEMI with apical akinesis

## 3. Management
- **Anticoagulation** for LV thrombus: **warfarin** (INR 2–3) for 3–6 months, repeat imaging
- **DOACs** are increasingly used (off-label); trials ongoing
- **Aneurysm**: medical therapy for HF, ICD if LVEF ≤35%, surgical resection if refractory arrhythmia/HF
- **Pseudoaneurysm**: **urgent surgery**
- Aspirin + statin; consider ACEi/ARB
- **Anticoagulation** generally not needed for aneurysm without thrombus

> **Pearls**
> - **True aneurysm** rarely ruptures; **pseudoaneurysm** ruptures → urgent surgery
> - **Anterior STEMI + apical akinesis** = high thrombus risk
> - **Warfarin 3–6 months** for LV thrombus, then reimage
> - Persistent ST↑ post-MI ≠ re-MI; consider aneurysm
