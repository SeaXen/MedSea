# Malignant Pericardial Effusion

## 1. Definition
**Malignant pericardial effusion** = pericardial fluid accumulation from **direct tumour involvement or metastasis**. Most common primary tumours: **lung, breast, melanoma, lymphoma, leukaemia**. Often presents with tamponade.

## 2. Key Features
- **Primary site**:
  - **Lung cancer** (especially adenocarcinoma) — most common
  - **Breast cancer**
  - **Haematologic**: lymphoma, leukaemia
  - **Melanoma**, oesophageal, renal cell
- **Pathogenesis**: direct extension, lymphatic/haematogenous spread, pericardial seeding
- **Presentation**: dyspnoea, orthopnoea, tachycardia, hypotension, **tamponade** (often)
- **Diagnosis**:
  - Echo (effusion, tamponade signs)
  - **Pericardial fluid cytology**: positive in 50–85% (sensitivity limited for some tumours)
  - **Pericardial biopsy** if cytology negative (higher yield for lymphoma)
  - **Tumour markers** in fluid (CEA, CA-125)
  - **CT/PET**: identify primary, assess pericardial thickening/nodularity
- **Tamponade** occurs in ~30%
- **Constrictive** physiology may develop

## 3. Management
- **Tamponade**: **emergency pericardiocentesis** (life-saving)
- **Prevent recurrence**:
  - **Pericardial window** (subxiphoid) — most effective
  - **Balloon pericardiotomy**
  - **Sclerotherapy** (bleomycin, talc) if window fails
  - **Radiation** for radiosensitive tumours (lymphoma, leukaemia)
- **Systemic therapy**: chemotherapy/immunotherapy per primary tumour
- **Supportive**: pericardial pain management
- **Prognosis**: poor — most patients have advanced malignancy
- **Avoid** prolonged drainage (pericardial reaction)

> **Pearls**
> - **Most common primaries**: lung, breast, lymphoma, melanoma
> - **Pericardial window** for prevention of recurrence
> - **Pericardial fluid cytology** positive in 50–85% — may need biopsy
> - **Prognosis poor** — most have advanced malignancy
