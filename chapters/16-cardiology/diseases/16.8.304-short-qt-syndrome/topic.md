# Short QT Syndrome

> [!info] **Topic Classification**
> **Section:** 08_Arrhythmias | **Category:** Cardiology

---

## 1. HIGH-YIELD SUMMARY (30-Second Review)

**Short QT Syndrome**: Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome — congenital accessory pathway (Bundle of Kent) causing pre-excitation of the ventricle. **ECG**: short PR (<120ms), delta wave (slurred upstroke), wide QRS (>110ms), secondary ST/T changes. **Prevalence**: 0.1-0.3%. **AVRT** (atrioventricular re-entrant tachycardia) 80% (orthodromic, narrow QRS; 5-10% antidromic, wide QRS). **AF in WPW** = high risk of VF (rapid conduction via accessory pathway, short refractory period).


## 2. ETIOLOGY & PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

**Accessory pathway** = embryonic connection between atria and ventricles bypassing AV node. **Anatomy**: left lateral (most common, ~50%), septal (30%), right (20%). **Conduction**: usually antegrade + retrograde (orthodromic AVRT most common, narrow QRS). **Antidromic AVRT**: antegrade via accessory, retrograde via AV node (wide QRS, resembles VT). **Pre-excitation**: antegrade conduction through pathway during sinus rhythm. **Short refractory period** (<250ms, shortest RR in AF <250ms) = high risk of VF/death.


## 3. CLINICAL FEATURES

**Asymptomatic** (incidental ECG). **AVRT** (paroxysmal SVT, palpitations, dyspnoea, chest pain, syncope). **AF with rapid ventricular response** (irregular wide complex tachycardia, can degenerate to VF — risk of SCD in ~0.1-0.5%/year). **Examination**: usually normal. **Documentary**: episodes.


## 4. DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH

**12-lead ECG**: short PR (<120ms), delta wave, wide QRS (>110ms), secondary ST-T changes. **Location**: V1 (left vs right), inferior leads (anteroseptal vs posteroseptal), lead I (lateral). **Holter** for paroxysmal. **EP study** (gold standard, mapping for ablation, refractory period measurement). **Exercise test** (loss of pre-excitation with exercise = low risk). **Risk stratification** in asymptomatic: shortest RR in AF <250ms, refractory period <240ms, multiple pathways.


## 5. SEVERITY ASSESSMENT & RISK STRATIFICATION

**Asymptomatic, no AF, no SVT** = low risk. **Symptomatic, recurrent AVRT** = ablation indicated. **AF with shortest RR <250ms, refractory period <240ms** = high risk SCD. **Cardiac arrest survivor** = highest risk. **Syncope in WPW** = concerning. **Family history of SCD** = concerning.


## 6. MANAGEMENT ALGORITHM

**Asymptomatic**: may not need treatment, consider risk stratification. **Acute AVRT** (orthodromic, narrow): vagal manoeuvres (Valsalva, carotid sinus massage — avoid bilateral), adenosine 6mg IV bolus (then 12mg, then 12mg), verapamil/diltiazem (avoid if wide QRS = antidromic), beta-blocker. **Acute AF with WPW** (wide, irregular, haemodynamic instability): **DCCV synchronised 200J**; if stable, **procainamide** (slow AF, prolongs refractory period) or **ibutilide** — **AVOID** adenosine, verapamil, diltiazem, digoxin, beta-blocker (may accelerate conduction via pathway → VF). **Long-term**: **catheter ablation** (first-line, >95% success for AVRT, 90% for accessory pathway), **flecainide**, **propafenone**, **sotalol**, amiodarone.


## 7. COMPLICATIONS & PROGNOSIS

**SCD/VF** (0.1-0.5%/year, especially in AF with short refractory period). **Recurrent AVRT**. **AF**. **Cardiomyopathy** (tachycardia-mediated, from chronic AVRT/AF). **Syncope**. **Stroke** (AF).


## 8. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

**Asymptomatic** in children: ablation if pathway demonstrates short refractory period. **Pregnancy**: ablation deferred if possible, medical management. **Athletes**: ablation recommended. **Syncope + WPW**: ablation. **AF + WPW**: ablation.


## 9. LATEST GUIDELINES & EVIDENCE

**ESC 2019 SVT (EHJ)**: WPW management. **AHA/ACC/HRS 2015 SVT**: similar. **NICE CG180 (2014)**: paroxysmal SVT. **Risk stratification** for asymptomatic. **Ablation class IA** for symptomatic. **Avoid** AV nodal blockers in AF with WPW.


## 10. CONFUSIONS & COMMON PITFALLS

**AF in WPW = EMERGENCY** — avoid AV nodal blockers (adenosine, verapamil, diltiazem, digoxin, beta-blocker), use procainamide/ibutilide or DCCV. **Asymptomatic**: not all need ablation. **Pre-excitation may be intermittent**. **Exercise test** may show loss of pre-excitation. **Multiple pathways** in 5-10%. **Mahaim fibre** = atriofascicular, decremental, wide complex tachycardia. **Coumel tachycardia**: PJRT (permanent junctional reciprocating tachycardia) — RP tachycardia. **Concordance** in antidromic AVRT — look for delta wave in different lead.


## 11. MNEMONICS & MEMORY AIDS

**WPW**: Wolff-Parkinson-White. **Delta wave**: slurred QRS upstroke. **Short PR**: <120ms. **AVRT**: atrioventricular re-entrant tachycardia. **Antidromic**: antegrade accessory (wide QRS). **Orthodromic**: antegrade AV node (narrow QRS). **Avoid AV nodal blockers in AF with WPW**: adenosine, verapamil, diltiazem, digoxin, beta-blocker. **Use procainamide/ibutilide/DCCV**. **Ablation >95% success**. **Maneuvers** in SVT: Valsalva, carotid sinus (unilateral, never bilateral).


## 12. DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

LBBB, RBBB, ventricular paced rhythm, idiopathic VT (RVOT, LVOT), antidromic AVRT, VT, hyperkalaemia, MI with Q waves, early repolarisation, Brugada, ARVC.


## 13. INVESTIGATIONS

12-lead ECG (delta wave, short PR, wide QRS), Holter (intermittent pre-excitation), exercise test (loss of pre-excitation), EP study (gold standard, ablation, refractory period), echo (structural, Ebstein — often associated with WPW).

## 14. EXAM DRILLS

### 14.1 MCQs

**Q1.** What is the most common aetiology of Short QT Syndrome?
A. Genetic predisposition
B. Acquired disease
C. Mixed aetiology
D. Idiopathic
E. Familial

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Most cases are acquired with genetic/environmental interaction. Recognise risk factors, presentation, and management.

**Q2.** First-line investigation for Short QT Syndrome:
A. ECG
B. Echocardiography
C. Cardiac MRI
D. Coronary angiography
E. Cardiac biomarkers

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Echo is first-line for structural/functional assessment; additional tests as indicated.

**Q3.** Most appropriate first-line therapy for Short QT Syndrome:
A. Lifestyle modification
B. Pharmacological therapy
C. Device therapy
D. Surgical intervention
E. Multidisciplinary care

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Guideline-directed medical therapy is cornerstone; other modalities as indicated.

**Q4.** Which is a contraindication to therapy in Short QT Syndrome?
A. Renal dysfunction
B. Hepatic dysfunction
C. Drug interactions
D. Patient preference
E. All of the above

> **Answer:** E
> **Explanation:** All must be considered. Individualise therapy.

**Q5.** The most common complication of Short QT Syndrome is:
A. Heart failure
B. Arrhythmia
C. Thromboembolism
D. Death
E. Quality of life reduction

> **Answer:** B
> **Explanation:** Arrhythmia common; severity determines outcome.

### 14.2 SBAs

**SBA1.** A 55-year-old presents with features of Short QT Syndrome. Most appropriate next step?
A. Discharge
B. Outpatient follow-up
C. Investigation
D. Empirical treatment
E. Multidisciplinary referral

> **Answer:** C
> **Rationale:** Confirm diagnosis before treatment.

**SBA2.** Patient with Short QT Syndrome on optimal therapy develops new symptoms. What is the next step?
A. Add another medication
B. Refer for invasive assessment
C. Reassess diagnosis
D. Multidisciplinary team discussion
E. All of the above

> **Answer:** E
> **Rationale:** Comprehensive reassessment with MDT input.
