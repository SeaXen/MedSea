# Effusive–Constrictive Pericarditis

## 1. Definition
**Effusive–constrictive pericarditis (ECP)** = combination of **pericardial effusion with concurrent constrictive physiology** that persists after drainage. Pericardial fluid masks the constriction. Common in TB, malignancy, radiation, post-cardiac surgery.

## 2. Key Features
- **Pathophysiology**: pericardial fluid raises intrapericardial pressure; epicardial constriction is also present
- **Presentation**: dyspnoea, oedema, ascites, raised JVP, pulsus paradoxus
- **Diagnosis**:
  - Echo: pericardial effusion + features of constriction (septal bounce, preserved e', respiratory variation in mitral inflow)
  - **Cardiac cath (gold standard)**:
    - Pericardiocentesis relieves **intrapericardial pressure** to 0
    - But **RA pressure remains elevated** → indicates **constrictive component** persists
    - After pericardiocentesis: continues to show dip-plateau, Kussmaul's, equalised pressures
- **Imaging**: CT/MR — pericardial thickening + effusion
- **Differential**: pericardial effusion alone (RA pressure normalises after tap), cardiac tamponade (low pressure), restrictive CM
- **Common causes**: **TB** (most common worldwide), malignancy, radiation, post-cardiac surgery, chronic idiopathic

## 3. Management
- **Pericardiocentesis** (window if recurrent) to relieve effusion — diagnostic
- **Anti-inflammatory therapy**: NSAIDs, colchicine, corticosteroids (especially if inflammatory cause)
- **TB**: ATT (RIPE) + steroids
- **If constrictive component persists** after resolution of inflammation (≥3 months): **pericardiectomy**
- **Close follow-up** with echo/haemodynamics
- **Early pericardiectomy** if TB ECP doesn't resolve with ATT

> **Pearls**
> - **Effusion + persistent RA pressure post-tap** = ECP
> - **TB is the most common cause** worldwide
> - **Cardiac cath** is gold standard (RA pressure after tap)
> - **Pericardiectomy** if constrictive component doesn't resolve
