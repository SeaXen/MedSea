# Etiology of Aortic Stenosis (Degenerative, Bicuspid, Rheumatic)

## 1. Overview
Aortic stenosis (AS) has three principal etiologies, each with characteristic demographic, anatomic, and prognostic features. **Calcific/degenerative AS** dominates in the elderly, **bicuspid aortic valve (BAV)** is the leading cause in adults <70 years, and **rheumatic AS** remains common in low- and middle-income countries.

## 2. The Three Major Etiologies

| Etiology | Age Group | Pathology | Key Features |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Calcific / degenerative** | >70 yr | Lipid accumulation, inflammation, osteoblast-like calcification of trileaflet valve | Most common cause in developed world; risk factors parallel atherosclerosis |
| **Bicuspid aortic valve (BAV)** | 40–60 yr | Congenitally two leaflets; abnormal shear → premature calcification | Associated with **aortopathy** (aneurysm, dissection); family clustering |
| **Rheumatic** | Younger, endemic regions | Commissural fusion + leaflet thickening + calcification | Always involves the mitral valve; rarely isolated AS |
| **Unicommissural / quadricuspid** | Rare | Congenital variants | Cause AS or AR, often missed |

## 3. Calcific / Degenerative AS
- **Mechanism:** endothelial injury → lipid deposition → macrophage infiltration → osteoblast-like differentiation → calcification (active, regulated process)
- **Risk factors:** age, HTN, hyperlipidaemia, diabetes, smoking, CKD — overlap with atherosclerosis
- **Statin trials** (SALTIRE, SEAS, ASTRONOMER) — **did not slow** progression (unlike atherosclerosis)
- **CT-AVC** (aortic valve calcium score): men >2000 AU, women >1200 AU = severe AS

## 4. Bicuspid Aortic Valve (BAV)
- Prevalence 1–2% population, M:F = 3:1
- **Sievers classification:** Type 0 (no raphe), Type 1 (one raphe, most common L-R), Type 2 (two raphes)
- **Associated lesions:** AR (especially in younger), coarctation, PDA, VSD, Turner syndrome
- **Aortopathy:** ascending aortic dilation in 50% — risk of dissection (≈ 8× general population)
- **Screening:** TTE/TEE/CT/MR for valve + aortic root + ascending aorta
- **Surgery:** replace valve when criteria met **+** replace ascending aorta if >45 mm (per ESC 2022)

## 5. Rheumatic AS
- Autoimmune sequela of Group A Streptococcal pharyngitis
- **Pathognomonic:** commissural fusion (vs pure cusp calcification in degenerative)
- Almost always **co-exists with rheumatic mitral disease**
- **WHO secondary prophylaxis:** benzathine penicillin G monthly
- Affects millions globally; leading cause of AS in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, indigenous populations

## 6. Uncommon / Rare Causes
- **Radiation** (mantle radiotherapy for Hodgkin lymphoma — latency 10–20 yr)
- **Ochronosis** (alkaptonuria)
- **Fabry disease, mucopolysaccharidoses**
- **Supravalvular / subvalvular AS** (Williams syndrome — elastin deletion)
- **Paget disease** (high-output, calcific extension)
- **SLE / RA** (rare valvulitis)

## 7. Clinical Implications
- **Degenerative AS:** treat as atherosclerosis — risk factor control; no proven disease-modifying drug
- **BAV:** family screening (first-degree relatives); lifelong aortic surveillance
- **Rheumatic:** secondary prophylaxis, treat concomitant mitral disease
- Choice of **TAVI vs SAVR** may be influenced by etiology (BAV with raphe — still acceptable for TAVI in selected cases)

> **Pearls**
> - **Calcific AS** is the leading cause in the elderly; **bicuspid AS** dominates <70 yr; **rheumatic AS** in endemic regions
> - BAV = rule out **aortopathy** (aneurysm, dissection) — image the aorta
> - Rheumatic AS almost always co-exists with mitral disease
> - Statins do NOT slow AS progression once established (SALTIRE, SEAS, ASTRONOMER)
