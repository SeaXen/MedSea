1 SCAI Shock Staging (A–E)

The Society for Cardiovascular Angiography and Interventions (SCAI) classification stratifies cardiogenic shock severity for triage and treatment escalation.

StageHemodynamicsPerfusionTreatment
-------------------------------------------
**A — At risk**NormalNormalObservation, treat underlying cause
**B — Beginning**Hypotension, CI ≥2.2No hypoperfusionFluids, low-dose inotropes
**C — Classic**Hypotension, CI <2.2HypoperfusionInotropes + vasopressors, consider MCS
**D — Deteriorating**Worsening despite therapyWorsening**Escalate MCS** urgently
**E — Extremis**Refractory, cardiac arrestSevere**VA-ECMO + CPR**
2 Escalation Pathway

Stage A → B: Fluid challenge, low-dose inotrope (dobutamine 2–5 µg/kg/min) Stage B → C: Add vasopressor (norepinephrine 0.05–0.5 µg/kg/min), ↑ inotrope Stage C → D: Initiate MCS — IABP, Impella, or VA-ECMO Stage D → E: Escalate to VA-ECMO ± add LV vent (Impella), consider ECMO-CPR

4 VA-ECMO Indications
  • Refractory cardiogenic shock
  • Cardiac arrest (eCPR with <60 min downtime)
  • Massive PE
  • Bridge to LVAD/transplant
  • Failure of other MCS
5 VA-ECMO Complications
  • Limb ischemia (need distal perfusion cannula)
  • LV distension (add Impella vent or atrial septostomy)
  • Harlequin (North-South) syndrome: differential oxygenation to upper body
  • Bleeding, thrombosis, hemolysis
  • Infection, stroke
6 When to Escalate
  • Persistent hypotension despite ≥2 vasopressors
  • Lactate >4 mmol/L
  • End-organ dysfunction (oliguria, AMS, ↑LFTs)
  • Worsening hemodynamics on existing MCS
  • Cardiac arrest (impending) → VA-ECMO
Pearls - IABP-SHOCK II: IABP no mortality benefit in cardiogenic shock - ECLS-SHOCK: VA-ECMO may ↑ mortality; use selectively - Always consider LV venting with VA-ECMO if LV not ejecting - Door-to-unloading concept: early MCS improves outcomes - Escalate before cardiac arrest — once arrest, prognosis poor
