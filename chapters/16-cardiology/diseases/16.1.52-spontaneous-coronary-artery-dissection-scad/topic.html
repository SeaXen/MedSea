CARDIOLOGY
Spontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD)
1 Definition

SCAD = non-traumatic, non-atherosclerotic separation of coronary artery wall layers with intramural haematoma compressing the true lumen. Mostly affects young/middle-aged women without traditional CV risk factors.

2 Key Features
  • Population: ~90% female, mean age 45–55; few CV risk factors
  • Triggers: emotional/physical stress, intense exercise, Valsalva, pregnancy/post-partum
  • Predisposing conditions: fibromuscular dysplasia (FMD) (~70%), connective tissue disease (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, Loeys-Dietz), hormonal therapy, multiparity
  • Angiographic types (Saw classification):
  • Type 1: contrast staining of arterial wall
  • Type 2: diffuse stenosis (most common)
  • Type 3: focal/mimics atherosclerosis
  • Presentation: ACS, including STEMI; sometimes VF arrest
  • Multivessel in ~10–15%; recurrent SCAD in 10–20% at 5 y
3 Management
  • Conservative = first-line if haemodynamically stable (most heal spontaneously in weeks)
  • PCI only for ongoing ischaemia, refractory pain, haemodynamic instability (high complication rate — dissection can extend)
  • CABG rare; for left main or recurrent
  • Antiplatelet: aspirin ± clopidogrel (limited evidence; avoid DAPT unless stented)
  • Statin: controversial; usually avoided unless dyslipidaemia
  • β-blocker: associated with ↓ recurrence
  • Screen for FMD (CT/MR angiography of neck, head, abdomen, pelvis)
  • Pregnancy counselling: recurrence risk
  • Cardiac rehab with caution
Pearls - SCAD = young woman + ACS + few risk factors → think FMD - Conservative management first-line (heals spontaneously) - PCI high-risk in SCAD (dissection can extend) - β-blockers reduce recurrence
