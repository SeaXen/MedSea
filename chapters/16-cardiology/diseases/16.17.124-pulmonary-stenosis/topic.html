CARDIOLOGY
Pulmonary stenosis
1 Definition & Background
  • Pulmonary stenosis (PS) — obstruction at valve, subvalve, or supravalve level; in adults usually valvar, congenital (bicuspid/dysplastic pulmonary valve, Noonan syndrome).
  • Pulmonary regurgitation (PR) — retrograde flow into RV during diastole; most common cause in adults is post-TOF repair with transannular patch.
2 Etiology & Pathophysiology
  • PS: congenital bicuspid/dysplastic valve, Noonan syndrome, carcinoid (rare), rheumatic, extrinsic compression.
  • PR: iatrogenic (transannular patch in TOF repair), endocarditis, carcinoid, pulmonary hypertension (rare), congenital absence/dysplasia.
  • PS physiology: RV outflow obstruction → RV hypertrophy → ↓pulmonary flow → fixed low cardiac output; severe PS may cause R→L shunt via PFO.
  • PR physiology: volume overload of RV → RV dilation → TR → RV failure; chronic PR well tolerated for years then decompensates.
3 Clinical Features
  • Mild PS/PR: usually asymptomatic.
  • Severe PS: exertional dyspnoea, fatigue, chest pain, syncope (fixed output), RV failure (oedema, hepatomegaly, JVP).
  • Severe PR (post-TOF): progressive exercise intolerance, RV failure, atrial/ventricular arrhythmia, sudden death risk.
  • Signs: systolic ejection murmur LUSB (PS), early diastolic decrescendo at LUSB (PR), RV heave, single/soft P2, TR murmur, RV S3/S4.
4 Diagnosis & Investigations
  • ECG: RVH, RBBB (post-TOF), RAD, P pulmonale.
  • CXR: prominent pulmonary artery (PS), RV enlargement (PR), oligaemic lung fields (severe PS).
  • TTE/TOE: peak/mean gradient across PV (mild <36, moderate 36–64, severe >64 mmHg), PR severity (diastolic flow reversal in PA branches), RV size & function, TR severity, associated lesions.
  • CMR: gold standard for PR severity and RV volumes (RVEDV index >160 mL/m² or RVESV >80 mL/m² = severe).
  • Exercise test: symptom evaluation, arrhythmia.
  • Catheter: diagnostic ± balloon valvuloplasty.
5 Management
  • PS: balloon pulmonary valvuloplasty (BPV) first line for valvar PS (peak gradient >50 mmHg or symptoms); surgery (valvotomy) if BPV not feasible.
  • PR post-TOF: pulmonary valve replacement (PVR) — surgical (bioprosthetic) or transcatheter (TPVR, Melody/Edwards SAPIEN). Indications: symptoms, RVEDVi >160 mL/m², RVESVi >80 mL/m², RVEF <47%, progressive TR, sustained arrhythmia.
  • Medical: diuretics for RV failure, arrhythmia management, endocarditis prophylaxis not routinely indicated (see IE prophylaxis guidance).
  • Follow-up: lifelong — TTE/CMR every 1–3 yr; exercise testing.
6 Complications & Prognosis
  • PS: RV failure, arrhythmia, endocarditis, sudden death (severe), R→L shunt if PFO/ASD.
  • PR post-TOF: RV failure, VT (macroreentrant, isthmus-dependent — risk ↑ with RV dilation), atrial arrhythmia, sudden death.
  • Re-intervention: 10–15% need re-PVR within 10 yr (bioprosthetic degeneration).
  • Prognosis after timely PVR is excellent; long-standing severe RV dilation/failure is the main determinant of outcome.
