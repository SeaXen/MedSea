CARDIOLOGY
Primary PCI protocol (door-to-balloon, stent choice, anticoagulation)
1 Definition

Primary PCI = emergent percutaneous coronary intervention in STEMI (or new LBBB with symptoms) without prior fibrinolysis. Goal: restore antegrade flow in the infarct-related artery (IRA). Preferred reperfusion strategy when achievable within 120 min of first medical contact (FMC).

2 Indications
  • STEMI with symptoms ≤12 h (Class I)
  • Cardiogenic shock irrespective of time window
  • New LBBB with symptoms (controversial — Sgarbossa-positive)
  • Persistent ischaemia 12–24 h (Class IIa)
  • Late presentation >24 h with occluded IRA — generally no PCI (OAT trial)
3 Time Targets
MetricTarget
------
**FMC → ECG**≤10 min
**Door-in → Door-out** (non-PCI hospital)≤30 min
**FMC → Device activation**≤90 min (PCI-capable)
**FMC → Device activation**≤120 min (transfer)
**Door → Balloon** (direct admission)≤60 min
Delays >120 min → consider fibrinolysis if no contraindications.
4 Pre-procedure Medications
  • Aspirin 150–300 mg chewed (loading)
  • P2Y12 inhibitor loading:
  • Ticagrelor 180 mg (preferred)
  • Prasugrel 60 mg (if no stroke/TIA, age <75, weight >60 kg)
  • Clopidogrel 600 mg (only if others unavailable)
  • Anticoagulant (one):
  • Unfractionated heparin (UFH) 70–100 U/kg bolus
  • Enoxaparin 0.5 mg/kg IV bolus
  • Bivalirudin 0.75 mg/kg bolus + infusion (if heparin contraindicated)
5 Stent Choice
  • Drug-eluting stent (DES) — standard of care (lower restenosis vs BMS)
  • Newer-generation everolimus- or zotarolimus-eluting stents preferred
  • BMS reserved for: large STEMI with need for dual antiplatelet interruption, bleeding risk, surgery planned
  • Bifurcation lesions — provisional stenting (main vessel first)
  • Aspiration thrombectomy — NOT routine (TOTAL trial); consider in large thrombus burden
6 Periprocedural Anticoagulation
  • UFH — most common; activated clotting time (ACT) target 250–300 s (300–350 with GP IIb/IIIa)
  • Bivalirudin — non-inferior to UFH; lower bleeding; transient ACS mortality signal in early trials mitigated by prolonging infusion
  • Enoxaparin — non-inferior to UFH (ATOLL trial)
  • Fondaparinux — avoid as sole anticoagulant in STEMI (catheter thrombosis risk)
  • GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors (abciximab, eptifibatide, tirofiban) — bail-out only (high bleeding, no routine benefit)
7 Vessel Access
  • Radial preferred (lower bleeding, mortality benefit in ACS — RIVAL, MATRIX)
  • Femoral if radial fails, hemodynamic instability, or anatomy unsuitable
8 Culprit-Only vs Multivessel PCI
  • Culprit-only at index procedure (default in shock: CULPRIT-SHOCK)
  • Staged non-culprit PCI within 1–6 weeks for residual disease (COMPLETE, FUTURE)
  • Immediate multivessel PCI in stable patients: supported by recent evidence but still debated
9 Post-PCI Care
  • DAPT: aspirin + P2Y12 (ticagrelor/prasugrel) ≥12 months
  • Anticoagulation: stop periprocedural agent (unless other indication)
  • Statin, beta-blocker (within 24 h if stable), ACEi/ARB (within 24 h)
  • Risk-stratify for ICD if LVEF ≤35% at 40 days
Pearls - FMC-to-device ≤90 min (PCI hospital) / ≤120 min (transfer) — every minute = myocyte loss - DES > BMS in STEMI (lower restenosis, similar safety) - Ticagrelor or prasugrel preferred over clopidogrel - Radial access > femoral in ACS - Avoid fondaparinux monotherapy (catheter thrombosis) - Culprit-only in shock; staged complete revascularisation if multivessel disease
