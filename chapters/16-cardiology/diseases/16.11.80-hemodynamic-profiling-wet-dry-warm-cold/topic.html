Hemodynamic profiling (wet/dry, warm/cold)
1 Why Profile?

Stevenson's bedside 2×2 haemodynamic matrix guides initial therapy in minutes. Avoids blind diuresis in low-output patients.

3 Profile-Based Therapy
Warm & Wet (most common)
  • IV loop diuretic (furosemide 20–40 mg IV stat, repeat or infusion)
  • Vasodilator if hypertensive (GTN)
  • Continue oral GDMT (BB, ACEi/ARB if BP allows)
Cold & Wet (cardiogenic shock spectrum)
  • Inotrope — dobutamine (β1), milrinone (PDE3), levosimendan (calcium sensitiser)
  • Vasopressor — noradrenaline (if SBP <80)
  • Diuretic — to decongest (use with inotrope)
  • Mechanical support — IABP, Impella, VA-ECMO if refractory
  • Right heart catheter to titrate
Cold & Dry
  • Cautious fluid challenge (250 mL NS over 15 min)
  • If no response → inotrope
  • Often early decompensation of advanced HFrEF
Warm & Dry
  • Optimise oral therapy
  • Discharge planning

Cardiac power output = (MAP × CO)/451 — most powerful predictor of mortality in cardiogenic shock. CPO <0.6 W ⇒ very high mortality.
5 Monitoring
  • Strict fluid balance, daily weight
  • U&E, K⁺, creatinine on diuretics
  • BP, HR, SpO₂, urine output
  • BNP/NT-proBNP for response
  • Reassess profile at least twice daily
Pearls - Warm & Wet = diuretic ± vasodilator - Cold & Wet = inotrope + diuretic ± MCS - Cold & Dry = fluid challenge then inotrope - Narrow pulse pressure = cold (low stroke volume) - Bendopnoea (dyspnoea bending forward) — earliest sign of congestion - S3 gallop is the most specific sign of elevated LV filling pressure

<callout icon="🎯" color="rose_bg"> CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE — Stevenson's 2×2 matrix is the bedside shortcut to ADHF therapy. Most admissions are Warm & Wet → IV diuretics. Cold & Wet requires inotropes and consideration of mechanical support. RHC may be needed in complex cases (ESC IIa). </callout>
