## 1. Overview
STEMI complications can be grouped as:
- **Arrhythmic** (most common)
- **Mechanical** (highest mortality)
- **Inflammatory** (pericarditis)
- **Hemodynamic** (HF, shock)
- **Thromboembolic** (LV thrombus)

## 2. Arrhythmic Complications

| Arrhythmia | Timing | Management |
|---|---|---|
| **VF / pulseless VT** | <24 h (most common <1 h) | Defibrillation, ACLS, **β-blocker** reduces risk |
| **Accelerated idioventricular rhythm (AIVR)** | Reperfusion marker | Observation (usually transient) |
| **Sinus bradycardia** | Inferior MI (RCA) | Atropine, pacing if symptomatic |
| **AV block** | Inferior MI (AV node ischaemia) | Atropine, temporary pacing |
| **New BBB / RBBB** | Proximal LAD | High mortality; consider pacing |
| **AF** | 5–20% | Rate control, anticoagulation |
| **Sustained VT** | Late (post-MI) | ICD workup, antiarrhythmics |

- **Lidocaine / amiodarone** for VT (prophylaxis no longer routine)
- **β-blocker** within 24 h reduces VF and mortality

## 3. Mechanical Complications (1–7 days)

| Complication | Vessel | Clinical | Diagnosis | Management |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Free wall rupture** | LAD | Sudden death, tamponade | Echo (pericardial effusion) | **Emergent surgery** (often fatal) |
| **VSR (ventricular septal rupture)** | LAD/RCA | New harsh holosystolic murmur, thrill, biventricular failure | Echo with colour Doppler, PA catheter (step-up) | **Surgical repair** (mortality >50%); IABP bridge |
| **Papillary muscle rupture** | RCA (posteromedial) | Acute severe MR, pulmonary oedema | Echo (TEE) | **Emergency surgery**; IABP, diuretics, vasodilators |

- Most occur **3–7 days** post-MI (coincides with macrophage-mediated collagen degradation)
- **Risk factors**: advanced age, female, hypertension, late reperfusion

## 4. Heart Failure & Cardiogenic Shock
- **LV failure** → acute pulmonary oedema
- **Cardiogenic shock** — typically within 24 h; mortality 50–80%
- **SCAI stages** A (at risk) → E (extremis)
- **Management**: inotropes (dobutamine, norepinephrine), IABP, Impella, VA-ECMO; revascularisation (CULPRIT-SHOCK — culprit-only)

## 5. Pericarditis

**Early (peri-infarction pericarditis):**
- Within **<4 days** of MI
- From transmural inflammation adjacent to pericardium
- Treatment: **high-dose aspirin** (avoid NSAIDs/steroids — impair healing, increase rupture risk)

**Late (Dressler's syndrome):**
- **2–10 weeks** post-MI (autoimmune)
- Fever, pleuritic chest pain, pericardial effusion
- Treatment: NSAIDs, colchicine; rarely steroids
- Higher risk of ventricular aneurysm formation

## 6. Left Ventricular Aneurysm
- **True aneurysm** (after transmural MI, scarred)
  - Persistent ST elevation after MI ("**South African flag sign**")
  - Apex most common (LAD territory)
  - **Risk of LV thrombus** → anticoagulation
  - Treat heart failure, consider surgical repair if symptomatic

## 7. LV Thrombus
- 5–20% of anterior MIs
- High risk in **anterior STEMI** with apical akinesis
- Diagnosis: **echo** (thrombus in apex), contrast echo or cardiac MRI
- **Anticoagulation** for 3–6 months (warfarin or DOAC)
- Repeat echo to confirm resolution

## 8. Recurrent Ischaemia / Reinfarction
- Chest pain + new ST changes post-MI
- Causes: stent thrombosis, recurrent plaque rupture, incomplete revascularisation
- **Urgent angiography** ± PCI

## 9. Right Ventricular Infarction
- **Proximal RCA** occlusion
- **Triad**: hypotension, clear lung fields, raised JVP
- **Avoid nitrates and diuretics** (preload-dependent)
- **IV fluids** + inotropes
- **ST elevation in V4R**

> **Pearls**
> - **VF** — most common cause of early death; **β-blocker** reduces risk
> - **Mechanical complications** (free wall, VSR, papillary rupture) peak at **3–7 days**
> - **VSR → LAD/RCA territory**; **papillary rupture → RCA** (posteromedial papillary muscle has single blood supply)
> - **Free wall rupture = surgical emergency** (often fatal)
> - **Dressler's = autoimmune**, weeks later; **peri-infarction = early**, treat with **high-dose aspirin**
> - **Avoid NSAIDs/steroids in early peri-infarction pericarditis** (impaired healing, ↑rupture)
> - **Anterior MI + apical akinesis** → anticoagulate for LV thrombus

**Q1.** Which of the following is characterised by the clinical triad: hypotension, JVP, muffled heart sounds?

  - **A.** STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis
  - **B.** Stable angina
  - **C.** Aortic dissection
  - **D.** Pericarditis

  > **Answer: A** — STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis
  >
  > *Source:* **Tamponade**: Beck's triad (hypotension, JVP, muffled heart sounds), pulsus paradoxus (>10 mmHg drop in SBP with inspiration), electrical alternans

**Q2.** Which of the following features is most specific or characteristic of STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis?

  - **A.** Pericarditis pain
  - **B.** A feature common to many acute inflammatory conditions
  - **C.** A non-specific sign that does not localise the diagnosis
  - **D.** An investigation finding rather than a clinical feature

  > **Answer: A** — Pericarditis pain
  >
  > *Source:* **Pericarditis pain**: pleuritic, ↑lying, ↓leaning forward, **trapezius ridge radiation** (specific for pericardial)

**Q3.** What is the most appropriate first-line therapy for STEMI complications mechanical arrhythmic HF pericarditis?

  - **A.** NSAIDs + colchicine
  - **B.** An advanced/surgical therapy reserved for refractory disease
  - **C.** Symptomatic treatment only, no disease-modifying therapy
  - **D.** Empiric broad-spectrum therapy without specific indication

  > **Answer: A** — NSAIDs + colchicine
  >
  > *Source:* **NSAIDs** (ibuprofen 600-800mg TDS, aspirin 750-1000mg TDS, indomethacin) + **colchicine 0.5mg BD** (1-3 months) for first episode and recurrence.
