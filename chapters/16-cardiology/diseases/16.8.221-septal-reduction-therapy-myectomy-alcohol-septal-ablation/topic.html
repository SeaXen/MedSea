SEA KNOWLEDGE STUDY NOTES
CARDIOLOGY
Septal reduction therapy (myectomy, alcohol septal ablation)
1 Indications

Septal reduction is indicated when symptoms persist despite optimal medical therapy (NYHA III–IV or recurrent syncope) AND LVOT gradient ≥50 mmHg at rest or with provocation. Asymptomatic patients do not need reduction therapy based on gradient alone.

2 Surgical Myectomy (Morrow Procedure)
  • Gold standard for septal reduction
  • Transaortic resection of basal septal bulge (~5–10 g)
  • Concomitant MV repair/replacement if intrinsic MV pathology
  • Intra-operative TEE to assess residual gradient
  • Operative mortality <1% in experienced centres
  • Success rate >90% — gradient abolishment, symptom improvement
Outcomes
  • Durable gradient reduction
  • Improved survival to normal population (long-term cohort data)
  • Restores exercise capacity
3 Alcohol Septal Ablation (ASA)
  • Percutaneous alternative for patients at higher surgical risk
  • Selective catheterisation of septal perforator → ethanol induces localised infarction → scar retraction → enlarges LVOT
  • Requires transient pacemaker (high AV block risk 10–15%)
  • Contraindications: significant septal thickness <15–16 mm, concomitant cardiac surgery need, marked MR
  • Risk of ventricular septal rupture and complete heart block

7 Volume & Expertise
  • Both procedures require high-volume centres (≥50 myectomies/yr or equivalent experience) for best outcomes
  • Discuss at multidisciplinary HCM team (cardiologist, surgeon, interventionalist, EP)
Pearls - Myectomy is the gold standard — gradient abolishment with low morbidity in expert hands. - Alcohol septal ablation is the percutaneous option; risk of AV block and VSD. - Indicated only when symptomatic despite OMT with gradient ≥50 mmHg. - Both procedures improve survival to near-normal in experienced centres. - High-volume centre is the strongest predictor of outcome.
