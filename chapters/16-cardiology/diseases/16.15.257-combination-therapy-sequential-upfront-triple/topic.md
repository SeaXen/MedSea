# Combination Therapy: Sequential and Upfront Triple

## 1. Rationale
PAH has three distinct pathogenic pathways (endothelin, NO, prostacyclin). Combining agents targeting different pathways produces **additive/synergistic benefit** and is now standard of care.

## 2. Sequential vs Upfront Combination

**Sequential (add-on) approach:** Older paradigm — start monotherapy, add second agent if inadequate response. Less aggressive.

**Upfront combination:** Initiate ≥2 agents at diagnosis. Supported by:
- **AMBITION trial:** Ambrisentan + tadalafil reduced time to clinical failure vs monotherapy in treatment-naive WHO FC II–III PAH.
- **TRITON trial:** Macitentan + tadalafil ± selexipag (upfront triple) — no clear advantage of initial triple over initial dual in lower-risk patients.
- **GRIPHON (selexipag):** Reduced morbidity/mortality as add-on.

## 3. Triple Therapy in Severe PAH
- **Parenteral prostacyclin + oral ERA + oral PDE5i** is the gold standard for high-risk or progressive disease
- Initial IV epoprostenol + macitentan + tadalafil shown to normalise haemodynamics in ~50% in small series

## 4. Practical Approach (2022 ESC/ERS)
- **Low risk:** Oral monotherapy acceptable (or dual)
- **Intermediate risk:** Upfront **oral dual** (ERA + PDE5i)
- **High risk / poor prognosis:** Upfront triple including **parenteral prostacyclin**, early transplant referral

## 5. Treatment Escalation
- Reassess risk at 3–6 months
- No improvement or intermediate-high risk → add 2nd, then 3rd agent
- Selexipag is a useful add-on across risk strata

> **Key Points**
> - **Upfront oral combination** is standard for most PAH (AMBITION).
> - **High-risk** patients need parenteral prostacyclin ± triple therapy.
> - Reassess risk; failure to reach low risk = escalate.
