# Takotsubo cardiomyopathy

Related: [[../Cardiology MOC|Cardiology MOC]] · [[../Cardiomyopathies and Myocardial Disease|Cardiomyopathies and Myocardial Disease]] · [[Primary cardiomyopathies]] · [[Acute Coronary Syndrome]] · [[Heart Failure]]

## Definition
Takotsubo cardiomyopathy is an acute, usually reversible stress-related cardiomyopathy characterized by **transient regional LV systolic dysfunction** that often extends beyond a single coronary territory and clinically mimics **acute coronary syndrome (ACS)**.

## Anatomy
- Classic pattern: **apical ballooning** with basal hyperkinesis.
- Mid-ventricular, basal, and focal variants also occur.
- Coronary arteries are often non-obstructive or insufficient to explain the degree of dysfunction.
- RV involvement can occur in more severe cases.

## Physiology
- Acute catecholamine surge is thought to cause myocardial stunning.
- Temporary LV systolic dysfunction leads to reduced EF and pulmonary edema in some patients.
- Dynamic LVOTO, MR, or arrhythmia may complicate the acute phase.
- Recovery usually occurs over days to weeks.

## Classification
- Apical Takotsubo (classic)
- Mid-ventricular variant
- Basal / reverse Takotsubo
- Focal variant

## Etiology / risk factors
- Emotional stress: grief, fear, shock
- Physical stress: sepsis, surgery, acute neurologic illness, asthma attack
- More common in postmenopausal women
- Adrenergic stimulation and autonomic dysregulation are implicated

## Pathophysiology
1. Triggering stress leads to catecholamine excess.
2. Myocardial stunning and microvascular dysfunction occur.
3. LV contractility becomes regionally impaired beyond one vascular territory.
4. Troponin rises modestly compared with the dramatic wall-motion abnormality.
5. Ventricular function then gradually recovers.

## Clinical features
### Symptoms
- Acute chest pain
- Dyspnea
- Palpitations
- Syncope in some patients
- Sometimes cardiogenic shock or acute pulmonary edema

### Signs
- May resemble ACS or acute heart failure
- Tachycardia, basal crackles, hypotension if severe
- New systolic murmur if dynamic LVOTO or MR develops

## Investigations
- **ECG**: ST elevation, T-wave inversion, QT prolongation, dynamic nonspecific changes
- **Troponin**: elevated but often less than expected for the amount of LV dysfunction
- **Echocardiography**: classic regional wall-motion abnormality, reduced EF, possible LVOTO/MR
- **Coronary angiography / CT coronary imaging**: needed to exclude obstructive ACS in acute presentations
- **Cardiac MRI**: helpful for distinguishing myocarditis/infarction and confirming edema without infarct pattern

## Interpretation frameworks
### ACS / troponin logic
- Takotsubo is an **ACS mimic** until proven otherwise.
- Treat initial presentation as possible ACS while excluding coronary occlusion.
- Clue: wall-motion abnormality exceeds a single coronary territory and troponin rise is modest relative to LV dysfunction.

### ECG framework
- Early ST elevation can mimic STEMI.
- Later deep T-wave inversion and QT prolongation are common.
- ECG alone cannot safely separate Takotsubo from MI at presentation.

### Heart-failure / hemodynamic profile
- Warm/wet or cold/wet phenotype may occur depending on severity.
- Look for pulmonary edema, hypotension, LVOTO, and MR.
- Inotropes can worsen dynamic LVOTO if present.

### Murmur / valve clues
- New systolic murmur may reflect functional MR or dynamic LVOTO rather than structural valvular disease.

## Diagnosis
Diagnosis is made when there is:
- acute transient LV regional dysfunction,
- a compatible stress trigger or classic phenotype,
- no obstructive coronary disease sufficient to explain it,
- and alternative diagnoses such as myocarditis are reasonably excluded.

## Differential diagnosis
- [[Acute Coronary Syndrome]]
- [[Myocarditis]]
- Acute decompensated HCM with LVOTO
- Pulmonary embolism with troponin leak
- Sepsis-related cardiomyopathy

## Management
### Acute phase
- Manage initially as possible ACS until coronary occlusion is excluded
- Oxygen, analgesia, and monitoring
- Diuretics if pulmonary edema
- Standard heart-failure therapy as tolerated
- Anticoagulation if severe LV dysfunction or apical thrombus risk

### Special hemodynamic cautions
- If **LVOTO** is present: avoid nitrates/inotropes that worsen obstruction; consider fluids carefully and beta-blockade if appropriate
- If **shock without LVOTO**: support based on hemodynamics, sometimes with vasopressors/mechanical support

### Longer-term
- Beta-blockers and ACEi/ARB often used during recovery
- Reassess LV function to document recovery
- Manage emotional/physical triggers where possible

## Drug interactions / contraindications / comorbidity cautions
- Do not stop ACS evaluation early just because Takotsubo seems likely.
- Inotropes can be harmful if dynamic LVOTO is driving shock.
- QT prolongation raises torsades risk; avoid QT-prolonging drugs when possible.

## Complications
- Acute heart failure / pulmonary edema
- Cardiogenic shock
- Dynamic LVOTO
- Functional MR
- LV apical thrombus and embolism
- Ventricular arrhythmia
- Rare recurrence

## Red flags / emergencies
- ST-elevation presentation
- Shock or severe pulmonary edema
- LVOTO physiology with hypotension
- Marked QT prolongation with arrhythmia risk
- Suspected apical thrombus

## One-Page Revision Summary
- Takotsubo = **stress cardiomyopathy** and major **ACS mimic**.
- Usually post-stress, often in older women.
- Troponin rises, ECG changes mimic MI, but coronary obstruction is absent or inadequate.
- Echo: apical ballooning or variant regional dysfunction.
- Recovery is usually good, but acute complications can be serious.
- Initial rule: **exclude ACS first**, then tailor treatment to hemodynamics, especially LVOTO.
