# Iron Deficiency in Heart Failure

<callout icon="🩸" color="red_bg">
**Topic:** Iron Deficiency in HF — Cardiology
**Style:** Sea Knowledge study note
**Audience:** FCPS / MRCP exam prep
</callout>

## 1. Definition
**Iron deficiency (ID) in HF** is defined as:
- **Ferritin <100 µg/L** (absolute ID), OR
- **Ferritin 100–299 µg/L with TSAT <20%** (functional ID)

Note: the **lower threshold** is used in HF (vs. <30 in general population) because ferritin is an acute-phase reactant.

## 2. Epidemiology
- **40–60%** of HF patients have ID (with or without anaemia)
- **Independently associated** with reduced functional capacity, QoL, and **mortality** (irrespective of anaemia)
- Common in HFrEF and HFpEF

## 3. Pathophysiology
- **Reduced absorption** — gut oedema, hepatic congestion, reduced hepcidin handling
- **Chronic inflammation** → ↑ hepcidin → iron sequestration
- **Blood loss** — GI bleeding (anticoagulation, antiplatelets)
- **Renal dysfunction** — ↓ erythropoietin
- **Impaired myocardial energetics** — iron is a cofactor for mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation

## 4. Clinical Impact
- **Worse NYHA class**
- **Reduced 6MWD and peak VO₂**
- **Increased HF hospitalisation and mortality**
- **Worse QoL** (KCCQ score)

## 5. Diagnosis
- **Ferritin + TSAT** in all HF patients
- **Iron studies**: ferritin, TSAT, serum iron
- **Repeat periodically** (annually or with decompensation)
- **Routine anaemia workup** if Hb low (B12, folate, blood loss)

## 6. Treatment
### Indication
- **HFrEF (LVEF <45%) + iron deficiency** (with or without anaemia) — **ESC Class I, Level A**
- **Symptomatic improvement** even in non-anaemic patients

### IV Iron (Preferred)
- **Ferric carboxymaltose (FCM)** — preferred agent
- **Ferric derisomaltose** (Ferroject)
- **Iron sucrose** — older, requires multiple infusions
- **Dose calculation** — based on weight and Hb (Ganzoni formula); up to 1000 mg per infusion (FCM)

### Oral Iron
- **NOT effective** in HF — absorption impaired (gut oedema, inflammation, hepcidin)
- **IRONOUT-HF** — no improvement in exercise capacity
- Reserve for **maintenance after IV replacement** if not a candidate for IV

### Trial Evidence
- **FAIR-HF** (FCM) — improved symptoms, NYHA class, QoL
- **CONFIRM-HF** — sustained benefit over 52 weeks
- **AFFIRM-AHF** — reduced HF hospitalisation after acute HF
- **HEART-FID** — borderline mortality benefit, reduced hospitalisation

## 7. Treatment Threshold
- **All symptomatic HFrEF** with ID should receive IV iron
- **Hb target** — no specific target; treat iron deficiency
- **Avoid blood transfusion** unless severe (<7–8 g/dL with symptoms)

## 8. Monitoring
- **Repeat iron studies** at 3 months
- **Re-infuse** if deficiency recurs
- **Hb response** — modest rise (0.5–1 g/dL)
- **Symptoms and 6MWD** — better markers than Hb

> **Pearls**
> - **Definition**: ferritin <100 OR ferritin 100–299 with TSAT <20%
> - **40–60%** of HF patients have ID
> - **IV iron** (ferric carboxymaltose) — **Class I, Level A** in HFrEF
> - **Oral iron does NOT work** in HF (IRONOUT-HF)
> - **Test in ALL HF patients**, regardless of Hb
> - **Improves symptoms, QoL, 6MWD** even without anaemia
> - **Reduced HF hospitalisation** in AFFIRM-AHF
> - **Repeat iron studies** at 3 months and re-treat if deficient

<callout icon="🎯" color="rose_bg">
**CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE —** Iron deficiency is **common, prognostically important, and treatable** in HF. **Screen all HF patients** with ferritin/TSAT. **IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose)** is Class I recommendation — improves symptoms, QoL, and reduces hospitalisation. **Oral iron does not work** in HF.
</callout>
