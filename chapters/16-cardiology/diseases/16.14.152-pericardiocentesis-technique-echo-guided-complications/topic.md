# Pericardiocentesis technique (echo-guided, complications)

## 1. Indication
- **Cardiac tamponade** (hemodynamic compromise)
- **Symptomatic large effusion** not responding to medical Rx
- **Diagnostic** for suspected infectious, malignant, or TB etiology

## 2. Pre-Procedure
- **IV access, cardiac monitoring**, BP, pulse oximetry
- **Echocardiogram** to confirm, characterize, and choose site
- **Coagulation** (INR <1.5, platelets >50,000 if elective)
- **NPO** (if non-emergent)
- **Sedation** (midazolam ± fentanyl) — be cautious in tamponade (preload-dependent)
- **IV fluids** to maintain preload (volume challenge if hypotensive)

## 3. Site Selection (Echo-Guided Preferred)
| Approach | Pros | Cons |
|----------|------|------|
| **Subxiphoid** | Avoids pleura/lung, classic | Higher bleeding risk (epigastric artery), requires angle |
| **Apical** (5th ICS, lateral to apex) | Best echo window | Pleural proximity, may traverse lung |
| **Parasternal** (5th ICS, left sternal border) | Direct path to pericardium | Avoid if possible (LAD, internal mammary, lung) |

**Echo-guided** reduces complications by 50%+ — preferred.

## 4. Technique (Echo-Guided)
1. **Position**: Supine, head up 30° (sitting up if severe pulmonary edema)
2. **Sterile prep and drape**
3. **Local anesthetic** (1% lidocaine) — skin, subcutaneous, periosteum
4. **Needle (16–18 G) attached to syringe**, with continuous negative pressure
5. **Insert at 30–45° angle** toward left shoulder (subxiphoid) or chosen target
6. **Aspirate** as needle advances — feel for "give" when entering pericardium
7. **Confirm** with echo (agitated saline injection or visualization of needle/wire)
8. **Insert guidewire**, dilator, then **pigtail catheter** over wire
9. **Connect to drainage bag**
10. **Aspirate fluid** — remove as much as possible
11. **Secure catheter** for continuous drainage

## 5. Drainage Considerations
- **Limit initial drainage** to 1 L (large volumes can cause acute RV dilation and decompensation)
- **Continuous drainage** for 24–48 h
- **Catheter removal** when drainage <25–50 mL/24 h

## 6. Complications
- **Cardiac perforation** (RV, RA) — usually self-tamponades
- **Arrhythmias** (VT, VF from needle contact)
- **Pneumothorax** (apical/parasternal approach)
- **Hemothorax** (LIMA injury parasternal)
- **Coronary artery laceration**
- **Pericardial decompression syndrome** (acute RV failure after drainage — rare)
- **Infection** (purulent pericarditis)
- **Pulmonary edema** (acute re-expansion)

## 7. Pericardial Fluid Analysis
Send for:
- **Cell count, differential**
- **Gram stain, bacterial, fungal, AFB culture**
- **Cytology** (3 samples ↑ yield)
- **Protein, LDH, glucose**
- **Adenosine deaminase (ADA)** for TB
- **PCR for TB** (GeneXpert)
- **pH** (TB: low)
- **Hematocrit** (hemopericardium)

## 8. Post-Procedure Care
- **Monitor BP, HR, JVP** — watch for re-accumulation
- **Repeat echo** in 24 h, then weekly
- **Treat underlying cause**

> **Pearls**
> - **Echo-guided** pericardiocentesis is **safer** than blind
> - **Subxiphoid** = most common and safest in experienced hands
> - **Don't drain >1 L initially** (decompression syndrome)
> - **Send fluid for cytology** in 3 separate samples (↑ diagnostic yield)
> - **Continued drainage** is more effective than single aspiration
