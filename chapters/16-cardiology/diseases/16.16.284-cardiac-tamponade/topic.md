# Cardiac tamponade

Related: [[../Cardiology MOC|Cardiology MOC]] · [[Pericardial effusion]] · [[../Pericardial, Endocardial, and Inflammatory Cardiac Disease|Pericardial, Endocardial, and Inflammatory Cardiac Disease]] · [[Shock and Hemodynamic Profiles]]

## Learning Objectives
- Define cardiac tamponade and explain its hemodynamics
- Recognize Beck's triad and pulsus paradoxus
- Differentiate tamponade from massive PE, tension pneumothorax, RV infarction
- Apply immediate management: urgent pericardial drainage
- Identify high-risk scenarios (post-procedure, trauma, aortic dissection)

## Definition
Cardiac tamponade is life-threatening hemodynamic compromise caused by pericardial pressure severe enough to impair cardiac filling and reduce cardiac output.

## Anatomy
- Pressure from pericardial fluid compresses thin-walled right-sided chambers first.
- Rapid accumulation is more dangerous than gradual accumulation.

## Physiology
- Raised intrapericardial pressure limits diastolic filling.
- Stroke volume falls, sympathetic tone rises, tachycardia develops.
- Ventricular interdependence becomes exaggerated.
- Untreated tamponade leads to obstructive shock.

## Classification
- Acute traumatic/hemorrhagic tamponade
- Medical tamponade from inflammatory, malignant, uremic, infectious causes
- Low-pressure tamponade in depleted patients

## Etiology / risk factors
- Malignant effusion
- Acute pericarditis with large effusion
- Post-procedure/post-surgical bleeding
- Trauma
- Aortic dissection
- TB/purulent pericardial disease

## Pathophysiology
1. Pericardial pressure rises.
2. RA/RV filling is impaired.
3. Venous return backs up, JVP rises.
4. Cardiac output falls, causing hypotension and shock.

## Clinical features
### Symptoms
- Severe dyspnea
- Chest discomfort
- Marked weakness, presyncope

### Signs
- Hypotension
- Tachycardia
- Raised JVP
- Muffled heart sounds
- Pulsus paradoxus
- Cool extremities in shock

## Investigations
- **Echo**: key test; effusion, RA/RV collapse, plethoric IVC, respiratory flow variation
- **ECG**: sinus tachycardia, low voltage, electrical alternans sometimes
- **CXR** may show large effusion if subacute, but do not delay treatment
- Bedside ultrasound is often decisive in unstable patients

## Interpretation frameworks
### Hemodynamic profile
- Tamponade = **obstructive shock**.
- Think cold, hypotensive, raised JVP, tachycardic patient.

### ECG
- Electrical alternans suggests a large swinging effusion but absence does not exclude tamponade.

### Murmur / valve clues
- Shock with quiet heart sounds and raised JVP favors tamponade over primary valvular collapse.

## Diagnosis
Diagnosis is clinical plus echo confirmation. In unstable patients, act on high suspicion and do not wait for nonessential tests.

## Differential diagnosis
- Massive pulmonary embolism
- Tension pneumothorax
- RV infarction
- Severe cardiogenic shock
- Constrictive pericarditis (chronic, not acute obstructive collapse)

## Management
- **Immediate pericardial drainage** when hemodynamically significant.
- Resuscitate while preparing definitive drainage.
- Avoid delaying drainage for extensive investigation.
- Temporary cautious fluid loading may support preload in selected hypotensive patients while awaiting drainage.
- Treat underlying cause after stabilization.

## Complications
- Cardiac arrest
- Recurrent effusion/tamponade
- Organ hypoperfusion

## Red flags / emergencies
- Hypotension + raised JVP + tachycardia
- Echo chamber collapse
- Tamponade after procedure/trauma
- Suspected aortic dissection with hemopericardium

## One-Page Revision Summary
- Tamponade = **pericardial pressure causing obstructive shock**.
- Core triad: hypotension, raised JVP, muffled heart sounds.
- Echo confirms; drainage treats.
- Rate of fluid accumulation is crucial.

## Answer Key with Explanations
### MCQs
1. **B** - Tamponade causes obstructive shock by limiting cardiac filling.
2. **B** - Thin-walled RA/RV compress first as pericardial pressure rises.
3. **A** - Echo is the primary diagnostic tool showing effusion + chamber collapse.
4. **D** - Beck's triad: hypotension, raised JVP, muffled heart sounds (NOT wide pulse pressure).
5. **B** - Pericardial drainage (pericardiocentesis or surgical window) is definitive treatment.
6. **A** - Pulsus paradoxus (>10 mmHg inspiratory SBP drop) is the classic bedside sign.
7. **B** - Electrical alternans suggests a large swinging effusion (heart swinging in fluid).
8. **A** - Aortic dissection with hemopericardium causes acute hemorrhagic tamponade.
9. **B** - Cautious fluid bolus transiently supports preload while preparing definitive drainage.
10. **B** - Tamponade is a life-threatening emergency requiring urgent drainage.

### SBAs
1. **B** - Hypotension + raised JVP + muffled sounds + RV collapse = tamponade (Beck's triad + echo).
2. **B** - Hemodynamically significant tamponade requires immediate pericardial drainage; do not delay for CT.
3. **A** - Massive PE also causes obstructive shock with raised JVP; echo and CT differentiate.
4. **B** - Acute tamponade: pericardium has no time to stretch, so small volumes cause hemodynamic collapse.
5. **A** - Pulsus paradoxus is the hallmark bedside sign of tamponade.
6. **B** - Clinical/echo diagnosis of tamponade with hemodynamic instability mandates drainage.
7. **B** - Post-procedure (pacemaker, cath) hypotension + raised JVP = suspect hemopericardium/tamponade.
8. **B** - Temporary fluids improve preload transiently while awaiting definitive drainage.
9. **B** - Hemodynamic compromise + chamber collapse on echo = tamponade vs stable effusion without collapse.
10. **A** - Untreated tamponade progresses to PEA/cardiac arrest from obstructed filling.
