CARDIOLOGY
Peripheral arterial pulse examination
1 Introduction

Systematic pulse examination is a PACES staple and detects aortic valve disease, vascular disease, arrhythmias, and cardiac output state. The radial pulse is the standard "rate & rhythm" pulse; brachial/carotid for character; femoral for coarctation.

2 Sites & Order
  • Upper limb: radial → brachial → axillary → subclavian → carotid
  • Lower limb: femoral → popliteal → posterior tibial → dorsalis pedis
  • Carotids palpate one at a time
3 Pulse Character
  • Rate (beats/min)
  • Rhythm — regular, regularly irregular (e.g. 2° HB with Wenckebach), irregularly irregular (AF)
  • Volume / amplitude
  • Character / waveform
  • Symmetry (right vs left, upper vs lower)
  • Radio-femoral delay
4 Characteristic Pulse Waveforms
PulseDescriptionCauses
---------
**Normal**Smooth upstroke, rounded summit—
**Slow-rising / plateau (parvus et tardus)**Late, weak peakSevere AS
**Collapsing (water-hammer)**Sudden ↑, sudden ↓, large amplitudeSevere AR, PDA, AV fistula, hyperdynamic circulation
**Bisferiens**Two systolic peaksMixed AS/AR, HOCM
**Pulsus alternans**Strong-weak alternationSevere LV dysfunction, pericardial effusion
**Pulsus paradoxus**Expiratory–inspiratory drop >10 mmHgTamponade, severe asthma, COPD, constrictive pericarditis (rare)
**Bigeminal**Coupled beatsDigoxin toxicity, ventricular bigeminy
**Jerky**Sharp peakHOCM
**Anacrotic**Slow upstroke with notchSevere AS
**Hyperdynamic**BoundingAnaemia, thyrotoxicosis, fever, pregnancy
5 Pulse Volume / Symmetry Findings
  • Radio-femoral delay → coarctation of aorta
  • Absent femoral pulse → aortoiliac disease (Leriche), coarctation
  • Unequal upper limb pulses → Takayasu, subclavian stenosis, dissection
  • Dorsalis pedis absent in 5–10% normals (use other signs of perfusion — colour, temperature, capillary refill)
  • Allen test — tests ulnar patency before radial ABG/cannulation
6 Special Tests
  • Capillary refill (>2 s = poor perfusion)
  • Buerger's angle — limb elevation to 45° for 1 min; pallor >30° = significant ischaemia
  • Buerger's test — then dependency → reactive hyperaemia (rubor)
  • Ankle-brachial pressure index (ABPI)
  • Normal 1.0–1.4
  • 0.9–0.7 mild disease, 0.7–0.4 moderate, <0.4 severe / critical limb ischaemia
  • >1.4 — calcified, non-compressible (diabetes, CKD)
7 Clinical Correlations
  • Collapsing pulse + displaced apex + early diastolic murmur = severe AR
  • Slow-rising + narrow pulse pressure + ejection systolic murmur = severe AS
  • Bisferiens + double apex + systolic murmur ↑with Valsalva = HOCM
  • Pulsus paradoxus + raised JVP + muffled heart sounds = tamponade
  • Radio-femoral delay + weak femoral + interscapular murmur = coarctation
  • Pulsus alternans + displaced apex + S3 = severe LV dysfunction
Pearls - Pulsus paradoxus is a misnomer — exaggeration of normal inspiratory fall in SBP (>10 mmHg). - Always feel brachial for character; carotid is sensitive for AS but only one side at a time (carotid massage / baroreceptor risk). - Bisferiens = "two beats" — best felt at brachial with arm raised.
