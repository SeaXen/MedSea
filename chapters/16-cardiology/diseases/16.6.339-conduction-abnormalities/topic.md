# Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome (Complete A-to-Z)

Related: [[../Cardiology MOC|Cardiology MOC]] · [[../Davidson Chapter 16 - Cardiology Hierarchy|Cardiology Hierarchy]] · [[../Arrhythmias and Cardiac Conduction Disorders|Arrhythmias and Cardiac Conduction Disorders]] · [[Supraventricular arrhythmias]] · [[Atrial fibrillation]] · [[Atrial flutter]] · [[AV block and conduction disorders]] · [[Pacemaker indications]]

> [!important]
> **Scope**: WPW is a pre-excitation syndrome with an accessory pathway. High-yield for FCPS/MRCP: ECG recognition, orthodromic/antidromic AVRT, WPW + AF = risk of VF, **avoid AV nodal blockers in WPW+AF**, ablation curative.

## Learning Objectives
- Define WPW syndrome and distinguish from WPW pattern (asymptomatic pre-excitation)
- Explain anatomy and physiology of accessory pathways (Kent bundle)
- Classify accessory pathways by location (left free wall, right free wall, posteroseptal, anteroseptal, midseptal)
- Recognize ECG features: short PR (<120 ms), delta wave, wide QRS (>120 ms), secondary repolarization abnormalities
- Differentiate orthodromic AVRT (narrow QRS) from antidromic AVRT (wide QRS) and atrial fibrillation with pre-excitation
- Explain why AV nodal blockers (verapamil, diltiazem, beta-blockers, digoxin, adenosine) are **contraindicated** in WPW + AF
- Apply acute management: procainamide/ibutilide for stable WPW+AF, immediate cardioversion for unstable
- Determine indications for catheter ablation (curative, Class I for symptomatic)
- Recognize risk stratification: asymptomatic WPW pattern vs symptomatic syndrome
- Manage special populations: pediatric, pregnancy, athletes, Ebstein anomaly association
- Identify red flags: syncope, sudden cardiac death risk, rapid AF conduction

## Definition
**Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome** = WPW **pattern** on ECG (pre-excitation) **+** symptomatic arrhythmias (AVRT, AF, atrial flutter).
**WPW pattern** = ECG pre-excitation in an asymptomatic individual.
**Accessory pathway (AP)** = abnormal myocardial connection (Kent bundle) bypassing the AV node, allowing direct ventriculoatrial or atrioventricular conduction.

## Anatomy and Physiology
| Structure | Description | Clinical Relevance |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Kent bundle** | Accessory pathway connecting atrium to ventricle | Bypasses AV node delay; enables re-entry |
| **Location** | Left free wall (50-60%), posteroseptal (20-30%), right free wall (10-15%), anteroseptal (5%), midseptal (<5%) | Determines ablation approach/risks (AV block risk with septal) |
| **Conduction properties** | **Anterograde** (atrium → ventricle) = pre-excitation on ECG<br>**Retrograde** (ventricle → atrium) = concealed pathway | Concealed AP = no delta wave; only retrograde conduction |
| **Refractory period** | Short anterograde ERP → risk of rapid conduction during AF | Shortest ERP <250 ms = high risk for VF |

## Classification
### By Pathway Location (ECG Localization Algorithm — Arruda et al.)
| Location | Delta Wave Polarity | Key ECG Features |
|----------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Left free wall** | Positive in I, aVL; negative in III, aVF | R/S >1 in V1; early transition |
| **Right free wall** | Negative in I, aVL; positive in III, aVF | R/S <1 in V1; late transition |
| **Posteroseptal** | Negative in II, III, aVF; positive in aVR | QR in V1; epsilon wave mimic |
| **Anteroseptal** | Positive in V1-V2; negative in III, aVF | RBBB-like in V1; risk of AV block during ablation |
| **Midseptal** | Positive in V1; isoelectric in III/aVF | Narrow R in V1; highest AV block risk |

### By Conduction Direction
- **Manifest WPW**: Anterograde conduction visible (delta wave on resting ECG)
- **Concealed AP**: Only retrograde conduction; no delta wave; presents as orthodromic AVRT
- **Intermittent WPW**: Pre-excitation comes and goes (vagal tone, rate-dependent)

## Pathophysiology
**Re-entry circuit** = AV node (slow conduction, long refractory) + Accessory pathway (fast conduction, short refractory).

| Arrhythmia | Circuit Direction | QRS Morphology | Rate | Key Features |
|------------|-------------------|----------------|------|--------------|
| **Orthodromic AVRT** (85-95%) | Antegrade AV node → Retrograde AP | **Narrow** (unless BBB) | 150-250 bpm | RP < PR; negative P in II/III/aVF |
| **Antidromic AVRT** (5-15%) | Antegrade AP → Retrograde AV node | **Wide** (pre-excited) | 150-250 bpm | RP > PR; mimics VT |
| **Atrial Fibrillation** (15-30%) | Multiple wavelets → AP (rapid antegrade) | **Irregular wide complex** | >300 bpm ventricular | **Risk of VF**; shortest RR <250 ms = high risk |

## Etiology and Risk Factors
- **Congenital**: Failure of annulus fibrosis insulation during embryogenesis
- **Associations**: Ebstein anomaly (posteroseptal AP), hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, PRKAG2 mutations (glycogen storage, pseudo-WPW), congenital heart disease
- **Triggers for arrhythmia**: Catecholamines, exercise, alcohol, drugs (sympathomimetics), electrolyte imbalance

## Clinical Features
### Symptoms
- **Palpitations** (most common): sudden onset/offset, regular (AVRT) or irregular (AF)
- **Presyncope/syncope**: rapid ventricular rate, hemodynamic compromise
- **Dyspnea, chest pain**: rate-related ischemia
- **Sudden cardiac death**: presenting feature in 0.1-0.6% (WPW + AF → VF)
- **Asymptomatic**: WPW pattern on routine ECG

### Signs
- **Tachycardia** (regular or irregular)
- **Hypotension** if rapid rate
- **Heart failure signs** if prolonged tachycardia (tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy)
- **Normal exam** between episodes

## Investigations
### Essential
| Test | Purpose | Key Findings |
|------|---------|--------------|
| **12-lead ECG** (sinus rhythm) | Diagnose pre-excitation | Short PR <120 ms, delta wave, wide QRS >120 ms, discordant ST-T |
| **ECG during tachycardia** | Differentiate mechanism | Orthodromic: narrow QRS, RP<PR<br>Antidromic: wide QRS<br>WPW+AF: irregular wide complex, changing morphology |
| **Echocardiogram** | Rule out structural disease (Ebstein, HCM) | Ebstein: apical displacement tricuspid, TR, RA enlargement |
| **Electrolytes, thyroid function, troponin** | Exclude triggers/complications | Normal unless secondary cause |

### Advanced
| Test | Indication | Findings |
|------|------------|----------|
| **Exercise stress test** | Risk stratification in asymptomatic | **Abrupt loss of pre-excitation** = longer ERP = lower risk<br>Persistent pre-excitation at peak exercise = high risk |
| **Holter monitor** | Document arrhythmia frequency | Capture spontaneous episodes |
| **Electrophysiology study (EPS)** | Definitive risk stratification, pre-ablation | Measure AP ERP, induce AVRT/AF, shortest pre-excited RR during AF |
| **Cardiac MRI** | If Ebstein/HCM suspected | Tissue characterization, volumeric assessment |

## Interpretation Frameworks

### ECG Localization of Accessory Pathway (Algorithm)
1. **QRS transition in precordial leads**: Early (V1-V2) = left-sided; Late (V5-V6) = right-sided
2. **Polarity in inferior leads**: Positive in II, III, aVF = posteroseptal/left lateral; Negative = anteroseptal/right
3. **Polarity in aVL**: Positive = left free wall; Negative = right free wall
4. **V1 morphology**: R/S >1 = left-sided; QS or rS = right-sided/posteroseptal
5. **Delta wave in I**: Positive = left-sided; Negative = right-sided

### Risk Stratification (Asymptomatic WPW Pattern)
| Risk Level | Criteria | Management |
|------------|----------|------------|
| **Low** | Intermittent pre-excitation, loss on exercise, long AP ERP (>250 ms), no inducible AF | Reassurance; no EPS needed |
| **High** | Persistent pre-excitation on exercise, short AP ERP (<250 ms), inducible AF with short RR (<250 ms), multiple APs, Ebstein anomaly | EPS + ablation recommended |

### WPW + AF Recognition (CRITICAL)
- **Irregularly irregular** wide-complex tachycardia
- **Changing QRS morphology** (fusion of conducted beats)
- **Very rapid rate** (>250 bpm, often 300+)
- **Shortest RR interval <250 ms** = high VF risk
- **Pre-excitation visible** even during AF (delta waves on some complexes)

## Diagnosis
**Definitive**: ECG showing pre-excitation (short PR + delta wave + wide QRS) + documented arrhythmia.

### Differential Diagnosis
| Condition | Differentiating Features |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Lown-Ganong-Levine (LGL) syndrome** | Short PR (<120 ms) **without** delta wave; normal QRS; AV nodal bypass tract (James fibers) |
| **Mahaim fibers** | Nodoventricular/fasciculoventricular; pre-excitation only during tachycardia; LBBB morphology |
| **Idiopathic ventricular tachycardia (RVOT)** | Regular wide QRS; no pre-excitation in sinus rhythm; adenosine responsive |
| **SVT with aberrancy / rate-dependent BBB** | No delta wave in sinus rhythm; ventricular rate usually <220 bpm |
| **Ventricular tachycardia** | AV dissociation, capture/fusion beats; WPW+AF is irregular |
| **Brugada syndrome** | Coved STE V1-V3; no delta wave; distinct genetic channelopathy |

## Management

### Acute Management
| Scenario | Treatment | Dose/Details | Contraindications |
|----------|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Hemodynamically UNSTABLE** (any tachycardia) | **Synchronized cardioversion** | 50-100J biphasic; escalate if needed | None (emergency) |
| **Stable Orthodromic AVRT** (narrow QRS) | Vagal maneuvers → Adenosine | Valsalva; Adenosine 6 mg → 12 mg IV push | Asthma (relative), 2nd/3rd degree AV block (no pacemaker) |
| | CCB / Beta-blocker (2nd line) | Verapamil 2.5-5 mg IV; Metoprolol 5 mg IV | Hypotension, HF, WPW+AF |
| **Stable Antidromic AVRT** (wide QRS) | **Procainamide** (preferred) | 10-15 mg/kg IV over 30-60 min | Prolonged QT, severe HF |
| | Amiodarone | 150 mg IV over 10 min | Hypotension, bradycardia |
| | **AVOID**: Adenosine, CCB, BB, digoxin | — | Can accelerate via AP |
| **Stable WPW + Atrial Fibrillation** | **Procainamide** (1st line) | 10-15 mg/kg IV over 30-60 min; monitor QT, BP | Prolonged QT, severe HF |
| | **Ibutilide** (alternative) | 1 mg IV over 10 min (repeat ×1) | QT >440 ms, HF, hypokalemia |
| | **Flecainide/Propafenone** | Flecainide 2 mg/kg IV (max 150 mg) | Structural HD, BBB, QRS >140 ms |
| | **AVOID**: Adenosine, verapamil, diltiazem, beta-blockers, digoxin, amiodarone (controversial) | — | **Can degenerate to VF** |

### Long-term Management
| Strategy | Indication | Details |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Catheter Ablation** (Class I) | **Symptomatic WPW syndrome** (AVRT, AF, flutter)<br>Asymptomatic high-risk (short ERP, inducible AF)<br>High-risk occupations (pilots, athletes, divers) | Success rate >95%<br>Complication: AV block 0.5-1% (septal pathways)<br>Curative — no long-term drugs needed |
| **Medical Therapy** (if ablation declined/deferred) | Prophylaxis for orthodromic AVRT | Flecainide 50-100 mg BD, Propafenone 150-300 mg BD<br>Sotalol 80-160 mg BD<br>**AVOID**: Digoxin, CCB, BB (can accelerate AF via AP) |
| **Observation** | Asymptomatic WPW pattern, low risk | Annual review; exercise test q1-2 years; educate on symptoms |

### Drug Cautions — ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATIONS IN WPW + AF
| Drug Class | Examples | Mechanism of Harm |
|------------|----------|-------------------|
| **AV nodal blockers** | Verapamil, diltiazem, beta-blockers, digoxin, adenosine | Block AV node → **all conduction via AP** → ventricular rate ↑↑ → VF |
| **Amiodarone** | Amiodarone | Weak AV nodal block + AP block; **case reports of VF**; avoid if WPW+AF suspected |

## Special Situations / Special Populations

### Pediatric WPW
- Most common SVT mechanism in infants
- Infant presentation: poor feeding, irritability, sweating, tachypnea (HF signs)
- **Adenosine safe** for orthodromic AVRT in infants
- Ablation deferred until >4-5 years (size, radiation); medical therapy interim
- Higher spontaneous resolution rate in infancy (up to 20-30%)

### Pregnancy
- Hemodynamic changes ↑ arrhythmia frequency
- **Ablation deferred** until postpartum (radiation)
- **Safe drugs**: Beta-blockers (metoprolol, propranolol), verapamil (2nd/3rd trimester)
- **Avoid**: Flecainide (limited data), amiodarone (fetal thyroid/goiter), adenosine (safe for acute)
- Vaginal delivery preferred; avoid prolonged Valsalva

### Athletes
- Higher vagal tone → intermittent pre-excitation
- **Exercise test mandatory** for risk stratification
- **Asymptomatic + low risk (loss on exercise)**: Can compete (Bethesda Conference)
- **High risk or symptomatic**: Ablation before return to play

### Ebstein Anomaly Association
- 10-25% have AP (usually posteroseptal, often multiple)
- Higher risk of AF, VT, SCD
- Ablation more complex (dilated RA, TR); surgical ablation at time of valve repair
- Caution with AV nodal blockers (high AF burden)

### Concealed Accessory Pathway
- No delta wave on resting ECG
- Presents as **orthodromic AVRT only** (narrow QRS)
- Diagnosis: EPS or adenosine response (AV block terminates tachycardia)
- Ablation equally curative

## Complications
| Complication | Mechanism | Prevention/Management |
|--------------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Ventricular fibrillation / SCD** | WPW + AF with rapid conduction via AP (short ERP) | Risk stratification; ablation for high-risk |
| **Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy** | Chronic rapid rate (AVRT/AF) | Rate/rhythm control; EF recovers after ablation |
| **Hemodynamic collapse** | Rapid ventricular rate in AF | Immediate cardioversion |
| **AV block post-ablation** | Septal pathway ablation near AV node | Cryoablation for septal; mapping precision |
| **Recurrence** | Incomplete ablation, multiple APs | Repeat EPS/ablation; success >95% ultimately |

## Red Flags
- **Syncope** with WPW = high-risk feature; urgent EPS/ablation
- **Shortest RR in AF <250 ms** = high VF risk
- **Multiple accessory pathways** = higher SCD risk
- **Ebstein anomaly** + WPW = complex substrate
- **WPW + HCM** (PRKAG2) = infiltrative, progressive

## One-Page Revision Summary
- WPW = short PR (<120 ms) + delta wave + wide QRS (>120 ms) + discordant ST-T
- Accessory pathway (Kent bundle) bypasses AV node → re-entry substrate
- **Orthodromic AVRT** (90%): narrow QRS, antegrade AV node/retrograde AP
- **WPW + AF**: irregular wide complex, **rapid (>300 bpm), risk VF**
- **NEVER give AV nodal blockers (verapamil, diltiazem, BB, digoxin, adenosine) in WPW+AF** → VF
- **Stable WPW+AF**: Procainamide IV or Ibutilide IV
- **Unstable**: Immediate cardioversion
- **Ablation curative** (Class I for symptomatic; >95% success)
- Asymptomatic WPW pattern: risk stratify with exercise test/EPS
- Ebstein anomaly: posteroseptal AP, multiple APs, higher SCD risk

## MCQs
1. The hallmark ECG triad of WPW syndrome is:
   a) Long PR, delta wave, narrow QRS
   b) Short PR, delta wave, wide QRS
   c) Normal PR, delta wave, wide QRS
   d) Short PR, no delta wave, wide QRS
   **Answer: b** — Short PR (<120 ms), delta wave, wide QRS (>120 ms).

2. Orthodromic AVRT in WPW has which QRS morphology?
   a) Wide (pre-excited)
   b) Narrow (unless pre-existing BBB)
   c) Variable morphology
   d) Biphasic in V1
   **Answer: b** — Antegrade via AV node → normal ventricular activation.

3. Which drug is CONTRAINDICATED in WPW with atrial fibrillation?
   a) Procainamide
   b) Ibutilide
   c) Verapamil
   d) Flecainide
   **Answer: c** — AV nodal blockers accelerate conduction via AP → VF risk.

4. Shortest pre-excited RR interval during AF indicating high VF risk:
   a) <300 ms
   b) <250 ms
   c) <200 ms
   d) <150 ms
   **Answer: b** — <250 ms = ERP of AP short enough for 1:1 conduction at VF rates.

5. Most common accessory pathway location:
   a) Right free wall
   b) Posteroseptal
   c) Left free wall
   d) Anteroseptal
   **Answer: c** — Left free wall 50-60%.

6. Exercise testing in asymptomatic WPW — which finding suggests LOW risk?
   a) Persistent pre-excitation at peak exercise
   b) Abrupt loss of pre-excitation during exercise
   c) Pre-excitation appearing only at peak exercise
   d) No change in delta wave
   **Answer: b** — Abrupt loss = longer AP ERP = lower risk.

7. Ablation success rate for WPW:
   a) 50-60%
   b) 70-80%
   c) 85-90%
   d) >95%
   **Answer: d** — >95% curative; low complication rate.

8. Association of WPW with Ebstein anomaly:
   a) No association
   b) 1-2%
   c) 10-25%
   d) 50%
   **Answer: c** — Posteroseptal pathways; often multiple.

9. In orthodromic AVRT, the RP interval compared to PR interval:
   a) RP > PR
   b) RP < PR
   c) RP = PR
   d) Variable
   **Answer: b** — Retrograde conduction via AP is fast; RP < PR.

10. First-line acute treatment for STABLE WPW + AF:
    a) Adenosine
    b) Verapamil
    c) Procainamide
    d) Amiodarone
    **Answer: c** — Procainamide prolongs AP refractory period; avoids AV nodal block.

11. Which pathway location carries highest risk of AV block during ablation?
    a) Left free wall
    b) Right free wall
    c) Anteroseptal / Midseptal
    d) Posteroseptal
    **Answer: c** — Septal pathways near AV node/His bundle.

12. WPW pattern vs WPW syndrome:
    a) Same thing
    b) Pattern = asymptomatic ECG finding; Syndrome = pattern + arrhythmias
    c) Syndrome = asymptomatic; Pattern = symptomatic
    d) Pattern requires ablation; Syndrome does not
    **Answer: b** — Syndrome = pre-excitation + documented tachyarrhythmia.

13. Antidromic AVRT QRS morphology:
    a) Narrow
    b) Wide (pre-excited)
    c) Narrow with LBBB
    d) Narrow with RBBB
    **Answer: b** — Antegrade via AP → fully pre-excited wide QRS.

14. Drug of choice for stable antidromic AVRT:
    a) Adenosine
    b) Verapamil
    c) Procainamide
    d) Beta-blocker
    **Answer: c** — Procainamide blocks AP; AV nodal blockers can accelerate.

15. Asymptomatic WPW pattern in a 25-year-old pilot — management:
    a) Reassurance only
    b) Annual ECG only
    c) EPS + ablation (high-risk occupation)
    d) Beta-blocker prophylaxis
    **Answer: c** — High-risk occupations (pilots, athletes, divers) = ablation recommended.

## SBA Questions
1. A 30-year-old man presents with palpitations. ECG shows regular narrow-complex tachycardia at 180 bpm, RP < PR, negative P waves in II/III/aVF. He has known WPW pattern on baseline ECG. Best acute management:
   a) IV Verapamil 5 mg
   b) IV Adenosine 6 mg rapid push
   c) IV Amiodarone 150 mg
   d) Synchronized cardioversion 100J
   **Answer: b** — Orthodromic AVRT (narrow QRS): adenosine is safe and effective; avoids AV nodal blockers in WPW+AF but this is narrow QRS AVRT.

2. A 45-year-old woman with known WPW presents with irregular wide-complex tachycardia at 280 bpm, changing QRS morphology, hypotension (BP 80/50). Best immediate management:
   a) IV Procainamide 10 mg/kg
   b) IV Ibutilide 1 mg
   c) Synchronized cardioversion 100J biphasic
   d) IV Verapamil 5 mg
   **Answer: c** — Hemodynamically unstable WPW+AF → immediate cardioversion. Drugs too slow/risky.

3. A 28-year-old asymptomatic pilot found to have WPW pattern on routine medical ECG. Exercise test shows abrupt loss of pre-excitation at 85% max predicted HR. EPS not done. Best management:
   a) Reassure; annual follow-up
   b) Ablation recommended (high-risk occupation)
   c) Start flecainide prophylaxis
   d) Start beta-blocker prophylaxis
   **Answer: b** — High-risk occupations (pilots, competitive athletes, divers) = ablation regardless of risk stratification.

4. A 35-year-old man with WPW has orthodromic AVRT refractory to adenosine. BP 110/70. Next best step:
   a) IV Verapamil
   b) IV Metoprolol
   c) IV Procainamide
   d) IV Digoxin
   **Answer: c** — Procainamide blocks AP; verapamil/beta-blocker/digoxin are AV nodal blockers (risk if AF develops).

5. A 22-year-old woman with WPW develops atrial fibrillation. ECG shows irregular wide-complex tachycardia, shortest RR 220 ms. She is hemodynamically stable. Best pharmacologic management:
   a) IV Diltiazem
   b) IV Amiodarone
   c) IV Procainamide
   d) IV Adenosine
   **Answer: c** — Procainamide prolongs AP ERP; avoids AV nodal block. Amiodarone has case reports of VF in WPW+AF.

6. A 40-year-old man with Ebstein anomaly and WPW (posteroseptal AP) presents with atrial fibrillation. Which statement is TRUE?
   a) Verapamil is safe for rate control
   b) Ablation carries higher AV block risk
   c) Medical therapy is preferred over ablation
   d) Ebstein anomaly is not associated with WPW
   **Answer: b** — Posteroseptal pathways near AV node; complex anatomy in Ebstein (dilated RA) increases ablation difficulty and AV block risk.

7. A 6-month-old infant presents with poor feeding, tachypnea, HR 240 bpm. ECG shows narrow-complex tachycardia, no visible P waves. Baseline ECG shows WPW pattern. Best acute management:
   a) IV Verapamil
   b) IV Adenosine 0.1 mg/kg
   c) IV Amiodarone
   d) IV Digoxin
   **Answer: b** — Adenosine is safe and first-line in infants for SVT/AVRT. Verapamil contraindicated <1 year; digoxin avoids in WPW.

8. A 55-year-old man with WPW and hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (PRKAG2 mutation) has recurrent AF. Which drug should be AVOIDED for rate control?
   a) Verapamil
   b) Disopyramide
   c) Amiodarone
   d) All of the above
   **Answer: a** — Verapamil (AV nodal blocker) contraindicated in WPW+AF. Disopyramide/amiodarone used in HCM but WPW+AF still avoids AV nodal blockers.

9. During EPS for WPW, atrial fibrillation is induced with shortest pre-excited RR interval of 210 ms. This indicates:
   a) Low risk for VF
   b) High risk for VF
   c) Need for beta-blocker
   d) No further risk stratification needed
   **Answer: b** — Shortest RR <250 ms = high risk for rapid conduction → VF.

10. A 32-year-old woman, 28 weeks pregnant, with WPW has recurrent orthodromic AVRT. Safe prophylactic option:
    a) Flecainide
    b) Metoprolol
    c) Amiodarone
    d) Sotalol
    **Answer: b** — Beta-blockers (metoprolol, propranolol) safe in pregnancy. Flecainide limited data; amiodarone contraindicated (fetal goiter); sotalol 2nd line.

  A: Short PR <120 ms + delta wave + wide QRS >120 ms + discordant ST-T.
