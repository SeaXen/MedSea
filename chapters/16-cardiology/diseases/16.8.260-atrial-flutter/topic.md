## Learning Objectives
- Define atrial flutter and distinguish from atrial fibrillation

- Recognize typical vs atypical flutter on ECG

## Definition
Atrial flutter is a **macro-reentrant atrial tachycardia** most commonly involving the **cavotricuspid isthmus** (typical/counterclockwise flutter). Atrial rate is typically 250-350 bpm with **sawtooth flutter waves** (F waves) in inferior leads (II, III, aVF).

## Classification
| Type | Features |
|------|----------|
| **Typical (Counterclockwise)** | Most common; negative sawtooth F waves in II, III, aVF; positive in V1 |
| **Clockwise (Reverse Typical)** | Positive sawtooth F waves in inferior leads; less common |
| **Atypical Flutter** | Non-CTI dependent; post-surgical, post-ablation, congenital; variable F wave morphology |

## Pathophysiology
- **Re-entry circuit**: Right atrial macro-reentry around tricuspid annulus via **cavotricuspid isthmus** (CTI)
- **Atrial rate**: 250-350 bpm (typically ~300 bpm)
- **AV conduction**: Usually 2:1 block → ventricular rate ~150 bpm; may be 3:1 (100 bpm), 4:1 (75 bpm), or variable
- **Progression**: Atypical flutter may develop after ablation/surgery; higher recurrence risk

## Clinical Features
- Palpitations, dyspnea, fatigue, exercise intolerance
- Hemodynamic compromise if rapid ventricular rate (>150 bpm) or pre-existing HF
- Thromboembolism risk (stroke, TIA) — **same as AF** (CHA2DS2-VASc based)
- May be asymptomatic, especially with controlled rate
- Association with AF: **50-80% develop AF** after flutter ablation

## ECG Features
| Feature | Typical Flutter | Atypical Flutter |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|
| **F waves** | Sawtooth, negative in II, III, aVF | Variable morphology |
| **Atrial rate** | 250-350 bpm (typically 300) | Variable |
| **Ventricular rate** | Usually 150 bpm (2:1) | Variable |
| **QRS** | Narrow (unless aberrancy/BBB) | Narrow/wide |
| **V1 lead** | Positive F waves (typical) | Variable |

## Differentiation from Atrial Fibrillation
| Feature | Atrial Flutter | Atrial Fibrillation |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Mechanism** | Macro-reentry (CTI) | Multiple wavelets/foci |
| **ECG** | Sawtooth F waves, regular R-R | No P waves, irregularly irregular R-R |
| **Ablation success** | >95% (CTI ablation) | 60-75% (pulmonary vein isolation) |
| **Recurrence** | Low for typical | Higher |
| **AF after ablation** | 30-50% develop AF | N/A |

## Management
### Acute
- **Rate control**: Beta-blocker, diltiazem/verapamil (avoid in WPW), digoxin (if HF/hypotensive)
- **Rhythm control**: **Synchronized DC cardioversion** (highly effective, 50-100J biphasic)
- **Anticoagulation**: Same as AF — CHA2DS2-VASc ≥2 (♂)/≥3 (♀) → anticoagulate (DOAC preferred)

### Long-term
| Strategy | Role |
|----------|------|
| **Catheter ablation** (CTI) | **First-line for typical flutter**; >95% acute success, low recurrence |
| Antiarrhythmic drugs | Amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide for rhythm control if ablation not done/failed |
| AV node ablation + pacemaker | Refractory rate control; selected HFrEF cases (BLOCK-HF) |

### Anticoagulation
- **Peri-cardioversion**: <48h → no pre-anticoagulation; >48h → 3w pre/4w post OR TOE-guided
- **Long-term**: CHA2DS2-VASc based; DOAC preferred over warfarin
- **Valvular flutter** (MS/mechanical valve): Warfarin only

## Special Populations
### Heart Failure
- **HFrEF**: Rate control with beta-blocker/digoxin; amiodarone for rhythm; ablation may improve outcomes
- **HFpEF**: Rate control + anticoagulation; ablation if symptomatic

### Post-Cardiac Surgery
- Incidence: 10-30% (CABG), 30-50% (valve surgery)
- Usually self-limiting (<6 weeks)
- Rate control first; amiodarone if rhythm control needed
- Anticoagulate per CHA2DS2-VASc

### WPW Syndrome
- **Avoid AV nodal blockers** (verapamil, beta-blockers, digoxin) — risk of 1:1 conduction → VF
- Use procainamide, ibutilide, or immediate cardioversion

### Chronic Kidney Disease
- Apixaban preferred (least renal clearance)
- Dose adjust per CrCl; avoid dabigatran if CrCl <30

### Pregnancy
- Avoid warfarin (1st trimester); LMWH throughout or LMWH→warfarin 2nd/3rd→LMWH near delivery
- Beta-blocker/digoxin for rate control

### 2:1 Flutter (Common Pitfall)
- **Ventricular rate ~150 bpm** with sawtooth F waves
- **Commonly missed** as sinus tachycardia or SVT
- **Clues**: Regular 150 bpm, clean sawtooth in II/III/aVF, V1 positive → think flutter
- **Management**: Treat as flutter; rate control, cardioversion, ablation, anticoagulate

## Complications
| Complication | Mechanism | Management |
|--------------|-----------|------------|
| Ischemic stroke/TIA | LA/LAA thrombus | Anticoagulation; acute stroke protocol |
| Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy | Chronic rapid rate | Rate/rhythm control → EF recovery |
| Progression to AF | Electrical remodeling | Ablation may reduce but not eliminate risk |
| Bleeding | Anticoagulation | DOAC preferred; reversal if major bleed |

## One-Page Revision Summary
- Atrial flutter = **macro-reentrant atrial tachycardia** (CTI-dependent = typical)
- **Sawtooth F waves + 150 bpm** = classic 2:1 typical flutter
- **CTI ablation** = first-line curative for typical flutter (>95% success)
- **Anticoagulate like AF** (CHA2DS2-VASc); DOAC preferred
- **2:1 flutter** = easily missed; regular 150 bpm + sawtooth = think flutter
- **WPW + flutter** = avoid AV nodal blockers (risk 1:1 VF)
- Post-op flutter often self-limiting; treat like AF

## MCQs
1. Typical atrial flutter ventricular rate is often:
   a) 100 bpm
   b) 150 bpm
   c) 200 bpm
   d) 250 bpm
   **Answer: b** — 2:1 conduction with atrial rate ~300 bpm.

2. Treatment of choice for recurrent typical atrial flutter:
   a) Beta-blockers
   b) Catheter ablation
   c) Amiodarone
   d) Digoxin
   **Answer: b** — CTI ablation >95% success.

3. 2:1 atrial flutter is commonly missed because:
   a) It causes no symptoms
   b) Ventricular rate ~150 mimics sinus tachycardia
   c) It always has wide QRS
   d) It only occurs in WPW
   **Answer: b** — Regular 150 bpm + sawtooth easily mistaken for sinus tach.

4. Anticoagulation in atrial flutter:
   a) Not needed
   b) Same as AF (CHA2DS2-VASc guided)
   c) Only aspirin
   d) Only warfarin
   **Answer: b** — Same thromboembolic risk profile.

5. Clockwise (reverse typical) atrial flutter:
   a) Has negative F waves in inferior leads
   b) Has positive F waves in inferior leads
   c) Is the most common type
   d) Cannot be ablated
   **Answer: b** — Reverse polarity of F waves.

6. The cavotricuspid isthmus is:
   a) The re-entry circuit boundary for typical flutter
   b) The AV node
   c) The bundle of His
   d) The sinus node
   **Answer: a** — CTI ablation targets this zone.

7. Atrial flutter in a patient with WPW:
   a) Treat with verapamil
   b) Avoid AV nodal blockers; use procainamide or cardioversion
   c) Digoxin is first choice
   d) Beta-blockers are safe
   **Answer: b** — AV blockers risk 1:1 conduction via accessory pathway.

8. Catheter ablation of typical flutter targets:
   a) Pulmonary veins
   b) Cavotricuspid isthmus
   c) AV node
   d) Left atrial appendage
   **Answer: b** — CTI line of block.

9. Atrial flutter after cardiac surgery:
   a) Always requires lifelong anticoagulation
   b) Usually self-limiting; rate control first; anticoagulate per CHA2DS2-VASc
   c) Never self-terminates
   d) Requires immediate ICD
   **Answer: b** — Often transient; rate control + anticoagulate per score.

10. 2:1 flutter with 150 bpm ventricular rate — why is it missed?
    a) It presents as chest pain only
    b) Mimics sinus tachycardia; regular rate, no obvious irregularity
    c) It always has wide QRS
    d) F waves are always absent
    **Answer: b** — Regular 150 bpm + sawtooth mimics sinus tach.

   b) Synchronized cardioversion
   c) IV procainamide
   d) Digoxin load
   **Answer: b** — Hemodynamically stable typical flutter: synchronized cardioversion is highly effective and first-line for rhythm control.

   a) IV verapamil
   b) IV diltiazem
   c) IV procainamide + synchronized cardioversion
   d) IV adenosine
   **Answer: c** — WPW + flutter = risk of 1:1 conduction via accessory pathway; avoid AV nodal blockers; procainamide or immediate cardioversion.

3. A 70-year-old post-CABG (day 3) develops HR 150, regular narrow QRS, sawtooth F waves. CHA2DS2-VASc = 3. Best management:
   a) Discharge on aspirin only
   b) Rate control + anticoagulation per score; most self-limiting
   c) Immediate ICD
   d) IV amiodarone only
   **Answer: b** — Post-op flutter often transient; rate control + anticoagulate per score.

4. A 32-year-old with atrial flutter refractory to flecainide. Next best step:
   a) Add digoxin
   b) Catheter ablation (CTI)
   c) Amiodarone
   d) AV node ablation
   **Answer: b** — Ablation is curative for typical flutter; preferred over long-term amiodarone in young patients.

5. A 75-year-old with typical flutter, CHA2DS2-VASc 5, HAS-BLED 3. Anticoagulation choice:
   a) Warfarin
   b) Apixaban 2.5 mg BD
   c) Rivaroxaban 15 mg OD
   d) Dabigatran 150 mg BD
   **Answer: b** — Apixaban 2.5 mg BD if ≥2: age ≥80, wt ≤60, Cr ≥1.5; lowest GI bleed risk.

6. A patient with atrial flutter has HR 150 bpm (2:1). Which maneuver best reveals sawtooth F waves?
   a) Valsalva
   b) Carotid sinus massage
   c) IV adenosine
   d) IV metoprolol
   **Answer: c** — Adenosine transiently increases AV block, revealing underlying flutter waves.

7. A 60-year-old with atypical flutter (post-ablation scar). Ablation target:
   a) Cavotricuspid isthmus
   b) Mitral isthmus
   c) Circuit mapping guided by entrainment/mapping
   d) Pulmonary veins
   **Answer: c** — Atypical flutter is non-CTI dependent; requires mapping/entrainment.

8. A 28-year-old woman with flutter on OCPs. Anticoagulation:
   a) Not needed
   b) CHA2DS2-VASc = 1 (female); consider anticoagulation
   c) Warfarin only
   d) Aspirin only
   **Answer: b** — Female sex adds 1 point; score 1 → consider anticoagulation per guidelines.

9. A patient with typical flutter develops new AF after CTI ablation. Significance:
   a) Ablation failed
   b) Atrial substrate for AF was present; ablation didn't create it
   c) Ablation caused AF
   d) Patient needs AV node ablation
   **Answer: b** — Shared atrial substrate; 30-50% develop AF post-flutter ablation.

10. A 60-year-old with flutter has 2:1 conduction. Why is it commonly missed?

    c) It's always wide QRS

## Flashcards
- Q: Typical atrial flutter re-entry circuit?
  A: Cavotricuspid isthmus (CTI) macro-reentry.
- Q: Sawtooth F waves polarity in typical flutter?
  A: Negative in II, III, aVF; positive in V1.
- Q: Ventricular rate in 2:1 typical flutter?
  A: ~150 bpm (atrial rate ~300 bpm).
- Q: First-line treatment for typical flutter?
  A: Catheter ablation (CTI) — curative, >95% success.
- Q: Anticoagulation in flutter?
  A: Same as AF — CHA2DS2-VASc ≥2 (♂)/≥3 (♀); DOAC preferred.
- Q: 2:1 flutter why missed?
  A: Ventricular rate 150 bpm mimics sinus tachycardia; sawtooth often unrecognized.
- Q: WPW + flutter danger?
  A: AV nodal blockers risk 1:1 conduction via accessory pathway → VF.
- Q: Ablation target for typical flutter?
  A: Cavotricuspid isthmus line of block.
- Q: Atypical flutter mechanism?
  A: Non-CTI dependent macro-reentry (post-surgical scar, post-ablation, mitral isthmus).
- Q: Post-op flutter management?
  A: Rate control + anticoagulate per CHA2DS2-VASc; usually transient.
