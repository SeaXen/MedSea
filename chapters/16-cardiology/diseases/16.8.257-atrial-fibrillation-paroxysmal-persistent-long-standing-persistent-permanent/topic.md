# Atrial Fibrillation (Complete A-to-Z)

> [!important]
> **Scope**: This note covers AF in general AND AF in heart failure. FCPS/MRCP exams test AF broadly; HF+AF is a high-yield subset.

## Learning Objectives
- Define AF and classify by pattern, duration, and Hemodynamic context
- Explain pathophysiology: triggers, substrate, perpetuation mechanisms
- Classify AF: first-detected, paroxysmal, persistent, long-standing persistent, permanent
- Apply CHA2DS2-VASc and HAS-BLED for anticoagulation decision-making
- Compare rate vs rhythm control: indications, agents, targets, trial evidence
- Manage anticoagulation: DOACs vs warfarin, dosing, peri-procedural, reversal
- Apply electrical and pharmacological cardioversion: timing, preparation, anticoagulation
- Determine catheter ablation indications: trial evidence (CASTLE-AF, CABANA, EAST-AFNET 4)
- Manage AF in special populations: HF (HFrEF/HFpEF), valvular, post-cardiac surgery, CKD, elderly, pregnancy, athletes
- Recognize and manage complications: thromboembolism, tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy, bleeding
- Apply peri-procedural anticoagulation bridging logic

## Investigations
**Essential**: ECG, TTE (LA size, LV function, valvular disease), troponin, thyroid function, renal/liver function, CXR
**Advanced**: Holter for rate assessment, TOE before cardioversion (if >48h or unknown duration), cardiac MRI for fibrosis/LA assessment

## Management Overview
| Domain | Key Principle |
|--------|---------------|
| **Anticoagulation** | CHA2DS2-VASc ≥2 (♂) or ≥3 (♀) → anticoagulate; DOAC preferred over warfarin |
| **Rate Control** | Target resting HR <80 bpm (lenient <110 if asymptomatic); beta-blocker/CCB/digoxin |
| **Rhythm Control** | Early rhythm control improves outcomes (EAST-AFNET 4); cardioversion or ablation |
| **Lifestyle** | Weight loss, alcohol reduction, OSA treatment, BP control reduce AF burden |

## Stroke Risk Stratification (CHA2DS2-VASc)
| Risk Factor | Points |
|-------------|--------|
| Congestive heart failure | 1 |
| Hypertension | 1 |
| Age ≥75 | 2 |
| Diabetes mellitus | 1 |
| Stroke/TIA/thromboembolism | 2 |
| Vascular disease (MI, PAD, aortic plaque) | 1 |
| Age 65–74 | 1 |
| Sex category (female) | 1 |

**Anticoagulate**: Score ≥2 (men) or ≥3 (women). Score 1 (men) or 2 (women): consider anticoagulation. Score 0: no anticoagulation.

## Bleeding Risk (HAS-BLED)
| Factor | Points |
|--------|--------|
| Hypertension (uncontrolled) | 1 |
| Abnormal renal/liver function | 1/1 |
| Stroke history | 1 |
| Bleeding history/predisposition | 1 |
| Labile INRs (on warfarin) | 1 |
| Elderly (>65) | 1 |
| Drugs/alcohol (antiplatelets, NSAIDs, alcohol) | 1/1 |

Score ≥3 = high bleeding risk (caution, not contraindication).

## Anticoagulation
### DOACs (Preferred over Warfarin)
| DOAC | Standard Dose | Renal Dose (CrCl 15–50) | CrCl <15 |
|------|---------------|-------------------------|----------|
| Apixaban | 5 mg BD | 2.5 mg BD if ≥2 of: age ≥80, weight ≤60kg, Cr ≥1.5 | Avoid (off-label 2.5 BD used) |
| Rivaroxaban | 20 mg OD | 15 mg OD | Avoid |
| Dabigatran | 150 mg BD | 110 mg BD | Avoid |
| Edoxaban | 60 mg OD | 30 mg OD | Avoid |

### Warfarin (for Valvular AF)
- Target INR 2.0–3.0 (mechanical mitral valve: 2.5–3.5)
- Bridge with LMWH if high thromboembolic risk during interruption

### Peri-procedural Management
| Procedure Risk | DOAC | Warfarin |
|----------------|------|----------|
| Low bleeding risk | Omit 1 dose (morning) | Continue (INR 2–3) |
| High bleeding risk | Hold 24–48h (renal function dependent) | Stop 5 days pre-op, bridge if high TE risk |

### Reversal Agents
- **Idarucizumab**: Dabigatran reversal
- **Andexanet alfa**: Apixaban/rivaroxaban/edoxaban reversal
- **PCC (4-factor)**: Warfarin reversal (INR >4.5 or urgent surgery)
- **Vitamin K**: Warfarin reversal (non-urgent)

## Rate Control
**Target**: Resting HR <80 bpm (LENIENT <110 bpm acceptable per RACE-II if asymptomatic)
**First-line**: Beta-blocker (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol, nebivolol) or non-DHP CCB (diltiazem, verapamil)
**Add-on**: Digoxin (sedentary, HFrEF, hypotensive)
**Refractory**: Amiodarone, AV node ablation + pacemaker/CRT

| Agent | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| Beta-blocker | First-line in HFrEF, hypertrophic CM, post-MI |
| Diltiazem/Verapamil | Avoid in HFrEF, WPW (risk VF) |
| Digoxin | Add-on for sedentary/HFrEF; slow onset; narrow therapeutic window |
| Amiodarone | Rate + rhythm control; avoid long-term if possible (toxicity) |

## Rhythm Control
### Electrical Cardioversion
**Indications**: Hemodynamic instability (immediate), symptomatic persistent AF, failed rhythm control drugs
**Anticoagulation**: 
- <48h duration: no pre-cardioversion anticoagulation needed (but post-cardioversion 4 weeks)
- >48h or unknown: anticoagulate ≥3 weeks before + ≥4 weeks after OR TOE-guided (no LA/LAA thrombus → cardiovert → anticoagulate 4 weeks)

### Pharmacological Cardioversion
| Agent | Use Case | Contraindications |
|-------|----------|-------------------|
| Flecainide/Propafenone | Paroxysmal AF, no structural heart disease | HFrEF, CAD, LVH, conduction disease |
| Amiodarone | HFrEF, structural heart disease | Long-term toxicity |
| Ibutilide/Dofetilide | In-hospital conversion, structural heart disease | QT prolongation, hypokalemia, renal impairment |

### Rhythm Control Drugs (Long-term)
| Drug | Population | Monitoring |
|------|------------|------------|
| Flecainide/Propafenone | Paroxysmal AF, normal heart | ECG QRS, LFTs |
| Sotalol | Persistent AF, preserved EF | QTc, renal function |
| Dronedarone | Paroxysmal/persistent, no HFrEF | LFTs, ECG (avoid in HFrEF/permanent AF) |
| Amiodarone | HFrEF, structural disease, refractory | Thyroid, LFTs, pulmonary, skin, eyes q6-12mo |

## Catheter Ablation
**Indications**: Symptomatic paroxysmal AF refractory to ≥1 AAD; selected persistent AF; HFrEF + AF (CASTLE-AF)
**Success Rates**: Paroxysmal 70–80% (single procedure); Persistent 50–60%
**Trial Evidence**:
- **CASTLE-AF**: Ablation in HFrEF → reduced all-cause mortality & HF hospitalization
- **CABANA**: Ablation vs drugs → no mortality benefit overall; per-protocol favored ablation
- **EAST-AFNET 4**: Early rhythm control (drugs/ablation) → reduced cardiovascular outcomes

**Complications**: PV stenosis (<1%), tamponade (1%), stroke/TIA (<1%), phrenic nerve injury, atrio-esophageal fistula (rare)

## AF in Special Populations
### Heart Failure
| Phenotype | Rate Control | Rhythm Control | Anticoagulation |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|-----------------|
| HFrEF (EF ≤40%) | Beta-blocker ± digoxin | Amiodarone, ablation (CASTLE-AF), avoid dronedarone | DOAC preferred |
| HFpEF (EF ≥50%) | Beta-blocker, CCB | Ablation if symptomatic | DOAC preferred |

### Valvular AF
- **Moderate-severe MS** or **mechanical valve**: Warfarin only (DOACs contraindicated)
- **Bioprosthetic valve** or **repair**: DOACs acceptable after 3 months

### Post-Cardiac Surgery AF
- Incidence 20–40% (CABG), 50–60% (valve)
- Usually self-limiting (<6 weeks)
- Rate control first; amiodarone for rhythm control if needed
- Anticoagulate per CHA2DS2-VASc; often transient

### Chronic Kidney Disease
- Apixaban preferred (least renal clearance)
- Dose adjust per CrCl; avoid dabigatran if CrCl <30
- Warfarin if CrCl <15 or dialysis

### Elderly (>80) / Frail
- Higher stroke AND bleeding risk
- Apixaban 2.5 mg BD (if ≥2: age ≥80, weight ≤60, Cr ≥1.5)
- Fall risk ≠ contraindication (stroke risk > bleed from fall)

### Pregnancy
- Warfarin teratogenic (1st trimester: embryopathy; 2nd/3rd: CNS hemorrhage)
- LMWH throughout or LMWH 1st trimester → warfarin 2nd/3rd → LMWH near delivery

### Athletes
- High vagal tone → paroxysmal AF
- Treat triggers (alcohol, sleep, electrolytes)
- Ablation often preferred over AADs (proarrhythmia risk)

### Hyperthyroidism
- Treat thyrotoxicosis first (AF often resolves)
- Beta-blocker for rate control
- Anticoagulate per CHA2DS2-VASc

## Complications
| Complication | Mechanism | Management |
|--------------|-----------|------------|
| Ischemic stroke/TIA | LA/LAA thrombus | Anticoagulation; acute stroke protocol |
| Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy | Chronic rapid rate (>110 bpm) | Rate/rhythm control → EF recovery in weeks-months |
| Major bleeding (GI, ICH) | Anticoagulation | Hold/reverse anticoagulant; GI workup |
| Cognitive decline/dementia | Silent cerebral infarcts | Optimal anticoagulation; rate control |
| Sudden cardiac death | Rare; WPW + rapid conduction | Urgent ablation if WPW |

## One-Page Revision Summary
- AF = irregularly irregular + no P waves + fibrillatory waves
- CHA2DS2-VASc ≥2 (♂) / ≥3 (♀) → anticoagulate (DOAC preferred)
- Rate control: beta-blocker/CCB target HR <80 (lenient <110)
- Rhythm control: early better (EAST-AFNET 4); ablation for symptomatic refractory
- Valvular AF (MS/mechanical valve) = warfarin only
- Ablation in HFrEF reduces mortality (CASTLE-AF)
- Early rhythm control improves outcomes (EAST-AFNET 4)
- Cardioversion: <48h no pre-anticoagulation; >48h need 3 weeks anticoag or TOE
- Tachycardia-mediated CM: EF recovers with rate control

## MCQs
1. First-line rate control agent in HFrEF + AF:
   a) Verapamil
   b) Beta-blocker
   c) Diltiazem
   d) Flecainide
   **Answer: b** — Beta-blockers first-line in HFrEF; CCBs worsen HFrEF.

2. Class Ic antiarrhythmics are contraindicated in:
   a) Lone AF
   b) HF with reduced EF
   c) Hyperthyroidism
   d) WPW syndrome
   **Answer: b** — Increase mortality in structural heart disease (CAST trial).

3. DOACs in non-valvular AF are preferred because:
   a) Cheaper
   b) Lower intracranial hemorrhage risk than warfarin
   c) No monitoring needed
   d) Both b and c
   **Answer: d** — Lower ICH, no INR monitoring, fewer drug interactions.

4. Catheter ablation in HFrEF + AF (CASTLE-AF):
   a) No benefit
   b) Reduces mortality and HF hospitalization
   c) Increases mortality
   d) Only for rate control
   **Answer: b** — Ablation > medical therapy in HFrEF.

5. Amiodarone in HF + AF is:
   a) Contraindicated
   b) Safest antiarrhythmic option
   c) First-line for rate control
   d) Only used for VT
   **Answer: b** — Avoid dronedarone/flecainide/propafenone in HFrEF.

6. Loss of atrial kick in HF + AF reduces cardiac output by:
   a) 5–10%
   b) 20–30%
   c) 50%
   d) No effect
   **Answer: b** — Atrial contribution is 20–30% in HF.

7. Anticoagulation in HF + AF is based on:
   a) Only age
   b) CHA2DS2-VASc score
   c) Only LVEF
   d) Only symptoms
   **Answer: b** — CHA2DS2-VASc guides anticoagulation.

8. Early rhythm control in AF (EAST-AFNET 4):
   a) Harmful in HF
   b) May be beneficial
   c) No effect
   d) Only for HFrEF
   **Answer: b** — Early rhythm control reduced cardiovascular outcomes.

9. AV node ablation + CRT is considered in:
   a) All AF patients
   b) Refractory rate control + HFrEF
   c) Only paroxysmal AF
   d) Only new AF
   **Answer: b** — When rate control fails despite drugs.

10. Which drug is avoided in HF + AF for rhythm control?
    a) Amiodarone
    b) Flecainide
    c) Dofetilide
    d) Sotalol
    **Answer: b** — Class Ic increases mortality in structural heart disease.

11. Apixaban dose reduction (2.5 mg BD) if ≥2 of:
    a) Age ≥75, weight <65kg, Cr >1.3
    b) Age ≥80, weight ≤60kg, Cr ≥1.5
    c) Age ≥70, weight <70kg, Cr >1.2
    d) Age ≥85, weight ≤55kg
    **Answer: b** — Standard criteria for 2.5 mg BD.

12. Cardioversion for AF >48h duration requires:
    a) No anticoagulation needed
    b) Anticoagulate 3 weeks before + 4 weeks after OR TOE-guided
    c) Only aspirin
    d) Heparin infusion only
    **Answer: b** — Prevent thromboembolism from atrial stunning.

13. Valvular AF requires:
    a) DOAC
    b) Warfarin
    c) No anticoagulation
    d) Aspirin only
    **Answer: b** — DOACs contraindicated in moderate-severe MS/mechanical valves.

14. AV node ablation + pacemaker is indicated for:
    a) First-line therapy
    b) Refractory rate control despite drugs
    c) Paroxysmal AF only
    d) Asymptomatic patients
    **Answer: b** — Last resort when rate control fails.

15. Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy:
    a) Irreversible LV dysfunction
    b) EF recovers with rate/rhythm control
    c) Only occurs in children
    d) Requires transplant
    **Answer: b** — EF typically recovers in weeks to months.

## SBA Questions
1. A 65-year-old with HFrEF (EF 30%) and new AF, HR 112, NYHA III. First step:
   a) Start flecainide
   b) Rate control with beta-blocker + anticoagulation
   c) Immediate cardioversion
   d) AV node ablation
   **Answer: b** — Rate control + anticoagulation first; avoid class Ic in HFrEF.

2. A 72-year-old with HFrEF + AF on amiodarone develops thyrotoxicosis. Next:
   a) Continue amiodarone
   b) Switch to alternative (dofetilide or ablation)
   c) Stop all rate control
   d) Add beta-blocker
   **Answer: b** — Amiodarone-induced thyrotoxicosis: stop amiodarone, consider dofetilide/ablation.

3. A 68-year-old with paroxysmal AF, CHA2DS2-VASc 4, CrCl 45 mL/min. Best anticoagulant:
   a) Warfarin
   b) Apixaban 5 mg BD
   c) Apixaban 2.5 mg BD
   d) Rivaroxaban 20 mg OD
   **Answer: b** — Apixaban 5 mg BD (dose reduce only if 2 of: age ≥80, weight ≤60, Cr ≥1.5).

4. A patient with mechanical mitral valve develops AF. Anticoagulation:
   a) Apixaban 5 mg BD
   b) Rivaroxaban 20 mg OD
   c) Warfarin INR 2.5–3.5
   d) Dabigatran 150 mg BD
   **Answer: c** — Mechanical mitral valve requires warfarin (INR 2.5–3.5); DOACs contraindicated.

5. A 75-year-old with persistent AF undergoes electrical cardioversion. Duration unknown. Anticoagulation:
   a) None needed
   b) TOE-guided: no thrombus → cardiovert → anticoagulate 4 weeks
   c) Heparin for 24h only
   d) Aspirin 300 mg only
   **Answer: b** — TOE-guided cardioversion for unknown duration.

6. A 55-year-old with paroxysmal AF, no structural heart disease, refractory to flecainide. Next:
   a) Add digoxin
   b) Catheter ablation
   c) Amiodarone
   d) AV node ablation
   **Answer: b** — Ablation preferred over long-term amiodarone in young patients.

7. A 80-year-old with AF, CHA2DS2-VASc 5, history of GI bleed on warfarin. Best anticoagulant:
   a) Stop anticoagulation
   b) Apixaban 2.5 mg BD
   c) Rivaroxaban 15 mg OD
   d) Dabigatran 110 mg BD
   **Answer: b** — Apixaban 2.5 mg BD (age ≥80 + low weight/renal); lowest GI bleed risk among DOACs.

8. A patient with paroxysmal AF has WPW on ECG during sinus rhythm. Avoided drug:
   a) Beta-blocker
   b) Verapamil
   c) Flecainide
   d) Amiodarone
   **Answer: b** — CCBs/beta-blockers can accelerate conduction via accessory pathway; avoid in WPW+AF.

9. A 70-year-old post-CABG (day 2) develops new AF, HR 130, stable. Management:
   a) Amiodarone IV immediate
   b) Rate control with beta-blocker; anticoagulate per CHA2DS2-VASc
   c) Immediate cardioversion
   d) Digoxin only
   **Answer: b** — Post-op AF usually self-limiting; rate control first; anticoagulate if CHA2DS2-VASc ≥2.

10. A 45-year-old athlete with paroxysmal AF, normal heart. Prefers no drugs. Best option:
    a) Lifestyle modification only
    b) Catheter ablation
    c) Flecainide
    d) Amiodarone
    **Answer: b** — Ablation curative; avoids long-term drug side effects in young active patient.

## Flashcards
- Q: AF ECG hallmark?
  A: Irregularly irregular R-R + absent P waves + fibrillatory (f) waves.
- Q: CHA2DS2-VASc threshold for anticoagulation?
  A: ≥2 (men), ≥3 (women).
- Q: Rate control target?
  A: Resting HR <80 bpm (lenient <110 if asymptomatic per RACE-II).
- Q: First-line rate control in HFrEF?
  A: Beta-blocker (bisoprolol/carvedilol/nebivolol/metoprolol succinate).
- Q: Rhythm control trial showing mortality benefit in HFrEF?
  A: CASTLE-AF (ablation); EAST-AFNET 4 (early rhythm control).
- Q: DOAC preferred over warfarin why?
  A: Lower ICH, no INR monitoring, fewer interactions.
- Q: Valvular AF = what?
  A: Moderate-severe MS or mechanical heart valve → warfarin only.
- Q: Cardioversion >48h unknown duration?
  A: Anticoagulate 3 weeks pre + 4 weeks post OR TOE-guided.
- Q: Tachycardia-mediated cardiomyopathy reversibility?
  A: EF recovers with rate/rhythm control (weeks-months).
- Q: Ablation success paroxysmal AF?
  A: 70–80% single procedure; persistent 50–60%.
- Q: Apixaban dose reduction criteria?
  A: ≥2 of: age ≥80, weight ≤60kg, Cr ≥1.5 → 2.5 mg BD.
- Q: WPW + AF avoided drugs?
  A: AV nodal blockers (verapamil, diltiazem, beta-blockers, digoxin).
