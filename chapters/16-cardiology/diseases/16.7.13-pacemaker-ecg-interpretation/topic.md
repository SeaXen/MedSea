# Pacemaker ECG Interpretation

## 1. Introduction
Pacemaker ECGs show **pacing spikes** before captured beats. Correct interpretation requires knowing the device type, settings, and the patient's intrinsic rhythm.

## 2. Nomenclature — NBG Code
| I | II | III | IV | V |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Chamber paced | Chamber sensed | Response to sensing | Programmability | Multisite pacing |
| A, V, D (O = none) | A, V, D (O = none) | T, I, D, R (O = none) | R, C, P, M, O | A, V, D, O |

Examples:
- **AAI** — atrial pacing, atrial sensing, inhibited by sensed atrial event
- **VVI** — ventricular pacing, ventricular sensing, inhibited by sensed QRS
- **DDD** — dual chamber pacing + sensing, dual response (P-synchronous ventricular pacing or inhibited by intrinsic activity)
- **VVI(R)** — rate-responsive VVI (sensor-driven, e.g. accelerometer)

## 3. Normal Pacemaker Patterns
- **Pacing spike → capture (P or QRS)**
- **Single chamber atrial:** spike before P, P-QRS-T
- **Single chamber ventricular:** spike before wide QRS (LBBB morphology from RV apex)
- **Dual chamber:** atrial spike → P → AV delay → ventricular spike → QRS (if no intrinsic conduction)

## 4. Pacing Modes in Clinical Use
- **VVI** — single-chamber ventricular (e.g. permanent AF + bradycardia)
- **AAI** — single-chamber atrial (rare; sinus node dysfunction with intact AV conduction)
- **DDD** — most common dual-chamber (preserves AV synchrony)
- **CRT (biventricular):** pacing from RV + coronary sinus (LV) → narrow QRS, restores synchrony in LBBB
- **ICD:** pacing + shock therapy for VT/VF

## 5. ECG Clues to Pacemaker Type
- **Paced QRS = LBBB morphology** = RV apex pacing
- **Paced QRS = RBBB morphology** = LV pacing (CRT, lead malposition, lead in LV via PFO/ASD)
- **Spike-P-spike-QRS** = dual-chamber (DDD)
- **Just spike-QRS** = single-chamber ventricular
- **CRT:** pacing spikes in BOTH lateral and inferior leads (RV + LV)

## 6. Pacemaker Malfunction
| Problem | Description | Causes |
|---|---|---|
| **Failure to pace** | No spike when expected | Battery depletion, lead fracture, oversensing, exit block |
| **Failure to capture** | Spike present but no P/QRS | Lead dislodgement, exit block, ischaemia, electrolyte |
| **Failure to sense (undersensing)** | Spike despite intrinsic activity (QRS in T) | Lead issue, low sensitivity setting |
| **Oversensing** | Pacing inhibited by non-cardiac signals (T waves, muscle) | Sensitivity too high, lead issue |
| **Hysteresis** | Long escape interval before pacing | Normal feature |

## 7. Special Patterns
- **Pacemaker-mediated tachycardia (PMT):** endless loop tachycardia — retrograde P → tracked → ventricular pace → retrograde P; treat with magnet, reprogramming
- **Mode switch** (in DDD) — switches to DDI/VVI in response to AT/AF (avoids rapid ventricular pacing)
- **Atrial non-capture** — spike but no P, AF or atrial lead issue
- **Cross-talk** — atrial output sensed by ventricular lead → inhibition (in DDD)

## 8. Magnet Response
- **Magnet on → asynchronous pacing** (DOO/VOO/AOO) at set rate; confirms device function
- ICD: magnet suspends therapy (in most devices)

## 9. Biventricular Pacing (CRT)
- Biventricular pacing produces narrower QRS than intrinsic LBBB
- If 100% pacing → expect QRS <130 ms
- Loss of biventricular capture (LV capture loss) → wide QRS, RBBB morphology, loss of benefit

> **Pearls**
> - **Failure to capture with spike** is a **medical emergency** — check lead, ischaemia, electrolytes.
> - **Hysteresis** prolongs the escape interval to encourage intrinsic rhythm (saves battery).
> - **CRT in LBBB + LVEF ≤35% + NYHA II–IV** = mortality benefit (CARE-HF, COMPANION).
> - Always check **% pacing** on device interrogation — <98% biventricular pacing negates CRT benefit.
