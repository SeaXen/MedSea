CARDIOLOGY
Medical therapy (vasodilators in chronic AR)
1 Role of Medical Therapy

There is no definitive medical therapy that slows AR progression or alters the need for surgery. Pharmacotherapy is adjunctive, used to:

  • Control hypertension (reduces AR severity, delays surgery)
  • Bridge symptomatic patients to surgery
  • Treat co-existing cardiovascular disease
2 Vasodilators — The Mainstay
Drug ClassExamplesRationale
---------
**ACE inhibitors**Enalapril, lisinopril, ramipril↓ Afterload → ↓ regurgitant volume
**ARBs**Losartan, valsartanAlternative if ACEi intolerant
**Dihydropyridine CCBs**Nifedipine, amlodipine↓ Afterload (especially if ACEi cough)
**Hydralazine**(Historical)↓ SVR; not first-line
Key Trial — Scognamiglio et al. (Nifedipine)
  • Nifedipine in asymptomatic severe AR with normal EF: delayed time to AVR compared to digoxin
  • ACEi data mixed (limited outcome benefit)
  • Use is wider in patients with HTN; in normotensives, avoid symptomatic hypotension
3 When to Use Vasodilators (ESC / AHA)
  • Chronic AR + systemic hypertension → first-line
  • Severe AR with symptoms awaiting surgery → bridge
  • Asymptomatic severe AR with normal EF → controversial; consider if borderline BP, LV dilating
  • Severe AR with LVEF 50–55% (declining) → bridge while planning surgery
  • After AVR → continuation if HTN or LV dysfunction
⚠️ Do NOT use vasodilators to avoid surgery in symptomatic severe AR or with EF <50% — operate.
4 Avoid in Severe AR
  • Intra-aortic balloon pump (IABP) — increases regurgitation
  • Excessive afterload reduction in hypotensives — worsens forward output
  • Beta-blockade as monotherapy for afterload — not first-line
  • Negative inotropes (verapamil, diltiazem) without rate benefit
5 Beta-Blockers
  • Not standard for AR alone
  • Use for: co-existing CAD, HFrEF, AF rate control, post-MI
  • In aortic root disease / Marfan: beta-blockers reduce aortic stress (rate, dP/dt) — recommended
  • In acute AR + dissection: beta-blocker first (reduce shear) before vasodilator
6 Statins
  • Earlier hope that statins slow AR progression (similar rationale to AS)
  • No consistent benefit in chronic AR (small trials)
  • Reasonable if concomitant atherosclerosis, hyperlipidaemia, CAD
7 Anticoagulation
  • Not routine in AR
  • Add if: AF, mechanical prosthetic valve, previous thromboembolism
  • Aortic dissection → immediate anticoagulation controversy (medical vs surgical)
8 Endocarditis Prophylaxis
  • Not required in native chronic AR (NICE 2008 guidelines)
  • Required if: prosthetic valve, prior IE, cyanotic unrepaired CHD, recent prosthetic material
9 Acute Severe AR — Medical Bridge to Surgery
DrugUse
------
**IV nitroprusside**↓ Afterload, ↓ regurgitant volume
**IV diuretics** (furosemide)↓ Pulmonary congestion
**IV inotropes** (dobutamine)Maintain forward output
**Avoid IABP**Increases AR
**Beta-blocker** (if dissection)Reduce shear
Goal: stabilise → emergency surgery (valve ± root)
10 Marfan / Connective Tissue
  • Beta-blocker (atenolol, propranolol, bisoprolol) — slows aortic dilation
  • ARB (losartan) — shown in mouse models to ↓ TGF-β → slows root dilation
  • Combination therapy (BB + ARB) — limited paediatric trial data
  • Avoid strenuous exercise, contact sports, isometric loading
11 Follow-up
ScenarioFrequency
------
Asymptomatic mild-moderate AR, normal LVTTE q 2–3 yr
Asymptomatic severe AR, normal LVTTE q 6–12 mo
Severe AR + LV dilation approaching thresholdTTE q 4–6 mo
Post-AVRTTE at 30 d, 6 mo, then yearly
Pearls - Vasodilators (ACEi / ARB / CCB) for chronic AR + HTN or pre-surgery - No medical therapy avoids the need for surgery once criteria met - Beta-blocker in Marfan / aortic root disease reduces shear stress - Avoid IABP in severe AR — worsens regurgitation - In acute AR with dissection: beta-blocker first, then nitroprusside
