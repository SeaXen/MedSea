CARDIOLOGY
Fibrinolysis (indications, contraindications, agents, rescue PCI)
1 Definition

Fibrinolysis = pharmacologic reperfusion using thrombolytic agents to dissolve the occlusive coronary thrombus in STEMI. Used when primary PCI cannot be performed within 120 min of first medical contact (FMC).

2 Indications
  • STEMI with symptoms ≤12 h (Class I)
  • No contraindications to fibrinolysis
  • PCI not available within 120 min of FMC
  • ECG shows ST elevation ≥1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads (or new LBBB with Sgarbossa)
  • True posterior MI (ST depression V1–V3 with confirmed posterior STEMI)
3 Absolute Contraindications
  • Prior intracranial haemorrhage (any time)
  • Known cerebral vascular malformation (e.g., AVM, aneurysm)
  • Known intracranial neoplasm
  • Ischaemic stroke within 3 months (excluding acute stroke <4.5 h)
  • Active internal bleeding (excluding menses)
  • Suspected aortic dissection
  • Significant closed-head or facial trauma within 3 months
  • Intracranial or intraspinal surgery within 2 months
4 Relative Contraindications
  • BP >180/110 mmHg (must lower first)
  • Recent major surgery (<3 weeks)
  • Recent GI bleed (<1 month)
  • Pregnancy
  • Active peptic ulcer
  • Prolonged CPR (>10 min)
  • Current anticoagulation with elevated INR
  • Non-compressible vascular puncture
  • Prior allergic reaction to fibrinolytic agent
5 Time Targets
  • Door → Needle ≤30 min
  • Best outcomes if given <3 h of symptom onset
  • Benefit diminishes but still present up to 12 h
  • Beyond 12 h → PCI preferred (or no reperfusion)

7 Adjunctive Therapy
  • Aspirin 150–325 mg chewed at presentation, then indefinitely
  • P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel 300 mg loading, then 75 mg daily)
  • Anticoagulation for ≥48 h (or up to 8 days):
  • Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SC q12h (preferred over UFH in fibrinolysis — ExTRACT)
  • UFH 60 U/kg bolus → 12 U/kg/h infusion
  • Fondaparinux 2.5 mg SC daily (effective, OASIS-6)
  • Statin, beta-blocker (avoid if shock/HF), ACEi
8 Monitoring Post-Fibrinolysis
  • ECG 60–90 min post-bolus — look for >50% ST resolution (success)
  • Cardiac biomarkers (troponin peaks earlier)
  • Reperfusion arrhythmias (idioventricular rhythm, AF, VT — usually transient)
  • Bleeding watch (intracranial, GI, puncture sites)
9 Successful vs Failed Reperfusion

Success (clinical):

  • ST resolution ≥50% at 60–90 min
  • Resolution of chest pain
  • Reperfusion arrhythmia
  • Idioventricular rhythm (AIVR)

Failure (consider rescue PCI):

  • Persistent ST elevation
  • Persistent pain
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • → Rescue PCI within 12 h (REACT, MERLIN trials — better outcomes than repeat fibrinolysis or conservative)
10 Rescue PCI
  • Urgent PCI after failed fibrinolysis
  • Indicated for: persistent ST elevation, chest pain, hemodynamic instability
  • Performed at the PCI-capable centre after transfer
  • Avoid repeat fibrinolysis (increased bleeding, no benefit)
  • Routine early PCI (3–24 h after successful fibrinolysis) — strategy of choice (CARESS-in-AMI, TRANSFER-AMI)
11 Bleeding Complications
  • Intracranial haemorrhage — most feared (0.5–1%, higher with TNK/rPA vs SK)
  • Major bleeding 1–2%
  • Management: stop fibrinolytic, cryoprecipitate, FFP, platelets, antifibrinolytics (tranexamic acid)
  • Avoid further antiplatelets/anticoagulants until bleeding controlled
Pearls - Door-to-needle ≤30 min if PCI unavailable within 120 min - Tenecteplase (TNK) = preferred (single bolus, weight-adjusted) - Clopidogrel is the P2Y12 of choice (ticagrelor/prasugrel not studied with fibrinolysis — risk of catastrophic bleeding) - Enoxaparin > UFH as adjunct (ExTRACT-TIMI 25) - ST resolution ≥50% at 90 min = success - Failed lysis → rescue PCI (do not repeat fibrinolysis) - Routine early PCI 3–24 h after successful lysis improves outcomes
