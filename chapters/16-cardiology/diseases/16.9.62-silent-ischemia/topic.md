# Silent Ischaemia

<callout icon="💔" color="red_bg">
**Topic:** Silent Ischaemia — Cardiology
**Style:** Sea Knowledge study infographic
**Audience:** FCPS / MRCP exam prep
</callout>

## 1. Definition
**Objective evidence of ischaemia (ST depression on ECG, perfusion defect, RWMA) in the absence of angina or anginal-equivalent symptoms.** Common — up to **2–4× more frequent** than symptomatic ischaemia.

### Forms
- **Type I:** Completely asymptomatic + no prior MI
- **Type II:** Documented CAD with both symptomatic AND asymptomatic episodes
- **Type III:** Post-MI asymptomatic ischaemia (high-risk)

---

## 2. Pathophysiology
- Higher pain threshold
- Autonomic neuropathy (diabetes, elderly) — **#1 cause**
- Smaller ischaemic burden
- Anterior / apical ischaemia less symptomatic (less vagal afferents)
- Beta-endorphin / higher endogenous opioid levels

---

## 3. Clinical Significance
- **Same prognosis as symptomatic ischaemia** for adverse events
- High-risk populations: **DM, elderly, post-MI, prior CABG**
- Often detected on screening stress test

---

## 4. Diagnosis
- **Exercise ECG / stress imaging** for screening
- **Ambulatory ECG (Holter)** — ST monitoring 24–48 h detects transient ischaemia
- Especially useful in DM, post-MI patients

---

## 5. Treatment
- Same as symptomatic ischaemia: **anti-anginal + disease-modifying**
- **Beta-blocker** most effective at reducing silent episodes
- **Revascularisation** if high-risk anatomy / large ischaemic burden
- DM: aggressive risk factor control, SGLT2i / GLP-1 RA
- Cardiac rehab, lifestyle modification

---

## 6. Key Pearls

> **Pearls**
- **Silent ischaemia is more common than symptomatic** in CAD
- **Diabetic neuropathy** is a major cause (autonomic denervation)
- **Same prognosis** as symptomatic ischaemia
- Detection: stress testing, Holter monitoring
- Treatment: same as symptomatic ischaemia (BB, risk factor control, revascularisation if indicated)
