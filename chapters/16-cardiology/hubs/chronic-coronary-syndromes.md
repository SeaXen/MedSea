# Chronic Coronary Syndromes (CCS)

<callout icon="💔" color="red_bg">
**Topic:** Chronic Coronary Syndromes — Cardiology
**Style:** Sea Knowledge study infographic
**Audience:** FCPS / MRCP exam prep
</callout>

## 1. Introduction
CCS (ESC 2019) replaced "stable angina" — encompasses **six clinical scenarios** of chronic, often slowly progressive coronary disease, beyond just exertional angina. Includes stable angina, microvascular angina, vasospastic angina, silent ischaemia, post-revascularisation, and asymptomatic post-ACS.

### Pathogenesis
**Atherosclerotic narrowing** of epicardial arteries (≥70% stenosis, ≥50% left main) → fixed supply limitation → demand–supply mismatch on exertion. **Ischaemia cascade**: perfusion heterogeneity → diastolic dysfunction → ECG changes → angina.

---

## 2. Six Clinical Scenarios (CCSAP 2019)
1. **Suspected CAD** with stable angina ± dyspnoea
2. **New-onset CCS** or recent destabilisation
3. **Symptomatic despite treatment**
4. **Asymptomatic post-revascularisation** (≥1 y)
5. **Asymptomatic 1 y post-ACS** or recent revasc
6. **Asymptomatic with detected ischaemia** (e.g. screening in DM, high-risk job)

### Pathophysiology Variants
- **Fixed atherosclerotic stenosis** (most common)
- **Vasospasm** (Prinzmetal, focal/diffuse epicardial)
- **Microvascular dysfunction** (Syndrome X, INOCA — normal angio)
- **Myocardial bridge** (systolic compression of LAD)
- **Recurrent ischaemia** post-revascularisation (restenosis, graft disease)

---

## 3. Classification
### Canadian Cardiovascular Society (CCS) Class
| Class | Limitation | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | None | Ordinary activity |
| **II** | Slight | Vigorous / rapid exertion |
| **III** | Marked | Ordinary activity |
| **IV** | At rest | Symptoms at rest |

### Pre-test Probability of CAD (age/sex/symptom)
- **Low (<15%)** — no test, or CAC scoring
- **Intermediate (15–50%)** — stress imaging (echo, nuclear, CMR) or CTCA
- **High (>50%)** — direct to invasive angio or functional test

---

## 4. Clinical Features
- **Typical angina (3):** (1) substernal pressure/heaviness, (2) provoked by exertion/emotion, (3) relieved by rest/nitrates in minutes
- **Atypical (women, elderly, DM):** dyspnoea, fatigue, nausea, jaw/back pain
- **Examination:** usually normal; S4, MR (papillary dysfunction), signs of dyslipidaemia (xanthelasma, corneal arcus)
- **Risk factors:** HTN, DM, dyslipidaemia, smoking, family, obesity
- **Atypical equivalents** common in women, elderly, diabetics

---

## 5. Investigations
### Resting & Stress
- **Rest ECG** — normal in 50%; may show Q waves, LVH, ST-T changes
- **Exercise ECG** — positive: ≥1 mm ST ↓ at 80 ms post-J in ≥2 contiguous leads
- **Stress echo** — regional wall motion abnormality (RWMA), more sensitive/specific
- **Stress nuclear (SPECT/PET)** — perfusion defect
- **Stress CMR** — perfusion + LGE
- **CTCA** — anatomy; rule-out for low–intermediate probability
- **Invasive angio** — gold standard
- **FFR / iFR** — functional significance of intermediate 50–90% lesions
- **CMR** — viability, scar, microvascular obstruction

### Severity & Risk Stratification
- **Duke Treadmill Score:** <−11 high-risk, −10 to +4 intermediate, ≥+5 low
- **Stress parameters:** time, workload, HR recovery, BP, ischaemia threshold
- **Anatomy:** 1, 2, 3 vessel, left main (≥50%)

---

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
| Condition | Key Features | Distinguishing Test |
|---|---|---|
| **Oesophageal spasm / GERD** | Burning, meal-related, no exertion | Endoscopy, pH, normal ECG |
| **MSK (costochondritis)** | Reproducible, localised | Local tenderness, normal ECG |
| **Aortic stenosis** | Exertional, ejection systolic murmur | Echo |
| **HCM** | Exertional, ↑with Valsalva | Echo, CMR |
| **Pericarditis** | Pleuritic, rub, diffuse STE + PR ↓ | Echo, ECG |
| **PE** | Pleuritic, hypoxia, ↑D-dimer | CTPA |
| **Aortic dissection** | Tearing → back, BP differential | CT angio |
| **Microvascular angina (INOCA)** | Female, abnormal stress, normal angio | CFR <2.0, IMR >25 |
| **Vasospastic (Prinzmetal)** | Rest pain, transient STE, CCB-responsive | Provocation test |
| **SCAD** | Young women, peripartum | CTCA / angio |

---

## 7. Complications
- **ACS** (plaque rupture)
- **MI** (occlusion)
- **Heart failure** (ischaemic cardiomyopathy)
- **Arrhythmia** (VT, AF, bradyarrhythmia)
- **Death** (5-yr mortality 5% with treatment, 15% without)
- **Repeated hospitalisation** for angina / revascularisation
- **Anxiety / depression** — common, often unrecognised

---

## 8. Management
### Lifestyle / Risk Factor Modification (ESC 2019)
- **Smoking cessation** (most powerful single intervention)
- **BP <130/80 mmHg**
- **LDL-C <1.4 mmol/L** (very high risk), <1.8 (high); non-HDL-C / ApoB secondary
- **HbA1c <7%**
- **BMI <25, exercise ≥150 min/week**, Mediterranean diet, alcohol <14 U/wk

### Anti-Anginal Ladder
1. **Beta-blocker** 1st line (bisoprolol, atenolol, metoprolol)
2. **CCB (dihydropyridine)** — amlodipine, nifedipine (add/alternative to BB)
3. **Long-acting nitrate** (isosorbide mononitrate) — nitrate-free interval 8–12 h
4. **Nicorandil** (K-ATP opener)
5. **Ranolazine** (late Na⁺ inhibitor, QT)
6. **Ivabradine** (sinus rhythm, HR ≥70)
7. **Trimetazidine** (metabolic modulator)

### Disease-Modifying Therapy
| Drug Class | Agent / Dose |
|---|---|
| **Antiplatelet** | Aspirin 75–100 mg, or Clopidogrel 75 mg |
| **Statin** | Atorvastatin 40–80 mg, Rosuvastatin 20–40 mg |
| **ACEi / ARB** | If HTN, DM, CKD, LV dysfunction |
| **Ezetimibe / PCSK9i** | If LDL target not reached |

### Revascularisation
- **Indicated** for refractory symptoms, CCS III–IV, LVEF <40%, left main / proximal LAD / 3-vessel disease
- **PCI** vs **CABG** — guided by **SYNTAX score** (≤22 → PCI, >22 → CABG), diabetes + multivessel → CABG preferred
- **ISCHEMIA (2020):** routine invasive adds no overall death/MI benefit vs optimal medical therapy (except left main, 3-vessel with LV dysfunction)

---

## 9. Post-Revascularisation Care
### Post-PCI
- **DAPT** — 1 month (BMS) to 6 months (DES); 12 months if ACS indication
- **Shorter (3–6 mo)** if high bleeding risk (PRECISE-DAPT >25)
- **Longer (>12 mo)** if high ischaemic risk (DAPT score ≥2)
- **Statin** + risk factor control indefinitely
- **In-stent restenosis** — DCB, repeat DES, or CABG

### Post-CABG
- **Aspirin 75–100 mg** indefinitely (± P2Y₁₂ if ACS)
- **Statin** lifelong
- **Beta-blocker** for ≥1 y (or indefinitely if LV dysfunction)
- **ACEi/ARB** if HTN, DM, LV dysfunction
- **Graft surveillance** — symptom-driven or annual stress test

---

## 10. Exam Pearls & High-Yield
<callout icon="⭐" color="purple_bg">
**12 high-yield points** the FCPS / MRCP examiner loves.
</callout>

- **Typical angina** = 3 of 3: substernal, exertional, relieved by rest/nitrates.
- **Atypical angina** (women, elderly, diabetics) = dyspnoea, jaw/back pain, fatigue.
- **CCS Class IV = angina at rest** (unstable equivalent — needs urgent workup).
- **Duke Treadmill Score <−11 = high risk** → refer for angio.
- **CTCA** is the test of choice to **rule out** CAD in low–intermediate risk.
- **FFR ≤0.80** = haemodynamically significant → revascularise.
- **SYNTAX score** guides PCI vs CABG (>22 → CABG, ≤22 → PCI equipoise).
- **Microvascular angina (INOCA)** = female, abnormal stress, normal angio, CFR <2.0.
- **Vasospastic (Prinzmetal)** = transient STE at rest, normal angio, CCB-responsive, **avoid beta-blockers**.
- **SCAD** = young women, peripartum, often missed — CTCA/angio.
- **ISCHEMIA (2020)** — routine invasive adds no overall mortality benefit over optimal medical therapy (except left main, 3-vessel).
- **LDL target <1.4 mmol/L** in very-high-risk (ESC 2023); add ezetimibe/PCSK9i if needed.

<callout icon="🎯" color="rose_bg">
**CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE —** CCS is a chronic, relapsing condition. **Lifestyle + statin + antiplatelet + ACEi** form the foundation. Anti-anginals are added **stepwise** (BB → CCB → nitrate → nicorandil → ranolazine → ivabradine). **Revascularisation** is reserved for refractory symptoms or high-risk anatomy (left main, 3-vessel, proximal LAD). The **ISCHEMIA trial** reminds us that stable CAD without high-risk anatomy does well with optimal medical therapy.
</callout>
