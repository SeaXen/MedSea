# TORCH infections in pregnancy

---

## Definition

TORCH infections (Toxoplasma, Other, Rubella, CMV, Herpes/HIV) and others (syphilis, parvovirus B19, varicella, listeria) can cause congenital infection with fetal loss, malformation, or long-term sequelae. Antenatal screening and prevention are key.

## Core Clinical Content

### Toxoplasmosis
Cat faeces, undercooked meat; hydrocephalus, intracranial calcifications, chorioretinitis; spiramycin ± pyrimethamine/sulfadiazine after 1st trimester

### Rubella
Vaccine-preventable; CRS — cataracts, sensorineural deafness, PDA; offer post-partum vaccination

### CMV
Most common congenital infection; sensorineural hearing loss, microcephaly, periventricular calcifications; no proven treatment

### Herpes simplex
Primary genital herpes near delivery → caesarean; recurrent — discussion; aciclovir safe

### Parvovirus B19
Hydrops fetalis; aplastic crisis in mother; serology + MCA Doppler

### Varicella
Pneumonitis in adult; fetal varicella syndrome (<20 weeks); shingles immunoglobulin to neonate if maternal rash 5 days before to 2 days after delivery

### Syphilis
Routine screen; penicillin treatment; desensitize if allergic; Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction

### Listeria
Unpasteurised cheese, deli meats; granulomatosis infantiseptica; ampicillin + gentamicin

### Zika
Microcephaly; travel avoidance; semen transmission 6 months; no sexual partners from endemic areas

### Screening
Rubella, syphilis, HIV, hepatitis B — all antenatal; varicella, CMV, toxoplasma if symptomatic
