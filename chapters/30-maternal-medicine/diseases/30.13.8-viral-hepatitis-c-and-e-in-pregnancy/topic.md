# Viral hepatitis C and E in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Viral hepatitis C (HCV) and E (HEV) in pregnancy are less common than HBV but carry specific risks. HCV has a low vertical transmission rate (5%) but is increased with co-infection (HIV, high viral load). HEV in pregnancy is associated with severe hepatitis and fulminant liver failure, particularly in the 3rd trimester.

## Core Clinical Content

### Hepatitis C (HCV)
Most common blood-borne virus; affects 1-2% pregnancies; vertical transmission 5% (higher with HIV co-infection or high VL)

### Hepatitis E (HEV)
Faecal-oral; endemic (Asia, Africa, Central America); 3rd-trimester mortality 20-30% (vs <1% in non-pregnant)

### HCV diagnosis
Anti-HCV; HCV RNA PCR; LFT; genotype

### HEV diagnosis
Anti-HEV IgM/IgG; HEV RNA; LFT; consider in unexplained hepatitis

### HCV management
Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) deferred until postpartum; monitor LFT; avoid invasive procedures in labour (scalp electrodes); avoid prolonged ROM; avoid instrumental if possible

### HCV delivery
Mode does not affect transmission; avoid internal fetal monitoring; avoid prolonged ROM >6 h

### HCV breastfeeding
Safe (if no cracked nipples)

### HCV neonate
Test at 12-18 months (anti-HCV); PCR if early

### HEV management
Supportive; ribavirin not used (teratogenic); consider delivery if severe; fulminant failure may need transplant

### HEV prevention
Avoid undercooked meat (zoonotic); safe water; HEV vaccine (Hecolin) in endemic areas
