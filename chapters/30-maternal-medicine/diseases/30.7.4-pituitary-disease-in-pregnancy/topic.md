# Pituitary disease in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Pituitary disease in pregnancy includes prolactinoma (most common), hypopituitarism, lymphocytic hypophysitis, and Sheehan syndrome (postpartum pituitary infarction). Physiological lactotroph hyperplasia enlarges the pituitary, affecting imaging and symptoms.

## Core Clinical Content

### Physiology
Pituitary enlarges 30-50% in pregnancy; lactotrophs proliferate; other axes relatively suppressed

### Prolactinoma
Most common; microadenomas (<10 mm) usually asymptomatic; macroprolactinomas need monitoring

### Management prolactinoma
Dopamine agonists (cabergoline preferred) — discontinue in pregnancy if possible; restart if symptoms or growth

### Visual fields
Monitor macroprolactinomas with monthly visual fields; MRI if symptoms

### Lymphocytic hypophysitis
Peripartum; mimics pituitary mass with headache, visual loss, hypopituitarism; biopsy may be needed

### Sheehan syndrome
Postpartum pituitary infarction following severe PPH; failure to lactate, amenorrhoea, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency

### Diabetes insipidus
May worsen in pregnancy (vasopressinase from placenta); desmopressin safe

### Hypopituitarism
Hydrocortisone + levothyroxine; growth hormone discontinued; close MDT monitoring

### Cushing disease
Rare; surgery in 2nd trimester if confirmed

### Postpartum
Reassess pituitary function 3-6 months; restart dopamine agonist if breastfeeding; MRI if macroadenoma
