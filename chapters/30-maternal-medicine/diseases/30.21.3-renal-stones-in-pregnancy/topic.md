# Renal stones in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Renal stone disease (nephrolithiasis) in pregnancy affects 1 in 1,500 pregnancies. Physiological hydronephrosis and stasis of pregnancy increase risk. Stones may present with flank pain, haematuria, or infection. Most pass spontaneously; management is supportive with intervention reserved for obstruction, infection, or intractable pain.

## Core Clinical Content

### Incidence
1 in 1,500 pregnancies; similar to non-pregnant; right > left

### Composition
Calcium oxalate (75%); calcium phosphate; uric acid; struvite; cystine

### Risk factors
Personal/family history; dehydration; high oxalate diet; hyperparathyroidism; gout; UTI; IBD; bariatric surgery

### Presentation
Severe flank pain radiating to groin; haematuria (90%); nausea/vomiting; dysuria; may mimic abruption, appendicitis, pyelonephritis

### Investigations
Urine dipstick + culture; USS (first-line; safe; hydronephrosis common); MRI urogram if needed; low-dose CT rarely

### Differential
Pyelonephritis; appendicitis; placental abruption; ovarian torsion; ectopic; AAA (older)

### Management conservative
Hydration; analgesia (paracetamol; codeine; morphine; avoid NSAIDs especially in 3rd trimester); tamsulosin (medical expulsive)

### Indications for intervention
Obstruction with infection; bilateral obstruction; solitary kidney; intractable pain/vomiting; declining renal function

### Interventions
Ureteric stent (PCN if stenting fails); ureteroscopy with laser lithotripsy (in experienced hands); ESWL contraindicated

### Delivery
Not affected unless obstructed in labour; vaginal preferred; monitor renal function
