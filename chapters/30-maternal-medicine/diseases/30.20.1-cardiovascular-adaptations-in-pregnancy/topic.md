# Cardiovascular adaptations in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Cardiovascular adaptations to pregnancy include increased blood volume, cardiac output and heart rate, reduced systemic vascular resistance, and a hyperdynamic, vasodilated circulation. Recognising these changes is essential to avoid misinterpreting normal pregnancy findings as pathology.

## Core Clinical Content

### Cardiac output
Rises 30-50%; peaks 32-34 weeks; HR + stroke volume contributions

### Stroke volume
Rises by ~30% in first two trimesters; falls in third trimester as IVC compression occurs

### Auscultatory findings
Ejection systolic murmur (90%); S3; wide split S2 are normal

### ECG changes
Sinus tachycardia; left axis shift; small Q in III/aVF; ST/T changes acceptable

### Peripheral oedema
Common in 80%; due to venous stasis and reduced oncotic pressure

### Supine hypotension
IVC compression from ~20 weeks — avoid supine position in late pregnancy

### Labour haemodynamics
Cardiac output rises further (300-500 mL per contraction)

### Postpartum autotransfusion
Placental return + IVC release; CO peaks immediately postpartum

### Preload and afterload
Afterload reduced (low SVR); preload increased (plasma volume)

### Clinical implications
Chest pain/dyspnoea at rest, syncope, AF, new MR — all abnormal and need workup
