# Myasthenia gravis in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Myasthenia gravis (MG) in pregnancy has a variable course — exacerbations are unpredictable, with risk highest in the first trimester and postpartum. Treatment with acetylcholinesterase inhibitors and corticosteroids is safe; immunosuppression and plasma exchange may be needed for myasthenic crisis.

## Core Clinical Content

### Epidemiology
Bimodal: early (<40, female predominance) and late (male predominance); 1 in 10,000-50,000

### Effect of pregnancy
Unpredictable; 30-40% worsen (1st trimester, postpartum); 30% improve; 30% stable

### Maternal risks
Myasthenic crisis (respiratory failure); exacerbation; aspiration; fatigue

### Fetal risks
Neonatal myasthenia (10-20% — transient); preterm; arthrogryposis (maternal anti-AChR antibodies)

### Diagnosis
Anti-AChR antibodies (85%); anti-MuSK; EMG (decremental response); CT chest (thymoma)

### Treatment
Pyridostigmine (first-line; safe); corticosteroids (safe); azathioprine (safe); IVIG and plasma exchange (safe; for crisis)

### Drugs to avoid
Magnesium sulphate (worsens MG — use with caution in PE); aminoglycosides; fluoroquinolones; beta-blockers; some anaesthetic agents

### Myasthenic crisis
Respiratory failure; bulbar weakness; treat with IVIG or plasma exchange + steroids; consider ITU/ventilation

### Delivery
Vaginal preferred; epidural (avoid opioid respiratory depression); forceps/ventouse if fatigue in 2nd stage; avoid suxamethonium

### Postpartum
Neonatal monitoring (respiratory, feeding) for 48-72 h; breastfeeding compatible with most drugs; high-dose steroids may need coverage
