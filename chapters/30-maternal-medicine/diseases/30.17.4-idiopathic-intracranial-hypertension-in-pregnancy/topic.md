# Idiopathic intracranial hypertension in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH, pseudotumour cerebri) in pregnancy typically presents with headache, visual disturbances, and papilloedema. It is more common in obese women of childbearing age. Management is conservative in mild cases; acetazolamide and surgical intervention may be needed for progressive visual loss.

## Core Clinical Content

### Epidemiology
Commoner in obese women of childbearing years; incidence 0.9-2.2 per 100,000; rising with obesity

### Pathophysiology
Impaired CSF absorption; raised intracranial pressure without mass lesion or hydrocephalus

### Presentation
Headache (worse lying down/Valsalva); pulsatile tinnitus; transient visual obscurations; diplopia (CN VI palsy); visual loss; papilloedema

### Investigations
Fundoscopy; visual fields; CT/MRI (rule out mass/venous sinus thrombosis); LP with opening pressure >25 cmH2O; CSF normal composition

### Differential
Cerebral venous sinus thrombosis (pregnancy risk); pre-eclampsia; hypertensive encephalopathy; intracranial mass

### Treatment
Weight management; acetazolamide (250-500 mg BD; limited data in pregnancy — case-by-case); topiramate (avoid — teratogenic); FBP monitoring

### Surgical
Optic nerve sheath fenestration (vision loss); VP shunt; LP shunt; venous sinus stenting (selected)

### Repeated LP
Therapeutic LPs to reduce pressure; not definitive

### Pregnancy considerations
Avoid dehydration; consider delivery route (assisted second stage to avoid Valsalva); mode of delivery per obstetric indication

### Postpartum
Symptoms often improve; continue monitoring; preconception weight loss; consider bariatric surgery; recurrence common
