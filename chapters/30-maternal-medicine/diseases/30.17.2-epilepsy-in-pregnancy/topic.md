# Epilepsy in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Epilepsy affects 0.5-1% of pregnant women. Most anti-epileptic drugs (AEDs) are teratogenic to varying degrees. Preconception counselling, monotherapy at the lowest effective dose with high-dose folic acid, and specialist antenatal care optimise outcomes.

## Core Clinical Content

### Prevalence
0.5-1% of pregnancies; AEDs in 0.3-0.5%

### Preconception
Switch to lowest-risk AED (lamotrigine/levetiracetam); monotherapy; lowest effective dose; folic acid 5 mg

### Teratogenicity
Sodium valproate highest (10% major malformations, neurodevelopmental delay); carbimazole; phenytoin; phenobarbital

### Safer AEDs
Lamotrigine and levetiracetam — lowest risk; monitor levels in 2nd/3rd trimesters (lamotrigine clearance rises)

### Fetal risks
Major malformations (4-10% with valproate); NTDs (valproate, carbamazepine); fetal AED syndrome

### Maternal risks
Increased seizure frequency in 30% (hormones, sleep deprivation, drug levels); SUDEP

### Antenatal care
AED levels each trimester; anomaly scan 18-20 weeks; alpha-fetoprotein; targeted USS

### Labour and delivery
Continue AEDs; IM vitamin K for neonate; avoid pethidine (metabolite lowers threshold); avoid dehydration/sleep deprivation

### Postpartum
AED levels may rise — reduce dose if toxicity; breastfeeding usually safe; contraception (avoid COCP with enzyme inducers)

### Counselling
Avoid valproate in women of childbearing age if possible; use LARC; emergency contraception unaffected
