# Group B Streptococcus (GBS) in pregnancy

---

## Definition

Group B Streptococcus (GBS, Streptococcus agalactiae) is a leading cause of early-onset neonatal sepsis (EONS), pneumonia, and meningitis. Vertical transmission occurs during labour from colonised mothers. Universal screening at 35-37 weeks with intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis (IAP) for at-risk women reduces early-onset disease by 80%.

## Core Clinical Content

### Epidemiology
Maternal carriage 20-30% (rectovaginal); vertical transmission 50% without prophylaxis; EONS 1 per 1000 in colonised

### Risk factors for EONS
Previous infant with invasive GBS; GBS bacteriuria in current pregnancy; positive GBS screen at 35-37 weeks; intrapartum fever; prolonged ROM ≥18 h; preterm labour <37 weeks

### Screening
Vaginal-rectal swab at 35-37 weeks; culture in selective broth; rapid intrapartum testing if needed (less sensitive)

### UK approach
Risk-based: IAP if previous infant GBS disease, GBS bacteriuria, or preterm labour/prolonged ROM

### US approach
Universal screening at 36-37 weeks; IAP for all positive

### Intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis (IAP)
Benzylpenicillin 3 g IV loading then 1.5 g 4-hourly until delivery; alternative ampicillin, cefazolin, or clindamycin (if allergic)

### Timing
At least 4 h of antibiotics before delivery; reduces EONS by 80%

### Preterm labour
IAP if unknown GBS status or positive; intrapartum antibiotics for all

### Complications
Neonatal sepsis (early-onset <7 days, late-onset 7-90 days); pneumonia; meningitis; 5-10% mortality

### Postpartum
Maternal sepsis (rare); secondary prevention for future pregnancy; no vaccine available
