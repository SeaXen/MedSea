# Pre-labour rupture of membranes (PROM-PPROM)

---

## Definition

Pre-labour rupture of membranes (PROM) is rupture of membranes before onset of labour. When it occurs before 37 weeks it is termed preterm pre-labour rupture of membranes (PPROM) and is associated with infection, preterm delivery, and neonatal morbidity. Management balances prolonging pregnancy for fetal maturity against infection risk.

## Core Clinical Content

### Definition
PROM: ROM before labour at term; PPROM: ROM before labour <37 weeks

### Diagnosis
History of gush; speculum showing pooling of fluid; nitrazine paper (amniotic fluid alkaline); ferning test; actim PROM (PAMG-1); USS oligohydramnios

### PPROM incidence
2-4% of pregnancies; 30-40% of preterm births

### Risk factors
Prior PPROM; infection (chorioamnionitis, GBS); multiple pregnancy; polyhydramnios; smoking; cervical insufficiency; low BMI

### Complications
Chorioamnionitis (10-30%); preterm delivery; cord prolapse; placental abruption; neonatal sepsis; pulmonary hypoplasia (<24 weeks); deformity

### Investigations
Maternal: FBC, CRP, temperature, pulse; swabs (GBS, chlamydia); high vaginal swab; USS (AFI, growth, presentation)

### Management PPROM
Expectant if no infection: steroids (24-34 weeks); magnesium <32 weeks; latency antibiotics (erythromycin + amoxicillin) 7-10 days; GBS prophylaxis; LMWH; monitor for chorioamnionitis

### Chorioamnionitis
Fever, tachycardia, uterine tenderness, foul discharge, fetal tachycardia — immediate delivery + IV antibiotics

### PROM at term
Induce labour at 24 h (reduce infection; expectant is acceptable if wishes); GBS prophylaxis if prolonged

### Prognosis
Latency inversely related to gestation; 50% deliver within 1 week PPROM at <34 weeks
