SEA KNOWLEDGE STUDY NOTES
SEXUALLY TRANSMITTED INFECTIONS
Neisseria gonorrhoeae Infections (Uncomplicated and Disseminated)
1 Learning Objectives
  • Recognise clinical syndromes (urethritis, cervicitis, pharyngitis, proctitis, DGI)
  • Apply diagnostic testing (NAAT = gold standard; culture for AST)
  • Treat with appropriate regimens (ceftriaxone monotherapy preferred)
  • Manage antimicrobial resistance and treatment failure
  • Understand DGI and its management
2 Aetiology and Transmission
  • N. gonorrhoeae = fastidious, Gram-negative diplococcus, oxidase-positive
  • Transmission: Sexual (genital, anal, oral); vertical (neonatal)
  • Incubation: 2–7 days (1–14 days)
  • Asymptomatic: Men 10–15%, Women 50–80%
3 Clinical Syndromes
Uncomplicated Genital Infection
PopulationPresentation
--------------------------
**Men (Urethritis)****Purulent, copious, yellow-green discharge**, dysuria, meatal erythema; onset 2–7d; asymptomatic 10–15%
**Women (Cervicitis)**Vaginal discharge, intermenstrual bleeding, dysuria; **mucopurulent cervicitis (MPC)**; asymptomatic 50–80%
**Rectal (MSM/W)**Often asymptomatic; proctitis (pain, mucus, bleeding, tenesmus)
**Pharyngeal**Usually asymptomatic; sore throat, erythema; **reservoir for resistance**
Disseminated Gonococcal Infection (DGI)
  • Incidence: 0.5–3% of gonorrhoea
  • Risk factors: Complement deficiency, menstruation, pregnancy, IVDU, HIV
  • Classic triad: Arthritis-dermatitis syndrome (tenosynovitis + pustular rash + fever)
  • Features: Migratory polyarthralgia → septic arthritis (1–2 joints), pustular/petechial rash (acral), tenosynovitis, fever, chills
  • Rare: Endocarditis, meningitis, hepatitis, myopericarditis
Neonatal Conjunctivitis (Ophthalmia Neonatorum)
  • Onset: 2–5 days post-delivery
  • Features: Profuse purulent discharge, eyelid oedema, chemosis
  • Risk: Corneal ulceration, perforation, blindness

5 Antimicrobial Resistance (Critical)
Resistance Patterns (WHO GASP / BASHH GRASP)
AntimicrobialResistance TrendClinical Impact
--------------------------------------------------
**Penicillins**High (penicillinase, chromosomal)Not used
**Tetracyclines**High (efflux, ribosomal)Not 1st-line
**Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin)****>90% in many regions****Not recommended**
**Azithromycin**Rising (mosaic mtrR, 23S rRNA)Not 1st-line monotherapy
**Ceftriaxone****Rare but emerging** (mosaic penA, porB mutations)**Last reliable option**
**Cefixime**Reduced susceptibilityOral alternative (inferior to ceftriaxone)
**Spectinomycin**LowIM only (not for pharyngeal)
WHO Dual Therapy (Historical) → **Current: Ceftriaxone Monotherapy**
EraRegimenRationale
-------------------------
**Pre-2010**CiprofloxacinHigh resistance
**2010–2020**Ceftriaxone + AzithromycinDual therapy for resistance prevention
**Current (WHO 2016, BASHH 2019, CDC 2021)****Ceftriaxone 500mg IM single dose** (1g if >150kg)High barrier, azithro resistance rising, no added benefit
6 Treatment
Uncomplicated Genital / Rectal / Pharyngeal Infection
Site1st-lineAlternative (Ceftriaxone Allergy)
---------------------------------------------------
**Urogenital / Rectal****Ceftriaxone 500mg IM single dose** (1g if >150kg)**Gentamicin 240mg IM single dose + Azithromycin 2g PO single dose**
**Pharyngeal****Ceftriaxone 1g IM single dose****Gentamicin 240mg IM + Azithromycin 2g PO**
Co-treatment for Chlamydia: Add Doxycycline 100mg BD × 7d if CT status unknown or positive Rationale: High co-infection rate; ceftriaxone does NOT cover CT
Disseminated Gonococcal Infection (DGI)
RegimenDoseDuration
-------------------------
**1st-line****Ceftriaxone 1g IV q24h****24–48h after clinical improvement** → switch to oral to complete **7 days total**
**Alternative**Ceftriaxone 1g IM q24hSame
**Severe (meningitis/endocarditis)**Ceftriaxone 2g IV q12h10–14 days
Switch to oral: Cefixime 400mg BD (if susceptible) or Doxycycline 100mg BD (if CT co-infection)
Neonatal Ophthalmia Neonatorum Prevention & Treatment
ScenarioRegimen
-------------------
**Prophylaxis (delivery)****Ceftriaxone 50mg/kg IM/IV single dose** (max 125mg) OR Povidone-iodine 2.5% drops
**Treatment (confirmed)****Ceftriaxone 25–50mg/kg IV/IM q12h × 7d** (max 125mg)
**Systemic (sepsis/meningitis)**Ceftriaxone 50mg/kg IV q12h × 7–10d
Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) — See PID Note
  • Ceftriaxone 500mg IM + Doxycycline 100mg BD 14d + Metronidazole 400mg BD 14d
7 Test of Cure (TOC)
ScenarioTimingMethod
--------------------------
**Uncomplicated (ceftriaxone)****Not routine** (cure rate >95%)NAAT at 2w if symptoms persist
**Pharyngeal****7–14 days** post-treatmentNAAT (higher failure risk)
**DGI**End of treatmentCulture + NAAT
**Treatment failure**1 week post-treatment**Culture + AST mandatory**
**Pregnancy**2–4 weeksNAAT
TOC not routine for uncomplicated genital/rectal after ceftriaxone (cure >95%) Mandatory culture + AST if treatment failure suspected
8 Partner Management
PopulationLook-back
-----------------------
**Genital GC****2 weeks**
**Pharyngeal GC****2 weeks**
**DGI****3 months** (sexual + household)
**Neonatal**Mother + household
Expedited Partner Therapy (EPT): Ceftriaxone 500mg IM + Doxycycline 100mg BD × 7d
9 Special Situations
ScenarioManagement
----------------------
**Pregnancy**Ceftriaxone 500mg IM (1g if >150kg) — safe; avoid spectinomycin
**Ceftriaxone Allergy (anaphylaxis)**Gentamicin 240mg IM + Azithromycin 2g PO single dose
**Ciprofloxacin Resistance****Never use** (global resistance >90%)
**Azithromycin Resistance**Do not use as monotherapy; doxycycline preferred for CT co-treatment
**Ceftriaxone Resistance (rare)**Consult specialist; options: Gentamicin + Azithromycin, Cefixime (if susceptible), Carbapenems
**HIV Co-infection**Same regimens; screen for syphilis, CT, TV
**MSM**3-site testing (urethral, rectal, pharyngeal)
10 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Ceftriaxone 500mg IM = 1st-line uncomplicated GC (1g if >150kg)
  • Pharyngeal GC = Ceftriaxone 1g IM (higher dose)
  • Ceftriaxone does NOT cover Chlamydia → add Doxycycline 100mg BD × 7d
  • Pharyngeal GC higher cure failure → Ceftriaxone 1g; TOC at 2w
  • DGI = Ceftriaxone 1g IV q24h → step down after 24–48h improvement
  • Neonatal ophthalmia prophylaxis = Ceftriaxone 50mg/kg IM (max 125mg)
  • Ciprofloxacin resistance >90% — never use
  • Azithromycin resistance rising — not monotherapy; doxycycline for CT co-treatment
  • Culture + AST mandatory for treatment failure, DGI, pharyngeal, surveillance
  • TOC not routine for uncomplicated genital after ceftriaxone

  • Ceftriaxone 500mg = genital/rectal; 1g = pharyngeal/DGI/pregnancy
  • Ceftriaxone does NOT cover Chlamydia — MUST add doxycycline if CT status unknown/positive
  • Azithromycin monotherapy NOT recommended (resistance); dual therapy no longer standard
  • Ciprofloxacin resistance >90% — never use
  • TOC for pharyngeal GC at 2 weeks (higher failure)
  • TOC not routine for uncomplicated genital after ceftriaxone
  • Ceftriaxone resistance rare but emerging — monitor GASP/GRASP
  • DGI = arthritis-dermatitis-tenosynovitis triad; ceftriaxone IV
13 Mnemonics
  • GC treatment: Ceftriaxone IM (500mg genital/rectal, 1g pharyngeal/DGI)
  • GC + CT co-treatment: Ceftriaxone + Doxycycline 7d
  • DGI triad: Arthritis + Dermatitis + Tenosynovitis = ADT
  • GC resistance: Cipro >90%, Azi rising, Ceftriaxone rare but emerging
  • Pharyngeal GC: 1g IM + TOC at 2w
  • Neonatal prophylaxis: Ceftriaxone 50mg/kg IM (max 125mg)

  • Add Doxycycline 7d for CT co-treatment
  • DGI = ceftriaxone 1g IV → oral step-down

Should Know
  • Neonatal ophthalmia prophylaxis/treatment
  • Ceftriaxone allergy alternative (Gentamicin + Azithromycin)
  • MSM 3-site testing
  • Resistance surveillance (GASP/GRASP)
  • Partner notification 2 weeks
Nice to Know
  • Emerging ceftriaxone resistance (mosaic penA)
  • Spectinomycin (IM alternative, not for pharyngeal)
  • Oral cefixime inferior to ceftriaxone
  • Gonococcal vaccine development
  • Rapid POC NAAT for GC
