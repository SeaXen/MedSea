SEA KNOWLEDGE STUDY NOTES
SEXUALLY TRANSMITTED INFECTIONS
Syphilis (Primary, Secondary, Latent, Tertiary)
1 Learning Objectives
  • Recognise clinical features of each syphilis stage
  • Interpret serology (non-treponemal + treponemal) correctly
  • Apply stage-appropriate penicillin regimens
  • Manage neurosyphilis, congenital syphilis, penicillin allergy
  • Understand Jarisch-Herxheimer reaction and partner notification
2 Aetiology and Transmission
  • T. pallidum subsp. pallidum = microaerophilic spirochete
  • Transmission: Sexual (genital/anal/oral), vertical (congenital), blood (rare)
  • Incubation: 10–90 days (mean 21 days)
  • Infectious stages: Primary, Secondary, Early Latent (<1 year)
3 Clinical Stages
Primary Syphilis (10–90 days post-exposure)
FeatureDescription
----------------------
**Chancre**Single, painless, clean-based, indurated ulcer at inoculation site
**Location**Genital (penis, vulva, cervix), anal, oral, fingers
**Lymphadenopathy****Regional**, bilateral, firm, non-tender, rubbery
**Duration**Heals spontaneously in 3–6 weeks (untreated)
**Serology**Non-treponemal (RPR/VDRL) may be negative early; treponemal (TPHA) becomes positive
Secondary Syphilis (4–10 weeks post-chancre)
FeatureDescription
----------------------
**Skin rash****Maculopapular**, **truncal**, **palmar/plantar** involvement, **copper-coloured**, non-pruritic
**Mucosal**Snail-track ulcers (oral), condylomata lata (genital/anal, infectious)
**Systemic**Fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy (generalised, non-tender), hepatitis, nephritis
**Neurological**Aseptic meningitis, cranial nerve palsies, ophthalmic (uveitis, optic neuritis)
**Duration**Weeks to months; relapses possible in first 2 years
Latent Syphilis (Seropositive, Asymptomatic)
CategoryDefinitionInfectivity
-----------------------------------
**Early Latent**<1 year (primary/secondary within 12 months)**Yes** (relapse to secondary possible)
**Late Latent**≥1 year (or unknown duration)**No** (except vertical transmission)
**Duration**Years to decadesCSF examination needed if neurological symptoms
Tertiary Syphilis (Years–Decades Post-Infection, ~30% Untreated)
ManifestationFeatures
-------------------------
**Gummatous****Gummas** = granulomatous lesions (skin, bone, liver, testis); destructive
**Cardiovascular****Aortitis** → aortic regurgitation, aneurysm (ascending), coronary ostial stenosis
**Neurosyphilis**See separate note
**Other**Bowel strictures, optic atrophy, Argyll Robertson pupils
Neurosyphilis (Can Occur at Any Stage)
FormTimingFeatures
------------------------
**Asymptomatic**AnyCSF abnormalities only
**Meningeal**Early (months)Headache, meningism, cranial nerve palsies
**Meningovascular**Months–yearsStroke (endarteritis), seizures
**General Paresis**Late (10–30y)Dementia, personality change, tremor, Argyll Robertson pupils
**Tabes Dorsalis**Late (20–30y)Posterior column loss (ataxia, lightning pains, absent reflexes), Argyll Robertson pupils
Congenital Syphilis (Vertical Transmission)
  • Transmission: All stages (highest in primary/secondary); placenta after 14 weeks
  • Early (<2y): Rhinorrhoea ("snuffles"), rash, hepatosplenomegaly, bony changes, pseudoparalysis
  • Late (>2y): Hutchinson's triad (notched incisors, interstitial keratitis, 8th nerve deafness), saddle nose, saber shins, Clutton's joints
  • Diagnosis: Maternal serology + infant serology/CSF/long bones X-ray/PCR
  • Treatment: Penicillin G (IV/IM) — see separate note
4 Serological Diagnosis
Test TypeExamplesUse
--------------------------
**Non-treponemal (NTT)****RPR** (Rapid Plasma Reagin), **VDRL** (Venereal Disease Research Lab)**Screening**, **quantitative** (titre = disease activity), monitor treatment
**Treponemal (TT)****TPHA** (T. pallidum Haemagglutination), **FTA-ABS**, **EIA/CIA**, **T. pallidum Particle Agglutination (TPPA)****Confirmatory**, qualitative (reactive/non-reactive), remains positive for life
Testing Algorithms
AlgorithmSequence
---------------------
**Traditional**NTT (RPR/VDRL) screen → if reactive → TT (TPHA/FTA-ABS) confirm
**Reverse (Automated)**TT (EIA/CIA) screen → if reactive → NTT (quantitative) for activity
Interpretation Framework
NTT (RPR/VDRL)TT (TPHA/FTA-ABS)Interpretation
---------------------------------------------------
NegativeNegativeNo syphilis
PositivePositive**Active/treated syphilis** (quantify NTT for activity)
NegativePositive**Treated syphilis**, late latent, or false negative NTT (prozone/early primary)
PositiveNegative**False positive NTT** (autoimmune, pregnancy, infections), early primary (pre-seroconversion), or treated with NTT not yet negative
Prozone effect: Very high antibody titre → false negative NTT (dilute serum to resolve)
5 Treatment (Penicillin = Only Reliable Cure)
Stage-Based Regimens
StageRegimenAlternative (Penicillin Allergy)
--------------------------------------------------
**Primary / Secondary / Early Latent (<1yr)****Benzathine Penicillin G 2.4MU IM single dose****Doxycycline 100mg BD × 14d** (or Ceftriaxone 1g IM/IV daily × 10–14d)
**Late Latent (≥1yr or unknown) / Tertiary (non-neuro)****Benzathine Penicillin G 2.4MU IM weekly × 3 doses** (total 7.2MU)**Doxycycline 100mg BD × 28d**
**Neurosyphilis / Ocular / Otosyphilis****Penicillin G 18–24MU IV daily (2–4MU q4h) × 14d****Ceftriaxone 2g IV daily × 14d** +/− Probenecid (some guidelines)
**Congenital Syphilis****Penicillin G 50,000U/kg IV q12h (≤7d) / q8h (>7d) × 10d****Ceftriaxone** (if no meningitis); desensitise if allergic
Note: No penicillin resistance documented; doxycycline/ceftriaxone alternatives less well studied for neurosyphilis
Jarisch-Herxheimer Reaction
  • Timing: 2–12 hours after first penicillin dose
  • Features: Fever, rigors, hypotension, tachycardia, worsening rash, headache
  • Mechanism: Cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-6) from spirochetal lysis
  • Management: Warn patient; supportive (fluids, antipyretics); do NOT stop penicillin
  • Risk higher: Secondary, early latent, high spirochetal load
6 Partner Notification and Contact Tracing
StageLook-back Period
-------------------------
**Primary****3 months** + duration of symptoms
**Secondary****6 months** + duration of symptoms
**Early Latent (<1yr)****1 year**
**Late Latent / Tertiary****Not routinely** (except pregnant partners, children)
**Congenital**Mother + siblings
Expedited Partner Therapy (EPT): Provide penicillin/alternative for partners where possible
7 Follow-Up and Test of Cure
StageClinical Follow-UpSerological Follow-Up (NTT Titre)
-------------------------------------------------------------
**Primary / Secondary**1, 3, 6, 12 months**4-fold (2 dilution) drop at 6m = cure**; non-reactive at 12m = adequate
**Early Latent**6, 12, 24 months4-fold drop at 12m
**Late Latent / Tertiary**12, 24 months4-fold drop at 24m
**Neurosyphilis**6, 12, 24 monthsCSF NTT + cell count/protein at 6, 12, 24m
Treatment failure: NTT titre fails to drop 4-fold by 12m (early) / 24m (late) → re-treat + CSF if neuro possible
8 Syphilis in HIV
  • Co-infection common: HIV ↑ syphilis transmission/acquisition
  • Atypical presentations: Multiple chancres, severe secondary, rapid progression to neuro
  • Serology: NTT titres may be lower; false negatives more common (prozone); TPHA may be delayed
  • Treatment: Same penicillin regimens; CSF examination recommended for ALL HIV+ syphilis (higher neuro risk)
  • Follow-up: More frequent (3, 6, 9, 12, 24 months); lower threshold for re-treatment
9 Syphilis in Pregnancy
  • Screening: All pregnant women at booking + 28w + delivery (high prevalence areas)
  • Vertical transmission: All stages; highest in primary/secondary; after 14 weeks gestation
  • Treatment: Benzathine penicillin (stage-appropriate) — only therapy proven to prevent congenital syphilis
  • Penicillin allergy: Desensitisation mandatory (doxycecline contraindicated in pregnancy)
  • Infant evaluation: If mother treated <4w before delivery / inadequate / no treatment → infant workup + treatment
10 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • Chancre = painless, clean, indurated + non-tender regional nodes
  • Secondary = truncal + palmar/plantar rash + condylomata lata + systemic
  • Latent = seropositive + asymptomatic; early <1yr (infectious), late ≥1yr
  • Tertiary = gumma (skin/bone/liver), aortic regurgitation/aneurysm, neurosyphilis
  • Neurosyphilis = IV Penicillin G 18–24MU × 14d (not benzathine)
  • Serology: NTT (RPR/VDRL) = quantitative, activity; TT (TPHA/FTA) = confirmatory, qualitative
  • Penicillin only cure; no resistance; benzathine for non-neuro; IV for neuro
  • Jarisch-Herxheimer: warn patient; supportive; don't stop penicillin
  • Partner notification: 3m (primary), 6m (secondary), 1y (early latent)
  • HIV co-infection: CSF exam all cases; lower threshold for neuro

12 Common Confusions / Exam Traps
  • Benzathine penicillin does NOT cross BBB — cannot treat neurosyphilis
  • Doxycycline = alternative for non-neuro; contraindicated in pregnancy
  • Late latent = ≥1 year or unknown duration → 3 weekly doses benzathine
  • Prozone effect = false negative RPR in high titre (dilute serum)
  • RPR negative, TPHA positive = treated syphilis or early primary or prozone
  • Condylomata lata = secondary syphilis (infectious); not HPV warts
  • Argyll Robertson pupil = accommodates but does not react to light (neurosyphilis)
13 Mnemonics
  • Stages: Primary → Secondary → Latent → Tertiary (PSLT)
  • Chancre: Clean, Hard, Asingle, Non-tender, Cternal, Rubbery nodes
  • Secondary: Palms/Soles + Copper rash + Condylomata Lata
  • Tertiary: Gumma, Aortitis, Neurosyphilis (GAN)
  • Jarisch-Herxheimer: Just Happens after Rx; Fever, Hypotension
  • Penicillin: Benzathine for non-neuro; IV for neuro
14 One-Page Revision Summary

Syphilis = T. pallidum

  • Primary: painless chancre + non-tender nodes
  • Secondary: truncal + palmar/plantar rash + condylomata lata
  • Latent: seropositive, asymptomatic (early <1y, late ≥1y)
  • Tertiary: gumma, aortitis, neuro
  • Neuro: IV Penicillin G 18–24MU × 14d
  • Serology: NTT (RPR) quantitative + TT (TPHA) confirmatory
  • Rx: Benzathine 2.4MU IM (1°/2°/early latent); 3× weekly (late latent/tertiary)
  • JH reaction: warn, supportive, don't stop penicillin
  • HIV: CSF exam all cases
