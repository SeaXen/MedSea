# Varicella, herpes zoster

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Vesiculobullous Disorders

## Definition

Varicella (chickenpox) is the primary VZV infection; herpes zoster (shingles) is reactivation. Varicella: children, fever, centripetal vesicular rash (lesions in different stages). Herpes zoster: dermatomal distribution, unilateral, does not cross midline, pain precedes rash, post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN) is main complication. VZV vaccine available (live attenuated).

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Varicella, herpes zoster presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Varicella | Primary VZV; children; fever + centripetal vesicular rash |
| Lesion stages | Macule, papule, vesicle, crust (all stages present simultaneously) |
| Complications | Pneumonia (adults), cerebellar ataxia, bacterial superinfection |
| Herpes zoster | Dorsal root ganglia reactivation; dermatomal; unilateral |
| Pain | Precedes rash by 2-3 days (prodromal); burning, lancinating |
| Distribution | Dermatomal; does not cross midline; thoraco-lumbar most common |
| Trigeminal | Ophthalmic (V1) — risk of keratitis, blindness; Ramsay Hunt (VII) — facial palsy, ear vesicles |
| PHN | Post-herpetic neuralgia: pain more than 3 months after rash; 10-20% of zoster over 50 years |
| Disseminated zoster | Immunocompromised; widespread; visceral involvement |
| Treatment | Aciclovir/valaciclovir/famciclovir (within 72h); gabapentin/amitriptyline for PHN |
| Vaccine | Live attenuated (Zostavax) or recombinant (Shingrix) for over 50 years |
