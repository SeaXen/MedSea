# Cutaneous tuberculosis

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Skin Infections

## Definition

Cutaneous tuberculosis comprises skin manifestations of Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection, accounting for 1-2% of TB cases. Multiple forms: lupus vulgaris (most common, plaques with apple-jelly nodules on diascopy), scrofuloderma (overlying lymph node/TB focus), tuberculosis verrucosa cutis (warty, inoculation), tuberculous chancre (primary inoculation), and others. Diagnosis: clinical, biopsy (granulomas, caseation, AFB), TB culture, PCR, IGRA/TST.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Cutaneous tuberculosis presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Lupus vulgaris | Brownish-red plaques with apple-jelly nodules on diascopy (most common) |
| Scrofuloderma | Overlying TB focus (lymph node, bone); bluish ulceration |
| TB verrucosa cutis | Warty plaque at inoculation site |
| TB chancre | Primary inoculation (non-immune); 2-4w after |
| Tuberculids | Hypersensitivity (papulonecrotic tuberculid, lichen scrofulosorum, erythema induratum) |
| Histology | Granulomas with caseation (variable), Langhans giant cells |
| AFB | Variable; PCR more sensitive |
| IGRA | Positive (QuantiFERON, T-Spot) |
| Treatment | Standard anti-TB therapy (RIPE 2 months, then RI 4 months; total 6 months) |
| Differential | Sarcoidosis, lupus vulgaris, leishmaniasis, leprosy, sporotrichosis |
| BCG | Live attenuated; contraindicated in immunocompromised |
