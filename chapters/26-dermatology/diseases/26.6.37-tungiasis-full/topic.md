# Tungiasis

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Skin Infections

## Definition

Tungiasis is a skin infestation by the sand flea Tunga penetrans (jigger flea, chigoe), common in tropical regions of Africa, Caribbean, South America, India. Female flea burrows into skin (usually feet, toes) producing a white nodule with central black dot (visible flea). Risk: walking barefoot. Treatment: extraction of flea, topical antibiotics, tetanus prophylaxis. Prevention: closed shoes, insecticides.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Tungiasis presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

| Site | Feet, toes (especially periungual, subungual) |
| Lesion | White nodule with central black dot (visible flea) |
| Burden | Often multiple |
| Transmission | Walking barefoot in contaminated soil |
| Complications | Secondary bacterial infection, sepsis, tetanus, autoamputation (severe) |
| Treatment | Extraction of flea (sterile instrument); topical antibiotics; tetanus prophylaxis |
| Prevention | Closed shoes, insecticides (permethrin spray on soil), regular foot inspection |
| Differential | Myiasis, verruca, foreign body, tungiasis (different species) |
| Travel | Increasing with tropical travel |
