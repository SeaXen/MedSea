# Drug-induced lupus

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Drug Eruptions

## Definition

Drug-induced lupus (DIL) is a lupus-like syndrome triggered by medications. Most common drugs: hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid, minocycline, quinidine, methyldopa, chlorpromazine, TNF inhibitors. Typically presents with arthralgia, myalgia, serositis, but skin involvement is less common than in idiopathic SLE (rash in 10-20%). Anti-histone antibodies are the hallmark (95% of DIL). Resolves with drug withdrawal.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Drug-induced lupus presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Common drugs | Hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid, minocycline, quinidine, methyldopa, chlorpromazine, TNF inhibitors |
| Anti-histone | Positive in 95% (hallmark) |
| Anti-dsDNA | Usually negative (vs idiopathic SLE positive) |
| ANA | Positive |
| Skin | Less common than idiopathic SLE (10-20%) |
| Symptoms | Arthralgia, myalgia, serositis (pleuritis, pericarditis), fever |
| Renal/CNS | Rare (vs idiopathic SLE) |
| Onset | Months to years of drug use |
| Resolution | Weeks-months after drug withdrawal (usually complete) |
| Slow acetylators | Higher risk (hydralazine, procainamide) |
