# Onychomycosis (see Skin Infections)

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Hair and Nail Disorders

## Definition

Onychomycosis is fungal infection of the nail (dermatophytes, yeasts, non-dermatophyte moulds). Most common cause: Trichophyton rubrum. Four clinical patterns: DLSO (distal lateral subungual onychomycosis, most common), white superficial (WSO), proximal subungual (PSO), endonyx, total dystrophic (TDO). Diagnosis: KOH, fungal culture, PCR, PAS histology. Treatment: oral terbinafine (DLSO, 12 weeks toenail/6 weeks fingernail) or itraconazole pulse; topical ciclopirox or amorolfine for mild.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Onychomycosis (see Skin Infections) presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Cause | Trichophyton rubrum (most common dermatophyte) |
| DLSO | Distal lateral subungual; most common; yellow-brown, subungual hyperkeratosis |
| WSO | White superficial; surface of nail plate; easily scraped off |
| PSO | Proximal subungual; immunosuppressed (HIV, transplant) |
| TDO | Total dystrophic; end-stage |
| Diagnosis | KOH (hyphae); fungal culture (gold); PCR; PAS histology |
| Treatment | Oral terbinafine 250mg OD 12w (toe) / 6w (finger); itraconazole pulse 200mg BD 1w/month x3-4 |
| Topical | Ciclopirox 8% lacquer, amorolfine 5% lacquer; for mild/early |
| Cure rate | Oral: 50-70% mycological cure; topical: 10-20% |
| Differential | Psoriasis, lichen planus, trauma, yellow nail syndrome |
