# AGEP emergency management

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Emergencies

## Definition

Acute generalised exanthematous pustulosis (AGEP) is a rapid-onset T-cell mediated neutrophilic drug reaction characterised by sterile pustules on erythematous oedematous background, with fever and neutrophilia. Onset within 48 hours of drug exposure. Most commonly caused by aminopenicillins, macrolides, terbinafine, diltiazem, hydroxychloroquine, sulphonamides. Mortality <5% (usually self-limiting in 1-2 weeks). AGEP is a dermatological emergency because the rapid onset and severe presentation can mimic pustular psoriasis, SJS, or sepsis, requiring prompt recognition and management.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

AGEP presents with abrupt onset (often within 12-48 hours of drug exposure) of fever (often >38°C) followed by widespread erythema with hundreds of small (1-5mm) sterile non-follicular pustules, coalescing into "lakes of pus". Lesions typically begin in intertriginous areas (neck, axillae, groin) and spread within hours. Associated features: leucocytosis with neutrophilia (>70% neutrophils), mild eosinophilia (30%), facial oedema (in 50%), mucous membrane involvement (20-30%, mild), pruritus or burning sensation. Differential diagnoses to exclude: pustular psoriasis, DRESS (latency 2-6 weeks), SJS/TEN (targetoid lesions, mucosal involvement), SSSS (mucosa spared, infants), drug hypersensitivity syndrome. The rapid onset (within 48h vs psoriasis's chronic course) is the key distinguishing feature.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and rapid onset after drug exposure
- FBC (leucocytosis with neutrophilia, mild eosinophilia)
- CRP, ESR (elevated)
- LFT, renal (mildly abnormal in some)
- Calcium (hypocalcaemia in severe)
- Skin biopsy if atypical: intraepidermal pustules, spongiform, neutrophilic infiltrate, oedema, no vasculitis
- Rule out: pustular psoriasis (personal/family history, slower), SJS/TEN (mucosa, targetoid), DRESS (latency 2-6 weeks, organ involvement)
- Blood culture if sepsis concern (negative in AGEP)

### Management
- **STOP culprit drug immediately** (most important intervention)
- Hospital admission for severe cases (extensive, systemic toxicity)
- Supportive care: IV fluids, antipyretics, monitoring
- Topical corticosteroids (moderate potency) for inflammation
- Oral antihistamines for pruritus
- Emollients, cooling measures
- Avoid topical antibiotics (not infection)
- For severe cases: short course oral corticosteroids (prednisolone 0.5mg/kg 1-2 weeks)
- Antibiotics only if secondary bacterial infection confirmed
- Mortality <5% with supportive care; usually self-limiting in 1-2 weeks
- Re-challenge contraindicated (recurrence common, may be more severe)
- Patch testing may help identify culprit drug after recovery (4-6 weeks later)

## Differential Diagnosis

| Differential | Distinguishing Feature |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Pustular psoriasis | Personal/family history of psoriasis, slower onset, chronic course, often on background of plaque psoriasis |
| DRESS | Latency 2-6 weeks (vs <48h in AGEP), eosinophilia, organ involvement (liver, kidney), atypical lymphocytes |
| SJS/TEN | Targetoid lesions, mucosal involvement (≥2 sites), full-thickness epidermal necrosis, Nikolsky positive |
| Subcorneal pustular dermatosis (Sneddon-Wilkinson) | Chronic, recurrent, subcorneal pustules, IgA pemphigus on DIF, annular pattern |
| SSSS | Mucosa spared, infants, Staph aureus exfoliative toxins, perioral crusting |
| Bacterial sepsis with pustules | Positive blood culture, focal pustules, systemic toxicity, ill patient |
| Pemphigus foliaceus | Chronic, flaccid bullae, DIF intercellular IgG, acantholysis on histology |
| Erythema multiforme | Targetoid lesions, no pustules, mucosal involvement, HSV/drug trigger |

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** Define AGEP and outline its clinical features.
   **A:** AGEP is a rapid-onset T-cell mediated neutrophilic drug reaction with sterile pustules on erythematous background, fever, neutrophilia, onset within 48 hours, mortality <5%. It is a dermatological emergency because it can mimic sepsis, pustular psoriasis, or SJS.

2. **Q:** What are the key clinical features of AGEP?
   **A:** Sudden onset, fever, hundreds of small sterile pustules, flexural onset, facial oedema, mild eosinophilia, mild mucosal involvement possible. Within 48 hours of drug exposure.

3. **Q:** Describe the management of AGEP.
   **A:** STOP culprit drug (most important), supportive, topical/oral steroids, antihistamines, monitoring. Usually self-limiting in 1-2 weeks. Mortality <5% with supportive care.

4. **Q:** How do you differentiate AGEP from pustular psoriasis?
   **A:** Pustular psoriasis: personal/family history, slower onset, chronic, on background of plaque psoriasis. AGEP: drug history, rapid onset (within 48h), self-limiting in 1-2 weeks.

5. **Q:** What scoring system is used for AGEP?
   **A:** EUROSCAR (European Severe Cutaneous Adverse Reaction): score ≥8 confirmed, 5-7 probable, <5 unlikely. Criteria include: fever, pustules (typical/atypical), neutrophils, mucosa, acute onset (<48h), drug history.

6. **Q:** What is the prognosis of AGEP?
   **A:** Self-limiting in 1-2 weeks; mortality <5%; recurrence common on re-challenge (often more severe). Patch testing may help identify culprit drug.

## Confusions and Mnemonics

1. **AGEP mnemonic**: **A**cute, **G**eneralised, **E**xanthematous, **P**ustulosis — onset within 48 hours, STOP drug.
2. **AGEP vs pustular psoriasis**: AGEP = drug, rapid, self-limiting; pustular psoriasis = chronic, personal/family history, slower.
3. **EUROSCAR**: fever, pustules, neutrophils, mucosa, acute onset, drugs.
4. **Drug list**: **A**minopenicillins, **M**acrolides, **T**erbinafine, **D**iltiazem, **H**CQ, **S**ulphonamides.

## Special Situations

| Situation | Consideration |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Pregnancy** | Limited data; avoid systemic agents; supportive care only |
| **Paediatric** | Rare; consider Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome; supportive care |
| **Elderly** | Comorbidities, polypharmacy, increased risk of secondary infection; STOP culprit; supportive |
