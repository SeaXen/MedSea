# Erysipelas

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Skin Infections

## Definition

Erysipelas is a superficial (dermal lymphatic) bacterial skin infection, sharply demarcated, raised, with intense erythema. Most commonly caused by Streptococcus pyogenes (groups A, C, G), less often Staph aureus. Affects face (especially) and legs. Often with fever, lymphadenopathy, lymphangitis. Risk factors: lymphoedema, venous insufficiency, obesity, diabetes, eczema, alcoholism, immunocompromise.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Erysipelas presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Cause | Strep pyogenes (most common); Staph aureus (less) |
| Layer | Superficial dermis + lymphatics |
| Border | Sharply demarcated, raised, indurated |
| Sites | Face (often bilateral), lower legs |
| Systemic | Fever, lymphangitis, lymphadenopathy, leucocytosis |
| Risk factors | Lymphoedema, venous insufficiency, obesity, diabetes, eczema, alcoholism |
| Diagnosis | Clinical; blood culture if systemic; ASOT rises after 1-3w |
| Treatment | IV penicillin (severe) or oral flucloxacillin (mild); elevate leg; treat underlying lymphoedema |
| Complications | Sepsis, glomerulonephritis (post-strep), abscess, necrotising fasciitis (rare) |
| Differential | Cellulitis (deeper, less demarcated), DVT, contact dermatitis, erysipelas-like (CAPD, familial Mediterranean fever) |
| Recurrence | Common; consider prophylactic penicillin if recurrent |
