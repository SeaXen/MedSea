# Post-inflammatory hypopigmentation

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Disorders of Pigmentation

## Definition

Post-inflammatory hypopigmentation is loss of pigment following skin inflammation (eczema, psoriasis, pityriasis alba, burns, procedures). Distinguish from vitiligo (complete depigmentation) and pityriasis alba (associated with atopic dermatitis). Often resolves over months; treatment: time, sun protection, topical corticosteroids or calcineurin inhibitors to reduce inflammation, phototherapy (PUVA, NB-UVB) for chronic cases.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Post-inflammatory hypopigmentation presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Cause | After skin inflammation (eczema, psoriasis, pityriasis alba, burns) |
| Mechanism | Melanocyte damage or melanin transfer disruption |
| Skin type | More visible in darker skin |
| Resolution | Months to years (faster than PIH) |
| Differential | Vitiligo, pityriasis alba, tinea versicolor, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation |
| Treatment | Time, sun protection, treat underlying inflammation, phototherapy (NB-UVB, PUVA) |
| Pityriasis alba | Common in children with atopic dermatitis; face (cheeks) |
| Avoid | Further trauma/inflammation |
