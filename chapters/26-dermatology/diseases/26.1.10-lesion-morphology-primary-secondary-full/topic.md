# Lesion morphology (primary, secondary)

Related: [[../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Structure Function and Approach

## Definition

Primary lesions are the initial, untreated skin changes (macule, papule, plaque, nodule, vesicle, bulla, pustule, wheal, cyst). Secondary lesions evolve from primary (scale, crust, erosion, ulcer, fissure, lichenification, atrophy, scar, keloid). Accurate description is the foundation of dermatological diagnosis.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Lesion morphology (primary, secondary) is a clinical entity within the Structure Function and Approach group. The presentation follows characteristic patterns of morphology, distribution, and evolution that allow clinical recognition supported by targeted investigation where needed. See the Davidson 24th Edition chapter for the complete morphological description and the high-yield summary below for the most testable features.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis is usually sufficient; targeted investigation based on differential
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Direct immunofluorescence (DIF) for autoimmune bullous or vasculitic presentations
- Baseline bloods: FBC, LFT, renal, HIV, hepatitis B/C, TB (IGRA) before systemic therapy
- Disease-specific serology (autoantibodies, IgE, allergy tests) as clinically indicated

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy appropriate to the condition (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, or antifungals as indicated)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, cyclosporine, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment, multidisciplinary review for complex cases

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Macule | <1cm flat colour change (freckle, café-au-lait) |
| Papule | <1cm solid raised lesion (acne, insect bite) |
| Plaque | >1cm flat-topped raised lesion (psoriasis, lichen planus) |
| Nodule | >1cm solid lesion with depth (dermatofibroma, lipoma) |
| Vesicle | <0.5cm fluid-filled (herpes, eczema) |
| Bulla | >0.5cm fluid-filled (pemphigus, bullous pemphigoid) |
| Pustule | Pus-filled (folliculitis, pustular psoriasis) |
| Wheal | Transient oedematous plaque (urticaria) |
| Scale | Increased keratin (psoriasis — silvery; eczema — fine; tinea — annular) |
| Crust | Dried serum/pus (impetigo — honey-coloured) |

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** Define a vesicle vs a bulla.
   **A:** Vesicle: <5mm fluid-filled; Bulla: >5mm fluid-filled.
2. **Q:** What is the difference between an erosion and an ulcer?
   **A:** Erosion: loss of epidermis only, heals without scarring; Ulcer: loss into dermis, heals with scarring.
3. **Q:** What does Nikolsky sign indicate and how is it elicited?
   **A:** Lateral pressure causes epidermal detachment, indicating intraepidermal blistering (pemphigus, SJS, SSSS); elicited by rubbing normal-appearing skin.

## Confusions and Mnemonics
