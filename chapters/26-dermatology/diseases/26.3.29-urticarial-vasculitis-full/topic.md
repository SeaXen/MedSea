# Urticarial vasculitis

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Urticaria Erythema and Purpura

## Definition

Urticarial vasculitis (UV) is a small-vessel vasculitis (leukocytoclastic) presenting as urticarial wheals, but unlike ordinary urticaria, individual lesions last more than 24 hours, are often painful/burning (not pruritic), and resolve with bruising/hyperpigmentation. May be idiopathic or associated with SLE, Sjögren, IgA deficiency, infection (Hep B/C), drugs, paraproteinaemia. Hypocomplementaemic UV is more severe (systemic involvement).

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Urticarial vasculitis presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Lesions last | More than 24 hours (vs urticaria less than 24h) |
| Symptoms | Painful, burning (vs urticaria itchy) |
| Resolution | With bruising/hyperpigmentation (key distinguishing) |
| DIF | Perivascular IgG/IgM/C3 in early lesions (less than 24h) |
| Histology | Leukocytoclastic vasculitis (fibrin, neutrophils, nuclear dust) |
| Complement | Low C3/C4 in hypocomplementaemic UV (more severe) |
| Associations | SLE, Sjögren, IgA deficiency, Hep B/C, paraprotein, drugs |
| Systemic | Arthralgia, abdominal pain, glomerulonephritis (esp. hypocomplementaemic) |
| Treatment | Antihistamines for itch, NSAIDs, dapsone, colchicine, immunosuppressants for severe |
| Vs CSU | Lesions last more than 24h, painful, bruise |
