# Hereditary angioedema (C1-INH deficiency)

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Urticaria Erythema and Purpura

## Definition

Hereditary angioedema (HAE) is an autosomal dominant disorder with deficiency (Type I, 85%) or dysfunction (Type II, 15%) of C1-inhibitor (C1-INH), leading to uncontrolled bradykinin production and recurrent angioedema without urticaria. Affects 1 in 50,000. Triggers: trauma, dental procedures, stress, oestrogen, ACE inhibitors.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Hereditary angioedema (C1-INH deficiency) presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Inheritance | Autosomal dominant |
| Type I | Low C1-INH levels (85%) |
| Type II | Normal/high C1-INH but dysfunctional (15%) |
| Type III | Female, oestrogen-dependent, normal C1-INH (rare) |
| Pathogenesis | Uncontrolled bradykinin → vasodilation, angioedema |
| No urticaria | Yes (distinguishing from histaminergic angioedema) |
| Sites | Face, lips, tongue, larynx (life-threatening), abdomen (severe pain), genitals, limbs |
| Duration | Attacks last 2-5 days |
| Triggers | Trauma, dental, surgery, stress, oestrogen, ACE inhibitors |
| Diagnosis | C1-INH level and function; C4 (low) |
| Treatment | C1-INH concentrate (Berinert, Cinryze), ecallantide (Kallikrein inhibitor), icatibant (bradykinin B2 antagonist), FFP if unavailable; prophylaxis with danazol, tranexamic acid, C1-INH |

## Differential Diagnosis

| Differential | Distinguishing Feature |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Histaminergic angioedema | Urticaria present, responds to antihistamines |
| Acquired angioedema | Older, low C1q |
