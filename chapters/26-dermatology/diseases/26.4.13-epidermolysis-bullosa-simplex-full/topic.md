# Epidermolysis bullosa simplex

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Vesiculobullous Disorders

## Definition

Epidermolysis bullosa simplex (EBS) is the mildest form of EB, with blistering at the basal layer (intraepidermal). Caused by mutations in KRT5 or KRT14 (keratin 5/14), leading to cytoskeletal fragility. Most common form of EB. Localised (Weber-Cockayne, palms/soles) and generalised severe (Dowling-Meara, herpetiform) forms.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Epidermolysis bullosa simplex presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Inheritance | Mostly autosomal dominant (de novo in 30%) |
| Genes | KRT5 (keratin 5) and KRT14 (keratin 14) |
| Split | Intra-basal layer (intraepidermal) |
| Healing | Without scarring (in most forms) |
| Localised (Weber-Cockayne) | Palms/soles only; worsens in hot weather and with friction |
| Generalised severe (Dowling-Meara) | Herpetiform grouped blisters; nails dystrophy; improves with age |
| Other forms | EB simplex generalised intermediate (Koebner) |
| Electron microscopy | Cytolysis of basal keratinocytes |
| Immunofluorescence antigen mapping | BP180/230 stain roof and floor (markers for level) |
| Management | Avoid friction, cooling; wound care; no specific therapy; genetic counselling |
