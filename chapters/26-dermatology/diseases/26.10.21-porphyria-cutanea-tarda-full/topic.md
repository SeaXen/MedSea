# Porphyria cutanea tarda

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Systemic Disease Manifestations

## Definition

Porphyria cutanea tarda (PCT) is the most common porphyria, due to deficiency of uroporphyrinogen decarboxylase (UROD) in the liver. Causes: hereditary (Type I), acquired (Type II, sporadic, most common), toxic (alcohol, oestrogens, iron, hepatitis C, HIV). Presents with photosensitivity, skin fragility, vesicles/bullae on sun-exposed areas (dorsum hands), hyperpigmentation, hypertrichosis, milia, scarring. Diagnosis: elevated urine uroporphyrins (pink-red urine, fluoresces coral under Wood's lamp); elevated faecal coproporphyrins. Treatment: phlebotomy (first-line), low-dose hydroxychloroquine, avoid triggers, sun protection.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Porphyria cutanea tarda presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

| Types | I hereditary (25%); II sporadic/Acquired (75%); toxic; familial (Type III) |
| Skin | Photosensitivity, skin fragility, vesicles/bullae on dorsum hands, milia, hyperpigmentation, hypertrichosis, scarring |
| Sites | Sun-exposed: dorsum hands, face, neck, ears |
| Triggers | Alcohol, oestrogens, iron overload, hepatitis C, HIV, smoking, OCP |
| Diagnosis | Urine uroporphyrins elevated (pink-red; Wood's lamp coral); faecal coproporphyrins elevated; plasma fluorescence peak at 615-620nm |
| Histology | Subepidermal bullae with festooning, caterpillar bodies, thickened dermal vessels |
| DIF | Granular IgG/C3 at BMZ and around dermal vessels |
| Treatment | Phlebotomy (first-line; reduces iron); low-dose hydroxychloroquine 100mg 2x/week; avoid triggers; sun protection |
| Hep C | Treat with DAAs (direct-acting antivirals) |
| Iron | Reduce iron overload |
