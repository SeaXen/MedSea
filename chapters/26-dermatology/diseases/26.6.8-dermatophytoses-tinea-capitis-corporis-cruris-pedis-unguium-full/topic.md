# Dermatophytoses (tinea capitis, corporis, cruris, pedis, unguium)

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Skin Infections

## Definition

Dermatophytoses are superficial fungal infections caused by dermatophytes (Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton). Tinea capitis (scalp, children, kerion, endothrix/ectothrix), tinea corporis (body, annular with central clearing), tinea cruris (groin, KOH+), tinea pedis (feet, interdigital, moccasin), tinea unguium (nails, onychomycosis). Diagnosis: KOH microscopy, fungal culture. Treatment: topical for limited; oral (terbinafine, itraconazole) for extensive, scalp, nails.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Dermatophytoses (tinea capitis, corporis, cruris, pedis, unguium) presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Cause | Dermatophytes (Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton) |
| Tinea capitis | Scalp, children; endothrix/ectothrix; kerion (inflammatory mass) |
| Tinea corporis | Body, annular with central clearing and active border |
| Tinea cruris | Groin, spares scrotum (unlike candida); often with pedis |
| Tinea pedis | Feet, interdigital (most common), moccasin, vesiculobullous |
| Tinea unguium | Nails, onychomycosis (DLSO, PSO, TDO, WSO) |
| Diagnosis | KOH (hyphae); fungal culture; Wood's lamp (some Microsporum) |
| Treatment | Topical: terbinafine, clotrimazole; oral: terbinafine (tinea capitis, unguium, corporis extensive), griseofulvin (capitis) |
| Tinea capitis | Requires oral (not topical); terbinafine or griseofulvin 6-8w |
| Tinea incognito | Tinea masked by topical steroids (loses typical features) |
| Prevention | Keep dry, breathable footwear, treat tinea pedis to prevent spread |
