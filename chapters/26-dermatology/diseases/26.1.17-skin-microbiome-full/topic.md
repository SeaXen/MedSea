# Skin microbiome

Related: [[../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

## Definition

The skin microbiome comprises bacteria, fungi, viruses, and mites that colonise the skin surface and appendages. Commensals (Cutibacterium, Staphylococcus, Corynebacterium, Malassezia) maintain homeostasis, while dysbiosis is implicated in atopic dermatitis, acne, rosacea, and psoriasis.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Skin microbiome is a clinical entity within the Structure Function and Approach group. The presentation follows characteristic patterns of morphology, distribution, and evolution that allow clinical recognition supported by targeted investigation where needed. ## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis is usually sufficient; targeted investigation based on differential
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Direct immunofluorescence (DIF) for autoimmune bullous or vasculitic presentations
- Baseline bloods: FBC, LFT, renal, HIV, hepatitis B/C, TB (IGRA) before systemic therapy
- Disease-specific serology (autoantibodies, IgE, allergy tests) as clinically indicated

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy appropriate to the condition (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, or antifungals as indicated)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, cyclosporine, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment, multidisciplinary review for complex cases

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** What is the most common skin commensal on sebaceous sites?
   **A:** Cutibacterium acnes (formerly Propionibacterium acnes).
2. **Q:** How does S. aureus relate to atopic dermatitis?
   **A:** S. aureus colonises 90% of atopic dermatitis skin (vs 5% normal); produces superantigens and biofilm; treatment includes bleach baths, topical antibiotics, and emollients.
3. **Q:** What is the role of Malassezia in skin disease?
   **A:** Causes pityriasis versicolor, seborrheic dermatitis, and Malassezia folliculitis; treated with topical antifungals (ketoconazole, selenium sulfide).
