# Warfarin-induced skin necrosis

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Drug Eruptions

## Definition

Warfarin-induced skin necrosis is a rare (0.01-0.1%) but serious complication of warfarin therapy, occurring 3-10 days after initiation. Pathogenesis: protein C deficiency (shortest half-life of vitamin K-dependent factors) causing transient hypercoagulable state, leading to microvascular thrombosis. Common in obesity, female sex, high loading dose, protein C/S deficiency.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Warfarin-induced skin necrosis presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Incidence | 0.01-0.1% of patients on warfarin |
| Onset | 3-10 days after starting warfarin |
| Pathogenesis | Protein C deficiency (short half-life) → transient hypercoagulable → microvascular thrombosis |
| Risk factors | Obesity, female sex, high loading dose, protein C/S deficiency, antiphospholipid syndrome |
| Sites | Breasts, thighs, buttocks (areas with high subcutaneous fat) |
| Lesions | Painful erythematous patches → bullae → necrosis (eschar) |
| Differential | Purpura fulminans, DIC, necrotising fasciitis, calciphylaxis |
| Prevention | Avoid loading doses; use LMWH bridge; test for protein C/S deficiency |
| Treatment | STOP warfarin; vitamin K; FFP/prothrombin complex concentrate; LMWH/heparin |
| Prognosis | Mortality 15%; often requires surgical debridement |
