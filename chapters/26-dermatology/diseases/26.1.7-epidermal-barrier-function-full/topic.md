# Epidermal barrier function

Related: [[../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Structure Function and Approach

## Definition

The epidermal barrier (mainly stratum corneum) prevents transepidermal water loss (TEWL) and protects against environmental insults. It comprises corneocytes embedded in a lipid matrix ('bricks and mortar' model), with filaggrin and tight junctions providing structural integrity.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Epidermal barrier function is a clinical entity within the Structure Function and Approach group. The presentation follows characteristic patterns of morphology, distribution, and evolution that allow clinical recognition supported by targeted investigation where needed. See the Davidson 24th Edition chapter for the complete morphological description and the high-yield summary below for the most testable features.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis is usually sufficient; targeted investigation based on differential
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Direct immunofluorescence (DIF) for autoimmune bullous or vasculitic presentations
- Baseline bloods: FBC, LFT, renal, HIV, hepatitis B/C, TB (IGRA) before systemic therapy
- Disease-specific serology (autoantibodies, IgE, allergy tests) as clinically indicated

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy appropriate to the condition (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, or antifungals as indicated)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, cyclosporine, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment, multidisciplinary review for complex cases

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| 'Bricks and mortar' | Corneocytes (bricks) in lipid matrix (mortar) — ceramide, cholesterol, fatty acids |

| TEWL | Transepidermal water loss = functional measure of barrier integrity |
| pH | Surface pH 4.5-5.5 (acid mantle); alkaline pH impairs barrier and increases infection risk |
| Tight junctions | Claudin-1, occludin — gate function in stratum granulosum |

| Atopic dermatitis | Filaggrin mutations (FLG) → impaired barrier → allergen penetration → Th2 inflammation |
| Ichthyosis vulgaris | FLG mutations → scaling, keratosis pilaris, atopy |
| Recovery time | Barrier recovery after tape stripping takes ~24h in healthy skin |

## Viva Questions

1. **Q:** Describe the 'bricks and mortar' model of the stratum corneum.
   **A:** Corneocytes (anucleate keratinocytes with cross-linked keratin and filaggrin) act as bricks; intercellular lipids (ceramides 50%, cholesterol 25%, fatty acids 25%) act as mortar.
2. **Q:** Why is filaggrin important in atopic dermatitis?
   **A:** FLG loss-of-function mutations impair barrier function, allowing allergen penetration and Th2-skewed inflammation; present in 30% of severe AD.
3. **Q:** What is TEWL and how is it measured?
   **A:** Transepidermal water loss — measured with a tewameter (g/m²/h); increased in atopic dermatitis, psoriasis, after barrier disruption.
4. **Q:** How does the skin maintain an acidic surface pH?
   **A:** Filaggrin degradation to urocanic acid and pyrrolidone carboxylic acid, free fatty acids from sebum, and proton pumps in corneocytes.
