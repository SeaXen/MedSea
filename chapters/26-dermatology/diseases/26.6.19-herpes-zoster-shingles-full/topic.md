# Herpes zoster (shingles)

Related: [[../../../Dermatology MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 29 - Dermatology Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 29: Dermatology** (24th Edition, Ibbotson) — Skin Infections

## Definition

Herpes zoster (shingles) is reactivation of latent varicella-zoster virus (VZV) in sensory ganglia, causing a painful dermatomal vesicular eruption. Risk increases with age (>50 years), immunosuppression, stress. Complications: post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN, 10-20% >50 years), ophthalmic zoster, Ramsay Hunt syndrome, dissemination. Treatment: aciclovir/valaciclovir/famciclovir within 72 hours; PHN: gabapentin/amitriptyline. Vaccine: Shingrix >50 years.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Herpes zoster (shingles) presents with characteristic features as defined in Davidson 24e Ch 29. The key clinical features are summarised below. The disease is recognised by its morphology, distribution pattern, and course. Severity may be assessed using disease-specific scoring systems where applicable. Always consider the full differential when assessing a patient.

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
- Clinical diagnosis based on morphology, distribution, and history
- Skin biopsy (H&E) for histological confirmation in atypical or refractory cases
- Disease-specific tests as clinically indicated (autoantibodies, DIF for autoimmune bullous, culture for infection, KOH for fungal)
- Baseline bloods (FBC, LFT, renal) and pre-biologic screening (TB IGRA, hepatitis B/C, HIV) before systemic therapy

### Management
- **First-line:** General measures (emollients, soap substitutes, trigger avoidance) and topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, vitamin D analogues, antifungals as appropriate)
- **Second-line:** Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic agents (methotrexate, ciclosporin, acitretin, azathioprine) for moderate-to-severe disease
- **Third-line:** Biologics or targeted small molecules (anti-TNF, anti-IL17/23, anti-IL4/13, JAK inhibitors) for refractory disease
- **Supportive:** Patient education, psychological support, quality-of-life assessment

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Category | Key Points |
|----------|------------|
| Cause | VZV reactivation in sensory ganglia |
| Distribution | Dermatomal, unilateral, does not cross midline |
| Thoracic | Most common site (50%) |
| Trigeminal | Ophthalmic V1: keratitis, blindness |
| Pain | Precedes rash by 2-3 days (prodromal) |
| Rash | Vesicles on erythematous base → pustules → crust over 7-10 days |
| Complications | PHN 10-20% over 50 years, dissemination (immunocompromised), secondary bacterial infection |
| Ophthalmic zoster | Hutchinson's sign (nasal tip vesicles); urgent ophthalmology |
| Ramsay Hunt | Facial palsy, ear pain, vesicles in external auditory meatus; CN VII + VIII |
| Treatment | Aciclovir/valaciclovir/famciclovir within 72h |
| PHN treatment | Gabapentin, pregabalin, amitriptyline, capsaicin, lidocaine patches |
| Vaccine | Shingrix (recombinant) >50 years; 90% efficacy |
