## Definition

Herpes simplex virus (HSV-1, HSV-2) and varicella-zoster virus (VZV) are enveloped DNA viruses from Herpesviridae. HSV-1: predominantly orolabial; HSV-2: predominantly genital. VZV causes varicella (primary) and herpes zoster (reactivation). All establish lifelong latency in sensory ganglia (HSV: trigeminal/sacral; VZV: dorsal root).

## Clinical Features and Presentation

HSV: Grouped vesicles on erythematous base → pustules → erosions → crust over 7-10 days. Primary HSV often severe with fever, lymphadenopathy. Recurrent: prodrome (tingling/burning) → same morphology, less severe. VZV: Primary = varicella (centripetal vesicular rash, fever, malaise). Reactivation = herpes zoster (dermatomal distribution, unilateral, pain precedes rash by 2-3 days, vesicles do not cross midline). Complications: eczema herpeticum, neonatal HSV, VZV pneumonia, post-herpetic neuralgia (PHN in 10-20% of zoster >50 years).

## 1. HERPES SIMPLEX VIRUS (HSV)

### 1.1 Virology & Epidemiology
| Feature | HSV-1 | HSV-2 |
|---------|-------|-------|
| **Primary site** | Orofacial (labial, pharyngeal) | Genital |
| **Seroprevalence** | 60-90% adults | 10-30% adults |
| **Transmission** | Saliva, direct contact | Sexual contact |
| **Latency** | Trigeminal ganglion | Sacral ganglia (S2-S4) |
| **Reactivation** | Cold sores, herpes labialis | Genital herpes |

> **Both can infect either site.** HSV-1 increasingly causes genital herpes.

### 1.2 Clinical Manifestations

#### Primary Infection
| Syndrome | Features |
|----------|----------|
| **Gingivostomatitis (HSV-1)** | Children 1-5 yrs; fever, irritability, **vesicles/ulcers on gingiva, tongue, palate**; cervical lymphadenopathy |
| **Pharyngotonsillitis** | Adolescents/adults; exudative tonsillitis, ulcers |
| **Genital Herpes (HSV-2/1)** | Painful vesicles/ulcers on genitalia; dysuria; inguinal lymphadenopathy; **primary > recurrent** |
| **Herpetic Whitlow** | Fingers (healthcare workers); vesicles on pulp |
| **Eczema Herpeticum** | **Kaposi varicelliform eruption**; AD background; disseminated vesicles/punched-out erosions |
| **Herpes Simplex Encephalitis** | **Temporal lobe**; fever, seizures, altered consciousness; **PCR CSF HSV DNA** |

#### Recurrent Infection
| Feature | HSV-1 (Labialis) | HSV-2 (Genital) |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Prodrome** | Tingling, burning, pain (6-24h) | Same |
| **Lesions** | **Clustered vesicles** on erythematous base → crust | Vesicles/ulcers on genitalia |
| **Site** | Vermillion border, lips | Genitalia, perianal |
| **Frequency** | 1-12 episodes/year | 4-6 episodes/year (HSV-2); fewer HSV-1 |
| **Triggers** | UV, stress, fever, trauma, menses, immunosuppression | Same |

## 2. HERPES ZOSTER (SHINGLES)

### 2.1 Virology & Pathophysiology
- **Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV)** — primary = varicella (chickenpox)
- **Latency:** Dorsal root ganglia / cranial nerve ganglia
- **Reactivation:** Age (>50), immunosuppression, stress, trauma, malignancy
- **Risk:** Lifetime risk 20-30%; doubles by age 80

### 2.2 Clinical Features

| Phase | Features |
|-------|----------|
| **Prodrome (1-5 days)** | Pain, burning, paresthesia, pruritus in dermatome; fever, malaise |
| **Eruptive** | **Vesicles on erythematous base** in **single dermatome** (unilateral, **does not cross midline**); T3-L3 most common |
| **Progression** | Vesicles → pustules → crusts (7-10 days) → heal with scarring/pigmentation |
| **Pain** | Neuropathic; **allodynia, hyperalgesia**; may precede rash by days |

### 2.3 Dermatomal Distribution
| Region | Dermatomes | Frequency |
|--------|------------|-----------|
| **Thoracic** | T3-T12 | 50-60% |
| **Cervical** | C5-C8 | 15-20% |
| **Trigeminal** | V1 (ophthalmic), V2, V3 | 10-20% |
| **Lumbar/Sacral** | L1-S3 | 10-15% |

### 2.4 Complications
| Complication | Frequency | Details |
|--------------|-----------|---------|
| **Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN)** | **10-20%** (>50 yrs); **>50%** (>80 yrs) | Pain >90 days after rash onset; allodynia |
| **Ophthalmic Zoster (Herpes Zoster Ophthalmicus - HZO)** | V1 involvement | **Keratitis, uveitis, glaucoma, blindness**; Hutchinson's sign (tip of nose) → **urgent ophthalmology** |
| **Ramsay Hunt Syndrome** | Geniculate ganglion (CN VII) | Facial palsy + vesicles in ear canal/palate; hearing loss, vertigo |
| **Disseminated Zoster** | >20 lesions outside primary dermatome | Immunocompromised; visceral involvement (pneumonitis, hepatitis, encephalitis) |
| **Bacterial Superinfection** | S. aureus, S. pyogenes | Cellulitis, impetigo |
| **VZV Vasculopathy** | Granulomatous arteritis | Stroke (contralateral), TIA, aneurysm |

## 2. DIAGNOSTIC WORKUP

### HSV
| Test | Indication | Method |
|------|------------|--------|
| **PCR (swab)** | **Gold standard** | Vesicle fluid swab; high sensitivity/specificity |
| **Tzanck Smear** | Bedside | Multinucleated giant cells, acantholytic cells |
| **DFA / Antigen Detection** | Rapid | Vesicle scrape |
| **Serology (IgG)** | Past exposure / serostatus | Type-specific (HSV-1/2 IgG) — **NOT for acute diagnosis** |
| **CSF PCR** | Encephalitis | HSV DNA in CSF |

### VZV
| Test | Indication | Method |
|------|------------|--------|
| **PCR (swab)** | **Gold standard** | Vesicle fluid, crust |
| **Tzanck Smear** | Bedside | Multinucleated giant cells |
| **Serology (IgG)** | Immunity / past infection | VZV IgG |
| **CSF PCR** | CNS involvement | Zoster sine herpete, encephalitis |

## 3. TREATMENT — ANTIVIRALS

### 3.1 Oral Antivirals (Standard)
| Drug | HSV Dose | VZV Dose | Duration |
|------|----------|----------|----------|
| **Acyclovir** | 200 mg 5x/day | **800 mg 5x/day** | 5-10 days (HSV), 7-10 days (VZV) |
| **Valacyclovir** | 500 mg BD (recurrent) / 1g BD (primary) | **1g TDS** | 5-10 / 7 days |
| **Famciclovir** | 250 mg TDS (recurrent) / 500 mg BD (primary) | **500 mg TDS** | 5-7 / 7 days |

> **Valacyclovir/Famciclovir preferred** — better bioavailability, less frequent dosing.

### 2.2 IV Antivirals (Severe / Immunocompromised / CNS)
| Drug | Dose | Indication |
|------|------|------------|
| **Acyclovir IV** | **10 mg/kg q8h** (adjust renal) | **Herpes encephalitis, neonatal HSV, disseminated VZV, immunocompromised** |
| **Foscarnet** | 40 mg/kg IV q8h | **Acyclovir-resistant HSV** (TK-negative) |
| **Cidofovir** | 5 mg/kg IV weekly | Acyclovir/Foscarnet-resistant |

### 2.3 Treatment Timing — **CRITICAL**
| Condition | Window for Max Benefit |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Primary HSV** | Within 48-72h of onset |
| **Recurrent HSV** | At **prodrome** (ideally) / within 24h |
| **Herpes Zoster** | **Within 72h** of rash onset (reduces PHN) |
| **HZO (Ophthalmic)** | **Immediate IV acyclovir** + urgent ophthalmology |
| **Immunocompromised** | **Immediate IV acyclovir** |

## 4. HERPES ZOSTER — SPECIFIC MANAGEMENT

### 4.1 Standard HZ (Immunocompetent)
| Drug | Dose | Duration |
|------|------|----------|
| **Valacyclovir** | 1g TDS | 7 days |
| **Famciclovir** | 500 mg TDS | 7 days |
| **Acyclovir** | 800 mg 5x/day | 7 days |

> **Start within 72h** of rash → reduces duration, viral shedding, **PHN risk**.

### 4.2 HZO (Ophthalmic) — **EMERGENCY**
- **IV Acyclovir 10-15 mg/kg q8h** (admit)
- **Urgent Ophthalmology** referral (within 24h)
- **Topical** ganciclovir/aciclovir eye drops
- **Corticosteroids** (controversial; if severe inflammation under ophthalmology supervision)

### 2.3 Post-Herpetic Neuralgia (PHN) — Prevention & Treatment
| Strategy | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Prevention** | **Early antiviral (≤72h)**; consider **low-dose amitriptyline/nortriptyline** prophylactically in >50 yrs |
| **First-line** | **Gabapentin** (900-3600 mg/day) / **Pregabalin** (150-600 mg/day) |
| **Second-line** | **TCAs** (Amitriptyline 10-75 mg ON); **Duloxetine** |
| **Topical** | **Lidocaine 5% patch**; **Capsaicin 0.075% / 8% patch** |
| **Interventional** | Nerve blocks, spinal cord stimulation (refractory) |

## 4. SPECIAL POPULATIONS

### 4.1 Immunocompromised (HIV, Transplant, Chemo, Biologics)
| Scenario | Management |
|----------|------------|
| **Mucocutaneous HSV** | Valacyclovir 1g BD × 7-14 days; chronic suppression if frequent |
| **Disseminated VZV** | **IV Acyclovir 10-15 mg/kg q8h × 7-10 days** |
| **Acyclovir Resistance** | **Foscarnet 40 mg/kg q8h IV** (TK-mutant); Cidofovir 5 mg/kg weekly |
| **Prophylaxis (HIV CD4<200)** | Acyclovir 400 mg BD / Valacyclovir 500 mg OD |
| **Post-Transplant Prophylaxis** | Valacyclovir 500 mg OD × 3-12 months |

### 4.2 Pregnancy
| Scenario | Management |
|----------|------------|
| **Primary HSV in pregnancy** | Acyclovir/Valacyclovir safe (Category B) — treat primary; **C-section if active lesions at delivery** |
| **Recurrent HSV** | Suppression from 36 wks (Acyclovir 400 mg BD / Valacyclovir 500 mg OD) |
| **VZV Exposure (non-immune)** | **VZIG (Varicella-Zoster Immune Globulin)** within 96h (ideally 48h); if rash develops → Acyclovir |
| **HZO in pregnancy** | IV Acyclovir + Ophthalmology |

### 4.3 Neonatal HSV
| Type | Features | Management |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **SEM (Skin, Eye, Mouth)** | Vesicles 24-72h post-birth | IV Acyclovir 20 mg/kg q8h × 14 days |
| **CNS** | Seizures, lethargy, CSF HSV DNA | IV Acyclovir 20 mg/kg q8h × **21 days** |
| **Disseminated** | Hepatitis, coagulopathy, pneumonitis | IV Acyclovir 20 mg/kg q8h × 21 days |

## 5. PREVENTION & VACCINATION

### 5.1 Varicella (Chickenpox) Vaccine
| Vaccine | Schedule | Efficacy |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Live attenuated (Varilrix, Varivax)** | 2 doses: 12-15 mo, 4-6 yr | >90% vs severe; 70-90% vs any varicella |

### 5.2 Herpes Zoster Vaccines
| Vaccine | Type | Schedule | Efficacy | Indication |
|---------|------|----------|----------|------------|
| **Zostavax** (ZVL) | Live attenuated | Single dose ≥60 yrs | 51% HZ, 67% PHN | **Contraindicated** immunocompromised |
| **Shingrix (RZV)** | **Recombinant subunit (AS01B adjuvant)** | **2 doses 2-6 months apart** | **>90% HZ, 89% PHN** | **≥50 yrs; immunocompromised ≥18 yrs (preferred)** |

> **Shingrix preferred** — higher efficacy, safe in immunocompromised.

## 5. KEY VIVA POINTS

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **HSV-1 vs HSV-2 clinical difference?** | HSV-1 = orolabial (cold sores); HSV-2 = genital; both can cause either |
| **Herpes Zoster vs Simplex distribution?** | HZ = **dermatomal, unilateral, no midline crossing**; HSV = grouped vesicles at mucocutaneous junctions |
| **Tzanck smear findings?** | **Multinucleated giant cells** (HSV, VZV); acantholytic cells (pemphigus) |
| **Acyclovir dose VZV?** | **800 mg 5x/day** (oral); 10-15 mg/kg q8h IV |
| **HZO management?** | **IV Acyclovir 10-15 mg/kg q8h + Urgent Ophthalmology** (within 24h) |
| **PHN definition?** | Pain persisting **>90 days** after rash onset |
| **PHN prophylaxis?** | Early antiviral ≤72h; consider low-dose amitriptyline in elderly |
| **Neonatal HSV types?** | SEM (skin/eye/mouth), CNS, Disseminated — all **IV Acyclovir 20 mg/kg q8h** |
| **HSV encephalitis CSF?** | **PCR HSV DNA** (gold standard); lymphocytic pleocytosis, high protein |
| **Acyclovir resistance mechanism?** | Thymidine kinase (TK) mutation / DNA polymerase mutation |
| **Foscarnet indication?** | Acyclovir-resistant HSV (TK-mutant); VZV resistant |
| **Zoster vaccine (Shingrix)?** | **RZV (recombinant)** 2 doses 2-6 mo apart; ≥50 yrs; >90% efficacy; safe immunocompromised |
| **Post-exposure VZIG?** | Non-immune exposed; within 96h (ideal 48h); dose 125 IU/10kg IM |
| **Ramsey Hunt Syndrome?** | Geniculate ganglion zoster → facial palsy + vesicles ear/palate |
| **Eczema Herpeticum treatment?** | **IV Acyclovir** urgent (Kaposi varicelliform eruption on AD background) |

*Prepared for FCPS/MRCP Dermatology — Herpes Simplex & Zoster Full Note*## MCQs (5)

1. **Q:** Which of the following is a key feature of Herpes_Simplex_Zoster?
   A. Key clinical features include characteristic morphology, distribution, and histopathological finding
   B. Always self-limiting without treatment
   C. Caused by bacterial infection only
   D. Restricted to palms and soles only
   E. No investigation ever needed
   **Answer: A**
   *Key clinical features include characteristic morphology, distribution, and histopathological findings.*

2. **Q:** What is the first-line treatment for mild Herpes_Simplex_Zoster?
   A. Topical or systemic therapy appropriate to disease severity
   B. Surgical excision
   C. IVIG
   D. No treatment needed
   E. Empirical antibiotics
   **Answer: A**
   *Topical therapy is first-line for most inflammatory skin disease; systemic for moderate-severe.*

3. **Q:** Which pre-treatment screening is essential before starting systemic therapy for Herpes_Simplex_Zoster?
   A. TB (IGRA), hepatitis B/C, HIV
   B. Pregnancy test only
   C. ECG only
   D. No screening needed
   E. Eye examination only
   **Answer: A**
   *Pre-biologic/immunosuppressant screening includes TB, hepatitis B/C, HIV, and pregnancy test in women of childbearing age.*

4. **Q:** Which complication should be actively monitored in Herpes_Simplex_Zoster?
   A. Secondary infection and organ-specific involvement
   B. Cardiac arrest as first presentation
   C. Renal failure in all patients
   D. Always malignancy transformation
   E. No complications
   **Answer: A**
   *Secondary bacterial infection is the most common complication; disease-specific organ involvement must be monitored.*

5. **Q:** What is the most appropriate investigation for suspected Herpes_Simplex_Zoster?
   A. Clinical diagnosis ± targeted biopsy/serology
   B. Empirical treatment only
   C. CT chest/abdomen/pelvis always
   D. No investigation
   E. ANA only
   **Answer: A**
   *Diagnosis is usually clinical; skin biopsy with H&E and DIF where autoimmune; serology as clinically indicated.*

   B. Serum IgE only
   C. CT chest/abdomen
   D. ANA only

   B. Phototherapy (NB-UVB) or conventional systemic

## Flashcards

- **Q:** What is the first-line treatment for Herpes_Simplex_Zoster?
  **A:** Topical therapy (corticosteroids, calcineurin inhibitors, or specific agents per disease); trigger avoidance; emollients.
- **Q:** What is a key clinical feature of Herpes_Simplex_Zoster?
  **A:** Key clinical features include characteristic morphology, distribution, and histopathological findings.
- **Q:** What pre-treatment screening is needed before systemic therapy?
  **A:** TB (IGRA), hepatitis B (HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs), hepatitis C, HIV, pregnancy test, baseline FBC/LFT/renal.
- **Q:** What is the most important complication of Herpes_Simplex_Zoster?
  **A:** Secondary infection (bacterial/viral); disease-specific organ involvement; treatment toxicity.
- **Q:** What scoring system applies?
  **A:** Disease-specific (e.g., PASI for psoriasis, EASI for atopic dermatitis, SCORTEN for SJS/TEN, SCORAD for AD).
