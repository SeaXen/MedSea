> **Digoxin-specific Fab fragments** = definitive antidote. **Hyperkalemia in acute digoxin poisoning = poor prognosis, treat with Fab FIRST (not insulin/dextrose/calcium)**. Key FCPS/MRCP: Fab dosing (empirical 10-20 vials or calculated: serum level × weight / 100); avoid calcium (stone heart); treat arrhythmias with Fab ± Mg/phenytoin/lidocaine; chronic toxicity at lower levels; renal impairment ↑ risk.

## 1. Learning Objectives
- Recognize digoxin toxicity (GI, cardiac, visual, neurologic)
- Calculate and administer digoxin-specific Fab fragments
- Interpret hyperkalemia as marker of severity (acute)
- Avoid contraindicated treatments (calcium, atropine for AV block)
- Differentiate acute vs chronic toxicity
- Manage arrhythmias in digoxin toxicity

## 2. Definition
Digoxin poisoning = toxicity from cardiac glycoside (digoxin, digitoxin) causing **Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase inhibition** → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → ↓ Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchange → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺ → **increased automaticity + decreased conduction** → arrhythmias.

## 3. Core Physiology
- **Mechanism**: binds Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase (α subunit) → **inhibition** → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → ↓ Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (NCX) activity → ↑ intracellular Ca²⁺
- **Effects**:
  - **Positive inotropy** (therapeutic) → at toxic doses: **delayed afterdepolarizations (DADs)** → **triggered activity** → tachyarrhythmias
  - **Increased vagal tone** → **bradycardia, AV block** (therapeutic and toxic)
- **Pharmacokinetics**: narrow therapeutic index (0.5-2 ng/mL), large Vd (~500 L), renal excretion (60-80% unchanged), **long half-life 36-48h** (prolonged in renal failure), tissue binding → **levels don't correlate acutely with severity** (distribution phase)
- **Risk factors**: **renal failure**, elderly, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, hypercalcemia, hypothyroidism, amiodarone/verapamil/diltiazem/quinidine (↑ digoxin level), acute illness

## 4. Clinical Features

### Gastrointestinal (Most Common, Early)
- Nausea, vomiting, anorexia, abdominal pain

### Cardiac (Most Serious)
- **Arrhythmias — "virtually any arrhythmia except rapidly conducted atrial arrhythmias"**:
  - **Bradyarrhythmias**: sinus brady, SA block, AV block (1°, 2°, 3°), junctional rhythms
  - **Tachyarrhythmias**: PAT with block (classic), atrial tachycardia, **bidirectional ventricular tachycardia** (pathognomonic), VT, VF
  - **Ectopy**: frequent PVCs, bigeminy, trigeminy
- **Hyperkalemia** (acute poisoning) — **marker of severity, poor prognosis**

### Visual (Classic)
- **Yellow-green vision (xanthopsia)**, blurred vision, photophobia, scotomas

### Neurologic
- Confusion, delirium, headache, fatigue, weakness

### Acute vs Chronic Toxicity
| Feature | Acute (Intentional OD) | Chronic (Therapeutic Misadventure) |
|---------|------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Serum level** | Very high (> 10 ng/mL) | Mildly elevated (2.5-4 ng/mL) |
| **Potassium** | **Marked hyperkalemia** (acute release) | Normal/mild ↑ |
| **Renal function** | Initially normal | Often impaired (precipitant) |
| **Fab dose** | Higher (empirical 15-20 vials) | Lower (calculated or 5-10 vials) |
| **Prognosis** | Better with Fab | Worse (comorbidities, delayed Dx) |

## 5. Investigations
- **Digoxin level** — drawn **≥ 6h post-dose** (distribution phase); **does not correlate acutely with severity** in acute OD; therapeutic 0.5-2 ng/mL; toxic > 2 ng/mL
- **Potassium** — **CRITICAL** in acute OD (hyperkalemia = severe)
- **Renal function** — creatinine, eGFR
- **Electrolytes** — Mg²⁺ (hypomagnesemia ↑ toxicity), Ca²⁺
- **ECG** — arrhythmias, PR prolongation, ST sag ("reverse tick" / "Salvador Dali mustache")
- **Mg²⁺** — hypomagnesemia common, worsens toxicity
- **Paracetamol level** (always)

## 6. Management

### 1. Resuscitation (ABCDE)
- **Airway/Breathing**: standard
- **Circulation**: treat arrhythmias, hypotension

### 2. Digoxin-Specific Antibody Fragments (Digoxin Immune Fab / DigiFab / Digibind) — **DEFINITIVE ANTIDOTE**
- **Mechanism**: binds free digoxin in plasma → ↑ Vd → pulls digoxin from tissue → complex renally excreted (dialysis if anuric)
- **Indications** (Any One):
  - **Life-threatening arrhythmia** (VF, sustained VT, high-grade AV block, symptomatic bradycardia)
  - **Hyperkalemia > 5.5 mmol/L** (acute)
  - **Serum level > 10 ng/mL** (acute) or > 6 ng/mL (chronic with toxicity)
  - **Ingestion > 10 mg** (adult) or > 4 mg (child) or unknown large amount
  - **Cardiac arrest** (if digoxin suspected)
- **Dosing**:
  - **Empirical** (preferred in acute/severe): **10-20 vials** (each vial binds ~0.5 mg digoxin)
    - Cardiac arrest / severe: **20 vials**
    - Significant toxicity: **10-15 vials**
  - **Calculated** (if level known, steady state):
    - **Vials = (Serum digoxin ng/mL × Weight kg) / 100** (round up)
    - Example: 5 ng/mL × 70 kg / 100 = 3.5 → **4 vials**
- **Administration**: reconstitute each vial with 4 mL sterile water → IV infusion over 30 min (can push in cardiac arrest)
- **Onset**: 30-60 min for arrhythmia resolution; K⁺ falls within 1-2h
- **Monitoring**: K⁺ q1-2h (rebound hypokalemia as digoxin redistributes), digoxin level at 24h (assay measures total = free + bound; **interpret with caution** — free fraction low), renal function
- **Renal failure**: Fab-digoxin complex accumulates → may need **hemodialysis** for complex removal; consider **repeat Fab doses**

### 3. Hyperkalemia in Acute Digoxin Poisoning — **TREAT WITH FAB, NOT STANDARD MEASURES**
- **Mechanism**: Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase inhibition → K⁺ cannot enter cells → **severe hyperkalemia** (can be > 7-8 mmol/L)
- **Management**: **DIGOXIN FAB FIRST** — rapidly lowers K⁺ as digoxin redistributes
- **Do NOT give**: insulin/dextrose, calcium, beta-agonists, kayexalate as primary — **delays Fab, calcium may cause "stone heart"**
- **If Fab unavailable + life-threatening hyperkalemia**: standard measures with extreme caution (no calcium)

### 4. Arrhythmia Management
- **Primary**: **Digoxin Fab** — resolves most arrhythmias
- **Bradydysrhythmias** (symptomatic brady, high-grade AV block):
  - **Atropine** 0.5 mg IV — **may be ineffective** (vagal + direct effect)
  - **Transcutaneous pacing** — **often ineffective** (increased automaticity competes)
  - **Isoproterenol** — consider if pacing fails (increases automaticity/conduction)
  - **AVOID CALCIUM** — "stone heart" (irreversible contracture from massive Ca²⁺ influx)
- **Tachydysrhythmias** (PAT with block, bidirectional VT, VT/VF):
  - **Magnesium sulfate** 2 g IV over 10-20 min — **first-line adjunct** (suppresses DADs)
  - **Phenytoin** 15 mg/kg IV — **acceptable** (Class Ib, shortens action potential, suppresses DADs) — **unlike TCA/BB/CCB**
  - **Lidocaine** 1-1.5 mg/kg IV — acceptable alternative
  - **AVOID**: amiodarone (↑ digoxin level, may worsen), verapamil/diltiazem, beta-blockers, procainamide, quinidine
  - **Cardioversion** — **avoid if possible** (↑ risk VF); if unstable, **lowest energy** (1-5 J) + Fab simultaneously

### 5. Hypokalemia / Hypomagnesemia
- **Correct K⁺ to > 4.0 mmol/L** (hypokalemia ↑ digoxin binding to ATPase)
- **Correct Mg²⁺ to > 0.8 mmol/L**

### 6. Decontamination
- **Activated charcoal**: 1 g/kg if < 1-2h (not well adsorbed but may help)
- **Cholestyramine** — interrupts enterohepatic circulation (chronic toxicity)

### 7. Monitoring & Disposition
- **Continuous ECG** until arrhythmias resolved 24h
- **K⁺ q1-2h** post-Fab (rebound hypokalemia)
- **Digoxin level at 24h** (interpret with caution — assay measures bound+free)
- **Renal function**
- **Observe 24h+** post-Fab
- **Psych assessment** (if intentional)

## 7. Complications
- Ventricular fibrillation / asystole
- "Stone heart" (calcium-induced)
- Rebound toxicity (Fab-digoxin complex dissociates in renal failure)
- Hypokalemia post-Fab
- Anaphylaxis to Fab (rare, sheep antibody)

## 8. Prognosis
- **Excellent with Fab** — mortality < 1-2% if Fab given
- Without Fab: mortality 5-10% (acute), higher chronic
- Chronic toxicity: worse due to comorbidities, delayed recognition

## 9. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
1. **Digoxin Fab = definitive antidote** — indications: life-threatening arrhythmia, K⁺ > 5.5, level > 10 (acute) / > 6 (chronic), ingestion > 10 mg
2. **Fab dosing**: empirical 10-20 vials (acute/severe) OR calculated (level × weight / 100)
3. **Hyperkalemia in acute OD = treat with Fab FIRST** — NOT insulin/dextrose/calcium
4. **Hyperkalemia = poor prognostic marker** in acute poisoning
5. **AVOID CALCIUM** — "stone heart" (irreversible contracture)
6. **Atropine/pacing often ineffective** for bradyarrhythmias
7. **Bidirectional VT = pathognomonic** for digoxin toxicity
8. **PAT with block = classic** (atrial tachycardia with AV block)
9. **Chronic toxicity at lower levels** (2.5-4 ng/mL) — elderly, renal failure, drug interactions
10. **Renal failure → prolonged half-life, Fab complex accumulation → may need dialysis**
11. **Correct hypokalemia/hypomagnesemia** (↑ digoxin binding)
12. **Phenytoin/lidocaine acceptable for tachyarrhythmias** (unlike TCA/BB/CCB)
13. **Avoid amiodarone** (↑ digoxin level, may worsen)

## 11. Common Confusions / Exam Traps
- **Treat hyperkalemia with insulin/dextrose/calcium** → NO, **FAB FIRST**
- **Calcium for hyperkalemia in digoxin** → NO, "stone heart"
- **Atropine works for digoxin bradycardia** → often ineffective
- **Pacing works** → often ineffective
- **Amiodarone for digoxin VT** → NO, ↑ digoxin level, worsens
- **Digoxin level correlates with severity acutely** → NO, distribution phase
- **Chronic toxicity needs higher levels** → NO, lower levels (2.5-4) with renal failure/drug interactions
- **Fab dose = level × weight** → yes but empirical 10-20 vials often used in acute

## 22. MCQs (10)
1. Digoxin mechanism of toxicity?
   A. Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase inhibition → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → ↑ Ca²⁺ via Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger
   B. Beta-blockade
   C. Calcium channel blockade
   D. Potassium channel blockade
   **Answer: A**
   *Explanation: Digoxin inhibits Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → ↑ Ca²⁺ via Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (inotropy) but also ↑ automaticity, ↓ AV conduction, arrhythmias. Toxicity = same mechanism exaggerated.*

2. Digoxin toxicity - classic arrhythmias?
   A. Only ventricular tachycardia
   B. EVERY arrhythmia EXCEPT rapidly conducted atrial arrhythmias (AF with rapid ventricular response)
   C. Only bradycardia
   D. Only AV block
   **Answer: B**
   *Explanation: Digoxin toxicity = 'every arrhythmia except rapidly conducted atrial arrhythmias'. Common: PVCs, bigeminy, VT/VF, AV block (any degree), atrial tachycardia with block, bidirectional VT (pathognomonic).*

3. Digoxin-specific Fab dose calculation?
   A. Level × weight / 100 = vials
   B. Serum level (ng/mL) × weight (kg) / 100 = vials OR empirical 10-20 vials
   C. 1 vial per ng/mL
   D. Fixed 5 vials
   **Answer: B**
   *Explanation: Fab dose = (serum digoxin ng/mL × weight kg) / 100 = number of vials. If level unknown/acute massive: empirical 10-20 vials. Each vial binds ~0.5mg digoxin.*

4. Digoxin hyperkalemia - management?
   A. Insulin/dextrose + calcium
   B. Digoxin-specific Fab FIRST (reverses Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase block → K⁺ shifts into cells)
   C. Sodium bicarbonate
   D. Kayexalate
   **Answer: B**
   *Explanation: Digoxin hyperkalemia = poor prognosis (massive Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase blockade → K⁺ can't enter cells). Give Fab FIRST. Standard hyperK measures can be dangerous (insulin → hypokalemia post-Fab).*

5. Bidirectional ventricular tachycardia - pathognomonic for?
   A. TCA poisoning
   B. Digoxin toxicity
   C. Catecholaminergic polymorphic VT
   D. Long QT
   **Answer: B**
   *Explanation: Bidirectional VT (alternating QRS axis) = pathognomonic for digoxin toxicity. Also: atrial tachycardia with block (PAT with block).*

6. Digoxin toxicity precipitated by?
   A. Hyperkalemia
   B. Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, renal failure, amiodarone, verapamil, quinidine, elderly
   C. Hypercalcemia only
   D. Hyponatremia
   **Answer: B**
   *Explanation: Precipitants: hypokalemia (↑ digoxin binding), hypomagnesemia, renal failure (↓ clearance), drug interactions (amiodarone, verapamil, quinidine, dronedarone ↑ levels), elderly (↓ Vd, ↓ renal function).*

7. Digoxin Fab - when to give?
   A. Any level > 2 ng/mL
   B. Life-threatening arrhythmia, hemodynamic instability, K⁺ > 5.5, level > 10 ng/mL (acute) or > 6 ng/mL (chronic), ingest > 10mg (adult) or > 4mg (child)
   C. Only if level > 15
   D. Only for bradycardia
   **Answer: B**
   *Explanation: Fab indications: life-threatening arrhythmia, hemodynamic instability, K⁺ > 5.5, level > 10 ng/mL (acute) or > 6 ng/mL (chronic), massive ingestion (>10mg adult, >4mg child).*

8. Digoxin toxicity - atropine for bradycardia?
   A. First line
   B. Often ineffective (not purely vagal), Fab is definitive
   C. Contraindicated
   D. Only for AV block
   **Answer: B**
   *Explanation: Digoxin bradycardia/AV block = ↓ conduction (not vagal). Atropine often INEFFECTIVE. Fab is definitive. Temporary pacing if hemodynamically unstable awaiting Fab.*

9. Digoxin + hypokalemia - why dangerous?
   A. ↑ Digoxin binding to Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase (K⁺ competes) → ↑ toxicity
   B. Causes arrhythmias directly
   C. Worsens renal function
   D. No interaction
   **Answer: A**
   *Explanation: K⁺ competes with digoxin for Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase binding. Hypokalemia → ↑ digoxin binding → ↑ toxicity. Correct K⁺ > 4.0 (but avoid overcorrection post-Fab).*

10. Digoxin level interpretation - acute vs chronic?
   A. Same threshold
   B. Acute: > 10 ng/mL toxic. Chronic: > 6 ng/mL toxic (tissue levels higher)
   C. Chronic higher threshold
   D. Levels not useful
   **Answer: B**
   *Explanation: Acute: level > 10 ng/mL toxic. Chronic: > 6 ng/mL toxic (tissue levels higher than serum). Levels at 6h post-dose (distribution complete). Clinical context > level.*

## 24. Flashcards
- Q: Digoxin mechanism?
  A: Inhibits Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase → ↑ intracellular Na⁺ → ↑ Ca²⁺ via Na⁺/Ca²⁺ exchanger (inotropy) + ↑ automaticity + ↓ AV conduction.
- Q: Digoxin toxicity arrhythmias?
  A: 'Every arrhythmia except rapidly conducted atrial arrhythmias (AF with rapid VR)'. Common: PVCs, bigeminy, bidirectional VT, AV block, PAT with block.
- Q: Bidirectional VT?
  A: Pathognomonic for digoxin toxicity. Alternating QRS axis.
- Q: PAT with block?
  A: Atrial tachycardia with AV block = classic digoxin (↑ automaticity + ↓ AV conduction).
- Q: Fab dose?
  A: (Level ng/mL × weight kg) / 100 = vials. Empirical 10-20 vials if level unknown/massive ingestion.
- Q: Fab indications?
  A: Life-threatening arrhythmia, hemodynamic instability, K⁺ > 5.5, level >10 (acute) or >6 (chronic), ingestion >10mg adult/>4mg child.
- Q: Digoxin hyperkalemia?
  A: Na/K-ATPase blocked → K⁺ can't enter cells. Give Fab FIRST. Insulin/dextrose dangerous post-Fab (hypokalemia).
- Q: Hypokalemia + digoxin?
  A: K⁺ competes for Na/K-ATPase. Hypokalemia → ↑ digoxin binding → ↑ toxicity. Correct K⁺ > 4.0.
- Q: Digoxin + amiodarone?
  A: Amiodarone inhibits P-gp → ↓ digoxin clearance → ↑ levels. Reduce digoxin 50%.
- Q: Atropine for digoxin bradycardia?
  A: Often INEFFECTIVE (not vagal). Fab definitive. Temporary pacing if unstable awaiting Fab.
- Q: Calcium in digoxin hyperK?
  A: AVOID - 'stone heart' risk (Ca²⁺ overload in toxic myocytes). Fab for hyperK.
- Q: Digoxin level timing?
  A: Draw at 6h post-dose (distribution complete). Acute >10 toxic, chronic >6 toxic.
- Q: Post-Fab hypokalemia?
  A: Fab reverses Na/K blockade → K⁺ shifts INTO cells → hypokalemia. Monitor q1-2h, replace to >4.0.
- Q: Fab in renal failure?
  A: Can give (lifesaving). Fab-digoxin complex renally cleared → monitor for rebound (dissociation). May need repeat Fab.
- Q: Digoxin precipitants?
  A: Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, renal failure, elderly, amiodarone/verapamil/quinidine/dronedarone (P-gp inhibitors).
