POISONING
Z-Drug (Zolpidem, Zopiclone) Poisoning
1 Learning Objectives
  • Recognize Z-drug toxidrome (sedation, ataxia, complex sleep behaviors, hallucinations)
  • Differentiate from benzodiazepine poisoning
  • Apply flumazenil with same contraindications as benzo
  • Manage complex sleep behaviors and hallucinations
2 Definition

Z-drug poisoning = toxicity from non-benzodiazepine hypnotics (zolpidem, zopiclone, eszopiclone, zaleplon) acting selectively at BZ₁ (α₁) subunit of GABAₐ receptor causing sedation with unique adverse effects (complex sleep behaviors, hallucinations).

3 Core Physiology
  • Mechanism: selective agonists at BZ₁ (α₁) subunit of GABAₐ receptor → ↑ Cl⁻ channel opening frequency → sedation, hypnosis, anterograde amnesia
  • Selectivity: α₁ > α₂, α₃, α₅ → more sedation/hypnosis, less anxiolysis, muscle relaxant, anticonvulsant
  • Pharmacokinetics:
  • Zolpidem: half-life 2-3h, rapid onset (15-30 min), no active metabolites
  • Zopiclone: half-life 5-6h, active metabolites (desmethylzopiclone)
  • Zaleplon: half-life 1h, ultra-short
  • No ceiling effect on respiratory depression (like benzos, but less pronounced alone)
4 Clinical Features
  • CNS: sedation → coma, ataxia, dysarthria, nystagmus (less than benzo)
  • Unique adverse effects:
  • Complex sleep behaviors: sleepwalking, sleep-driving, sleep-eating, sleep-cooking — amnesia for events
  • Hallucinations (visual, auditory) — more common than benzo
  • Anterograde amnesia (marked)
  • Next-day impairment (driving risk)
  • Respiratory: mild depression alone; severe with alcohol/opioid co-ingestion
  • Cardiovascular: mild hypotension, bradycardia
  • GI: nausea, vomiting
5 Differential Diagnosis
  • Benzodiazepine: similar but less complex sleep behaviors, less hallucinations, longer half-life (usually)
  • Opioid: miosis, naloxone responsive
  • Alcohol: odor, metabolic acidosis
  • GHB: rapid coma → rapid arousal, myoclonus
  • Anticholinergic: dry, hot, dilated, delirium
6 Investigations
  • Glucose (bedside)
  • ABG/VBG: usually normal unless mixed OD
  • Paracetamol level (always)
  • Ethanol level
  • Urine drug screen: may miss zolpidem/zaleplone (not standard benzo immunoassay)
  • ECG: usually normal
  • CXR: aspiration if co-ingestion
7 Management
1. Supportive Care (Mainstay)
  • Airway: protect if GCS < 8, respiratory depression — intubate
  • Breathing: O₂, ventilation if needed
  • Circulation: fluids for hypotension
  • Monitoring: continuous SpO₂, ECG, GCS q15-30min
2. Flumazenil — **WORKS BUT SAME INDICATIONS/CONTRAINDICATIONS AS BENZO**
  • Mechanism: competitive BZD receptor antagonist
  • Indications: ONLY procedural oversedation, diagnostic uncertainty (same as benzo)
  • Contraindications: TCA co-ingestion (QRS>100), seizure hx, chronic use, head injury
  • Dose: 0.2 mg IV q1min max 1mg, then 1mg/hr if re-sedation
  • Duration: 30-60 min → re-sedation common
3. Decontamination
  • Activated charcoal: 1 g/kg if < 1-2h (delayed gastric emptying possible)
  • WBI: extended-release formulations (rare)
4. Specific Complication Management
  • Complex sleep behaviors: safety counseling, discontinue drug
  • Hallucinations: usually self-limiting; low-dose antipsychotic if severe (avoid if possible)
  • Next-day impairment: warn against driving
8 Complications
  • Aspiration pneumonia
  • Falls/trauma (complex sleep behaviors)
  • Respiratory depression (with co-ingestants)
  • Next-day driving impairment
9 Prognosis
  • Excellent with supportive care
  • Short half-life → rapid recovery
  • Full recovery typical
10 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  1. BZ₁-selective → more sedation, less anxiolysis/muscle relaxant
  2. Shorter half-life than benzos: zolpidem 2-3h, zopiclone 5-6h, zaleplon 1h
  3. Complex sleep behaviors (sleepwalking, sleep-driving) — hallmark
  4. Hallucinations more common than benzo
  5. Flumazenil reverses but same contraindications as benzo (TCA, seizure hx, chronic use)
  6. Urine screen may miss zolpidem/zaleplone
  7. Short obs (4-6h zolpidem/zaleplon; 6-8h zopiclone)
11 Common Viva Questions
  1. Z-drug vs benzodiazepine differences
  2. Complex sleep behaviors
  3. Flumazenil use in Z-drug poisoning
  4. Half-life differences
  5. Urine screen limitations
12 Common Confusions / Exam Traps
  • Z-drugs = benzos → different receptor selectivity, different adverse effects
  • Flumazenil not work → works (same receptor) but same contraindications
  • All Z-drugs same half-life → zolpidem 2-3h, zopiclone 5-6h, zaleplon 1h
  • Urine screen detects all → misses zolpidem/zaleplone
13 Mnemonics
  • Z-DRUGS: Zolpidem (2-3h), Zopiclone (5-6h), Zaleplon (1h)
  • Z-EFFECTS: Z (sleep behaviors), D (hallucinations), R (respiratory depression mild), U (ultra-short zaleplon), G (GABA BZ1 selective), S (shorter than benzo)
