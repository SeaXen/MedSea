2 Definitions / Key Concepts
TermDefinition
------------------
**Phylloquinone (K1)**Plant form (green leafy veg, plant oils); major dietary source (~90% intake); side chain: phytyl (saturated)
**Menaquinones (K2, MK-n)**Bacterial/animal forms; side chain: unsaturated isoprenoid (n repeats); MK-4 (animal tissues), MK-7 (natto, fermented foods)
**Menadione (K3)**Synthetic naphthoquinone; no side chain; water-soluble; toxic (haemolysis, kernicterus) — not used therapeutically
**γ-Glutamyl Carboxylase (GGCX)**Vitamin K-dependent enzyme; carboxylates Glu → γ-carboxyglutamate (Gla) on target proteins; requires CO₂, O₂, vitamin K hydroquinone
**Gla Residue**γ-carboxyglutamic acid; binds Ca²⁺ via dicarboxylic acid; essential for protein function (clotting, bone, vascular)
**VKORC1**Vitamin K epoxide reductase complex subunit 1; recycles vitamin K epoxide → quinone → hydroquinone (active cofactor); warfarin target
**Vitamin K Cycle**KH₂ (hydroquinone) → GGCX (Glu→Gla) → KO (epoxide) → VKORC1 (KO→KH₂) → repeat
**Phytomenadione**Synthetic K1 analogue; used for prophylaxis/treatment (IM, IV, PO); Konakion MM (mixed micellar) for IV
**HDN (Haemorrhagic Disease of Newborn)**Classical (day 1–7), Late (2–12w, exclusively breastfed, cholestasis); prevented by 1 mg IM phytomenadione at birth
**Osteocalcin (Bone Gla Protein, BGP)**Osteoblast product; γ-carboxylated (3 Gla); binds hydroxyapatite; serum = bone formation marker; uncarboxylated (ucOC) = vit K status
**Matrix Gla Protein (MGP)**Vascular smooth muscle/chondrocyte product; γ-carboxylated (5 Gla); inhibits vascular calcification (binds Ca crystals); MGP-knockout → arterial calcification
**Protein C & S**Vitamin K-dependent anticoagulants; Protein C (activated by thrombomodulin-thrombin) → inactivates Va/VIIa; Protein S = cofactor
**PT/INR**Prothrombin time; extrinsic pathway (factor VII); most sensitive to vit K deficiency (t½ 6h); INR standardised
**aPTT**Activated partial thromboplastin time; intrinsic pathway; normal in early vit K deficiency, prolonged if severe
**PIVKA-II**Protein Induced by Vitamin K Absence-II (uncarboxylated prothrombin); sensitive marker of vit K deficiency
**Superwarfarins**Brodifacoum, bromadiolone, difenacoum; long-acting rodenticides; inhibit VKORC1 irreversibly; half-life days–weeks; treatment: high-dose phytomenadione weeks–months
**Coumarin Resistance**VKORC1 mutation (e.g., VKORC1 -1639G>A) → reduced warfarin sensitivity; requires higher dose
3 Core Content
Section 1: Forms, Sources & Absorption

Vitamin K1 (Phylloquinone): Green leafy vegetables (spinach, kale, broccoli, Brussels sprouts), plant oils (soybean, canola). Contributes ~90% dietary intake. Bioavailability: 5–10% from vegetables (matrix-bound), higher with fat/mechanical disruption. Absorption: mixed micelles → enterocyte → chylomicron. Vitamin K2 (Menaquinones MK-n): Bacterial synthesis (gut microbiota: MK-6 to MK-13), animal tissues (MK-4 from menadione in feed), fermented foods (natto = MK-7, cheese = MK-8/MK-9). MK-4 in meat, dairy, eggs. MK-7 (natto) — long half-life (72h vs K1 1–2h), better extrahepatic distribution. Menadione (K3): Synthetic, water-soluble; converts to MK-4 in tissues; toxic (haemolysis, kernicterus in neonates) — NOT used clinically. Absorption: Requires bile salts, pancreatic lipase; chylomicron secretion; lymphatic transport. K1: hepatic uptake via chylomicron remnant; MK-7: longer circulation, extrahepatic delivery.

Section 2: γ-Glutamyl Carboxylase & Vitamin K Cycle

Reaction: Glu (target protein) + CO₂ + O₂ + KH₂ (vitamin K hydroquinone) → Gla + KO (vitamin K epoxide) + H₂O. Enzyme: γ-glutamyl carboxylase (GGCX); ER membrane-bound; processive (multiple Glu per substrate). Vitamin K Cycle:

  1. KH₂ (hydroquinone) = active cofactor for GGCX
  2. GGCX uses KH₂ → carboxylates Glu → Gla → produces KO (epoxide)
  3. VKORC1 reduces KO → K (quinone) → KH₂ (hydroquinone) using NAD(P)H
  4. Warfarin inhibits VKORC1 → KO accumulates → KH₂ depleted → ↓γ-carboxylation

Target proteins (17 human Gla-proteins):

  • Coagulation: Factors II (prothrombin, 10 Gla), VII (10 Gla), IX (12 Gla), X (11 Gla)
  • Anticoagulation: Protein C (9 Gla), Protein S (11 Gla), Protein Z
  • Bone: Osteocalcin (BGP, 3 Gla), Matrix Gla Protein (MGP, 5 Gla)
  • Other: Gas6, PRGP1/2, TMG3/4, periostin
Section 3: Warfarin Pharmacology

Mechanism: Competitive inhibitor of VKORC1 (binds epoxide binding site); prevents KO → KH₂ recycling. Onset: Delayed (factor half-lives): Factor VII t½ 6h (PT ↑ first, 12–24h), IX t½ 24h, X t½ 40h, II t½ 60h; full effect 3–5 days. Monitoring: INR (target therapeutic range: 2.0–3.0 most indications, 2.5–3.5 mechanical mitral valve). Dosing: Genetic (CYP2C9 2/3 poor metaboliser, VKORC1 -1639G>A low expression), age, weight, diet, interacting drugs. Reversal: Vitamin K1 (phytomenadione) 1–10 mg IV/PO (IV: faster, anaphylaxis risk <0.1%); PCC (4-factor) for urgent; FFP if PCC unavailable. Interactions:

  • ↑INR: Antibiotics (↓gut flora K2), amiodarone, metronidazole, TMP-SMX azoles, PPIs, cranberry, NSAIDs
  • ↓INR: Enzyme inducers (rifampicin, carbamazepine, phenytoin, barbiturates, St John's wort), vitamin K-rich foods
Section 4: Vitamin K Deficiency

Adults (rare):

  • Malabsorption: Coeliac, Crohn's, CF, biliary obstruction, short bowel, bariatric
  • Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum (3rd gen cephalosporins, carbapenems) → ↓gut flora MK synthesis
  • Warfarin: Intentional iatrogenic deficiency
  • TPN without vitamin K: Weeks without supplementation
  • Severe malnutrition

Neonates (HDN):

  • Physiological: Low placental transfer, sterile gut, low breast milk K1 (1–4 µg/L vs formula 50–100 µg/L)
  • Classical HDN: Day 1–7; bleeding (umbilical, GI, circumcision, intracranial)
  • Late HDN: 2–12 weeks; exclusively breastfed; cholestasis, malabsorption, antibiotics; intracranial haemorrhage 30–50%
  • Prevention: 1 mg phytomenadione IM at birth (universal); oral alternative: 2 mg at birth, 2 mg day 4–7, 2 mg week 4 (less reliable)

Clinical features: Bleeding (ecchymosis, haematuria, GI bleed, intracranial), ↑PT/INR (early), ↑aPTT (late), normal platelet count, normal fibrinogen, ↑PIVKA-II.

Section 6: Bone & Vascular Metabolism

Osteocalcin (BGP): Osteoblast product; 3 Gla residues; γ-carboxylation → binds hydroxyapatite (Ca²⁺); serum osteocalcin = bone formation marker; uncarboxylated osteocalcin (ucOC) ↑ with vit K deficiency; total osteocalcin reflects turnover. Matrix Gla Protein (MGP): Vascular smooth muscle cells, chondrocytes; 5 Gla; potent inhibitor of vascular calcification (binds Ca-phosphate crystals, inhibits BMP-2); MGP-knockout mice → severe arterial calcification, death by 6w. Vitamin K & Bone Health: Observational: low K intake → ↑fracture risk, ↓BMD; RCTs: K1 1–5 mg/day or MK-4 45 mg/day (Japan) → ↓fracture (hip/vertebral); MK-7 (natto) 180 µg/day → ↓ucOC, ↑BMD. Vascular Calcification: MGP requires γ-carboxylation to inhibit calcification; warfarin → ↓MGP carboxylation → ↑vascular calcification (animal models; human observational). MK-7 vs K1: MK-7 (natto) longer half-life, better extrahepatic distribution (bone, vessel), more potent at carboxylating osteocalcin/MGP at lower doses.

7 Mnemonics
  • K forms: K1 = PLANTS (phylloquinone), K2 = BACTERIA/ANIMALS (menaquinones MK-n), K3 = SYNTHETIC TOXIC (menadione)
  • γ-Carboxylase: GGCX = Glu → Gla (needs CO₂, O₂, KH₂); produces KO (epoxide)
  • VKORC1: RECYCLES KO → KH₂; WARFARIN INHIBITS VKORC1
  • Coagulation factors: 1972 = 10 Gla (X), 9 Gla (VII), 7 (IX), 2 (II) — wait: II=10, VII=10, IX=12, X=11 → Ten-Ten-Twelve-Eleven
  • Anticoagulant proteins: C & S = Coagulation inhibition (Protein C & S + Protein Z)
  • Factor half-lives: VII SIX TEN TWO = 7, 6, 24, 40, 60 (VII 6h, IX 24h, X 40h, II 60h) — shortest VII
  • HDN: CLASSICAL = Day 1–7; LATE = 2–12 weeks (breastfed + cholestasis) → ICH
  • PT first: PT = Prothrombin (factor VII) Test = extrinsic; Shortest t½
  • Warfarin reversal: PCC = Immediate (factors); K1 = Sustained (days); Bleeding = BOTH
  • Bone: Osteocalcin = Bone formation marker; ucOC = uncarboxylated = vit K status
  • MGP: Matrix Gla Protein = Major Guard against Pathologic calcification
  • Superwarfarin: BRODIFACOUM = Big dose K1, Really long (weeks-months), Oral/IV, Daily, INR monitor, For Antidote, Continue, On and Until normal, Months

14 Exam Answer Modes
Long Answer / Essay (20 min)

Topic: "Vitamin K: biochemistry, deficiency states, warfarin interaction, and bone/vascular roles"

  • Forms: K1 (phylloquinone, plants), K2 (menaquinones MK-n, bacteria/animal), K3 (menadione, toxic)
  • γ-Glutamyl carboxylase: Glu → Gla (Ca²⁺ binding); requires KH₂, CO₂, O₂; produces KO
  • Vitamin K cycle: KH₂ → GGCX (Glu→Gla) → KO → VKORC1 (KO→KH₂) — warfarin inhibits VKORC1
  • Targets: Coagulation (II, VII, IX, X), Anticoagulation (C, S, Z), Bone (Osteocalcin 3 Gla, MGP 5 Gla)
  • Warfarin: inhibits VKORC1 → ↓KH₂ → ↓γ-carboxylation; onset delayed by factor half-lives (VII 6h, IX 24h, X 40h, II 60h)
  • Deficiency: Adults (malabsorption, antibiotics, warfarin); Neonates HDN (classical 1–7d, late 2–12w breastfed/cholestasis → ICH)
  • Prophylaxis: 1 mg phytomenadione IM at birth; Treatment: 1 mg IV/PO day 1, 2, 14
  • Warfarin reversal: Bleed = PCC + K1 5–10 mg IV; Minor = K1 1–2 mg PO
  • Bone: Osteocalcin (formation), ucOC (vit K status), MGP (vascular calcification inhibitor)
  • MK-7: longer half-life, extrahepatic distribution, better osteocalcin/MGP carboxylation
Short Note (7 min)

Topic: "Haemorrhagic Disease of the Newborn (HDN)"

  • Physiology: Low placental transfer, sterile gut, low breast milk K1 (1–4 µg/L)
  • Classical HDN: Day 1–7; bleeding (umbilical, GI, circumcision, intracranial); prevented by IM prophylaxis
  • Late HDN: 2–12 weeks; exclusively breastfed + cholestasis/malabsorption/antibiotics; ICH 30–50% mortality
  • Prophylaxis: 1 mg phytomenadione IM at birth (universal); Oral alternative: 2 mg birth, day 4–7, week 4 (less reliable)
  • Treatment: 1 mg phytomenadione IV/PO; repeat if INR not corrected; FFP/PCC if life-threatening bleeding
Viva Answer (3 min)

Q: "Why does PT/INR rise before aPTT in vitamin K deficiency?" "A: Factor VII has the shortest half-life (6 hours) among vitamin K-dependent factors. PT tests the extrinsic pathway (factor VII). aPTT tests intrinsic pathway (factors VIII, IX, XI, XII) — factor IX t½ 24h, factor X t½ 40h, factor II t½ 60h. Therefore PT/INR prolongs within 12–24h of warfarin/deficiency, aPTT only later when factors IX, X, II fall."
