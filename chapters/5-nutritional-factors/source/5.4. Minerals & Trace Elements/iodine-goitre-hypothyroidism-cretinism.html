2 Definitions / Key Concepts
TermDefinition
------------------
**Iodine (I)**Trace element; required for thyroid hormone; 70–80% in thyroid gland
**T4 (Thyroxine, Tetra-iodothyronine)**4 iodines; major thyroid hormone; inactive pro-hormone (converted to T3 in periphery)
**T3 (Tri-iodothyronine)**3 iodines; biologically active thyroid hormone; 20% thyroid, 80% peripheral conversion
**rT3 (Reverse T3)**Inactive metabolite of T4; 5'-deiodinase → 5-deiodinase
**Thyroid Peroxidase (TPO)**I⁻ oxidation → iodination of tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin + MIT/DIT coupling
**Sodium-Iodide Symporter (NIS, SLC5A5)**Basolateral thyroid follicular cell; I⁻ uptake (against gradient, trapped); also gastric mucosa, salivary, mammary, choroid plexus
**Pendrin (SLC26A4)**Apical thyroid iodide efflux into colloid
**Thyroglobulin (Tg)**Matrix for thyroid hormone synthesis; storage; TPO substrate; serum marker
**Wolff-Chaikoff Effect**Excess I⁻ → transient ↓thyroid hormone synthesis (organification block) — escape in normal, sustained in autoimmune
**Jod-Basedow Phenomenon**Excess I⁻ in iodine-deficient subject → hyperthyroidism (toxic multinodular, Graves)
**Iodine-Induced Goitre**Chronic I excess → inhibits TPO, ↓organification; neonates (maternal excess)
**Endemic Goitre**Goitre prevalence >5% in school-age children; environmental I deficiency
**Cretinism**Severe foetal I deficiency; 2 types (neurological, myxoedematous)
**Neurological Cretinism**Spastic diplegia, squint, deaf-mutism, mental retardation, gait disorder; partially I-responsive
**Myxoedematous Cretinism**Severe growth retardation, dwarfism, hypothyroidism, myxoedema; responds to I + thyroxine
**Iodisation Programme**Universal salt iodisation (USI) — 88% global households (2020 WHO)
**Urinary Iodine Concentration (UIC)**Population marker of I status; schoolchildren: <100 µg/L = deficient; <20 = severe
**Amiodarone**37% I by weight; T3/T4 effects (hyper/hypo); type 1 (I-induced), type 2 (destructive thyroiditis)
**Lithium**Inhibits thyroid hormone release; goitre, hypothyroidism
3 Core Content
Section 1: Iodine Metabolism

Sources: Seafood, seaweed (kelp, kombu), dairy (cattle feed supplemented), eggs, iodised salt; content variable (mmol/g sea fish, mg/kg seaweed). RDA:

  • Adult: 150 µg/day
  • Pregnancy: 200 µg/day (↑demand; first-trimester critical for foetal brain)
  • Lactation: 200 µg/day
  • Children 0–6m: 110 µg; 7–12m: 130 µg; 1–8y: 90 µg
  • Smokers: no specific ↑; goitrogens (thiocyanate in tobacco) ↓I uptake

Absorption: Stomach, jejunum; >90% absorbed as I⁻; NIS (SLC5A5) in thyroid basal cell traps I⁻ (20–40× plasma concentration); also gastric, salivary, mammary, choroid plexus, placenta. Renal: Filtered + reabsorbed; >90% excreted in urine (24h); UIC = population marker. Faecal: <10% (enterohepatic recycling). Sweat: Tropical climates ↑ loss (relevant for athletes/endemic areas).

Section 2: Thyroid Hormone Synthesis
  1. I⁻ trapping: NIS (basal) → I⁻ concentrated in thyroid (20–40×)
  2. Efflux to colloid: Pendrin (apical) → I⁻ into colloid
  3. Oxidation: TPO + H₂O₂ → I⁻ → I⁰ (highly reactive) + I⁺
  4. Organification: I⁺ → tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin (Tg) → MIT (mono-iodotyrosine) + DIT (di-iodotyrosine)
  5. Coupling: MIT + DIT = T3; DIT + DIT = T4; TPO catalysed
  6. Storage: Tg-T3/T4 complex stored in colloid (3 months' supply)
  7. Release: TSH stimulates endocytosis → lysosomal proteolysis → T3, T4, MIT, DIT release; I⁻ recycled (dehalogenase)

Peripheral conversion: T4 → T3 (5'-deiodinase type 1, liver/kidney; type 2, brain/pituitary); rT3 (5-deiodinase); also generates T2, T1.

Section 3: Iodine Deficiency Spectrum (IDD)
PopulationIodine Deficiency Effects
-------------------------------------
**Foetus**Spontaneous abortion, stillbirth, congenital anomalies, **cretinism** (neurological/myxoedematous), congenital hypothyroidism, perinatal mortality
**Newborn**Neonatal goitre, neonatal hypothyroidism, mental retardation, deafness
**Child**Endemic goitre, juvenile hypothyroidism, growth retardation, impaired cognition, ↓school performance
**Adult**Goitre, hypothyroidism, myxoedema, impaired fertility, nodular thyroid disease
**Pregnancy**Maternal hypothyroidism, pre-eclampsia, placental abruption, postpartum thyroiditis

Cretinism (2 types):

  1. Neurological (Type 1): Severe mental retardation, deaf-mutism, spastic quadriplegia/diplegia, squint, gait disorder; normal stature; partially I-responsive (in-utero damage); associated with endemic goitre areas.
  2. Myxoedematous (Type 2): Severe growth retardation (dwarfism), severe hypothyroidism, myxoedema, dry skin, delayed bone age; responds to I + thyroxine.
Section 4: Endemic Goitre & Iodine Deficiency

Endemic goitre: Goitre prevalence >5% in school-age children; ~2 billion at risk globally; mountainous areas (Himalayas, Andes, Alps), flood plains, glaciated areas (I leached from soil). Clinical features: Goitre (visible/palpable; firm, irregular, may be nodular in chronic); hypothyroidism (fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, bradycardia, dry skin, myxoedema, menorrhagia); intellectual impairment in children; cretinism.

Diagnosis: ↓TSH (subclinical hypo), ↓FT4, ↑Thyroglobulin (best population marker); UIC; thyroid ultrasound volume; ± anti-TPO/anti-Tg for autoimmune co-morbidity.

Section 5: Iodine Excess & Drug-Induced

Sources of excess: Amiodarone (37% I), iodinated contrast, kelp supplements, Lugol's solution, seaweed. Wolff-Chaikoff Effect: Excess I⁻ → transient inhibition of organification (TPO block); escape within 24–48h (normal thyroid); failure in autoimmune → sustained hypothyroidism. Iodine-Induced Goitre (Maternal/Neonatal): Maternal excess → foetal goitre (I crosses placenta); neonatal hypothyroidism; treat with levothyroxine, not I. Iod-Basedow (Hyperthyroidism): I excess in I-deficient subject with autonomy (multinodular, Graves) → hyperthyroidism; transient; treat with β-blocker, antithyroid if severe. Amiodarone Thyroid Effects:

  • Type 1 (Iodine-induced): Underlying thyroid abnormality (goitre, nodule) + excess I; treat with antithyroid drugs
  • Type 2 (Destructive thyroiditis): Direct cytotoxicity; treat with prednisolone
Section 6: Drug-Induced Goitre & Hypothyroidism
DrugMechanism
-----------------
**Lithium**Inhibits TPO, blocks T3/T4 release; **most common drug-induced hypothyroid**
**Amiodarone**37% I + TPO inhibitor; type 1/2 above
**Antithyroid drugs (PTU, MMI, carbimazole)**TPO inhibition
**Iodinated contrast**I excess → Wolff-Chaikoff
**Sulfonamides**TPO inhibition
**Ethionamide, PAS (TB drugs)**Goitrogenic
**Soybean/isoflavones (infant formula)**Goitrogenic in I deficiency
**Perchlorate, thiocyanate**NIS inhibitors

Goitrogens in food: Cabbage, kale, broccoli, cassava (thiocyanate); industrial pollutants; selenium deficiency (worsens I deficiency).

Section 7: Iodine Excess — Pathology

Acute I toxicity: Rare; chemical burns (topical), iodism (chronic I excess → salivation, lacrimation, bristling hair, headache, skin rash). Chronic I excess: Hypothyroidism (Wolff-Chaikoff), goitre, hyperthyroidism (Jod-Basedow in I-deficient), thyroiditis.

Section 8: Treatment & Prevention

Universal Salt Iodisation (USI):

  • 20–40 mg I per kg salt (15–30 ppm in tropical; 30–80 ppm in temperate)
  • 88% global households (2020 WHO target ≥90%)
  • Cost-effective (~0.05 USD/person/year)

Other interventions:

  • Iodised oil (lipiodol): IM/oral; 0.5–1 mL; long-acting (2–5 years); for remote areas
  • Iodine tablets: Pregnancy; potassium iodide 100–200 µg/day
  • Lugol's solution: Pre-operative thyroid (Plummer's); Graves; lactation inhibition (historical)
  • Levothyroxine: For hypothyroidism (replacement, not I repletion per se)

Radiation protection (Nuclear): KI (potassium iodide) 130 mg/day for adults; blocks thyroid uptake of radioactive I-131 (>95% protection if given 1–2h pre-exposure); WHO stockpile for nuclear accidents.

Monitoring: School-age children UIC (≥100 µg/L adequate); urinary iodine + thyroid volume (B-mode US) every 5 years; thyroglobulin (population).

Short Note (7 min)

Topic: "Iodine Deficiency in Pregnancy"

  • Maternal I requirement: 200 µg/day (vs 150 µg adult); first-trimester critical for foetal brain development
  • Foetal I deficiency consequences: Cretinism (neurological, myxoedematous), congenital hypothyroidism, stillbirth, spontaneous abortion, pre-eclampsia
  • Prenatal supplementation: 150 µg iodine + dietary (dairy, eggs, seafood); ensure prenatal vitamin contains iodine
  • Hypothyroid in pregnancy: Levothyroxine (replacement; not just I); TSH target <2.5 mU/L (1st trimester), <3.0 (2nd/3rd)

  6. B — Myxoedematous cretinism: severe growth retardation (dwarfism), hypothyroidism, myxoedema; responds to I + thyroxine.
  7. B — Wolff-Chaikoff: excess I⁻ → transient TPO/organification block → hypothyroidism; escape in normal thyroid.
  8. B — Jod-Basedow: excess I⁻ in I-deficient subject with autonomous nodule → hyperthyroidism.
  9. C — Amiodarone 37% iodine by weight → type 1 (I-induced hyperthyroid) or type 2 (destructive thyroiditis).
  10. C — KI 130 mg adult dose for radiation protection; most effective within 1–2 hours of exposure.
SBAs
  1. C — Pregnancy I requirement 200 µg/day (vs 150 adult); first-trimester critical for foetal brain; ensure prenatal vitamin contains I.
  2. B — Type 1 amiodarone thyrotoxicosis: I-induced (pre-existing autonomous thyroid); treat with antithyroid drugs (PTU/MMI); consider stopping amiodarone.
  3. B — Lithium-induced hypothyroidism: continue lithium (mood control), add levothyroxine replacement; monitor TFT every 3 months.
  4. B — Neurological cretinism: I-deficient area + spastic diplegia + deaf-mutism; partially I-responsive.
  5. B — Jod-Basedow: I supplementation in I-deficient subject with autonomous nodule → hyperthyroidism; treat symptomatically + antithyroid.
