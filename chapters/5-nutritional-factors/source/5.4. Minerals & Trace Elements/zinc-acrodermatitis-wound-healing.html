------------------

3 Core Content
Section 1: Zinc Metabolism

Body content: 2–3 g in adult; 60% muscle, 30% bone, 0.1% plasma; liver, kidney, brain, retina also rich. Absorption: Duodenum, proximal jejunum; ZIP4 (SLC39A4) apical import; passive diffusion at high dose; ZnT1 basolateral export; MT sequesters in enterocyte. Regulation: ZIP4 expression ↑in Zn deficiency (adaptive); MT binds Zn (prevents overshoot); paracellular (tight junctions) at high dose. Enhancers: Animal protein (muscle, fish, eggs), histidine, cysteine. Inhibitors: Phytates (whole grains, legumes), oxalates, polyphenols, Fe (competes), Cu (competes), Ca (dairy), oral contraceptives. Plasma: Bound to albumin 60%, α2-macroglobulin 30%, transferrin 10%; 0.1% free; plasma Zn poorly reflects tissue stores. Excretion: Pancreatic (Zn in pancreatic juice) 30%; intestinal (desquamation); sweat (minor); renal 10% (filtered + reabsorbed). Tissue turnover: Slow; body pool replaced every 1–2 weeks.

Section 2: Functions
  1. Structural: >300 zinc finger proteins (DNA/RNA binding, transcription factors); steroid receptors (oestrogen, androgen, glucocorticoid); receptor tyrosine kinases; GPCRs; cytoskeletal (tubulin).
  2. Catalytic: >300 Zn metalloenzymes:
  • Alcohol dehydrogenase (Zn + NAD⁺)
  • Carbonic anhydrase (CO₂ + H₂O ↔ H₂CO₃; Zn + His)
  • Alkaline phosphatase (Zn + Mg²⁺)
  • Carboxypeptidases (pancreatic)
  • RNA polymerase (DNA transcription)
  • Reverse transcriptase (HIV)
  • Matrix metalloproteinases (collagen degradation)
  • Cu/Zn-SOD (SOD1, antioxidant)
  1. Regulatory: Transcription factors, immune (T-cell development, cytokine production), signal transduction, wound healing (collagen synthesis, epithelial cell migration), growth/development, fertility/testosterone, antioxidant, apoptosis.
Section 3: Requirements & Sources

RDA (IOM 2001):

  • Adult male: 11 mg/day (was 15 mg)
  • Adult female: 8 mg/day
  • Pregnancy: 11 mg/day (UK 7; some 12–15)
  • Lactation: 12 mg/day
  • Children: 2–8 mg by age
  • Smokers/alcohol: +20–30%

UL: 40 mg/day (acute toxicity); chronic high → Cu deficiency Sources (mg/100 g):

  • Oysters 78, Beef 12, Pumpkin seeds 10, Cashews 5, Chickpeas 2, Lentils 1.5
  • Animal protein > plant (phytate binding reduces bioavailability)
Section 4: Acrodermatitis Enteropathica (AE)

Genetics: AR; SLC39A4 (ZIP4) mutation on chromosome 8q24; loss of ZIP4 function → ↓Zn absorption; presents after weaning (breast milk Zn ligand compensates partially). Classic Triad:

  1. Dermatitis: Perioral, perianal, perineal, acral, hands, feet; erythematous, scaly, vesiculobullous, pustular, eczematoid; alopecia, nail dystrophy, paronychia
  2. Diarrhoea: Chronic, watery; malabsorption
  3. Alopecia: Total/partial
  4. Mood changes: Apathy, irritability, depression

Other: Hypogonadism, growth retardation, immune dysfunction (candidiasis, recurrent infections), ophthalmic (blepharitis, conjunctivitis, photophobia), taste/smell loss. Biochemistry: ↓Plasma Zn <70 µg/dL; ↓Alkaline phosphatase (Zn-dependent); low sweat Zn. Treatment: Zn supplementation 3 mg/kg/day elemental (lifelong); clinical response in 5–10 days; monitor plasma Zn 3-monthly.

Section 5: Acquired Zinc Deficiency
CategoryExamples
--------------------
**Malabsorption**Coeliac, Crohn's, short bowel, bariatric (RYGB), CF, biliary atresia
**Chronic disease**Liver cirrhosis (↑renal Zn loss), CKD/dialysis, DM (hyperzincuria)
**Drugs****Penicillamine** (chelation), diuretics, ACE inhibitors, antimetabolites, OCP
**↑ Demand**Pregnancy, lactation, premature infants, growth, burns, surgery, sepsis
**Diet**Vegan, alcoholism, elderly ("tea-and-toast"), anorexia, formula without Zn
**Genetic**AE (ZIP4), acrodermatitis enteropathica, MTF1 mutations (rare)
**Hepatic**Acrodermatitis dysseborrhoica in liver disease (empirical Zn)

Clinical features (acquired):

  • Skin: Perioral/perianal dermatitis, eczema, hyperkeratosis, seborrhoeic rash; similar to AE
  • Hair: Alopecia, brittle hair
  • Nails: Beau's lines, paronychia, onychodystrophy
  • GI: Diarrhoea, anorexia, taste abnormalities
  • Neuro: Depression, irritability, cognitive decline, taste (dysgeusia) and smell (anosmia) loss
  • Immune: ↓T-cell function, lymphopenia, recurrent infections (skin, respiratory, GI)
  • Reproductive: Hypogonadism, ↓spermatogenesis, ↓testosterone, infertility
  • Wound healing: Delayed; collagen synthesis impairment
  • Eye: Night blindness (Zn in retinol dehydrogenase), photophobia
Section 6: Zinc in Wilson's Disease

Mechanism: Zn induces metallothionein (MT) in enterocyte; MT has high affinity for Cu; Cu bound to MT is shed into faeces with enterocyte desquamation; ↓Cu absorption (1 mEq/day reduction); mild chelation effect on body Cu. Indications: Maintenance (chelators first line for acute neurological WD), pregnancy, presymptomatic, children; CHELATE trial (2024): Zn non-inferior to penicillamine for neurological WD. Dose: Adults 50 mg PO ×3/day; children (5–15y) 25 mg ×3/day; empty stomach (avoid food/iron/calcium reducing absorption). Time to effect: 6–8 weeks; titrate to 24h urinary Cu <100 µg/day, non-ceruloplasmin Cu <25 µg/dL. Side effects: GI (nausea, abdominal pain); monitor LFT, Zn levels; rare Cu deficiency with chronic use. Vs Penicillamine/Trientine: Zn only weakly chelates body Cu; not for active neurological disease; better for maintenance/pregnancy/paediatrics.

Section 8: Zinc Excess (Toxicity)

Acute: Nausea, vomiting, GI distress, headache, lethargy (single dose 1–2 g); Zn from contaminated food/water (galvanised containers, high-zinc denture cream). Chronic (industrial, ZnO fumes): Metal fume fever (acute, self-limiting, 12–48h), copper deficiency, ↓immune function, ↓HDL, sideroblastic anaemia (Cu depletes). Drug interactions: Penicillamine, EDTA (chelate Zn); quinolones/tetracyclines ↓absorption (separate by 2h); PPIs ↓Zn absorption (long-term). High-dose supplement risks: >100 mg/day → GI upset, Cu deficiency, immune impairment.

Section 9: Special Populations & Clinical Scenarios

Pregnancy/lactation: ↑Demand; Zn deficiency → pre-eclampsia, IUGR, preterm birth, neurodevelopmental delay; supplementation 15–30 mg/day; IZiNCG recommends 15 mg in pregnancy. Elderly: Poor diet, polypharmacy (PPIs, diuretics), chronic disease → marginal status; supplementation 15–30 mg/day may improve immune function. HIV: Zn deficiency common; supplementation may slow disease progression but excess → viral replication (theoretical). Malaria: Zn supplementation reduces severity in children (P. falciparum); WHO recommends in endemic areas. Pneumonia: Zn deficiency → ↑susceptibility, severity; supplementation reduces incidence in children.
