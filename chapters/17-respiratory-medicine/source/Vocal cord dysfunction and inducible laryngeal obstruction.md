# Vocal cord dysfunction (VCD) and inducible laryngeal obstruction

> [!important]
> **Vocal cord dysfunction (VCD)** = **paradoxical adduction of vocal cords** during inspiration, mimicking asthma. Common in young women, athletes, with psychiatric comorbidity. Diagnosed by **laryngoscopy** (adduction during inspiration). Treatment: **speech therapy + reassurance** — NOT asthma medications.

Related: [[Asthma]], [[Upper airway obstruction and stridor]], [[Foreign body aspiration in adults]], [[Wheeze and stridor approach]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: **VCD mimics asthma** but is **inspiratory stridor**, **flat inspiratory limb on flow-volume loop**, **laryngoscopy shows paradoxical adduction**. **Treatment: speech therapy, NOT bronchodilators/steroids**. Often misdiagnosed as refractory asthma.

## Definition

**Vocal cord dysfunction (VCD)** = inappropriate adduction (closing) of the vocal cords during inspiration, causing **functional upper airway obstruction**.

### Other terms
- **Inducible laryngeal obstruction (ILO)** (preferred)
- **Paradoxical vocal fold motion (PVFM)**
- **Functional upper airway obstruction**
- **"Pseudo-asthma"**

## Aetiology

### Triggers
| Trigger | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| **Exercise** | Athletes, high-intensity |
| **Stress, anxiety** | Major role |
| **GERD** | Laryngeal irritation |
| **Post-nasal drip** | Laryngeal irritation |
| **Irritants** (perfume, smoke, chemicals) | Laryngeal hyperresponsiveness |
| **Infection** (URTI) | Laryngeal inflammation |
| **Post-intubation** | Laryngeal irritation |
| **Neurological** (brainstem, vagus) | Laryngeal dysfunction |
| **Idiopathic** | Often with psychiatric comorbidity |

### Demographics
- **Young women** (most common)
- **Athletes** (high performance)
- **Healthcare workers** (occupational exposure)
- **Psychiatric comorbidity** (anxiety, PTSD, depression)
- Often with **asthma** (50% have both — diagnostic confusion)

## Pathophysiology

### Mechanism
- **Vocal cords** normally abduct (open) during inspiration
- In VCD, **paradoxical adduction** (closing) during inspiration
- Causes **extrathoracic variable obstruction**
- Triggered by laryngeal irritants, stress, exercise

### Contributing factors
- **Laryngeal hyperresponsiveness**
- **Visceral hyperresponsiveness** (functional)
- **Cortical hyperresponsiveness** (stress-induced)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Dyspnoea** (sudden, episodic)
- **Wheeze / stridor** (inspiratory)
- **Throat tightness**
- **Voice change** (hoarseness, aphonia)
- **Cough**
- **Nocturnal symptoms** (often better, unlike asthma)
- **Trigger-related** (exercise, stress, irritants)
- **Anxiety** (often)

### Signs
- **Inspiratory stridor** (loudest over neck)
- **Throat clearing**
- **Monomorphic wheeze** (often over neck/larynx)
- **Normal chest exam** between episodes
- **No response to bronchodilators**

## Diagnosis

### Investigations
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| **Spirometry (flow-volume loop)** | **Flattening of inspiratory limb** (variable extrathoracic obstruction) |
| **Laryngoscopy** (direct or fibreoptic) | **Paradoxical adduction of vocal cords during inspiration** (gold standard) |
| **Asthma tests** (spirometry, BD reversibility, FeNO) | **Normal** (or mild asthma concurrent) |
| **Methacholine challenge** | Often negative (or mild) |
| **ABG** | Normal (between episodes) |
| **CXR** | Normal (no parenchymal disease) |

### Diagnostic criteria (ERS/ATS)
- **Symptoms** of upper airway obstruction (dyspnoea, stridor, wheeze)
- **Variable extrathoracic obstruction** on flow-volume loop
- **Laryngoscopy** showing paradoxical vocal cord adduction

## Management

### Speech therapy
- **First-line** (and most effective)
- **Respiratory retraining** (breathing exercises)
- **Throat relaxation techniques**
- **Avoid triggers**
- **PVFM techniques** (sniff, pursed-lip breathing)

### Reassurance
- **Explain functional nature**
- **Avoid unnecessary medications**
- **Address anxiety/psychiatric comorbidity**

### Treat triggers
- **GERD**: PPI
- **Post-nasal drip**: INCS, antihistamines
- **Anxiety**: CBT, relaxation
- **Asthma** (concurrent): standard asthma Rx

### Pharmacological
- **Heliox** (helium-oxygen mixture, reduces turbulence)
- **Topical anaesthetics** (rarely)
- **Botulinum toxin** (severe, refractory)
- **Tracheostomy** (life-threatening, last resort)

### Avoid
- **High-dose ICS** (no benefit)
- **Multiple courses of oral steroids** (no benefit; harm)
- **Unnecessary intubation** (often wakes up with normal cords)

## MCQs (10)

1. VCD is best diagnosed by:
   A. **Laryngoscopy (paradoxical vocal cord adduction)**
   B. Spirogram
   C. CXR
   D. None
   E. ABG
   **Answer: A** — Laryngoscopy.

2. The flow-volume loop in VCD shows:
   A. **Flattening of inspiratory limb (variable extrathoracic obstruction)**
   B. Normal
   C. Restrictive
   D. None
   E. Both limbs
   **Answer: A** — Inspiratory limb.

3. The first-line treatment of VCD is:
   A. Inhaled steroids
   B. **Speech therapy + reassurance**
   C. Salbutamol
   D. Antibiotics
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Speech therapy.

4. VCD is most common in:
   A. **Young women (with psychiatric comorbidity)**
   B. Elderly men
   C. Children
   D. None
   E. All
   **Answer: A** — Young women.

5. The most common trigger of VCD is:
   A. Cold
   B. **Stress, exercise, irritants**
   C. None
   D. Pollen
   E. Food
   **Answer: B** — Stress/exercise.

6. A VCD patient on high-dose steroids should:
   A. **Have steroids tapered (no benefit)**
   B. Continue
   C. Increase
   D. None
   E. Switch antibiotics
   **Answer: A** — Taper (no benefit).

7. Concurrent asthma in VCD occurs in approximately:
   A. 0%
   B. **50% (often confused)**
   C. 100%
   D. None
   E. 25%
   **Answer: B** — 50%.

8. The pathophysiology of VCD is:
   A. **Paradoxical vocal cord adduction during inspiration**
   B. Bronchospasm
   C. Mucus hypersecretion
   D. None
   E. Inflammation
   **Answer: A** — Paradoxical adduction.

9. Heliox in VCD works by:
   A. **Reducing turbulence (less dense, laminar flow)**
   B. Bronchodilation
   C. Steroid effect
   D. None
   E. Antibiotic
   **Answer: A** — Less turbulent.

10. A 25-year-old athlete has exercise-induced "wheeze" not responding to salbutamol. Diagnosis:
    A. Asthma
    B. **VCD (consider)**
    C. COPD
    D. None
    E. PE
    **Answer: B** — VCD.

## SBA Questions (10)

1. The most useful first test in suspected VCD:
   A. **Spirometry with flow-volume loop (inspiratory limb flattening)**
   B. CXR
   C. Echo
   D. None
   E. ABG
    **Answer: A** — Flow-volume loop.

2. The best treatment for refractory VCD:
   A. **Speech therapy +/− botulinum toxin (severe)**
   B. Steroids
   C. Antibiotics
   D. Wait
   E. None
    **Answer: A** — Speech +/− Botox.

3. The most common reason for misdiagnosis of VCD:
   A. Asthma
   B. **Refractory asthma (treated with high-dose steroids)**
   C. None
   D. COPD
   E. PE
    **Answer: B** — Refractory asthma.

4. A 30-year-old with episodic dyspnoea, stridor, normal exam between. Most likely:
   A. Asthma
   B. **VCD**
   C. COPD
   D. None
   E. Pneumonia
    **Answer: B** — VCD.

5. The most useful treatment for GERD-related VCD:
   A. Steroids
   B. **PPI + speech therapy**
   C. Antibiotics
   D. Wait
   E. None
    **Answer: B** — PPI + speech therapy.

6. The most common vocal cord movement in VCD:
   A. Normal abduction on inspiration
   B. **Paradoxical adduction on inspiration**
   C. Normal adduction
   D. None
   E. Variable
    **Answer: B** — Paradoxical adduction.

7. VCD symptoms worse with:
   A. Sleep
   B. **Exercise, stress, irritants**
   C. Rest
   D. None
   E. Cold
    **Answer: B** — Exercise, stress, irritants.

8. The role of intubation in severe VCD:
   A. **Often wakes up normal (avoid if possible)**
   B. Always
   C. Never
   D. None
   E. Routine
    **Answer: A** — Often wakes normal.

9. The most common psychiatric comorbidity in VCD:
   A. Depression
   B. **Anxiety, PTSD**
   C. None
   D. Bipolar
   E. Psychosis
    **Answer: B** — Anxiety, PTSD.

10. The most useful differential from VCD:
    A. **Asthma (differentiable on flow-volume loop, laryngoscopy, response to Rx)**
    B. None
    C. PE
    D. Pneumonia
    E. None
    **Answer: A** — Asthma.
