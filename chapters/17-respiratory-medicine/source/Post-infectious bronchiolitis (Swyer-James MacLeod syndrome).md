# Post-Infectious Bronchiolitis (Swyer-James/MacLeod Syndrome)

Related: [[Bronchiolitis]], [[Bronchiectasis]], [[Airway Diseases/Bronchiolitis and obliterative bronchiolitis|Bronchiolitis and obliterative bronchiolitis]]

> [!important]
> **Post-infectious bronchiolitis** = chronic airway damage following severe viral/bacterial infection in childhood. **Swyer-James/MacLeod syndrome** = unilateral hyperlucent lung with bronchiectasis, classically post-adenovirus. **Key FCPS/MRCP**: unilateral hyperlucency on CXR, air trapping on expiration, history of severe childhood infection, differentiation from emphysema/PERT.

## Learning Objectives
- Recognise post-infectious bronchiolitis and Swyer-James syndrome
- Apply HRCT findings (unilateral hyperlucency, air trapping, bronchiectasis)
- Differentiate from emphysema, pneumothorax, pulmonary embolism
- Manage conservatively with targeted therapy for complications

## Definition
**Post-infectious bronchiolitis** = chronic airflow obstruction and parenchymal damage following severe lower respiratory tract infection in early childhood (often <2 years), leading to **bronchiolar obliteration, bronchiectasis, and air trapping**.

**Swyer-James/MacLeod Syndrome** = specific unilateral form, classically following **adenovirus** (types 3, 7, 21) or **mycoplasma** infection.

## Aetiology & Risk Factors
| Agent | Typical Age | Features |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Adenovirus** (types 3, 7, 21) | <2 years | Most common cause of Swyer-James; necrotising bronchiolitis |
| **Mycoplasma pneumoniae** | School age | Can cause unilateral or bilateral |
| **RSV** | Infancy | Usually bilateral; can cause severe bronchiolitis |
| **Measles** | Childhood | Giant cell pneumonia → bronchiolitis |
| **Influenza / Parainfluenza** | Variable | Less common |
| **Bacterial** (pertussis, TB) | Variable | Post-pertussis bronchiectasis |

## Pathophysiology
1. **Severe viral infection** → necrosis of bronchial epithelium
2. **Impaired healing** → fibrosis of bronchioles → **constrictive bronchiolitis**
3. **Loss of elastic recoil** → **air trapping**, hyperinflation
4. **Impaired clearance** → recurrent infection → **bronchiectasis**
5. **Vascular hypoplasia** → reduced perfusion → **unilateral hyperlucency** on CXR

## Clinical Features
| Feature | Swyer-James (Unilateral) | Bilateral Post-Infectious |
|---------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Onset** | Childhood infection history | Childhood infection history |
| **Symptoms** | Chronic cough, recurrent wheeze, recurrent pneumonia | Chronic cough, sputum, dyspnoea |
| **Examination** | Unilateral reduced air entry, hyperresonance | Bilateral crackles, wheeze |
| **Growth** | Normal (unlike CF) | May have failure to thrive |
| **Laterality** | **Unilateral** (classic) | Bilateral (often symmetrical) |

## Investigations

### Chest X-Ray (Classic Findings)
| Finding | Swyer-James (Unilateral) | Bilateral |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------|
| **Lucency** | **Marked unilateral hyperlucency** (hyperinflation) | Bilateral hyperinflation |
| **Vascularity** | **Reduced vascular markings** (oligemia) on affected side | Normal or reduced |
| **Bronchiectasis** | Tram-track/ring shadows on affected side | Bilateral tram-tracks |
| **Mediastinum** | Shifted **away** from affected side (hyperinflation) | Central |

### HRCT (Diagnostic Gold Standard)
| Finding | Significance |
|---------|--------------|
| **Unilateral hyperlucency** | Air trapping (air volume ↑) |
| **Expiratory air trapping** | Affected side remains lucent on expiration |
| **Bronchiectasis** | Tram-track, signet ring; often middle lobe/lingula |
| **Vascular hypoplasia** | Reduced vessel size/number on affected side |
| **Mosaic attenuation** | Patchy lucency (air trapping) |
| **Mediastinal shift** | Away from affected side (unilateral) |

### Pulmonary Function Tests
| Pattern | Findings |
|---------|----------|
| **Spirometry** | Obstructive (FEV₁/FVC <0.7), often moderate-severe |
| **Lung volumes** | ↑ RV, ↑ RV/TLC (air trapping) |
| **DLCO** | Usually **preserved** (unlike emphysema) |
| **Flow-volume loop** | Expiratory plateau (air trapping) |

### Ventilation-Perfusion Scan
- **Ventilation**: delayed washout on affected side (air trapping)
- **Perfusion**: reduced on affected side (hypoxic vasoconstriction + vascular hypoplasia)
- **Mismatch**: V/Q mismatch (ventilation > perfusion)

## Differential Diagnosis
| Condition | Differentiating Features |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Emphysema** | Bilateral, upper lobe, smoking history, DLCO ↓ |
| **Pneumothorax** | Acute onset, visceral pleural line, no bronchiectasis |
| **Pulmonary embolism** | Acute, wedge infarct, D-dimer ↑, CT angiography |
| **Congenital lobar emphysema** | Infant presentation, progressive hyperinflation, no bronchiectasis |
| **Swyer-James vs CF** | CF = bilateral, pancreatic insufficiency, sweat Cl⁻ ↑ |
| **Unilateral hyperlucency** | Also: pneumothorax, large bulla, compensatory hyperinflation, Pancoast tumour |

## Management
### Conservative (Mainstay)
- **Airway clearance**: daily physiotherapy, PEP devices, dornase alfa (off-label)
- **Bronchodilators**: LAMA/LABA for airflow limitation
- **Infection control**: prompt antibiotics for exacerbations (guided by culture)
- **Vaccinations**: pneumococcal, influenza, pertussis, COVID-19

### Specific Complications
| Complication | Management |
|--------------|------------|
| **Recurrent pneumonia** | Prompt antibiotics (guided by culture); consider prophylactic antibiotics if frequent |
| **Haemoptysis** | Bronchial artery embolisation (BAE) if massive; vitamin K, tranexamic acid |
| **Massive haemoptysis** | Emergency BAE; surgical resection if localised and recurrent |
| **Respiratory failure** | NIV/ventilation; tracheostomy if prolonged |
| **Cor pulmonale** | LTOT if hypoxaemic; diuretics |
| **Recurrent exacerbations** | Consider prophylactic macrolide (azithromycin 250mg 3x/week) |

### Surgery (Rare)
- **Lobar resection** (lobectomy) if **localised disease** (e.g., unilateral middle lobe) with recurrent life-threatening haemoptysis or intractable infection
- **Lung transplant** if end-stage bilateral disease (rare)

## Prognosis
- **Generally good** with conservative management
- **Life expectancy** near normal if unilateral, non-progressive
- **Progressive decline** if bilateral, recurrent infections, comorbid CTD/immunodeficiency
- **Quality of life** impacted by chronic cough, recurrent infections

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
1. **Swyer-James** = unilateral hyperlucent lung + bronchiectasis + air trapping, classically post-adenovirus
2. **CXR**: unilateral hyperlucency, reduced vascular markings, mediastinal shift **away** from affected side
3. **HRCT**: unilateral hyperlucency, **expiratory air trapping**, bronchiectasis, vascular hypoplasia
4. **Key discriminator**: **unilateral** (Swyer-James) vs bilateral (CF, bilateral post-infectious)
5. **DLCO preserved** (vs emphysema where DLCO ↓)
5. **Adenovirus** = classic cause of Swyer-James
6. **Management**: conservative (clearance, bronchodilators, prompt antibiotics)
6. **Surgery** only for localised disease with life-threatening haemoptysis/recurrent infection
7. **DLCO preserved** (vs emphysema = DLCO ↓)

## Common Confusions / Exam Traps
- **Swyer-James ≠ emphysema**: unilateral, DLCO preserved, bronchiectasis present
- **Swyer-James ≠ pneumothorax**: chronic, bronchial markings visible, no visceral pleural line
- **Swyer-James ≠ CF**: unilateral vs bilateral, no pancreatic insufficiency, sweat Cl⁻ normal
- **Compensatory hyperinflation** (e.g., post-lobectomy) ≠ Swyer-James (no prior surgery)
- **Vascular hypoplasia** = reduced vessels on affected side (not increased)
