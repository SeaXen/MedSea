# Foreign body aspiration in adults

> [!important]
> **Foreign body (FB) aspiration** in adults is often **subtle or missed**. Common FBs: **food (meat, bones), teeth, dental instruments**. Common in: elderly, neurological disease (stroke, dementia), alcohol intoxication, dental procedures. **Bronchoscopy** (rigid preferred) is diagnostic and therapeutic.

Related: [[Upper airway obstruction and stridor]], [[Post-obstructive pneumonia]], [[Aspiration pneumonia]], [[Pneumonia]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: **Sudden cough/choking + unilateral wheeze + localised crackles = foreign body until proven otherwise** → **rigid bronchoscopy**. **In elderly, neurological disease, alcoholics, dental procedures** = high suspicion. **Recurrent post-obstructive pneumonia** in same lobe = consider FB.

## Definition

**Foreign body (FB) aspiration** = inhalation of foreign material into the **airways** (larynx, trachea, bronchi).

## Aetiology

### Common objects
| Object | Notes |
|--------|-------|
| **Food** (meat, peanuts, bones, fish) | Most common in adults |
| **Teeth, dental restorations** | Dental procedures, trauma |
| **Dental instruments** | During procedures |
| **Coins** (children) | Rare in adults |
| **Bones** (chicken, fish) | Common |
| **Vomitus** (aspiration) | Common in alcoholics |
| **Pills** | Elderly |
| **Foreign bodies** (e.g. pins, teeth) | Trauma, drug smuggling |

### Risk factors
- **Elderly** (impaired swallow, dentition)
- **Neurological disease** (stroke, dementia, Parkinson's, MS, MG, GBS)
- **Alcohol intoxication** (↓consciousness, ↓gag reflex)
- **Sedation** (anaesthesia, drugs)
- **Dental procedures**
- **Tracheostomy / intubation**
- **Achalasia, oesophageal stricture**
- **Psychiatric** (pica, deliberate)

## Pathophysiology

### Distribution
- **Right main bronchus** more often (wider, more vertical) in adults
- Depends on position at aspiration (upright → lower lobes; supine → upper lobes)
- **Common locations**: right lower lobe, right middle lobe, left lower lobe

### Consequences
- **Airway obstruction** (partial/complete)
- **Post-obstructive pneumonia** (recurrent, same lobe)
- **Atelectasis** (distal collapse)
- **Lung abscess** (chronic FB)
- **Bronchiectasis** (chronic)
- **Pneumothorax, pneumomediastinum** (rare)
- **Death** (massive obstruction, complete laryngeal)

## Clinical Features

### Acute
- **Sudden cough, choking**
- **Stridor** (laryngeal)
- **Wheeze** (unilateral, focal)
- **Dyspnoea**
- **Cyanosis**
- **Loss of consciousness** (severe)
- **Cardiac arrest** (massive)

### Subacute / chronic
- **Persistent cough**
- **Recurrent pneumonia** (same lobe)
- **Haemoptysis**
- **Wheeze, stridor**
- **Lung abscess** (fever, foul sputum)
- **Non-resolving pneumonia**

### Signs
- **Focal wheeze** (monophonic, fixed)
- **↓Breath sounds** distally
- **Crackles** (post-obstructive)
- **Hyperresonance** (ball-valve effect)
- **Stridor** (laryngeal)

## Diagnosis

### Investigations
| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **CXR (PA + lateral, inspiration + expiration)** | Radiopaque FB, air-trapping, atelectasis, post-obstructive pneumonia |
| **CT chest** | **More sensitive** for radiolucent FB |
| **Bronchoscopy** | **Gold standard** — diagnostic + therapeutic |
| **Spirometry (flow-volume loop)** | Variable intrathoracic obstruction |
| **ABG** | Severity |

### CXR findings
- **Radiopaque FB**: direct visualisation
- **Radiolucent FB**: indirect signs
  - **Air-trapping** (hyperinflation distal to ball-valve)
  - **Atelectasis** (complete obstruction)
  - **Post-obstructive pneumonia** (consolidation)
  - **Mediastinal shift** (with atelectasis)
- **Normal CXR** does not exclude FB
- **Inspiratory + expiratory** films: look for air-trapping

## Management

### Emergency
- **A–B–C** assessment
- **O₂** (target SpO₂ 94–98%)
- **Heimlich manoeuvre** (complete laryngeal obstruction, conscious)
- **Back blows + abdominal thrusts** (choking)
- **Direct laryngoscopy + Magill forceps** (laryngeal FB)
- **Cricothyroidotomy/tracheostomy** (if intubation fails)
- **Heimlich** is contraindicated if patient can still cough/cough effectively (let them cough)

### Bronchoscopy
- **Rigid bronchoscopy** (gold standard for FB removal in adults)
  - Better airway control
  - Larger forceps
  - General anaesthesia
- **Flexible bronchoscopy** (alternative for small, peripheral FB)

### Supportive
- **O₂, ventilation** as needed
- **Antibiotics** if post-obstructive pneumonia (after removal)
- **Physiotherapy**
- **Bronchodilators** (wheeze)

### Prevention
- **Dental care** (especially elderly)
- **Avoid talking while eating** (supine)
- **Aspiration precautions** (stroke, dementia)
- **Choking first aid** education

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Domain | Key points |
|--------|------------|
| **Definition** | Inhaled foreign material |
| **Risk factors** | Elderly, neurological, alcohol, dental |
| **Distribution** | Right main bronchus more common |
| **Symptoms** | Sudden cough, choking, unilateral wheeze |
| **Best imaging** | CXR (inspiration + expiration) + CT |
| **Treatment** | **Rigid bronchoscopy** (gold standard) |
| **Emergency** | Heimlich + laryngoscopy + intubation/tracheostomy |
| **Recurrent pneumonia** | Same lobe = consider FB |

## MCQs (10)

1. The most common location of foreign body aspiration in adults:
   A. **Right main bronchus**
   B. Left main bronchus
   C. Trachea
   D. None
   E. Larynx
   **Answer: A** — Right (wider, more vertical).

2. The most common foreign body in adults:
   A. Teeth
   B. **Food (meat, bones, peanuts)**
   C. Coins
   D. Pins
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Food.

3. The most useful investigation for suspected FB:
   A. CXR
   B. **CT chest + bronchoscopy**
   C. Spirogram
   D. ABG
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — CT + bronchoscopy.

4. The first-line treatment of foreign body aspiration:
   A. Steroids
   B. **Bronchoscopy (rigid preferred)**
   C. Antibiotics
   D. Wait
    **Answer: B** — Rigid bronchoscopy.

5. CXR in FB may show:
   A. **Air-trapping + atelectasis + consolidation**
   B. Normal
   C. Mass
   D. None
   E. Cavitation
    **Answer: A** — Indirect signs.

6. A normal CXR excludes FB:
   A. Yes
   B. **No (radiolucent FB can be missed)**
   C. Always
   D. None
   E. Sometimes
    **Answer: B** — No.

7. The emergency treatment of complete laryngeal obstruction in conscious patient:
   A. Cricothyroidotomy
   B. **Heimlich + back blows**
   C. Intubation
   D. Wait
   E. None
    **Answer: B** — Heimlich.

8. The most common cause of recurrent lobar pneumonia:
   A. Asthma
   B. **Foreign body (or endobronchial tumour)**
   C. COPD
   D. None
   E. Pneumonia
    **Answer: B** — FB or tumour.

9. The risk factor most associated with FB aspiration in adults:
   A. Asthma
   B. **Elderly with neurological disease (stroke, dementia)**
   C. COPD
   D. None
   E. Pneumonia
    **Answer: B** — Elderly + neurological.

10. The flow-volume loop in FB (intrabronchial) shows:
    A. **Variable intrathoracic obstruction (flattened expiratory limb)**
    B. Normal
    C. Fixed
    D. None
    E. Variable extrathoracic
    **Answer: A** — Variable intrathoracic.

## SBA Questions (10)

1. A 70-year-old with dementia has sudden cough, unilateral wheeze, post-obstructive pneumonia. Most likely:
   A. Asthma
   B. **Foreign body aspiration**
   C. COPD
   D. None
   E. PE
    **Answer: B** — FB.

2. The best first investigation for suspected FB in adult:
   A. **CXR (inspiration + expiration) + CT**
   B. ABG
   C. Spirogram
   D. None
   E. Echo
    **Answer: A** — CXR + CT.

3. The treatment of choice for FB in adult:
   A. Wait
   B. **Rigid bronchoscopy under GA**
   C. Steroids
   D. None
   E. Antibiotics
    **Answer: B** — Rigid bronchoscopy.

4. A 60-year-old with sudden complete laryngeal obstruction. Emergency Rx:
   A. Wait
   B. **Heimlich +/− cricothyroidotomy if fails**
   C. Steroids
   D. None
   E. Antibiotics
    **Answer: B** — Heimlich.

5. The most common lung region for FB aspiration in upright adults:
   A. **Right lower lobe (posterior basal segment)**
   B. RUL
   C. LUL
   D. None
   E. Trachea
    **Answer: A** — RLL.

6. The most common lung region for FB in supine adults:
   A. RLL
   B. **RUL (posterior segment) / superior segment of lower lobes**
   C. LUL
   D. None
   E. Lingula
    **Answer: B** — Posterior upper / superior lower.

7. FB aspiration in a 50-year-old after dental procedure. Most likely:
   A. Bone
   B. **Tooth or dental instrument**
   C. Food
   D. None
   E. Coin
    **Answer: B** — Dental.

8. The most common cause of chronic lung abscess in adult:
   A. Pneumonia
   B. **Foreign body (anaerobic superinfection)**
   C. TB
   D. None
   E. PE
    **Answer: B** — FB (anaerobic).

9. The first step in management of stable FB aspiration:
   A. Bronchoscopy (urgent)
   B. **Bronchoscopy (semi-urgent, within 24 h)**
   C. Steroids
   D. Wait
   E. None
    **Answer: B** — Semi-urgent (24 h).

10. The most useful prevention of FB aspiration in elderly:
    A. Diet
    B. **Dental care + aspiration precautions + avoid alcohol**
    C. Exercise
    D. None
    E. Steroids
    **Answer: B** — Dental care + precautions.

FB aspiration in adults: often subtle. Risk factors: elderly, neurological, alcohol, dental. Most common object: food. Most common location: right main bronchus.
