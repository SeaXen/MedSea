# Trapped lung and fibrothorax

> [!important]
> **Trapped lung** occurs when a **visceral pleural peel** prevents lung re-expansion after pleural drainage. **Fibrothorax** is a **thickened visceral/parietal pleura** from chronic inflammation (TB, empyema, haemothorax). Both cause **restrictive physiology** and persistent pleural space. Treatment: **decortication** (surgical).

Related: [[Pleural Effusion]], [[Empyema and pleural infection]], [[Hemothorax]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: **Trapped lung** = visceral peel prevents re-expansion. **Fibrothorax** = thickened pleura (TB, empyema, haemothorax). Both cause persistent pleural space. Diagnosis: **CT chest** (thickened pleura) + **pleural manometry** (negative pressure on drainage). Treatment: **decortication** (surgical removal of peel).

## Definition

- **Trapped lung**: visceral pleural peel (fibrous) prevents lung re-expansion after drainage
- **Fibrothorax**: diffuse pleural thickening +/− calcification (chronic)
- Often overlap (trapped lung is one form of fibrothorax)

## Aetiology

| Cause | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| **Empyema** (chronic) | Most common cause of trapped lung |
| **Haemothorax** (organised) | Retained blood → peel |
| **Tuberculosis** | Chronic pleural disease |
| **Post-CABG** (coronary artery bypass) | Post-surgical |
| **Uraemic pleuritis** | CKD with pleural disease |
| **Connective tissue** (RA, SLE) | Chronic pleuritis |
| **Asbestos exposure** | Pleural plaques + diffuse thickening |

## Pathophysiology

### Mechanism
1. **Pleural inflammation** (acute)
2. **Fibrin deposition** (subacute)
3. **Fibroblast proliferation** (organising)
4. **Visceral/parietal pleural peel** (chronic)
5. **Constriction** → lung cannot re-expand

### Consequences
- **Restrictive physiology** (↓FVC, ↓TLC)
- **Persistent pleural space** (after drainage)
- **Recurrent effusion** (re-accumulation)
- **Dyspnoea** (progressive)
- **Infection risk** (retained fluid)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Dyspnoea** (progressive, on exertion)
- **Fatigue**
- **Exercise intolerance**
- **Pleuritic chest pain**
- **Recurrent infections** (if persistent effusion)

### Signs
- **Dullness to percussion**
- **↓Breath sounds**
- **↓Chest expansion** (affected side)
- **Scoliosis** (chronic, in children)
- **Clubbing** (chronic)

## Diagnosis

### Investigations
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| **CXR** | Persistent opacity, pleural thickening, ± calcification |
| **CT chest** | **Gold standard** — thickened pleura (visceral/parietal), trapped lung, calcification |
| **Pleural US** | Thickened pleura, septations, echogenic |
| **Pleural manometry** | **Negative pressure** on drainage (suggesting trapped lung) |
| **PFTs** | Restrictive: ↓FVC, ↓TLC, normal/↑FEV₁/FVC |
| **Pleural biopsy** | If unclear (TB, RA, malignancy) |

### Trapped lung diagnostic criteria
- **Pleural manometry**: initial pleural pressure <−20 cmH₂O (or becomes very negative with drainage)
- **No re-expansion** despite adequate drainage
- **CT**: visceral pleural thickening

## Management

### Conservative
- **Observation** (asymptomatic, minimal)
- **Chest drain** (if persistent effusion)
- **Indwelling pleural catheter** (recurrent effusion)
- **Pleurodesis** (if re-accumulation)
- **Pulmonary rehabilitation**

### Surgical
- **Decortication** (thoracotomy or VATS)
  - **Indication**: symptomatic trapped lung + persistent pleural space
  - **Removal of peel** → re-expansion
  - **Outcomes**: ↑FVC, ↑QoL
- **Decortication timing**: best after 3–6 months (peel mature)

### Specific
- **Empyema**: VATS decortication early (organised) or open
- **TB**: anti-TB + delayed decortication (active TB contraindicated acutely)
- **Malignancy**: pleurodesis +/− decortication (if good prognosis)

## MCQs (10)

1. The most common cause of trapped lung is:
   A. TB
   B. **Empyema (chronic, organised)**
   C. Pneumothorax
   D. None
   E. Asthma
   **Answer: B** — Empyema.

2. The best imaging for trapped lung:
   A. CXR
   B. **CT chest (gold standard)**
   C. Spirogram
   D. None
   E. ABG
   **Answer: B** — CT.

3. Pleural manometry in trapped lung shows:
   A. **Very negative pressure (e.g. <−20 cmH₂O)**
   B. Normal
   C. Positive
   D. None
   E. Variable
   **Answer: A** — Very negative.

4. The best treatment for symptomatic trapped lung:
   A. Observation
   B. **Decortication (VATS or open)**
   C. Steroids
   D. Wait
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Decortication.

5. Trapped lung causes which pattern of PFT:
   A. Obstructive
   B. **Restrictive (↓FVC, ↓TLC)**
   C. Normal
   D. Mixed
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Restrictive.

6. The best timing for decortication:
   A. Immediately
   B. **3–6 months (peel mature)**
   C. 1 year
   D. Never
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — 3–6 months.

7. The most common cause of fibrothorax is:
   A. Acute pneumonia
   B. **Chronic empyema, TB, retained haemothorax**
   C. None
   D. Asthma
   E. PE
   **Answer: B** — Chronic inflammation.

8. VATS decortication in organised empyema:
   A. **Effective (when early, before peel too thick)**
   B. Always fail
   C. Wait
   D. None
   E. Steroids
   **Answer: A** — Effective.

9. Fibrothorax on CXR shows:
   A. **Persistent opacity + pleural thickening ± calcification**
   B. Normal
   C. Hyperlucent
   D. None
   E. Mass
   **Answer: A** — Thickening.

10. A patient with chronic empyema has trapped lung. Best treatment:
    A. Wait
    B. **VATS decortication (or open if mature peel)**
    C. Steroids
    D. Antibiotics
    E. None
    **Answer: B** — Decortication.

## SBA Questions (10)

1. The most common cause of trapped lung in TB-endemic area:
   A. **TB (chronic pleural)**
   B. Empyema
   C. None
   D. PE
   E. Asthma
   **Answer: A** — TB.

2. The most useful test to confirm trapped lung:
   A. **Pleural manometry (negative pressure on drainage)**
   B. Spirogram
   C. CXR
   D. None
   E. Echo
   **Answer: A** — Manometry.

3. Fibrothorax on CT shows:
   A. **Diffuse pleural thickening ± calcification**
   B. Normal
   C. Mass
   D. None
   E. Effusion
   **Answer: A** — Diffuse thickening.

4. A patient with chronic pleural disease (decortication) has ↓FVC. Mechanism:
   A. Obstructive
   B. **Restrictive (mechanical constraint)**
   C. None
   D. Mixed
   E. Unknown
   **Answer: B** — Restrictive.

5. The most useful additional test in TB-related trapped lung:
   A. **Sputum AFB + culture +/− biopsy**
   B. Echo
   C. None
   D. ABG
   E. Spirogram
   **Answer: A** — AFB workup.

6. Decortication is contraindicated in:
   A. **Active TB (acute) or unfit patient**
   B. Symptomatic
   C. None
   D. All
   E. Asbestos
   **Answer: A** — Active TB or unfit.

7. A 60-year-old with chronic empyema + trapped lung + persistent fever. Best:
   A. Wait
   B. **Drain + antibiotics + delayed decortication**
   C. Steroids
   D. None
   E. Discontinue antibiotics
   **Answer: B** — Drain + abx + decortication.

8. The most common cause of fibrothorax in developed countries:
   A. **Empyema**
   B. TB
   C. None
   D. Trauma
   E. PE
   **Answer: A** — Empyema.

9. Trapped lung with indwelling pleural catheter is useful for:
   A. **Symptomatic control (recurrent effusion)**
   B. Cure
   C. None
   D. Surgery
   E. Steroids
   **Answer: A** — Symptomatic.

10. The most useful additional treatment for trapped lung + active TB:
   A. **Anti-TB therapy first, then decortication**
   B. Steroids alone
   C. Decortication immediately
   D. None
   E. Wait
   **Answer: A** — Anti-TB first.

## Summary

Trapped lung = visceral peel prevents re-expansion (most common: empyema). Fibrothorax = pleural thickening (TB, empyema, haemothorax). CT + pleural manometry (very negative pressure) for diagnosis.
