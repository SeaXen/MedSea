# Aspiration pneumonia

> [!important]
> **Aspiration pneumonia** is lung infection caused by **inhalation of oropharyngeal/gastric contents** into the lower airways. It is common in patients with **impaired consciousness, dysphagia, GERD, or poor oral hygiene**. Anaerobic bacteria are typically involved. Treatment differs from typical CAP.

Related: [[Pneumonia]], [[ABG Interpretation]], [[Oxygen Therapy and NIV]], [[Respiratory Failure]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: Aspiration pneumonia = **dependent lung segments** (posterior upper lobe, superior lower lobe — when supine; basal segments — when upright). **Anaerobic cover** is needed (e.g. amoxicillin-clavulanate or clindamycin). Always consider **lung abscess/empyema** in delayed presentations.

## Definition

**Aspiration pneumonia** = lung infection caused by inhalation of oropharyngeal/gastric contents.

### Types
- **Aspiration pneumonia** (bacterial, often anaerobes)
- **Aspiration pneumonitis** (Mendelson syndrome — chemical, sterile)
- **Chronic aspiration** (recurrent micro-aspiration)

## Risk Factors

### Patient-related
- **Altered consciousness** (alcohol, drugs, sedation, post-ictal, stroke)
- **Dysphagia** (neurological disease: stroke, Parkinson, dementia, MS, MG, GBS)
- **GERD** (gastro-oesophageal reflux)
- **Vomiting**
- **Old age**
- **Poor oral hygiene** (anaerobic load)
- **Tube feeding** (NG, PEG, tracheostomy)

### Iatrogenic
- **Sedation** (midazolam, opioids)
- **Anaesthesia** (perioperative)
- **Intubation** (peri-extubation)

## Pathophysiology

### Sequence
1. **Aspiration** of oropharyngeal/gastric contents
2. **Chemical injury** (gastric acid, if present)
3. **Impairment of host defences** (cough, ciliary, alveolar macrophages)
4. **Bacterial colonisation** (anaerobes, mixed flora)
5. **Inflammation** and infection
6. **Pneumonia**, abscess, empyema

### Bacteria involved
| Setting | Common organisms |
|---------|------------------|
| **Community, mild** | Mixed anaerobes (*Peptostreptococcus*, *Prevotella*, *Bacteroides*) |
| **Severe / hospital** | + Gram-negatives (Klebsiella, E. coli, Pseudomonas), Staph aureus |
| **Aspiration pneumonitis** (chemical) | Sterile initially; bacterial superinfection 48–72 h |

### Distribution
- **When upright/seated**: **basal segments** of lower lobes (especially right middle lobe, posterior segment of RUL)
- **When supine**: **posterior segments of upper lobes** and **superior segments of lower lobes** (right > left due to anatomy of right main bronchus)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Cough** (productive, may be foul-smelling)
- **Fever**
- **Dyspnoea**
- **Pleuritic chest pain** (if pleural involvement)
- **Putrid sputum** (anaerobic infection)
- **Anorexia, weight loss** (subacute)
- **Symptoms may be subtle** in elderly/immunocompromised

### Signs
- **Fever, tachycardia, tachypnoea**
- **Dullness to percussion, crackles** (consolidation)
- **Halitosis** (anaerobic infection, lung abscess)
- **Clubbing** (chronic)
- **Signs of dehydration**

## Diagnosis

### Investigations
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| **CXR** | Consolidation in dependent segments (posterior upper lobe, superior lower lobe when supine; basal segments when upright) |
| **CT chest** | More sensitive; abscess/empyema |
| **Sputum** | Gram stain, culture (often mixed flora) |
| **FBC** | ↑WCC (may be normal in elderly) |
| **CRP, ESR** | ↑ |
| **Blood cultures** | If severe |
| **ABG** | If hypoxaemic (Type 1) |
| **Bronchoscopy** | If foreign body suspected or no improvement |

## Management

### Antibiotics
- **Empirical** for **aspiration pneumonia** (anaerobes +/− Gram-negatives):
  - **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** (1.2 g IV TDS) or
  - **Clindamycin** (600 mg IV QDS) or
  - **Piperacillin-tazobactam** (severe, hospital-acquired)
  - **+ Metronidazole** if anaerobic cover not in primary
- **Duration**: 5–7 days (mild); 7–14 days (severe/lung abscess)
- **Adjust** to sputum culture

### Aspiration pneumonitis (chemical, no infection)
- **Supportive** (O₂, hydration)
- **Antibiotics NOT routine** unless:
  - Persistent symptoms
  - Failure to improve at 48 h
  - Suspected bacterial superinfection
- Consider steroids (controversial)

### Supportive
- **O₂** if hypoxaemic
- **Hydration** (often dehydrated)
- **Positioning** (semirecumbent, head elevated 30°)
- **Aspiration precautions** (NBM if high risk, NG, suction)
- **Sputum clearance** (physiotherapy)
- **PPE**: gowns if MRSA risk

### Treat underlying cause
- **GERD**: PPI, lifestyle, surgery (fundoplication)
- **Dysphagia**: SALT assessment, modified diet, NG/PEG
- **Alcohol**: cessation, support
- **Drug reduction**

### Complications
- **Lung abscess** (anaerobes)
- **Empyema**
- **ARDS**
- **Respiratory failure**
- **Sepsis**
- **Death** (especially in frail elderly)

## Prevention
- **Aspiration precautions** (semirecumbent, NBM, NG, suction)
- **Dysphagia screen** in stroke, elderly
- **Oral hygiene** (reduces anaerobic load)
- **Avoid sedatives**
- **Treat GERD**
- **PPI** in high risk (controversial — ↑pneumonia risk)

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Domain | Key points |
|--------|------------|
| **Risk factors** | ↓consciousness, dysphagia, GERD, alcohol, elderly |
| **Distribution** | Dependent segments (posterior upper lobe, superior lower lobe) |
| **Anaerobes** | *Peptostreptococcus*, *Prevotella*, *Bacteroides* |
| **Antibiotic** | Amoxicillin-clavulanate or clindamycin |
| **Severe/HAP** | Piperacillin-tazobactam |
| **Aspiration pneumonitis** | Supportive; antibiotics only if bacterial superinfection |

## MCQs (10)

1. Aspiration pneumonia in supine patients affects:
   A. Lower lobes only
   B. **Posterior segments of upper lobes + superior segments of lower lobes**
   C. RUL only
   D. Diffuse
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Posterior upper + superior lower.

2. The most common organisms in community-acquired aspiration pneumonia:
   A. *S. aureus*
   B. **Mixed anaerobes (oropharyngeal flora)**
   C. *Pseudomonas*
   D. *M. tuberculosis*
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Anaerobes.

3. First-line antibiotic for community-acquired aspiration pneumonia:
   A. **Amoxicillin-clavulanate**
   B. Vancomycin
   C. Meropenem
   D. Azithromycin
   E. None
   **Answer: A** — Amox-clav.

4. The major risk factor for aspiration pneumonia is:
   A. Smoking
   B. **Altered consciousness / dysphagia**
   C. Allergy
   D. Exercise
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Consciousness/dysphagia.

5. Aspiration pneumonitis (Mendelson syndrome) requires:
   A. Aggressive antibiotics
   B. **Supportive (antibiotics only if bacterial superinfection)**
   C. Surgery
   D. Steroids
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Supportive.

6. Lung abscess from aspiration is best treated with:
   A. **Prolonged antibiotic (4–6 weeks) covering anaerobes**
   B. Surgery
   C. Steroids
   D. Aspirin
   E. None
   **Answer: A** — Prolonged antibiotics.

7. The most useful preventive measure for aspiration in stroke:
   A. Antibiotics
   B. **Dysphagia screen before oral intake**
   C. Surgery
   D. Steroids
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Dysphagia screen.

8. Position to prevent aspiration in bedbound:
   A. Supine
   B. **Semirecumbent (head 30° elevated)**
   C. Prone
   D. Trendelenburg
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Semirecumbent.

9. The most common complication of aspiration pneumonia:
   A. ARDS
   B. **Lung abscess / empyema**
   C. PE
   D. None
   E. Asthma
   **Answer: B** — Abscess/empyema.

10. A stroke patient has cough and consolidation in the right lower lobe. Most likely:
    A. PE
    B. **Aspiration pneumonia (dependent segment)**
    C. TB
    D. Asthma
    E. None
    **Answer: B** — Aspiration.

## SBA Questions (10)

1. A 70-year-old with stroke has right lower lobe consolidation. Most likely pathogen:
   A. *S. aureus*
   B. **Anaerobes (mixed oropharyngeal)**
   C. *Pseudomonas*
   D. TB
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Anaerobes.

2. Best initial antibiotics for severe hospital-acquired aspiration pneumonia:
   A. Amoxicillin
   B. **Piperacillin-tazobactam ± metronidazole**
   C. Azithromycin
   D. Doxycycline
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Pip-tazo.

3. Aspiration pneumonitis (chemical) is differentiated from aspiration pneumonia by:
   A. Onset
   B. **Sterile initial insult (no bacteria)**
   C. Imaging
   D. ABG
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Sterile.

4. The most effective preventive measure for aspiration in tube-fed patients:
   A. Antibiotics
   B. **Semirecumbent position (head 30° up) +/− prokinetics**
   C. Surgery
   D. Steroids
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Position.

5. The radiographic distribution in supine aspiration is:
   A. Basal lower lobes
   B. **Posterior upper lobe + superior lower lobe**
   C. RML only
   D. Random
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Posterior upper + superior lower.

6. Aspiration pneumonitis treatment with steroids is:
   A. Always
   B. **Controversial (not routinely recommended)**
   C. Mandatory
   D. First-line
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Controversial.

7. Lung abscess in aspiration pneumonia is best detected by:
   A. CXR alone
   B. **CT chest**
   C. Spirogram
   D. Echo
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — CT chest.

8. A 65-year-old with alcohol use has foul-smelling sputum and right lower lobe cavitation. Treatment:
   A. Stop alcohol only
   B. **Amoxicillin-clavulanate (4–6 weeks) for lung abscess**
   C. Steroids
   D. Surgery
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Prolonged antibiotics.

9. A stroke patient fails dysphagia screen. Next:
   A. Normal diet
   B. **NBM + NG feeding + SALT assessment**
   C. Antibiotics
   D. Steroids
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — NBM + NG.

10. The most important host defence against aspiration:
    A. Stomach acid
    B. **Cough + ciliary clearance + alveolar macrophages**
    C. Mucus
    D. Diaphragm
    E. None
    **Answer: B** — Cough + clearance.
