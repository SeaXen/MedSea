# Pancoast tumor and local invasion syndromes

> [!important]
> **Pancoast (superior sulcus) tumor** is a **lung cancer at the lung apex** that invades adjacent structures (brachial plexus, sympathetic chain, ribs, vertebrae), causing a **characteristic syndrome** (shoulder pain, Horner's syndrome, hand muscle wasting). Treatment: **combined chemoradiotherapy + surgery** (if resectable).

Related: [[Lung Cancer]], [[Small-cell vs non-small-cell lung cancer|SCLC vs NSCLC]], [[Superior vena cava obstruction|SVC obstruction]], [[Paraneoplastic pulmonary syndromes|Paraneoplastic]], [[Malignant central airway obstruction]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: **Apical lung tumour + shoulder pain + Horner's syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) = Pancoast tumour**. Most are **NSCLC** (often squamous or adenocarcinoma). Imaging: **chest CT + MRI** (chest wall, brachial plexus invasion). Treatment: trimodality (chemo + RT + surgery) if resectable; otherwise chemoRT.

## Definition

**Pancoast (superior sulcus) tumour** = **lung cancer at the lung apex** (superior sulcus) that invades adjacent structures (chest wall, brachial plexus, sympathetic chain, vertebrae, ribs, subclavian vessels).

## Pathophysiology

### Anatomy of superior sulcus
- **Lung apex** projects above the **clavicle** and **first rib**
- Surrounded by:
  - **Brachial plexus** (C8, T1, T2) — inferior trunk
  - **Sympathetic chain** (stellate ganglion) — at C7–T1
  - **Subclavian vessels** (artery, vein)
  - **First rib, vertebrae** (T1, T2)
  - **Phrenic nerve**, recurrent laryngeal nerve

### Mechanism of symptoms
- **Brachial plexus invasion** → arm pain, weakness, sensory loss (C8–T1)
- **Sympathetic chain/stellate ganglion** → **Horner's syndrome** (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis)
- **Rib/vertebrae** → bone pain, pathological fracture
- **Subclavian vessels** → arm oedema, venous distension
- **Recurrent laryngeal nerve** → hoarseness (left side)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Shoulder pain** (most common initial symptom)
- **Arm pain** (radiating down ulnar side)
- **Weakness, muscle wasting** of intrinsic hand muscles (T1)
- **Sensory loss** (C8–T1 dermatome: medial forearm, 4th/5th fingers)
- **Horner's syndrome**: ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis (ipsilateral face)
- **Hoarseness** (recurrent laryngeal nerve)
- **Dyspnoea, cough, haemoptysis** (lung tumour)
- **Weight loss**, fatigue

### Signs
- **Ptosis + miosis + anhidrosis** (Horner's)
- **Wasting of intrinsic hand muscles** (ulnar innervated)
- **Weak arm** (especially hand grip)
- **Sensory loss** (medial arm/hand)
- **Palpable supraclavicular mass** (lymph nodes)
- **Horner's triad**

## Diagnosis

### Investigations
| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **CXR** | Apical mass, rib erosion, apical cap |
| **CT chest** | Tumour extent, chest wall, vessels, nodes |
| **MRI chest** | **Best for brachial plexus + chest wall + vertebral invasion** |
| **PET-CT** | Staging (N + M) |
| **Biopsy** (CT-guided / bronchoscopic / supraclavicular node) | Histology + molecular |
| **Staging workup** | PET-CT, brain MRI, EBUS, mediastinoscopy |

### Histology
- Most are **NSCLC**: squamous, adenocarcinoma, large cell
- Occasionally SCLC (less common in apex)
- **Molecular testing** (EGFR, ALK, PD-L1) for adenocarcinoma

### Staging
- **T3/T4** (based on size + invasion of chest wall/vertebrae/mediastinum)
- **N0–N3** based on nodes
- **M0/M1** (brain, bone, liver, adrenal)

## Management

### Multimodality treatment
- **Trimodality** = **chemotherapy + radiotherapy + surgery** (if resectable)
  - **Induction chemoradiotherapy** (concurrent)
  - **Surgical resection** (lobectomy + chest wall reconstruction)
  - **Adjuvant chemotherapy** (selected)

### Definitive chemoradiotherapy
- If unresectable (involves major vessels/vertebrae/mediastinum)
- **Concurrent chemoradiation** (60–66 Gy + platinum-based chemo)
- **Durvalumab** (PD-L1 inhibitor) consolidation if unresectable stage III

### Palliative
- **Distant metastases**: systemic therapy (chemo, targeted, immunotherapy)
- **Pain control**: analgesia, nerve blocks, palliative RT
- **Horner's syndrome**: usually not reversible

## Other Local Invasion Syndromes

### Superior vena cava obstruction
- **Most common cause**: lung cancer (esp. small cell, squamous)
- **Symptoms**: facial swelling, arm swelling, distended neck veins, headache
- **Treatment**: endovascular stent +/− chemo/RT (see SVC obstruction file)

### Recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy
- **Most common cause**: lung cancer (left upper lobe compression)
- **Symptoms**: hoarseness, weak voice
- **Treatment**: treat underlying cancer

### Phrenic nerve palsy
- **Cause**: apical tumour, surgery, radiation
- **Symptoms**: dyspnoea, orthopnoea, ↑hemi-diaphragm
- **Treatment**: treat underlying

### Horner's syndrome
- **Cause**: apical tumour (Pancoast), sympathetic chain
- **Symptoms**: ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis
- **Not reversible** in most cases

### Pancoast syndrome
- Triad: **shoulder/arm pain + Horner's + hand muscle wasting**

## MCQs (10)

1. The most common histology of Pancoast tumour is:
   A. Small cell
   B. **NSCLC (squamous, adenocarcinoma)**
   C. Carcinoid
   D. Mesothelioma
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — NSCLC.

2. Pancoast syndrome includes:
   A. **Shoulder pain + Horner's + hand muscle wasting**
   B. Cough, dyspnoea
   C. SVC obstruction
   D. None
   E. Hoarseness
   **Answer: A** — Classic triad.

3. Horner's syndrome is caused by:
   A. **Stellate ganglion/sympathetic chain involvement**
   B. Vagus
   C. Phrenic
   D. None
   E. Recurrent laryngeal
   **Answer: A** — Sympathetic.

4. The best imaging for Pancoast tumour is:
   A. **CT + MRI (chest wall, brachial plexus)**
   B. CXR
   C. PET
   D. None
   E. Echo
   **Answer: A** — CT + MRI.

5. The best initial treatment for resectable Pancoast tumour is:
   A. Surgery alone
   B. **Trimodality: chemoradiotherapy + surgery**
   C. RT alone
   D. Wait
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Trimodality.

6. SVC obstruction in lung cancer is most commonly caused by:
   A. **Small cell lung cancer**
   B. Squamous
   C. Adenocarcinoma
   D. None
   E. Carcinoid
   **Answer: A** — SCLC.

7. The most useful first treatment for SVC obstruction due to cancer is:
   A. Chemo
   B. **Endovascular stent +/− chemo/RT**
   C. Wait
   D. Steroids
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Stent.

8. The most common cause of recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy in lung cancer is:
   A. **Left upper lobe tumour (compression of left RLN)**
   B. Right upper lobe
   C. Lower lobe
   D. None
   E. Mediastinal
   **Answer: A** — Left upper lobe.

9. The most common cause of hoarseness in lung cancer is:
   A. Laryngeal tumour
   B. **Recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy**
   C. Pleural effusion
   D. None
   E. COPD
   **Answer: B** — RLN palsy.

10. Phrenic nerve palsy in lung cancer causes:
    A. Wheeze
    B. **Hemi-diaphragm paralysis (orthopnoea, dyspnoea)**
    C. None
    D. Cough
    E. Cyanosis
    **Answer: B** — Hemi-diaphragm.

## Summary

Pancoast = apical lung tumour invading adjacent structures. Triad: shoulder pain + Horner's + hand muscle wasting. Most are NSCLC. Imaging: CT + MRI. Treatment: trimodality (resectable) or chemoRT (unresectable). Other local invasion: SVC obstruction (SCLC), RLN palsy (left upper lobe), phrenic palsy, Horner's.
