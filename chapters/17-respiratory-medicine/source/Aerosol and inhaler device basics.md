# Aerosol and inhaler device basics

> [!important]
> Selection of the **correct inhaler device** and ensuring correct technique are crucial for asthma/COPD control. **70–80% of patients make at least one error** when using inhalers. The choice of device depends on patient factors (age, dexterity, inspiratory flow, preferences).

Related: [[Asthma]], [[COPD]], [[Acute severe asthma]], [[Difficult-to-treat and severe asthma]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: Pressurised metered-dose inhaler (pMDI) + spacer is the **gold standard for most patients**. Dry powder inhalers (DPIs) require adequate inspiratory flow (>30 L/min). Soft mist inhalers (SMI) are good for patients with poor coordination. **Always check technique at every visit**.

## Learning Objectives
- List inhaler types and their characteristics
- Select the right device for each patient
- Teach correct inhaler technique
- Recognise common errors
- Apply to acute and chronic settings

## Inhaler Types

### Pressurised metered-dose inhaler (pMDI)
- **Pressurised canister** + plastic mouthpiece
- **Requires coordination**: press + slow deep breath
- **Always use with spacer** (especially for acute, children, elderly)
- Dose counter: newer devices have it
- **Advantage**: portable, multi-dose
- **Disadvantage**: coordination, propellant, oropharyngeal deposition (rinsing recommended)

### Breath-actuated MDI (BA-MDI)
- **Auto-triggered** by inspiratory effort
- No coordination needed
- E.g. Autohaler, Easibreathe
- Useful for patients with poor hand-breath coordination

### Dry powder inhalers (DPI)
- **Breath-actuated** — patient inhales to disperse dose
- **Require inspiratory flow >30 L/min** (some >60 L/min)
- Easier coordination than pMDI
- **Not for acute severe asthma** (insufficient flow)
- Examples: Accuhaler/Diskus, Turbohaler, HandiHaler, Ellipta, Breezhaler
- **Disadvantage**: moisture-sensitive, requires adequate flow

### Soft mist inhalers (SMI)
- **Slow-moving aerosol** (longer inhalation time)
- Good for poor coordination
- E.g. Respimat
- Lower oropharyngeal deposition

### Nebulisers
- **Jet nebuliser** (compressed air) or **ultrasonic**
- For **acute severe asthma/COPD** (when MDI + spacer insufficient)
- O₂-driven (to deliver O₂ + bronchodilator)
- 5–10 min per treatment
- Types: bronchodilator, steroid (budesonide), antibiotic (colistin), mucolytic

### Spacers
- **Add-on device** for pMDI
- Slows aerosol; reduces oropharyngeal deposition
- Multiple breaths easier than single breath
- **Essential for**: children, acute asthma, poor coordination
- Use with tidal breathing (or single deep breath with breath-hold)

## Device Selection

| Patient | Best device |
|---------|-------------|
| **Most patients** | pMDI + spacer |
| **Acute severe asthma** | pMDI + spacer (8–10 puffs) or nebuliser |
| **Children** | pMDI + spacer (with mask if <3 years) |
| **Elderly, poor coordination** | BA-MDI, DPI (good inspiratory flow), or SMI |
| **Cognitive/dexterity issues** | DPI, BA-MDI, or nebuliser |
| **High-dose ICS** | DPI (e.g. Diskus) or pMDI + spacer (rinse mouth after) |
| **Severe airflow limitation (severe acute)** | Nebuliser |

## Inhaler Technique

### pMDI (with spacer)
1. **Shake** the inhaler
2. **Attach** to spacer
3. **Exhale** fully (away from inhaler)
4. **Place** mouthpiece in mouth, seal lips
5. **Press** canister once
6. **Breathe** slowly and deeply (or 4–5 tidal breaths)
7. **Breath-hold** 10 s if possible
8. **Repeat** for additional doses
9. **Rinse mouth** after steroids

### DPI
1. **Load** dose (mechanism varies)
2. **Exhale** fully (away from device)
3. **Seal** lips around mouthpiece
4. **Inhale** quickly and deeply (critical — fast flow needed)
5. **Breath-hold** 10 s
6. **Exhale** slowly
7. **Rinse mouth** after steroids

### Nebuliser
1. Add medication to chamber
2. Connect to compressor/O₂ (6–8 L/min)
3. Seal mouthpiece/mask
4. Breathe tidally until nebuliser sputters (~5–10 min)
5. Tap chamber periodically to maximise output

## Common Errors

| Error | Solution |
|-------|----------|
| **Pressing pMDI before inhaling** | Spacer removes this issue |
| **Inhaling too fast with DPI** | Use DPI only if flow adequate; otherwise pMDI + spacer |
| **Not holding breath after pMDI** | Breath-hold 10 s if possible |
| **Exhaling into DPI** | Exhale away, then seal lips |
| **Failing to rinse mouth after ICS** | Rinse with water (prevent thrush, dysphonia) |
| **Multiple puffs in quick succession** | Wait 30 s between puffs; shake between |
| **Dirty spacer** | Wash weekly in soapy water, air-dry |
| **Expired medication** | Check expiry; pMDI shows dose counter |

## Clinical Pearls

- **Switch device** if patient cannot use it correctly
- **Recheck technique at every visit**
- **One device per visit** (don't introduce multiple changes)
- **Use the same device** for all inhalers when possible (consistency)
- **Spacers** should be replaced yearly
- **Nebulisers** are not better than pMDI + spacer for stable patients
- **In acute asthma**: 8–10 puffs of salbutamol via spacer = 5 mg neb

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Domain | Key points |
|--------|------------|
| **pMDI + spacer** | Gold standard for most patients |
| **DPI** | Need inspiratory flow >30 L/min |
| **SMI** | Good for poor coordination |
| **Nebuliser** | Acute severe; not for stable |
| **Spacer** | Essential with pMDI; wash weekly |
| **Breath-hold** | 10 s after inhalation |
| **Rinse mouth** | After ICS (prevent thrush) |
| **Check technique** | Every visit |
| **Common error** | Pressing before inhalation (use spacer) |

## MCQs (10)

1. The gold-standard inhaler device for most patients is:
   A. DPI
   B. **pMDI + spacer**
   C. Nebuliser
   D. SMI
   E. Turbohaler
   **Answer: B** — pMDI + spacer.

2. DPIs require an inspiratory flow of at least:
   A. 10 L/min
   B. **30 L/min**
   C. 60 L/min
   D. 100 L/min
   E. 5 L/min
   **Answer: B** — >30 L/min for most DPIs.

3. In acute severe asthma, an MDI + spacer delivers how many puffs equivalent to a 5 mg nebuliser:
   A. 2
   B. 4
   C. **8–10**
   D. 20
   E. 50
   **Answer: C** — 8–10 puffs = 5 mg neb.

4. After using an inhaled corticosteroid, patients should:
   A. Lie down
   B. **Rinse mouth with water**
   C. Eat
   D. Take SABA
   E. Drink caffeine
   **Answer: B** — Prevent oral thrush.

5. Breath-hold after inhaler use:
   A. Not needed
   B. **10 seconds if possible**
   C. 30 seconds
   D. 1 minute
   E. Until wheeze stops
   **Answer: B** — 10 s breath-hold.

6. Soft mist inhaler (SMI) example:
   A. Ventolin
   B. **Respimat**
   C. Seretide Accuhaler
   D. Pulmicort Turbohaler
   E. Spiriva HandiHaler
   **Answer: B** — Respimat is SMI.

7. Spacers should be cleaned:
   A. Daily
   B. **Weekly (in soapy water, air-dry)**
   C. Monthly
   D. Yearly
   E. Never
   **Answer: B** — Weekly.

8. Nebuliser first-line in:
   A. Stable asthma
   B. **Acute severe asthma (or when MDI+spacer insufficient)**
   C. Mild COPD
   D. Smoking cessation
   E. Routine asthma
   **Answer: B** — Acute severe.

9. Common DPI error:
   A. Pressing too hard
   B. **Inhaling too slowly (need fast deep breath)**
   C. Holding breath
   D. Rinsing mouth
   E. Exhaling
   **Answer: B** — Need fast flow.

10. Spacer role:
    A. Increase dose
    B. **Reduce oropharyngeal deposition + improve coordination**
    C. Replace nebuliser
    D. Increase flow
    E. None
    **Answer: B** — Reduces deposition + coordination.

## SBA Questions (10)

1. A patient with asthma cannot coordinate pMDI. Best device:
   A. Higher dose pMDI
   B. **DPI, BA-MDI, or SMI**
   C. Tablet
   D. Injection
   E. Syrup
   **Answer: B** — Alternative device.

2. A child <3 years needs inhaled asthma medication. Best:
   A. DPI
   B. **pMDI + spacer with face mask**
   C. MDI alone
   D. Tablet
   E. Syrup
   **Answer: B** — pMDI + spacer + face mask.

3. A patient on high-dose budesonide DPI develops oral thrush. Action:
   A. Stop ICS
   B. **Rinse mouth after use + check technique**
   C. Increase dose
   D. Switch to tablet
   E. Surgery
   **Answer: B** — Rinse + technique.

4. Spacer vs nebuliser in stable asthma:
   A. Nebuliser better
   B. **Spacer as effective + cheaper + faster**
   C. Equal
   D. Spacer worse
   E. Nebuliser only
   **Answer: B** — Spacer as effective, cheaper, faster.

5. A patient on DPI cannot generate adequate flow. Switch to:
   A. Higher dose DPI
   B. **pMDI + spacer (or nebuliser)**
   C. Continuation
   D. Lower dose
   E. Surgery
   **Answer: B** — Switch to pMDI + spacer.

6. A patient on multiple inhalers uses different devices. Action:
   A. Continue
   B. **Consolidate to same device type when possible**
   C. Add more
   D. Stop
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Consolidate.

7. Nebuliser driving gas in acute severe asthma:
   A. Air
   B. **Oxygen (6–8 L/min)**
   C. Helium
   D. N₂O
   E. CO₂
   **Answer: B** — O₂-driven.

8. pMDI propellant is:
   A. CFC
   B. **HFA (ozone-friendly)**
   C. Helium
   D. CO₂
   E. N₂O
   **Answer: B** — Modern pMDI use HFA.

9. A patient uses inhaler but feels no benefit. First action:
   A. Increase dose
   B. **Check inhaler technique**
   C. Stop
   D. Add another
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Technique first.

10. The most common cause of poor asthma control is:
    A. Wrong diagnosis
    B. **Poor inhaler technique + non-adherence**
    C. Steroid resistance
    D. Severity
    E. None
    **Answer: B** — Technique + adherence.

## Summary

Inhaler devices: pMDI (with spacer = gold standard), DPI (need flow >30 L/min), BA-MDI, SMI, nebuliser (acute). Spacer reduces oropharyngeal deposition + aids coordination. Always check technique at every visit. Rinse mouth after ICS. Breath-hold 10 s.
