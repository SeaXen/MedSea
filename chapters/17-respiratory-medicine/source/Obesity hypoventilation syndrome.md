# Obesity hypoventilation syndrome

Parent: [[Upper Airway and Sleep-Related Breathing Disorders/Sleep-related breathing disorders|Sleep-related breathing disorders]]  
Heading: [[Upper Airway and Sleep-Related Breathing Disorders]]  
Related: [[Respiratory MOC]], [[Obstructive sleep apnea]], [[Respiratory Failure and Ventilatory Support/Type 2 respiratory failure|Type 2 respiratory failure]], [[Oxygen Therapy and NIV]], [[ABG Interpretation]]

> [!important]
> **Obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS)** is defined by **obesity plus chronic daytime alveolar hypoventilation** causing **awake hypercapnia**, after excluding other major causes of hypoventilation.

## Definition
OHS is classically defined by:
- **BMI ≥30 kg/m²**
- **Daytime PaCO2 >45 mmHg (6.0 kPa)**
- Sleep-disordered breathing is usually present
- No alternative explanation such as major neuromuscular, chest wall, or severe intrinsic lung disease alone

## Anatomy
### Relevant anatomical factors
- Increased chest wall and abdominal mass reduce respiratory system compliance
- Diaphragmatic excursion is mechanically limited by central obesity
- Upper airway narrowing often coexists, especially with OSA

## Physiology
### Normal physiology
Adequate ventilation requires:
- effective respiratory drive
- sufficient respiratory muscle performance
- compliant chest wall-lung system
- patent upper airway

### Physiological disturbances in OHS
- Increased work of breathing due to obesity
- Reduced chest wall compliance
- Lower functional residual capacity
- Ventilation-perfusion disturbance in dependent lung regions
- Blunted ventilatory response to CO2 in some patients
- Frequent coexistence of OSA leading to recurrent nocturnal hypoventilation

## Classification
### Practical clinical classification
- OHS with coexisting OSA
- OHS without significant OSA
- Stable chronic OHS
- Acute decompensated OHS with acute-on-chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure

## Etiology / Risk Factors
- Severe obesity
- Central adiposity
- Coexisting OSA
- Sedatives / alcohol
- Reduced mobility
- Chronic sleep-disordered breathing
- Obesity-related cardiometabolic disease

## Pathophysiology
1. Obesity increases mechanical load on the respiratory system
2. Tidal breathing becomes less efficient
3. Nocturnal hypoventilation develops, often with OSA overlap
4. Over time, CO2 retention persists into wakefulness
5. Chronic hypoxemia/hypercapnia may cause pulmonary hypertension and right heart strain

## Clinical Features
### Symptoms
- Daytime somnolence
- Loud snoring
- Morning headache
- Fatigue
- Reduced exercise tolerance
- Dyspnea
- Poor concentration

### Signs
- Obesity
- Hypoxemia
- Hypercapnic features in advanced cases
- Peripheral edema
- Raised JVP / signs of cor pulmonale in severe disease
- Cyanosis in advanced decompensation

## Investigations
### 1. ABG
Most important confirming test:
- **Daytime hypercapnia**
- Hypoxemia may coexist
- HCO3- may be elevated in chronic compensation

### 2. Sleep study
- Evaluates coexisting OSA and nocturnal hypoventilation

### 3. Serum bicarbonate
- Elevated bicarbonate may suggest chronic CO2 retention
- Not diagnostic alone

### 4. Pulse oximetry / overnight oximetry
- Shows nocturnal desaturation but does not fully define the syndrome

### 5. CXR / imaging
- May exclude alternative cardiopulmonary pathology
- Can show cardiomegaly or pulmonary vascular prominence in advanced disease

### 6. Cardiac assessment
- ECG / echocardiography if pulmonary hypertension or right heart strain suspected

## Interpretation Frameworks
### A. ABG interpretation
Typical chronic OHS pattern:
- PaCO2 elevated while awake
- PaO2 low or low-normal
- bicarbonate elevated if chronic compensation is present

### B. Oxygen therapy cautions
- OHS is a **hypercapnic-prone state**
- Excess uncontrolled oxygen can worsen CO2 retention in some patients
- Use oxygen carefully with monitoring, especially during acute decompensation

### C. Sleep-disordered breathing overlap
- OHS often coexists with OSA
- Isolated OSA may have normal daytime PaCO2; **awake hypercapnia favors OHS**

### D. Imaging clues
- Imaging is supportive rather than diagnostic
- Use imaging mainly to exclude competing pathology or complications

## Diagnosis
Diagnosis requires all of the following:
- obesity
- chronic daytime hypercapnia
- exclusion of other major causes of alveolar hypoventilation
- supportive sleep-disordered breathing assessment in most cases

## Differential Diagnosis
- Isolated OSA without daytime hypercapnia
- COPD with chronic hypercapnia
- Neuromuscular respiratory weakness
- Kyphoscoliosis / chest wall disease
- Hypothyroidism-related hypoventilation
- Drug-induced hypoventilation

## Management
### General measures
- Weight reduction is central
- Treat comorbid diabetes, hypertension, and heart failure risk
- Avoid sedatives and excess alcohol

### Ventilatory support
- **CPAP** if OSA is dominant and hypercapnia is mild/overlap-driven
- **NIV/BiPAP** often required when significant hypoventilation/hypercapnia persists

### Oxygen
- Give only when indicated
- Monitor ABG because uncontrolled oxygen can aggravate hypercapnia

### Acute decompensation
- Treat as acute-on-chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure
- Controlled oxygen, ABG monitoring, NIV where appropriate, and identify precipitating factors such as infection or sedative exposure

## Complications
- Pulmonary hypertension
- Cor pulmonale
- Polycythemia in chronic hypoxemia
- Recurrent hospital admission
- Severe daytime disability
- Increased cardiovascular risk

## Red Flags / Emergencies
- Drowsiness / confusion from hypercapnia
- Acute-on-chronic respiratory acidosis
- Cor pulmonale / fluid overload
- Severe hypoxemia or decompensation during infection

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- OHS = **obesity + daytime hypercapnia** after exclusion of other causes
- Many patients also have **OSA**
- Persistent awake hypercapnia distinguishes OHS from uncomplicated OSA
- Oxygen must be used cautiously because of **CO2 retention risk**
- NIV is often more important than oxygen alone

## Common Confusions / Exam Traps
- Do not label every obese snorer as OHS
- OHS is not diagnosed by nocturnal desaturation alone
- Do not miss chronic hypercapnia on ABG
- Do not give uncontrolled high-flow oxygen without reassessment in hypercapnic-prone patients

## One-Page Summary
- OHS = obesity + chronic daytime alveolar hypoventilation
- Key ABG clue: **awake PaCO2 >45 mmHg**
- Frequently overlaps with OSA
- Main issues: sleep-disordered breathing, chronic hypercapnia, pulmonary hypertension/cor pulmonale risk
- Management: weight reduction, CPAP/NIV depending on physiology, cautious oxygen, monitor ABG

## MCQs
1. OHS is best defined by obesity plus:
   - A. Isolated nocturnal snoring
   - B. Daytime hypercapnia
   - C. Hemoptysis
   - D. Pleural effusion
   - **Answer: B**

2. A key ABG feature of OHS is:
   - A. Very low PaCO2
   - B. Normal PaCO2 in all cases
   - C. PaCO2 >45 mmHg while awake
   - D. Isolated metabolic alkalosis only
   - **Answer: C**

3. OHS commonly overlaps with:
   - A. Pneumothorax
   - B. OSA
   - C. Sarcoidosis
   - D. Bronchiectasis
   - **Answer: B**

4. In OHS, uncontrolled oxygen may:
   - A. Always cure hypoventilation
   - B. Worsen CO2 retention
   - C. Eliminate need for ventilation
   - D. Prevent obesity
   - **Answer: B**

5. A major chronic complication of untreated OHS is:
   - A. Left pneumothorax
   - B. Cor pulmonale
   - C. Tension gastrothorax
   - D. Hemothorax
   - **Answer: B**

6. A patient with obesity, snoring, and normal daytime PaCO2 most likely has:
   - A. Proven OHS
   - B. Possible OSA rather than confirmed OHS
   - C. Definite PE
   - D. ILD
   - **Answer: B**

7. Central long-term management of OHS includes:
   - A. Weight reduction
   - B. Routine antibiotics
   - C. Anticoagulation for all
   - D. Bronchoscopy for all
   - **Answer: A**

8. Which ventilatory strategy is often required in significant persistent hypercapnia?
   - A. Nebulized salbutamol only
   - B. NIV/BiPAP
   - C. Chest drain
   - D. Pleurodesis
   - **Answer: B**

9. Elevated serum bicarbonate in OHS often reflects:
   - A. Acute hemorrhage
   - B. Chronic CO2 retention with renal compensation
   - C. Simple viral URTI
   - D. Lobar collapse alone
   - **Answer: B**

10. A diagnostic requirement for OHS is exclusion of:
   - A. Any snoring history
   - B. Other causes of alveolar hypoventilation
   - C. All cardiac disease
   - D. All lung disease of any degree
   - **Answer: B**

## SBA Questions
1. A 44-year-old woman with BMI 46 kg/m² has daytime somnolence and ABG shows PaCO2 52 mmHg. Most likely diagnosis?  
   **Answer:** Obesity hypoventilation syndrome

2. Best test to confirm chronic daytime hypoventilation in suspected OHS?  
   **Answer:** Arterial blood gas

3. An obese patient with sleep-disordered breathing is drowsy and hypercapnic. Best ventilatory support consideration if persistent?  
   **Answer:** NIV/BiPAP

4. What best distinguishes OHS from simple OSA?  
   **Answer:** Persistent awake hypercapnia

5. Why should oxygen be used carefully in OHS?  
   **Answer:** It may worsen CO2 retention in hypercapnic-prone patients

6. A chronic complication of untreated OHS is:  
   **Answer:** Pulmonary hypertension

7. A raised serum bicarbonate in an obese sleepy patient should make you think of:  
   **Answer:** Chronic CO2 retention

8. Long-term disease-modifying measure in OHS?  
   **Answer:** Weight reduction

9. Which overlap is most common in OHS?  
   **Answer:** OSA overlap

10. During acute infection with worsening hypercapnia in OHS, immediate reassessment should include:  
   **Answer:** Controlled oxygen with repeat ABG monitoring
