# Hemothorax

> [!important]
> **Hemothorax** is blood in the pleural space. Most common cause: **chest trauma** (rib fractures, penetrating). Other causes: iatrogenic, malignancy, PE, aortic dissection. **Massive haemothorax (>1500 mL or >200 mL/h)** is a **surgical emergency** — requires **chest drain +/− thoracotomy**.

Related: [[Pleural Effusion]], [[Pneumothorax]], [[Empyema and pleural infection]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: **Massive haemothorax = >1500 mL or >200 mL/h drainage = surgical emergency** (thoracotomy if bleeding persists). Initial management: **chest drain** + resuscitation. Diagnostic: **fluid haematocrit >50% of blood**. Most resolve with chest drain alone.

## 1. Definition

**Hemothorax** = blood in the pleural space.

### Severity
- **Small** (<300 mL)
- **Moderate** (300–1500 mL)
- **Massive** (>1500 mL or >200 mL/h)

## 2. Aetiology

### Trauma (most common)
- **Penetrating** (stab, gunshot)
- **Blunt** (rib fractures, deceleration)
- **Iatrogenic** (central line, CVC, pleural tap, biopsy, surgery)

### Non-traumatic
| Cause | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| **Malignancy** (lung, mesothelioma, metastasis) | Spontaneous |
| **Pulmonary embolism** (with infarction) | Rare |
| **Aortic dissection / rupture** | Catastrophic |
| **Coagulopathy** | Spontaneous |
| **Anticoagulation** | Spontaneous bleed |
| **Ruptured vascular malformation** | Rare |
| **Connective tissue disease** (Ehlers-Danlos, Marfan) | Rare |
| **Endometriosis** (catamenial) | Cyclical |

## 3. Pathophysiology

### Mechanism
- **Trauma**: intercostal artery (most common), internal mammary, pulmonary vessels, aortic injury
- **Malignancy**: tumour invasion of vessels
- **PE**: pulmonary infarction
- **Aortic dissection**: rupture into pleura (usually left)

### Consequences
- **Lung collapse** (compression)
- **Hypovolaemic shock** (massive)
- **Empyema** (retained blood → infected)
- **Fibrothorax** (organised haematoma)
- **Death** (if untreated massive)

## 4. Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- **Pleuritic chest pain**
- **Dyspnoea**
- **Cough**
- **Hypotension, tachycardia** (massive)
- **Anxiety, restlessness**

### Signs
- **Dullness to percussion**
- **↓Breath sounds** (affected side)
- **↓Vocal fremitus, ↓vocal resonance**
- **Tracheal deviation** (massive, away from affected side)
- **Shock** (massive: tachycardia, hypotension, cool peripheries)
- **Cyanosis**
- **Subcutaneous emphysema** (if associated with PTX)

## 5. Diagnosis

### Investigations
| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **CXR** | Blunting of costophrenic angle (small); opacity (large) |
| **CT chest** | **Gold standard** — quantifies blood, identifies source |
| **Pleural US** (bedside) | Rapid diagnosis, guides drain |
| **Pleural tap** | Haematocrit (≥50% blood = haematocrit) |
| **CT angiography** | Aortic injury (if suspected) |
| **FBC, coagulation** | Severity, coagulopathy |
| **Cross-match** | If massive |
| **Drain output** | >200 mL/h = surgical |

### CXR findings
- **Small** (<300 mL): blunting of costophrenic angle
- **Larger**: opacity with meniscus
- **Massive**: complete white-out, mediastinal shift
- **Associated PTX**: air-fluid level (hydropneumothorax)

### Diagnostic criteria
- **Pleural fluid haematocrit ≥50% of blood** = haemothorax
- Otherwise: blood-stained fluid (e.g. from malignancy, PE)

## 6. Management

### General
- **A–B–C assessment**
- **IV access** (×2 large-bore)
- **Fluid resuscitation** (crystalloid, then blood)
- **Cross-match** (if massive)
- **Analgesia** (avoid NSAIDs — coagulopathy)
- **O₂** if hypoxaemic
- **Treat coagulopathy** (vitamin K, FFP, platelets)

### Chest drain
- **All haemothoraces** (≥moderate) require **chest drain**
- **Large-bore** (32–40 Fr)
- **Position**: posterior/lateral
- **Auto-transfusion** (if massive; cell salvage)
- **Monitor output**: >200 mL/h = surgical

### Surgical
- **Thoracotomy** (urgent) indications:
  - **Massive haemothorax** (>1500 mL initial or >200 mL/h)
  - **Persistent bleeding** (>200 mL/h for 2–3 h)
  - **Haemodynamic instability** despite resuscitation
  - **Cardiac/vascular injury** suspected (great vessels, pericardium)
  - **Penetrating trauma** with significant haemothorax
- **VATS** (video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery) for selected cases

### Conservative
- **Small** (<300 mL) — observation
- **Symptomatic treatment**

### Complications
- **Empyema** (retained blood → infected)
- **Fibrothorax** (organised, requires decortication)
- **Trapped lung**
- **Recurrent bleeding**
- **ARDS**
- **Death**

### Retained haemothorax
- **Definition**: blood remaining after chest drain
- **Management**: **VATS** within 7 days (to prevent empyema, fibrothorax)

## 8. MCQs (10)

1. The most common cause of hemothorax is:
   A. Malignancy
   B. **Trauma (rib fracture, penetrating)**
   C. PE
   D. None
   E. Aortic dissection
   **Answer: B** — Trauma.

2. Massive hemothorax is defined as:
   A. >500 mL
   B. >1000 mL
   C. **>1500 mL or >200 mL/h drainage**
   D. >300 mL
   E. None
   **Answer: C** — >1500 mL or >200 mL/h.

3. Diagnostic pleural fluid haematocrit for hemothorax:
   A. >10%
   B. >25%
   C. **≥50% of blood haematocrit**
   D. >75%
   E. None
   **Answer: C** — ≥50% blood Hct.

4. The most common vessel injured in chest trauma hemothorax:
   A. Pulmonary artery
   B. **Intercostal artery**
   C. Aorta
   D. None
   E. Subclavian
   **Answer: B** — Intercostal.

5. First-line treatment of hemothorax:
   A. Wait
   B. **Chest drain + resuscitation**
   C. Surgery
   D. Antibiotics
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Chest drain.

6. Surgical emergency for hemothorax is:
   A. Any hemothorax
   B. **Massive (>1500 mL or >200 mL/h) or persistent**
   C. Small
   D. None
   E. Chronic
   **Answer: B** — Massive or persistent.

7. Retained hemothorax is best treated by:
   A. Antibiotics
   B. **VATS within 7 days**
   C. Wait
   D. Steroids
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — VATS.

8. The most common late complication of untreated hemothorax:
   A. ARDS
   B. **Empyema + fibrothorax**
   C. PE
   D. None
   E. Heart failure
   **Answer: B** — Empyema + fibrothorax.

9. Hemothorax associated with aortic dissection:
   A. Small
   B. **Massive, often left-sided, catastrophic**
   C. Right
   D. Bilateral
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Massive, left.

10. The role of auto-transfusion (cell salvage) in massive hemothorax:
    A. Never
    B. **Yes (re-infuse drained blood)**
    C. Antibiotic
    D. Wait
    E. None
    **Answer: B** — Yes, cell salvage.

   D. Steroids
   E. None
   **Answer: A** — Thoracotomy.

2. The most useful imaging in chest trauma:
   A. CXR
   B. **CT chest with contrast**
   C. Spirogram
   D. None
   E. ABG
   **Answer: B** — CT.

3. Drain output >200 mL/h indicates:
   A. Normal
   B. **Active surgical bleeding**
   C. None

   E. None
   **Answer: B** — Active bleeding.

4. Hemothorax is most common on the:
   A. **Side of trauma (or any side)**
   B. Always right
   C. Always left
   D. Bilateral
   E. None
   **Answer: A** — Side of trauma.

   A. Trauma
   B. **Aortic rupture into pleura**

   D. None
   E. Malignancy
   **Answer: B** — Aortic rupture.

   A. Wait
   B. **VATS within 7 days**
   C. Steroids
   D. Antibiotics
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — VATS.

7. The most common long-term complication of organized hemothorax is:
   A. ARDS
   B. **Fibrothorax (trapped lung)**
   C. PE
   D. None
   E. Heart failure
   **Answer: B** — Fibrothorax.

   A. PTX
   B. **Cardiac tamponade**

   D. None

9. The most appropriate first test in chest trauma:
   A. **CXR + eFAST**
   B. CT
   C. ABG

   E. None
   **Answer: A** — CXR + eFAST.

10. The role of tranexamic acid in trauma:
    A. None

    D. Steroids

    **Answer: B** — Yes.
