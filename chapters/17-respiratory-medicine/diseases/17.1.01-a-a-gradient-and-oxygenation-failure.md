# A-a gradient and oxygenation failure

> [!important]
> The **A-a gradient** (alveolar-arterial O₂ difference) is a key calculation in respiratory physiology. It distinguishes the **mechanisms of hypoxaemia**: normal A-a gradient with ↑PaCO₂ = hypoventilation; ↑A-a gradient = V/Q mismatch, shunt, or diffusion limitation.

Related: [[ABG Interpretation]], [[Oxygen Therapy and NIV]], [[Respiratory Failure]], [[Ventilation-perfusion mismatch and shunt|V/Q mismatch]], [[Diffusion limitation and dead space]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: Normal A-a gradient (5–15 mmHg, or 1–2 kPa; ↑with age). A-a gradient = PAO₂ − PaO₂. In hypoventilation (e.g. opioid, NM weakness) the A-a gradient is normal but PaCO₂ is ↑. In V/Q mismatch, shunt, or diffusion limitation, A-a gradient is ↑.

## 2. Definition

**A-a gradient** = alveolar O₂ tension (PAO₂) − arterial O₂ tension (PaO₂)

### Calculation
**PAO₂ = FiO₂ × (Patm − PH₂O) − PaCO₂ / R**

- **FiO₂** = fraction of inspired O₂ (0.21 on room air)
- **Patm** = atmospheric pressure (760 mmHg at sea level)
- **PH₂O** = water vapour pressure (47 mmHg)
- **PaCO₂** = arterial CO₂ tension
- **R** = respiratory quotient (0.8 at rest)

### Simplified (sea level, room air)
**PAO₂ ≈ 150 − PaCO₂/0.8**
**A-a gradient = (150 − PaCO₂/0.8) − PaO₂**

### Example
- PaO₂ = 80 mmHg, PaCO₂ = 40 mmHg, FiO₂ 0.21
- PAO₂ = 150 − 40/0.8 = 150 − 50 = 100 mmHg
- **A-a gradient = 100 − 80 = 20 mmHg** (normal)

## 3. Normal Values

### Normal A-a gradient
- **Young adult**: 5–10 mmHg (~1–1.5 kPa)
- **Adult**: 5–15 mmHg (~1–2 kPa)
- **Elderly**: up to 20 mmHg (~3 kPa) acceptable
- **Formula** (age correction): A-a gradient = (Age/4) + 4 mmHg

### Hypoxaemia
- **PaO₂ <80 mmHg** (10.7 kPa) on room air
- **Severe**: PaO₂ <60 mmHg (8 kPa)
- **Critical**: PaO₂ <45 mmHg (6 kPa)

## 4. Mechanisms of Hypoxaemia

| Mechanism | PaO₂ | PaCO₂ | A-a gradient | Response to O₂ |
|-----------|------|--------|--------------|----------------|
| **Hypoventilation** | ↓ | ↑ | **Normal** | Yes (improve) |
| **V/Q mismatch** | ↓ | Variable | ↑ | Yes (improve) |
| **Shunt** | ↓ | Normal/low | ↑↑ | **No/poorly** |
| **Diffusion limitation** | ↓ | Variable | ↑ | Yes (improve) |
| **Low FiO₂** (altitude) | ↓ | ↓ (hypervent) | Normal | Yes |
| **Anaemia** | PaO₂ normal; ↓CaO₂ | — | Normal | No (CaO₂ issue) |

## 5. Clinical Application

### Hypoxaemia with normal A-a gradient
- **Hypoventilation** (most common cause)
- Causes: opioid, sedative, NM weakness, chest wall restriction, OSA
- ABG: ↓PaO₂ + ↑PaCO₂ + normal A-a gradient
- Treatment: support ventilation (NIV, intubation)

### Hypoxaemia with ↑A-a gradient
- **V/Q mismatch**: asthma, COPD, mild pneumonia
- **Shunt**: ARDS, lobar pneumonia, atelectasis, R→L cardiac shunt
- **Diffusion limitation**: ILD, pulmonary oedema, PH
- ABG: ↓PaO₂ + variable PaCO₂ + ↑A-a gradient

### P/F Ratio (PaO₂/FiO₂)
- **ARDS (Berlin definition)**: P/F <300 with bilateral infiltrates
  - Mild: 200 < P/F ≤ 300
  - Moderate: 100 < P/F ≤ 200
  - Severe: P/F ≤ 100
- Used for ARDS severity assessment

## 6. Diagnosis

### Step 1: Confirm hypoxaemia
- ABG: PaO₂ <80 mmHg

### Step 2: Look at PaCO₂
- **↑PaCO₂** = hypoventilation component
- **↓PaCO₂** = hyperventilation compensating

### Step 3: Calculate A-a gradient
- **Normal A-a + ↑PaCO₂** = pure hypoventilation
- **↑A-a + ↑PaCO₂** = V/Q mismatch with hypoventilation (e.g. severe COPD)
- **↑A-a + ↓PaCO₂** = V/Q mismatch or shunt (compensatory hypervent)

### Step 4: Test response to O₂
- **Improvement with O₂** = V/Q mismatch or hypoventilation
- **No improvement** = true shunt

## 7. Management

### Treat underlying cause
- **Hypoventilation**: reversal of opioid, support ventilation
- **V/Q mismatch**: bronchodilators, antibiotics, diuretics
- **Shunt**: PEEP, prone, treat cause
- **Diffusion**: treat cause (steroids for ILD, diuretics for oedema)

### Oxygen therapy
- Target SpO₂ 94–98% (88–92% if hypercapnic risk)
- **100% O₂ test**: significant shunt = no improvement

## 9. MCQs (10)

1. Normal A-a gradient in a young adult is:
   A. 0–5 mmHg
   B. **5–15 mmHg**
   C. 20–30 mmHg
   D. 40–50 mmHg
   E. >50 mmHg
   **Answer: B** — 5–15 mmHg.

2. Hypoxaemia with normal A-a gradient and ↑PaCO₂ suggests:
   A. PE
   B. **Hypoventilation**
   C. Shunt
   D. ILD
   E. Altitude
   **Answer: B** — Hypoventilation.

3. A 70-year-old has PaO₂ 75, PaCO₂ 40, FiO₂ 0.21. A-a gradient:
   A. 5 mmHg
   B. 10 mmHg
   C. **~35 mmHg (age-corrected normal)**
   D. 50 mmHg
   E. 100 mmHg
   **Answer: C** — Age-corrected: (70/4) + 4 = 21.5; actual = (150 − 50) − 75 = 25. Both normal.

4. P/F ratio <100 with bilateral infiltrates defines:
   A. Mild ARDS
   B. Moderate ARDS
   C. **Severe ARDS**
   D. Heart failure
   E. Pneumonia
   **Answer: C** — Severe ARDS.

5. Hypoxaemia with ↑A-a gradient that does NOT respond to 100% O₂ suggests:
   A. V/Q mismatch
   B. **True shunt**
   C. Hypoventilation
   D. Anaemia
   E. Altitude
   **Answer: B** — True shunt refractory to O₂.

6. The formula for PAO₂ on room air (sea level) is:
   A. 100
   B. **150 − PaCO₂/0.8**
   C. 100 − PaCO₂
   D. 100 × FiO₂
   E. 200 − PaCO₂
   **Answer: B** — Simplified alveolar gas equation.

7. Anaemia causes hypoxaemia via:
   A. ↓PaO₂
   B. **↓CaO₂ (↓Hb) with normal PaO₂**
   C. Shunt
   D. V/Q mismatch
   E. ↑PaCO₂
   **Answer: B** — ↓O₂ carrying capacity.

8. ARDS Berlin definition includes:
   A. P/F <300 with bilateral infiltrates + cardiac cause
   B. **P/F <300 with bilateral infiltrates + non-cardiac cause**
   C. P/F <200
   D. ↑PaCO₂
   E. Anaemia
   **Answer: B** — P/F <300 + bilateral infiltrates + non-cardiac oedema.

9. The most common cause of ↑A-a gradient is:
   A. Anaemia
   B. **V/Q mismatch**
   C. Altitude
   D. Anxiety
   E. Obesity
   **Answer: B** — V/Q mismatch most common.

10. A 60-year-old with severe COPD has PaO₂ 50, PaCO₂ 55, A-a 35. Pattern:
    A. Pure hypoventilation
    B. **V/Q mismatch with hypoventilation**
    C. Pure V/Q mismatch
    D. Shunt
    E. Anaemia
    **Answer: B** — ↑A-a + ↑PaCO₂ = V/Q mismatch + hypoventilation.
