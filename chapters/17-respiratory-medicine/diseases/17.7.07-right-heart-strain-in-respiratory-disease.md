# Right-heart strain in respiratory disease

> [!important]
> **Right-heart strain (RHS)** is a critical finding indicating **RV pressure overload** or **dysfunction**. Most commonly due to **massive/submassive PE**, but also in PH, cor pulmonale, ARDS, RV infarction. Recognition is vital for risk stratification and management (thrombolysis, inotropes).

Related: [[Pulmonary Embolism]], [[Pulmonary hypertension]], [[Cor pulmonale]], [[D-dimer, CTPA, and Wells score framework]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: RHS = poor prognosis in PE → consider thrombolysis. Signs: **RV/LV ratio ≥1.0 on CT, McConnell's sign (RV free wall akinesia + apical sparing), RBBB, T-inversion V1–V4, ↑BNP/troponin, hypotension**. Always check for **massive PE** if SBP <90 + RV strain.

## 1. Definition

**Right-heart strain (RHS)** = evidence of RV pressure overload or dysfunction, often from acute or chronic pulmonary disease.

## 2. Causes

### Acute
- **Massive / submassive PE** (most common acute cause)
- **ARDS** (severe)
- **Acute RV infarction**
- **Cardiac tamponade** (compresses RV)
- **Acute severe asthma** (severe dynamic hyperinflation)

### Chronic
- **Pulmonary hypertension** (PAH, CTEPH)
- **Cor pulmonale** (COPD, ILD)
- **Chronic PE**
- **Congenital heart disease** (ASD, ToF, Eisenmenger)

## 3. Clinical Features

### Symptoms
- Dyspnoea
- Syncope/pre-syncope
- Chest pain (RV ischaemia)
- Fatigue

### Signs
- **Tachycardia** (early)
- **Hypotension** (severe)
- **JVP ↑** (with prominent a wave if sinus rhythm, cv wave in TR)
- **Loud P2**
- **RV heave** (left sternal border)
- **Tricuspid regurgitation** (pansystolic murmur ↑ with inspiration)
- **Pulsatile liver** (severe TR)
- **Peripheral oedema** (chronic)
- **Cool peripheries** (low output)
- **Cyanosis**

## 4. Diagnosis

### Investigations
| Test | Finding |
|------|---------|
| **ECG** | T-inversion V1–V4, RBBB, RVH, right axis, P pulmonale, S1Q3T3 (classic PE) |
| **CXR** | Enlarged central PAs, RV enlargement, oligaemia (PE) |
| **Echo (bedside)** | **RV dilation (RV/LV ≥1)**, septal flattening (D-sign), **McConnell's sign** (RV free wall akinesia + apical sparing), TR jet velocity (PAP estimate) |
| **CTPA** | RV/LV ratio ≥1, septal bowing, contrast reflux into IVC |
| **Biomarkers** | ↑BNP/NT-proBNP, ↑troponin (RV strain) |
| **RHC** | Gold standard; mPAP >20, PVR, PCWP |

### Echo findings in acute PE (RHS)
- **RV dilation** (RV end-diastolic diameter >42 mm or RV/LV >1)
- **Septal flattening** ("D-sign")
- **McConnell's sign**: RV free wall hypokinesis + apical sparing
- **TAPSE <16 mm** (tricuspid annular plane systolic excursion)
- **TR jet velocity >2.8 m/s** (suggests PH)
- **60/60 sign**: TR jet ≤60 ms + pulmonary acceleration time <60 ms

### ECG in PE
- **Sinus tachycardia** (most common, ~80%)
- **S1Q3T3** (classic, rare)
- **T-inversion V1–V4** (RV strain)
- **RBBB** (new)
- **Right axis deviation**
- **P pulmonale**

### CT signs of severe PE / RV strain
- **RV/LV ratio ≥1.0** (best single sign)
- Septal bowing
- Reflux of contrast into IVC/hepatic veins
- PA diameter >PA aorta diameter

## 5. Management

### General
- **Treat underlying cause** (PE → anticoagulation ± thrombolysis)
- **Haemodynamic support**: IV fluids (cautious), vasopressors (noradrenaline), inotropes (dobutamine)
- **O₂** (correct hypoxaemia)
- **Avoid** intubation if possible (↑RV afterload)

### Specific to PE
| Severity | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|
| **Low risk** (no RV dysfunction) | Anticoagulation alone |
| **Intermediate (submassive)** | Anticoagulation; rescue lysis if deteriorates |
| **Massive** (SBP <90/shock + RV dysfunction) | **Systemic thrombolysis** (alteplase) |

### Specific to PH
- Treat underlying PH (PAH Rx, PTE for CTEPH)
- Avoid PAH Rx in group 2 (left heart)

## 6. Prognosis
- **RHS in PE** = higher mortality (10–25% vs <5% in low-risk PE)
- **RHS in chronic disease** = poor prognostic factor
- Reversible if underlying cause treated

## 7. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary

| Domain | Key points |

   A. S1Q3T3

3. CT RV/LV ratio ≥1.0 indicates:
   A. Normal
   B. **RV strain (severe PE)**

   E. None

   A. Common
   B. **Classic but rare**

   D. Sensitive

5. TAPSE <16 mm indicates:
   A. Normal
   B. **RV dysfunction**

   **Answer: B** — RV dysfunction.

6. The most useful biomarker for RV strain in acute PE:
   A. **BNP / NT-proBNP**
   B. ALT
   C. ALP
   D. Bilirubin
   E. None
   **Answer: A** — BNP.

7. A 50-year-old with massive PE, SBP 80, RV dilation on echo. Best:
   A. Anticoagulation only
   B. **Systemic thrombolysis (alteplase)**
   C. Aspirin
   D. Watch
   E. Steroids
   **Answer: B** — Massive PE = thrombolysis.

8. Septal flattening on echo ("D-sign") is seen in:
   A. LV failure
   B. **RV pressure overload (PE, PH)**
   C. ASD only
   D. Aortic disease
   E. None
   **Answer: B** — RV pressure overload.

9. The most common acute cause of RHS:
   A. ARDS
   B. **Massive / submassive PE**
   C. PH
   D. RV infarct
   E. Tamponade
   **Answer: B** — PE most common.

10. Hemodynamic support in acute RHS includes all EXCEPT:
    A. Cautious IV fluids

    E. O₂
    **Answer: D** — Excessive fluid worsens RV.

## 10. Flashcards

- **Q: McConnell's sign?**
  A: RV free wall akinesia + apical sparing (specific for acute PE).

- **Q: D-sign?**
  A: Septal flattening on echo (RV pressure overload).

- **Q: CT RV/LV ratio?**
  A: ≥1.0 = RV strain.

- **Q: TAPSE?**
  A: <16 mm = RV dysfunction.

  A: Sinus tachy (most common), S1Q3T3, T-inversion V1–V4, RBBB.

- **Q: Biomarkers in PE?**
  A: ↑BNP, ↑troponin = intermediate/high risk.

- **Q: Massive PE Rx?**
  A: Systemic thrombolysis (alteplase).

- **Q: Submassive PE Rx?**
  A: Anticoagulation; rescue lysis if deteriorates.

- **Q: Septal flattening cause?**
  A: RV pressure overload (PE, PH).

- **Q: Avoid in RV strain?**
  A: Excessive fluid resuscitation.
