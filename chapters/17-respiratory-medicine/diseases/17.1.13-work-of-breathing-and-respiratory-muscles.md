# Work of breathing and respiratory muscles

> [!important]
> **Work of breathing (WOB)** is the energy expended by respiratory muscles to overcome elastic and resistive loads. It is markedly increased in obstructive (asthma, COPD) and restrictive (ILD, chest wall) disease, and can lead to respiratory muscle fatigue and failure if sustained.

Related: [[Respiratory Failure]], [[Neuromuscular respiratory weakness]], [[Chest wall restriction and kyphoscoliosis]], [[ABG Interpretation]]

> [!tip] **FCPS/MRCP pearl**: WOB = 0.5 J/L normally; ↑3–10× in disease. Respiratory muscle fatigue is a key reason for ventilatory failure in NM disorders, severe asthma, and COPD. Accessory muscle use, paradoxical breathing, and ↑RR signal impending fatigue.

## 2. Definition

**Work of breathing (WOB)** = energy required to move the lungs and chest wall during breathing. Normal quiet breathing: **0.5 J/L** (~3–5% of total body O₂ consumption).

### Components of WOB
| Component | Description | Contributors |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| **Elastic work** | Overcome elastic recoil (lung + chest wall) | Surface tension, elastin, collagen |
| **Resistive work** | Overcome airway and tissue resistance | Airway diameter, flow rate, viscosity |
| **Inertive work** | Overcome inertia of tissues/gas | Usually negligible |

### Work equation
**W = ∫P × dV** (area of pressure-volume loop)

- **P** = pressure generated by respiratory muscles
- **V** = volume moved
- Work done = area inside the pressure-volume loop (Campbell diagram)

### O₂ cost of breathing
- **Normal**: 3–5% of total VO₂ (rest)
- **Mild disease**: 10–15%
- **Severe disease (e.g. ARDS)**: 25–30% or more

## 3. Respiratory Muscles

### Primary inspiratory muscles
| Muscle | Innervation | Action |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| **Diaphragm** | Phrenic nerve (C3–C5) | Principal muscle of inspiration; ↓diaphragm contracts → inspiration |
| **External intercostals** | Intercostal nerves | Lift ribs, expand thoracic cage |
| **Scalenes** | Cervical spinal nerves | Stabilise/elevate upper ribs (active in normal inspiration) |

### Accessory inspiratory muscles
- **Sternocleidomastoid** (CN XI) — lifts sternum
- **Pectoralis major/minor** — arm fixed, pulls sternum
- **Trapezius, serratus anterior** — shoulder fixation

### Expiratory muscles (active in disease, not normal quiet breathing)
- **Internal intercostals**
- **Abdominal muscles** (rectus, obliques, transversus)

### Other muscles
- **Muscles of upper airway** (genioglossus, etc.) — maintain airway patency

## 4. Pathophysiology

### WOB in disease
| Disease | Mechanism | WOB |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| **Asthma/COPD** | ↑Airway resistance + dynamic hyperinflation (flattens diaphragm) | ↑↑ |
| **ILD** | ↓Compliance (elastic work ↑) | ↑ |
| **Pneumonia/consolidation** | ↓Compliance + shunt | ↑ |
| **Pulmonary oedema** | ↓Compliance + ↑fluid | ↑↑ |
| **Chest wall disease** (kyphoscoliosis, obesity) | ↓Chest wall compliance | ↑ |
| **NM weakness** (GBS, MG) | ↓Muscle strength | ↑ to maintain ventilation |
| **Pleural effusion/PTX** | ↓Lung expansion | ↑ |

### Pressure-volume relationships
- **Compliance (C) = ΔV/ΔP**
- ↓Compliance → more work to expand
- ↑Compliance (e.g. emphysema) → elastic recoil lost, more work to exhale

### Muscle fatigue
- WOB > critical threshold → muscle fatigue → respiratory failure
- **Diaphragm fatigue** when:
  - Pdi (transdiaphragmatic pressure) <40% of maximum
  - Sustained for >45 min
- **MVV** (max voluntary ventilation) falls with fatigue

## 5. Clinical Signs of Increased WOB

### Inspection
- **Tachypnoea** (most sensitive)
- **Use of accessory muscles** (SCM, scalenes)
- **Intercostal/subcostal recession**
- **Tracheal tug** (downward pull on inspiration)
- **Paradoxical breathing** (abdominal paradox — sign of diaphragmatic fatigue)
- **Nasal flaring**
- **Pursed-lip breathing** (COPD — auto-PEEP)
- **Tripoding** (leaning forward)

### Palpation/percussion
- ↓Chest expansion
- Hyperresonance (obstructive)

### Auscultation
- **Wheeze** (obstructive)
- **Crackles** (ILD, oedema)
- **Diminished breath sounds** (NM, severe obstruction)

## 6. Diagnosis

### Spirometry
- **FEV₁, FVC, MVV** — indirect measures of muscle strength
- **MVV <60% predicted** suggests muscle weakness
- **Vital capacity <1 L or <15 mL/kg** = severe muscle weakness

### Maximum inspiratory/expiratory pressures
- **MIP (Pi_max)** = max inspiratory pressure at mouth (normal: >−80 cmH₂O)
- **MEP (Pe_max)** = max expiratory pressure
- ↓MIP suggests respiratory muscle weakness (GBS, MG, ALS, muscular dystrophy)

### Electromyography (EMG) of diaphragm
- Assess phrenic nerve function
- Useful in NM disorders

### Ultrasonography
- **Diaphragm thickness** and movement
- ↑Thickness fraction with inspiration = normal (>30%)

## 7. Management

### Reduce WOB
- **Treat underlying disease** (bronchodilators, steroids, diuretics, antibiotics)
- **Position**: sitting up, leaning forward (improves diaphragm mechanics)
- **O₂ therapy** (reduces drive to hyperventilate)
- **CPAP/BiPAP** (offloads respiratory muscles)
- **Treat pain** (reduces splinting)

### Support respiratory muscles
- **NIV** (CPAP for OSA, BiPAP for hypercapnic failure)
- **Mechanical ventilation** (invasive if needed)
- **Physiotherapy** (clearance, breathing exercises)
- **Nutritional support** (avoid overfeeding, maintain muscle mass)

### Treat underlying cause
- **NM weakness**: treat GBS (IVIG/plasma exchange), MG (pyridostigmine, immunosuppression)
- **Chest wall**: braces, weight loss (obesity), surgical correction (kyphoscoliosis)
- **COPD**: bronchodilators, oxygen, pulmonary rehab
- **Asthma**: bronchodilators, steroids

   A. <1%
   B. **3–5%**
   C. 15–20%
   D. 30–40%
   E. >50%
   **Answer: B** — 3–5%.

5. A sign of diaphragmatic fatigue is:
   A. Tachypnoea
   B. **Abdominal paradox**
   C. Wheeze
   D. Cyanosis

6. Pursed-lip breathing is a sign of:
   A. Asthma
   B. **COPD with auto-PEEP**
   C. Pneumonia
   D. PE
   E. Heart failure

7. Normal maximum inspiratory pressure (MIP) is approximately:

   C. −150 cmH₂O
   D. +50 cmH₂O
   E. +200 cmH₂O

   A. Supine
   B. **Sitting up, leaning forward**

9. WOB is increased in all of the following EXCEPT:

   **Answer: B** — Normal has low WOB.

10. MVV <60% predicted suggests:
    A. Healthy

## 13. Summary

WOB = energy for breathing. Normal: 0.5 J/L. Components: elastic + resistive. Diaphragm (C3–C5) is principal. Increased in asthma, COPD, ILD, NM weakness, obesity. Clinical signs: tachypnoea, accessory muscle use, abdominal paradox. Diagnosis: spirometry, MIP, diaphragm ultrasound. Management: treat cause, position, NIV, intubation if fatigue.
