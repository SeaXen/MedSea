# Tracheobronchomalacia (TBM)

Related: [[Bronchiectasis]], [[COPD]], [[Airway Diseases/Other obstructive and small-airway disorders|Other obstructive and small-airway disorders]], [[Functional Anatomy, Physiology, and Investigations/Indications for invasive ventilation in respiratory disease|Indications for invasive ventilation]]

> [!important]
> **TBM** = excessive airway collapse during expiration due to **weakened tracheal/bronchial cartilage** or **loss of structural support**. **Expiratory stridor/flow limitation**, recurrent infections, failed extubation. **Dynamic CT/bronchoscopy** = diagnostic. Key FCPS/MRCP: primary vs secondary, inspiratory/expiratory CT, bronchoscopy grading, stenting vs surgery.

## Learning Objectives
- Distinguish **primary (congenital)** vs **secondary (acquired)** TBM
- Recognise clinical features and differentiate from asthma/COPD/stridor
- Apply imaging (dynamic CT) and bronchoscopy for diagnosis and grading
- Manage conservatively vs stenting vs surgical aortopexy/tracheopexy

## Definition
**Tracheobronchomalacia (TBM)** = **excessive dynamic collapse** of trachea and/or bronchi during expiration (>50% reduction in cross-sectional area) due to **weakness of cartilaginous support** or **loss of posterior membranous support**.

## Classification

| Type | Cause | Typical Age | Key Features |
|------|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **Primary (Congenital)** | Developmental cartilage deficiency | Infancy/childhood | Often self-limiting (improves by 2-3 yrs); may need stenting if severe |
| **Secondary (Acquired)** | **Common** in adults | Adulthood | Chronic inflammation (COPD, asthma), post-intubation, tracheostomy, relapsing polychondritis, tumours, compression (vascular, goitre), post-lung transplant |

## Pathophysiology
- **Normal**: tracheal cartilage (C-rings) + posterior membranous wall → maintain patency
- **TBM**: cartilage weakening/atrophy → **posterior wall invaginates** during expiration → **dynamic collapse** → airflow limitation, mucus stasis, recurrent infection
- **Expiratory flow limitation** → air trapping, dyspnoea, failed weaning

## Clinical Features

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Cough** | Chronic, often productive (mucus stasis) |
| **Dyspnoea** | Exertional, worsens with supine position |
| **Wheeze/Stridor** | **Expiratory** (vs inspiratory in fixed obstruction); "honking" cough |
| **Recurrent infections** | Bronchitis, pneumonia (mucus stasis) |
| **Failed extubation** | Common ICU presentation (post-intubation TBM) |
| **Chest pain** | Tracheal traction |

## Investigations

### 1. Dynamic CT (Expiratory CT) — **First-line imaging**
- **Inspiratory + expiratory** thin-section CT
- **Diagnostic**: **>50% reduction** in tracheal/bronchial cross-sectional area on expiration
- **Quantify**: % collapse = (Insp area - Exp area) / Insp area × 100%
- **Localize**: Trachea, main bronchi, segmental bronchi

### 2. Bronchoscopy — **Gold Standard**
- **Dynamic visualisation**: collapse on expiration/coughing
- **Grading (Murai/Bragdon)** — **percent luminal reduction**:
  | Grade | Collapse | Management |
  |-------|----------|------------|
  | **Mild** | 25-50% | Conservative |
  | **Moderate** | 50-75% | Consider stenting |
  | **Severe** | >75% | Stent/surgery |
- **Associated findings**: mucus plugs, granulation tissue, purulent secretions

### 3. Pulmonary Function Tests
- **Spirometry**: obstructive pattern, **expiratory flow limitation** (flattened expiratory limb on flow-volume loop)
- **MIP/MEP**: respiratory muscle weakness may coexist

### 4. CT Angiography / MRI
- **Vascular compression** (pulsatile compression by aortic arch, innominate artery)
- **Goitre/thyroid** mass effect

## Differential Diagnosis
| Condition | Differentiating Features |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Asthma** | Reversible obstruction, no dynamic collapse on CT/bronchoscopy |
| **COPD** | Emphysema/chronic bronchitis fixed obstruction; no dynamic collapse |
| **Fixed tracheal stenosis** | Inspiratory + expiratory obstruction; fixed narrowing on CT |
| **Vocal cord dysfunction** | Inspiratory stridor, laryngeal closure on inspiration |
| **Foreign body** | Sudden onset, unilateral findings |
| **Relapsing polychondritis** | Ear/nose cartilage inflammation, saddle nose, systemic features |

## Management

### 1. Conservative (Mild/Moderate)
- **Treat underlying cause**: COPD/asthma optimisation, smoking cessation
- **Airway clearance**: chest physio, mucolytics (dornase alfa, hypertonic saline)
- **Antibiotics**: for recurrent infections (guided by culture)
- **CPAP/NIV**: nocturnal CPAP splints airway (esp. if OSA coexists)
- **Avoid sedation/supine** if possible

### 2. Stenting (Moderate/Severe, failed conservative)
| Stent Type | Indications | Complications |
|------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Silicone (Dumon®)** | Removable; trachea/main bronchi | Migration, granulation, mucus plugging |
| **Self-expanding metal (SEMS)** | Permanent; malignant/long-term | Granulation, fracture, migration, difficult removal |
| **Hybrid** | Combined | Intermediate |

> **Stenting bridge** to surgery or definitive management; **silicone preferred if removable needed**.

### 3. Surgical (Severe, refractory, localised)
| Procedure | Indication |
|-----------|------------|
| **Tracheopexy** (posterior tracheal wall suspension to spine/sternum) | Severe tracheomalacia, failed stenting |
| **Aortopexy** (aortic arch suspension to sternum) | Vascular compression + TBM (esp. innominate artery) |
| **Bronchopexy** | Segmental bronchial malacia |
| **Resection + anastomosis** | Short segment, severe, refractory |
| **Tracheal reconstruction** (slide tracheoplasty) | Long-segment congenital TBM |

## Special Populations

### Post-Intubation TBM
- **Incidence**: 1-2% after prolonged intubation (>7 days)
- **Mechanism**: pressure necrosis from cuff pressure >30 cmH₂O, ischaemia
- **Presentation**: failed extubation, stridor, re-intubation
- **Management**: tracheostomy → stenting (silicone preferred) → tracheopexy if refractory

### Relapsing Polychondritis
- **Autoimmune**: cartilage destruction (ears, nose, trachea, bronchi, joints)
- **TBM**: severe, progressive; **steroid + immunosuppressant** (methotrexate, azathioprine, rituximab)
- **Stenting** often needed; surgery high risk

### Paediatric Primary TBM
- Often **self-limiting** (improves by 2-3 years as cartilage matures)
- **Severe**: CPAP, stenting (silicone), aortopexy
- **Congenital vascular ring/sling** → surgical division

## Complications
- Recurrent pneumonia, bronchiectasis
- Respiratory failure, chronic hypercapnia
- Stent complications: migration, granulation, mucus plugging, infection
- Tracheoesophageal fistula (rare, stent erosion)

## Prognosis
- **Primary (congenital)**: often improves by age 2-3
- **Secondary (COPD/post-intubation)**: chronic, progressive without intervention
- **Stenting**: symptomatic relief 70-80%; long-term complications common
- **Surgery**: tracheopexy/aortopexy 80-90% success in experienced centres

## FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
1. **TBM** = >50% expiratory collapse of airway on dynamic CT/bronchoscopy
2. **Primary (congenital)** vs **Secondary (acquired: COPD, post-intubation, polychondritis, compression)**
3. **Dynamic CT (insp/exp)** = first-line imaging; **bronchoscopy** = gold standard grading
4. **Expiratory stridor/wheeze** (vs inspiratory in fixed stenosis)
5. **Post-extubation stridor** = suspect post-intubation TBM
6. **Stenting** (silicone preferred removable) vs **surgery** (tracheopexy/aortopexy)
6. **Post-intubation TBM** = failed extubation, stridor, consider tracheostomy → stent/tracheopexy
7. **Relapsing polychondritis** = systemic cartilage destruction + TBM

## Common Confusions / Exam Traps
- **Inspiratory vs expiratory**: TBM = **expiratory** collapse/stridor; fixed stenosis = inspiratory + expiratory
- **Bronchoscopy** = gold standard but **dynamic CT** often first-line
- **Stenting** = bridge, not cure; complications common (granulation, migration)
- **Post-intubation TBM** ≠ laryngeal oedema (both cause stridor, but TBM = tracheal collapse)
- **Tracheomalacia** = trachea; **bronchomalacia** = bronchi; often coexist

## Mnemonics
- **TBM FEATURES**: **T**racheal **B**ronchial **M**alacia = **Expiratory** collapse, **Stridor**, **M**ucus stasis, **R**ecurrent infections
- **DIAGNOSIS**: **D**ynamic **C**T (insp/exp) → **B**ronchoscopy (gold standard)
- **GRADING**: **M**ild 25-50%, **M**oderate 50-75%, **S**evere >75%
- **STENT vs SURGERY**: **S**tent = bridge (silicone removable); **S**urgery = definitive (tracheopexy/aortopexy)
- **POST-INTUBATION**: Failed extubation → Stridor → Bronchoscopy → Stent/Tracheopexy

## Summary
**Tracheobronchomalacia (TBM)** = **>50% expiratory airway collapse** due to cartilage/membranous weakness. **Primary** = congenital (often self-limiting); **Secondary** = COPD, post-intubation, relapsing polychondritis, vascular compression. **Diagnosis**: **Dynamic CT (insp/exp)** first-line (>50% collapse); **bronchoscopy** gold standard (grading: mild 25-50%, mod 50-75%, severe >75%). **Management**: conservative (CPAP, clearance) → **silicone stent** (removable bridge) → **surgery** (tracheopexy/aortopexy). **Post-intubation TBM** = failed extubation. **Expiratory stridor** = TBM; **inspiratory stridor** = fixed stenosis/VCD.

   A. Genetic
   B. **Acquired (post-intubation, post-tracheostomy, COPD, chronic infection)**
   C. Malignancy
   D. Asthma

    **Answer: B** — Stenting for severe focal disease; tracheoplasty for diffuse.
