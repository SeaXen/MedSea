# Obstructive sleep apnea

Parent: [[Upper Airway and Sleep-Related Breathing Disorders/Sleep-related breathing disorders|Sleep-related breathing disorders]]  
Heading: [[Upper Airway and Sleep-Related Breathing Disorders]]  
Related: [[Respiratory MOC]], [[Obesity hypoventilation syndrome]], [[Respiratory Failure and Ventilatory Support/Type 2 respiratory failure|Type 2 respiratory failure]], [[Oxygen Therapy and NIV]], [[ABG Interpretation]]

> [!important]
> **Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)** is recurrent **partial or complete upper-airway collapse during sleep**, causing intermittent hypoxemia, sleep fragmentation, loud snoring, and excessive daytime sleepiness.

## 1. Definition
OSA is characterized by repetitive episodes of:
- **Apnea** = cessation of airflow for at least 10 seconds despite ongoing respiratory effort
- **Hypopnea** = reduction in airflow with oxygen desaturation and/or arousal

## 2. Anatomy
### Upper airway structures relevant to OSA
- Nasopharynx, oropharynx, soft palate, uvula, tongue base, and hypopharynx
- The pharynx is a **collapsible muscular tube** without rigid cartilaginous support
- Airway caliber is influenced by:
  - soft-tissue bulk
  - mandibular size and position
  - tonsillar tissue
  - neck circumference
  - neuromuscular tone of pharyngeal dilator muscles

### Anatomical risk factors
- Obesity with parapharyngeal fat deposition
- Retrognathia / micrognathia
- Macroglossia
- Enlarged tonsils / adenoids
- Nasal obstruction

## 3. Physiology
### Normal sleep-related airway physiology
- During sleep, especially REM sleep, pharyngeal dilator muscle tone falls
- In healthy people the airway remains patent despite reduced tone

### Pathophysiology in OSA
- Narrow pharyngeal airway + reduced dilator tone during sleep
- Negative inspiratory pressure further promotes airway collapse
- Recurrent obstruction causes:
  - intermittent hypoxemia
  - hypercapnia in some episodes
  - repeated arousals
  - sympathetic activation
  - sleep fragmentation

### Physiological consequences
- Daytime somnolence from fragmented sleep
- Systemic hypertension from sympathetic surges
- Pulmonary hypertension / cor pulmonale in advanced disease or overlap states
- Increased cardiovascular and cerebrovascular risk

## 4. Classification
### By sleep-study severity using apnea-hypopnea index (AHI)
- **Mild:** 5-14 events/hour
- **Moderate:** 15-29 events/hour
- **Severe:** 30 or more events/hour

### By clinical context
- Simple OSA
- OSA with obesity hypoventilation overlap
- OSA with chronic lung disease / overlap syndrome

## 5. Etiology / Risk Factors
- Obesity
- Male sex
- Increasing age
- Family history
- Craniofacial narrowing
- Enlarged tonsils
- Alcohol or sedative use
- Hypothyroidism
- Acromegaly
- Nasal obstruction
- Supine sleep posture

## 6. Pathophysiology
Stepwise mechanism:
1. Sleep reduces pharyngeal muscle tone
2. A narrowed upper airway collapses during inspiration
3. Airflow stops despite chest/abdominal effort
4. Oxygen saturation falls and CO2 may rise transiently
5. Brief arousal restores tone and airflow
6. Cycles repeat many times nightly

## 7. Clinical Features
### Symptoms
- Loud habitual snoring
- Witnessed apneas
- Choking/gasping during sleep
- Non-refreshing sleep
- Excessive daytime sleepiness
- Morning headache
- Poor concentration / irritability
- Reduced libido
- Nocturia

### Signs
- Obesity
- Large neck circumference
- Crowded oropharynx
- Hypertension
- Features of metabolic syndrome
- In severe/late disease: signs of pulmonary hypertension or right heart failure

## 8. Investigations
### 1. Sleep study
- **Polysomnography** is the gold standard
- Records airflow, respiratory effort, oxygen saturation, sleep stage, and arousals

### 2. Overnight oximetry
- Useful screening tool
- Recurrent nocturnal desaturation suggests sleep-disordered breathing
- Less definitive than full polysomnography

### 3. ABG
- Usually normal in uncomplicated OSA while awake
- Consider if severe obesity, daytime somnolence with chronic hypercapnia, or suspected obesity hypoventilation syndrome

### 4. Clinical assessment
- BMI, neck circumference, blood pressure
- Epworth Sleepiness Scale
- Airway examination

### 5. Comorbidity evaluation
- ECG, echocardiography if pulmonary hypertension/cardiac disease suspected
- Thyroid function if clinically indicated

## 9. Interpretation Frameworks
### A. ABG interpretation
- Normal daytime ABG does **not** exclude OSA
- Persistent daytime hypercapnia suggests **obesity hypoventilation syndrome** rather than simple isolated OSA

### B. Oxygen logic
- Nocturnal oxygen desaturation in OSA is due to repeated airway obstruction
- **Oxygen alone is not definitive treatment** because it does not splint the airway open
- CPAP treats the mechanism by preventing collapse

### C. Imaging clues
- Imaging is not diagnostic in most cases
- Lateral airway narrowing, obesity-related soft tissue crowding, or craniofacial abnormalities may be contributory findings

## 10. Diagnosis
Diagnosis is based on:
- compatible symptoms/signs
- and objective demonstration of obstructive sleep-disordered breathing on sleep study

## 11. Differential Diagnosis
- Simple snoring without apnea
- **Obesity hypoventilation syndrome**
- Central sleep apnea
- Hypothyroidism-related somnolence
- Sedative/alcohol-related sleepiness
- Narcolepsy
- Depression / poor sleep hygiene

## 12. Management
### General measures
- Weight reduction
- Avoid alcohol and sedatives at night
- Sleep-position advice where relevant
- Treat nasal obstruction if present

### Specific therapy
#### Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP)
- First-line for many symptomatic moderate-severe patients
- Splints the upper airway open during sleep
- Improves symptoms, sleep quality, and blood pressure control in many patients

#### Mandibular advancement device
- Option in mild-moderate disease or CPAP intolerance

#### Surgery
- Selected anatomical cases only
- Examples: tonsil surgery, upper airway surgery, maxillofacial intervention in select patients

### Management of comorbidity
- Treat hypertension, diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular risk factors
- Evaluate for overlap with obesity hypoventilation syndrome

## 13. Complications
- Resistant hypertension
- Arrhythmia
- Ischemic heart disease
- Stroke risk increase
- Pulmonary hypertension
- Road traffic/work accidents due to sleepiness
- Poor quality of life

## 14. Red Flags / Emergencies
- Severe daytime somnolence impairing driving or machinery operation
- Suspected obesity hypoventilation with daytime hypercapnia
- Pulmonary hypertension or cor pulmonale
- Marked nocturnal desaturation with cardiorespiratory instability

## 15. FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
- OSA = **upper airway obstruction during sleep with preserved respiratory effort**
- Severity is commonly graded by **AHI**
- Gold standard diagnosis = **polysomnography**
- **CPAP** is a key treatment because it corrects the mechanism
- Daytime hypercapnia suggests **OHS**, not isolated simple OSA

## 16. Common Confusions / Exam Traps
- Do not confuse **OSA** with **central sleep apnea**
- Do not assume oxygen alone replaces CPAP
- Do not miss the overlap with obesity hypoventilation syndrome
- Snoring alone is not enough for diagnosis

## 17. One-Page Summary
- OSA is repetitive upper-airway collapse during sleep
- Typical clues: loud snoring, witnessed apneas, daytime sleepiness, obesity, hypertension
- Gold standard: polysomnography
- Severity: mild 5-14, moderate 15-29, severe ≥30 events/hour
- Treatment: weight loss, avoid sedatives/alcohol, CPAP, oral appliances in selected cases
- Think of OHS if there is **daytime hypercapnia**

## 19. MCQs
1. The key pathological event in obstructive sleep apnea is:
   - A. Reduced medullary respiratory drive
   - B. Recurrent upper airway collapse during sleep
   - C. Diffusion defect at alveolar membrane
   - D. Loss of surfactant
   - **Answer: B**

2. The gold standard test for OSA is:
   - A. CXR
   - B. Spirometry
   - C. Polysomnography
   - D. ABG alone
   - **Answer: C**

3. Severe OSA is usually defined by AHI:
   - A. 1-4/hour
   - B. 5-14/hour
   - C. 15-29/hour
   - D. ≥30/hour
   - **Answer: D**

4. A common symptom of OSA is:
   - A. Hemoptysis
   - B. Loud snoring
   - C. Clubbing
   - D. Pleuritic chest pain
   - **Answer: B**

5. First-line mechanism-correcting treatment in many symptomatic cases is:
   - A. Oxygen by nasal cannula only
   - B. CPAP
   - C. Oral steroids
   - D. Loop diuretic
   - **Answer: B**

6. Daytime hypercapnia in an obese patient with sleep-disordered breathing suggests:
   - A. Pneumothorax
   - B. OHS
   - C. Isolated mild OSA only
   - D. Pulmonary embolism
   - **Answer: B**

7. A recognized risk factor for OSA is:
   - A. Low BMI
   - B. Obesity
   - C. Neutropenia
   - D. Hyperthyroidism only
   - **Answer: B**

8. OSA contributes to all except:
   - A. Hypertension
   - B. Daytime somnolence
   - C. Pulmonary embolism as direct defining feature
   - D. Road traffic accidents
   - **Answer: C**

9. In OSA, respiratory effort during obstructive episodes is usually:
   - A. Absent
   - B. Preserved

## 20. SBA Questions
1. A 52-year-old obese man snores loudly, has witnessed apneas, and falls asleep while watching TV. Best investigation?  
   **Answer:** Polysomnography

2. A patient with OSA asks why oxygen alone is insufficient. Best explanation?  
   **Answer:** Oxygen does not prevent upper-airway collapse; CPAP splints the airway open.

3. A 48-year-old woman with BMI 42 kg/m² has sleepiness and elevated PaCO2 while awake. Most likely associated diagnosis?  
   **Answer:** Obesity hypoventilation syndrome

4. Most important modifiable risk factor for OSA?  
   **Answer:** Obesity

5. An AHI of 22/hour indicates:  
   **Answer:** Moderate OSA

6. A crowded oropharynx and large neck circumference in a sleepy snorer suggest:  
   **Answer:** Obstructive sleep apnea

7. Best first-line treatment for symptomatic severe OSA?  
   **Answer:** CPAP

8. A sleepy patient with snoring is a bus driver. Most urgent counseling point?  
   **Answer:** Driving risk due to daytime somnolence

9. Repeated sympathetic surges in OSA increase risk of:  
   **Answer:** Systemic hypertension

10. Which finding would most strongly push you to think beyond isolated OSA?  
   **Answer:** Persistent daytime hypercapnia
