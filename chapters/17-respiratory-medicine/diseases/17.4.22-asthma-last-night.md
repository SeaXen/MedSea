# Asthma — Last Night Before Exam

Related: [[Asthma]], [[COPD]], [[ABG Interpretation]], [[Oxygen Therapy and NIV]]

## 1. Core Definition
- **Asthma** is a chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterized by:
  - **variable respiratory symptoms**
  - **variable expiratory airflow limitation**
  - airway hyperresponsiveness
- Usually symptoms are **episodic and reversible**

## 2. Classic Clinical Picture
- Episodic wheeze
- Episodic breathlessness
- Cough, especially at night/early morning
- Chest tightness
- Symptoms vary over time and intensity
- Triggers: dust, pollen, exercise, viral infection, cold air, smoke

> [!important]
> Core contrast with COPD: asthma is **variable and reversible**, COPD is **persistent and not fully reversible**.

## 3. Key Pathophysiology
- Airway inflammation
- Bronchial hyperresponsiveness
- Bronchoconstriction
- Mucosal edema
- Mucus plugging
- Usually eosinophilic/atopic pattern in classic asthma
- Can progress to severe airflow limitation during acute attack

## 4. Must-Know Risk Factors / Associations
- Atopy
- Allergic rhinitis
- Family history
- Eczema
- Occupational exposure
- Viral triggers
- Exercise
- Aspirin/NSAID sensitivity in some patients

## 5. Anatomy Pearls
- Disease mainly affects the **bronchi and bronchioles**
- Smooth muscle constriction narrows airways
- Mucosal edema + mucus further reduce lumen
- In chronic disease there may be airway remodeling

## 6. Physiology Pearls
- Expiratory airflow limitation is **variable**
- Bronchospasm increases airway resistance
- Air trapping can occur in acute severe asthma
- V/Q mismatch causes hypoxemia in severe attack
- Rising PaCO2 in acute severe asthma is an ominous sign

## 7. Diagnosis
### Suggestive pattern
- recurrent variable wheeze/cough/breathlessness
- symptoms worse at night/early morning
- trigger-related symptoms
- improvement with bronchodilator

### Objective support
- obstructive spirometry with reversibility
- peak flow variability
- normal tests do not always exclude asthma if the patient is asymptomatic between attacks

## 8. Important Investigations
- **Spirometry with bronchodilator reversibility**
- Peak expiratory flow monitoring
- Pulse oximetry in acute attack
- **ABG** in severe/life-threatening asthma
- CBC / eosinophils if relevant
- Chest X-ray only if complication or alternative diagnosis suspected

## 9. Spirometry Pearls
- Obstructive pattern may be seen
- Reversibility supports asthma
- Peak flow variability is a classic exam clue

## 10. Acute Severe Asthma Warning Signs
- Unable to complete sentences
- Respiratory rate high
- Tachycardia
- Use of accessory muscles
- Silent chest
- Cyanosis
- Exhaustion
- Confusion
- Normalizing or rising PaCO2 = dangerous sign

> [!warning]
> In acute severe asthma, a **silent chest** or **rising CO2** means impending respiratory failure.

## 11. Differentials
- [[COPD]]
- Anaphylaxis
- Heart failure
- Vocal cord dysfunction
- Foreign body
- Pulmonary embolism

## 12. Long-Term Management
### Core principles
- Trigger avoidance
- Inhaler technique review
- Adherence review
- Stepwise controller therapy
- Written action plan when possible

### Drug ladder concept
- Reliever inhaler
- **ICS** is the key controller therapy
- Add LABA if not controlled
- Higher-step therapy for persistent/severe disease
- Consider biologic/specialist care in severe asthma

## 13. Acute Asthma Management
### Initial treatment
- Oxygen to maintain adequate saturation
- Repeated **short-acting beta-agonist**
- Add **ipratropium** in severe attack
- **Systemic corticosteroids** early
- Assess response repeatedly
- ABG if severe / deteriorating

### If life-threatening / poor response
- Senior help / ICU input
- Consider IV therapies according to protocol
- Monitor closely for exhaustion and CO2 rise

## 14. Red Flags / Emergencies
- Silent chest
- Cyanosis
- Hypotension or exhaustion
- Confusion / drowsiness
- Rising or normal PaCO2 in a severe attack
- Poor response to initial bronchodilator therapy

## 15. Complications
- Status asthmaticus
- Respiratory failure
- Pneumothorax / pneumomediastinum rarely
- Mucus plugging
- Side effects of recurrent steroids

## 16. Top FCPS/MRCP One-Liners
- Asthma causes **variable** airflow obstruction.
- Symptoms are often **worse at night and early morning**.
- **ICS is the cornerstone of controller treatment**.
- A **silent chest** is a life-threatening sign.
- In severe asthma, **rising PaCO2 is ominous**.
- Acute treatment = oxygen + SABA + steroids ± ipratropium.

## 17. Ultra-Short Viva Answers
### Define asthma
A chronic inflammatory airway disease characterized by variable respiratory symptoms and variable expiratory airflow limitation due to bronchial hyperresponsiveness and reversible bronchoconstriction.

### Core difference from COPD
Asthma is **episodic/variable and reversible**; COPD is more persistent and incompletely reversible.

### Most important controller drug
**Inhaled corticosteroid**.

### Dangerous ABG clue in acute severe asthma
**Rising or normal PaCO2** in a patient working hard to breathe.

## 18. Last 10 Things to Memorize
1. Variable wheeze/cough/breathlessness
2. Often worse at **night/early morning**
3. Trigger-related symptoms are common
4. Reversibility supports diagnosis
5. **ICS** is the main controller
6. Acute attack: oxygen + SABA + steroids
7. Add ipratropium in severe attack
8. **Silent chest** = life-threatening
9. Rising PaCO2 = bad sign
