# Pleural effusions (symptomatic management)

Related: [[../../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 8: Pain and Palliative Care** (24th Edition, Colvin, Fallon) — Non-Pain Symptoms in Palliative Care

## Definition

Pleural effusion in palliative care is accumulation of fluid in the pleural space, common in advanced cancer (lung, breast, lymphoma, mesothelioma, ovarian) and end-stage cardiac, renal or liver failure. Often recurrent on same side. Causes breathlessness, cough, chest discomfort. Diagnosis: clinical, CXR (blunted costophrenic angle, meniscus), confirmed by ultrasound (loculation, guided drainage). Management: symptomatic drainage (thoracentesis, up to 1-1.5L at a time to avoid re-expansion pulmonary oedema); indwelling pleural catheter (IPC, e.g., Rocket or PleurX) for recurrent malignant effusion (drains at home 2-3 times/week); pleurodesis (talc via chest drain or VATS) for malignant effusion (success 60-90%); treat underlying cause; opioids for dyspnoea; oxygen if hypoxic.

## Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: Max thoracentesis at a time? A: 1-1.5L (to avoid re-expansion pulmonary oedema).

2. 2. Q: IPC for what? A: Recurrent malignant pleural effusion; home drainage 2-3x/week.

3. 3. Q: Pleurodesis success? A: 60-90% with talc slurry or poudrage (VATS).

4. 4. Q: Treat cause? A: Cancer (chemo/RT), heart failure (diuretic), liver/renal failure.

5. 5. Q: When to avoid thoracentesis? A: Coagulopathy, small effusion, loculated.


## Confusions and Mnemonics

1. **Pleural effusion in pall care** = Drain + IPC or pleurodesis; treat cause + dyspnoea
