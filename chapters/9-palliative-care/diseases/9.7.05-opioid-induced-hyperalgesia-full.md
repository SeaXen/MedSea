# Opioid-induced hyperalgesia

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Opioid Management**

## Definition

Opioid-induced hyperalgesia (OIH) is a state of heightened pain sensitivity caused by prolonged opioid exposure, characterised by diffuse pain that is more widespread and severe than the original pain, and not relieved by opioid escalation. Mechanism: NMDA receptor activation, descending facilitation, glial activation, dynorphin release. Differentiate from tolerance (improves with escalation) and disease progression. Treatment: opioid reduction (20-30%), opioid rotation, NMDA antagonists (ketamine), non-opioid analgesics, multidisciplinary approach.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Opioid-induced hyperalgesia (OIH) is increased pain sensitivity from opioid use (prolonged/high dose), distinct from tolerance. Features: diffuse, poorly localised pain beyond original site, allodynia, hyperalgesia, often worsening despite dose escalation. Mechanism: NMDA receptor activation, central sensitisation, neuroinflammation. Risk factors: high dose, prolonged use, rapid escalation, genetic predisposition.


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Suspect when: pain worsens despite increasing dose, pain beyond original site, allodynia/hyperalgesia. Rule out disease progression. Opioid rotation helps distinguish (rotation improves OIH but not tolerance).

### Management
Reduce opioid dose (10-25% per week) or rotate to alternative (methadone has NMDA antagonist; lower OIH). NMDA antagonists: ketamine (oral, IV, intranasal), dextromethorphan. Non-opioid analgesia: paracetamol, NSAIDs, adjuvants (gabapentin, TCAs). Address central sensitisation.

## Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: OIH vs tolerance? A: Tolerance = same pain, need more opioid; OIH = increased pain sensitivity (worse with more opioid).

2. 2. Q: Mechanism? A: NMDA receptor activation, central sensitisation, neuroinflammation.

3. 3. Q: Management? A: Reduce dose, rotate to methadone (NMDA antagonist), ketamine, adjuvants.

4. 4. Q: How distinguish? A: Rotate opioid; if improves = OIH; if worse = tolerance/progression.

5. 5. Q: Risk factors? A: High dose, prolonged use, rapid escalation.


## Confusions and Mnemonics

1. **OIH = diffuse + more with opioid** (paradox)
2. **Tolerance = right shift, improves with more**; **OIH = diffuse, worse with more**
3. **OIH management = reduce + rotate + ketamine**
