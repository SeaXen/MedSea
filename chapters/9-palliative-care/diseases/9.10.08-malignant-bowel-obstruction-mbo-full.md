# Malignant bowel obstruction (MBO)

Related: [[../../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 8: Pain and Palliative Care** (24th Edition, Colvin, Fallon) — End of Life Care

## Definition

Malignant bowel obstruction is mechanical or functional obstruction of the bowel in patients with advanced cancer, most commonly ovarian, colorectal, gastric, pancreatic, or small bowel cancer. Causes: mechanical (intrinsic tumour, adhesions, extrinsic compression, hernia, intussusception) and functional (paraneoplastic pseudo-obstruction, opioid-induced, autonomic dysfunction). Clinical features: abdominal pain (colicky, then continuous), distension, vomiting (early in high, late in low obstruction), constipation/obstipation, absent flatus. Diagnosis: clinical + abdominal X-ray (dilated loops, air-fluid levels, gasless), CT abdomen to determine site/cause. Management: surgery (consider if prognosis >2-3 months, single level, performance status OK), medical (analgesia — opioid + anticholinergic hyoscine butylbromide; antiemetic — cyclizine, haloperidol, levomepromazine; antisecretory — hyoscine butylbromide, glycopyrronium, octreotide for inoperable; NG tube for vomiting, IV fluids for dehydration, dexamethasone to reduce oedema). Palliative stenting (colorectal, gastric outlet).

## Clinical Features and Presentation

MBO is mechanical or functional obstruction in advanced cancer (ovarian, colorectal, gastric, pancreatic, small bowel). Causes: tumour, adhesions, paraneoplastic, opioid, anticholinergic. Symptoms: pain (colicky, then continuous), distension, vomiting (early in high, late in low), constipation, absent flatus.


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Clinical + AXR (dilated loops, air-fluid levels) + CT (site, level, cause).

### Management
Surgery: consider if prognosis >2-3 months, single level, good PS. Medical: analgesia (opioid + anticholinergic hyoscine butylbromide), antiemetic (cyclizine, haloperidol, levomepromazine — avoid metoclopramide in mechanical), antisecretory (hyoscine butylbromide 20mg SC, glycopyrronium 800mcg/24h, octreotide 250-500mcg/24h), dexamethasone 6-8mg IV, NG tube (vomiting), IV fluids (cautiously), stenting (colorectal, gastric outlet), venting G-tube.

## Differential Diagnosis

| Differential | Distinguishing Feature |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Mechanical vs functional | Drug-induced, paraneoplastic |
| Non-malignant obstruction | Adhesion, hernia |
| Constipation | Faecal loading |



## Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: First-line medical? A: Analgesia (opioid + anticholinergic), antiemetic (cyclizine, haloperidol), antisecretory (hyoscine, glycopyrronium, octreotide), dexamethasone.

2. 2. Q: Why avoid metoclopramide? A: Prokinetic; worsens colic, contraindicated in mechanical obstruction.

3. 3. Q: Octreotide? A: Inoperable MBO; reduces secretions and pain.

4. 4. Q: Surgery consideration? A: Single level, good PS, prognosis >2-3 months.

5. 5. Q: Dexamethasone? A: Reduces bowel wall oedema, may relieve obstruction partially, antiemetic.
