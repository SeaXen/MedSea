# Catastrophic haemorrhage in palliative care

Related: [[../../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 8: Pain and Palliative Care** (24th Edition, Colvin, Fallon) — Palliative Care Emergencies

## Definition

Catastrophic haemorrhage is sudden, massive, often terminal bleeding in palliative care patients, requiring immediate emergency management. Causes: tumour erosion into major vessel (carotid blowout in head/neck cancer, aortic rupture, tumour friability — gastric, lung, cervical), coagulopathy (DIC, liver failure, anticoagulation), oesophageal varices (liver disease). Risk factors: prior bleeding, large necrotic tumour, proximity to major vessel, anticoagulation, low platelets, infection. Management: acute — call for help, sit up, apply direct pressure, IV fluids/cross-match, transfuse; consider reversing anticoagulation, tranexamic acid, platelets; control of bleeding (endoscopic/surgical/radiological if appropriate); deep sedation (midazolam + opioid IV) for catastrophic terminal bleed; anticipatory prescribing (midazolam 10mg + diamorphine 10mg, dark syringe, pre-drawn); support for family and staff.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Clinical diagnosis, bloods (FBC, coagulation, cross-match), bloods for source (rarely useful in terminal event).

### Management
Acute: call for help, sit up, apply direct pressure, IV fluids, cross-match, transfuse; consider reversing anticoagulation (vitamin K for warfarin, PCC for DOACs, protamine for heparin), tranexamic acid 1g IV/PO, platelets if thrombocytopenic. Control bleeding (endoscopic, surgical, radiological). Terminal bleed: deep sedation (midazolam 10mg + diamorphine 10mg IV, repeat) for catastrophic terminal bleed; family presence and support; staff debrief. Anticipatory prescribing: pre-drawn syringe (midazolam 10mg + diamorphine 10mg, dark syringe) in fridge; family education.
