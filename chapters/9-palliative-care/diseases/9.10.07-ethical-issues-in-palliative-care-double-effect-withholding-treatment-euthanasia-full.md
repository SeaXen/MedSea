# Ethical issues in palliative care (double effect, withholding treatment, euthanasia)

Related: [[../../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 8: Pain and Palliative Care** (24th Edition, Colvin, Fallon) — End of Life Care

## Definition

Ethical issues in palliative care include: doctrine of double effect (palliative care principle — if an intervention (e.g., high-dose opioid) has two effects, one good (pain relief) and one bad (potential respiratory depression, possible hastening of death), it is morally acceptable if the intention is the good effect, the action is not intrinsically wrong, and the bad effect is not the means to the good); withholding vs withdrawing treatment (both ethically equivalent, withdrawing often more difficult emotionally but legally equivalent); euthanasia (active, intentional ending of life on request, illegal in UK and most countries, distinct from palliative sedation or withholding treatment); physician-assisted suicide (PAS, legal in some jurisdictions — Switzerland, Netherlands, Belgium, US states Oregon/Washington). MDT, patient, family discussions; ethics committee; documentation.

## Clinical Features and Presentation

Ethical issues in palliative care: doctrine of double effect (intervention with two effects — good relief, bad potential harm; intention good, morally acceptable), withholding vs withdrawing treatment (ethically equivalent), euthanasia (active intentional ending of life on request, illegal in UK), physician-assisted suicide (PAS, legal in some jurisdictions).


## Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
None (clinical/ethical).

### Management
Doctrine of double effect: intention matters, both effects known, good effect not means to bad; allows high-dose opioids, palliative sedation. Withholding: not starting treatment (e.g., ventilation). Withdrawing: stopping treatment; ethically equivalent to withholding. Euthanasia: active intentional ending on request; illegal UK. PAS: physician-assisted suicide; legal in some jurisdictions (Switzerland, Netherlands, Belgium, US states). Palliative sedation: not euthanasia. Capacity: patient capacity for decisions. MDT: multidisciplinary team; ethics committee. Documentation: decisions, discussions, reasoning, family.
