# Pain in non-communicating and dementia patients

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Pain Physiology & Assessment**

## 1. Definition

Pain assessment in non-communicating patients (dementia, stroke, learning disability, intubated, end of life) is challenging as self-report is not possible. Use observational tools: PAINAD (Pain Assessment IN Advanced Dementia), Abbey Pain Scale, Doloplus-2 (elderly), FLACC (children), BPS (Behavioural Pain Scale, ICU). Behavioural indicators: facial expression (grimacing), vocalisation (moaning, calling out), body movement (guarding, rigidity), behaviour (agitation, aggression), physiological (tachycardia, hypertension).

## 2. Clinical Features and Presentation

Pain in non-communicating and dementia patients is under-recognised and under-treated. Signs: facial expression (grimacing, frowning), vocalisation (groaning, moaning, calling out, asking for help), body language (guarding, rubbing, restlessness, rigidity), behavioural changes (aggression, agitation, withdrawal, sleep disturbance, refusal of care, decreased appetite), physiological (tachycardia, hypertension, sweating). Tools: PAIN-AD (Pain in Advanced Dementia, 5 items 0-10 each), Abbey Pain Scale (6 items), Doloplus-2, BPS (Behavioural Pain Scale for ICU), CPOT (Critical-Care Pain Observation Tool).


## 3. Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Behavioural and physiological observation; proxy report from carers/family; consider common pain triggers (positioning, hygiene, procedures, continence care). Disease-specific: full blood count, ESR, CRP, imaging for occult fracture, urinary retention (bladder scan), faecal impaction, oral thrush, pressure ulcers.

### Management
Identify cause (thorough assessment using tools), treat cause, regular paracetamol (low threshold), regular opioid (if pain suspected, trial with careful titration and monitoring), topical (lignocaine patch, capsaicin for localised). Non-pharmacological: positioning, comfortable environment, music, touch, massage. Treat delirium, anxiety, depression. Family involvement, communication. Trial of analgesia with monitoring (N-of-1 trial).

## 6. Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: How to assess pain in advanced dementia? A: PAIN-AD scale; behavioural observation; family/carer report.

2. 2. Q: Why is pain under-treated in dementia? A: Communication difficulties, atypical presentation, attribution to disease/behavioural issues, lack of tools.

3. 3. Q: Trial of analgesia principle? A: If pain suspected, treat and monitor; if no improvement, consider non-pain cause.

4. 4. Q: Common under-recognised pain in dementia? A: Musculoskeletal, dental, constipation, urinary retention, pressure ulcers, fracture (especially post-fall).

5. 5. Q: PAIN-AD components? A: Breathing, vocalisation, facial expression, body language, consolability.
