# Incident pain

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Cancer Pain Syndromes**

## 1. Definition

Incident pain is a subtype of breakthrough pain that is triggered by a specific event or activity (movement, coughing, weight-bearing, dressing changes, swallowing). Common in bone metastases (incident on weight-bearing), post-surgical, post-radiation, mucositis, neuropathic pain. Often severe, transient (peaks 3-10 min, lasts ~30 min), with rapid onset. Treatment: optimal background opioid; rescue dose (10% of total daily dose); rapid-onset opioids (sublingual fentanyl — Abstral, Effentora, PecFent, intranasal fentanyl); activity modification; radiotherapy for bone metastases; preventive analgesia (NSAID 30-60 min before activity).

## 2. Clinical Features and Presentation

Incident pain (volitional or non-volitional) is pain triggered by an activity or event (movement, cough, dressing, weight-bearing, swallowing, defecation) in a patient with otherwise stable background pain. Common in bone metastases (movement), neuropathic (e.g., dressing-related in PHN), and procedural pain. Predictable (volitional) or unpredictable (non-volitional).


## 3. Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Characterise trigger and predictability. Distinguish from breakthrough, end-of-dose, and background pain.

### Management
Pre-empt with rescue opioid dose 15-30 min before activity (volitional incident). For non-volitional, consider immediate-onset opioid (sublingual, buccal, intranasal fentanyl). Optimise background pain control. Non-pharm: pacing, positioning, physiotherapy, immobilisation, supportive devices. Interventional: nerve blocks for predictable incident pain (e.g., coeliac plexus for pancreatic cancer).

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

| Differential | Distinguishing Feature |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Breakthrough pain | Spontaneous, less predictable |
| End-of-dose failure | Predictable, before next regular dose |
| Uncontrolled background | Constant, not activity-related |



## 6. Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: Definition of incident pain? A: Pain triggered by activity or event in patient with stable background pain.

2. 2. Q: Management of volitional incident pain? A: Pre-empt with rescue dose 15-30 min before activity.

3. 3. Q: Immediate-onset opioid for non-volitional? A: Sublingual, buccal, or intranasal fentanyl (rapid onset 5-15 min).

4. 4. Q: Common cause in cancer? A: Bone metastases, pathological fracture, neuropathic (plexus, nerve).

5. 5. Q: Interventional option for predictable incident pain? A: Nerve blocks (e.g., coeliac plexus block for pancreatic cancer — pre-empts pain).
