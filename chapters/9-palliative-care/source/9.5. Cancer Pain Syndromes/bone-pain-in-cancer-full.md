# Bone pain in cancer

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Cancer Pain Syndromes**

## 1. Definition

Bone pain is the most common cancer pain syndrome, caused by primary bone tumours or, more commonly, bone metastases (breast, prostate, lung, kidney, thyroid, multiple myeloma). Pathophysiology: osteoclast activation, inflammatory mediators, periosteal stretching, microfractures, nerve entrapment. Features: deep, aching, worse on movement/weight-bearing (incident pain), constant background. Treatment: WHO ladder + NSAIDs, bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid 4mg IV q3-4w) or denosumab (120mg SC q4w), radiotherapy (local, hemibody), radiopharmaceuticals (strontium-89, samarium-153, radium-223 for prostate), orthopaedic stabilisation.

## 2. Clinical Features and Presentation

Bone pain in cancer is common (60-80% of metastatic cancer, especially breast, prostate, lung, kidney, thyroid, myeloma). Mechanisms: tumour-induced osteolysis, osteoblast activation, mechanical instability, periosteal stretching, pathological fracture, nerve compression, cytokine release. Pain: deep, aching, worse on weight-bearing/movement, often worse at night. May be incident pain (movement-related). Pathological fracture is a major complication.


## 3. Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Imaging: X-ray (lytic/blastic lesion, but needs 30-50% bone loss to be visible), CT (early bone destruction), MRI (soft tissue + marrow, most sensitive), bone scan (Tc-99m, sensitive for metastases). Bloods: calcium (hypercalcaemia), alkaline phosphatase (bone turnover), FBC (anaemia, marrow infiltration), PSA (prostate), paraprotein (myeloma). Biopsy for histology if primary unknown.

### Management
Multimodal: WHO ladder + adjuvant analgesia; bone-targeted agents (bisphosphonates — zoledronic acid 4mg IV monthly, denosumab 120mg SC monthly, both reduce SREs and pain); radiotherapy (external beam for localised pain, 8Gy single fraction or 20Gy/5 fractions; radiopharmaceuticals — strontium-89, samarium-153 for widespread bone mets); surgery for instability/pathological fracture; cementoplasty (vertebroplasty, kyphoplasty for vertebral lesions); interventional (nerve blocks, intrathecal); supportive (mobility, weight-bearing, orthotics).

## 6. Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: Most common cancers with bone metastases? A: Breast, prostate, lung, kidney, thyroid, myeloma.

2. 2. Q: Bone-targeted therapy options? A: Bisphosphonates (zoledronic acid), denosumab (anti-RANKL); reduce SREs and pain.

3. 3. Q: Radiotherapy doses for bone pain? A: Single 8Gy (most common) or 20Gy/5 fractions; radiopharmaceuticals (strontium-89, samarium-153) for widespread.

4. 4. Q: Mechanism of bone pain? A: Tumour osteolysis, osteoblast activation, periosteal stretching, mechanical instability, cytokine release.

5. 5. Q: Vertebral augmentation? A: Vertebroplasty/kyphoplasty for vertebral compression fractures; cement injection; rapid pain relief.
