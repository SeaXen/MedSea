# Opioid pharmacology overview

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Opioid Analgesics**

## 1. Definition

Opioids bind to opioid receptors (μ, δ, κ) for analgesia. Full agonists: morphine, oxycodone, hydromorphone, fentanyl, methadone, codeine (prodrug), tramadol. Partial agonists: buprenorphine (μ partial, κ antagonist). Mixed agonist-antagonist: pentazocine, nalbuphine, butorphanol. Side effects mediated by μ-receptors: constipation (gut), N&V (CTZ), sedation (CNS), respiratory depression (brainstem), pruritus (μ in mast cells), miosis (Edinger-Westphal nucleus). Tolerance, dependence (physical), addiction (psychological — different).

## 2. Clinical Features and Presentation

Opioids bind to μ (mu), κ (kappa), δ (delta) opioid receptors in CNS, peripheral nerves, GI. μ-receptor: analgesia, sedation, euphoria, respiratory depression, miosis, constipation, pruritus, N&V. κ: analgesia, sedation, diuresis, dysphoria. δ: analgesia, anxiolysis. Receptor types: full agonist (morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl, methadone, codeine, hydromorphone, tramadol, tapentadol), partial agonist (buprenorphine — ceiling effect for respiratory depression at higher doses), mixed agonist-antagonist (pentazocine, nalbuphine — avoid with full agonist due to withdrawal).


## 3. Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Pain assessment, baseline renal function (morphine metabolites accumulate in renal failure — switch to fentanyl/buprenorphine), liver function (metabolites in liver failure), respiratory rate, sedation score (Pasero Opioid-Induced Sedation Scale, POSS), bowel function. No routine blood monitoring needed for stable opioid. ECG before methadone (QT prolongation).

### Management
WHO ladder: mild pain = paracetamol ± NSAID; moderate = weak opioid (codeine, tramadol); severe = strong opioid (morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl, buprenorphine). Always co-prescribe laxative (especially for constipation) and antiemetic (for N&V first 1-2 weeks). Regular + breakthrough doses. Switch to alternative opioid if inadequate response or side effects (rotation). Naloxone for overdose (3-5mcg IV, repeat).

## 6. Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: Three opioid receptors? A: μ (mu, analgesia, RD, constipation), κ (kappa, analgesia, sedation, dysphoria), δ (delta, analgesia, mood).

2. 2. Q: Weak vs strong opioid? A: Weak: codeine, tramadol (ceiling effect); strong: morphine, oxycodone, fentanyl, buprenorphine.

3. 3. Q: Buprenorphine classification? A: Partial agonist; ceiling for respiratory depression but not for analgesia (controversial at high doses).

4. 4. Q: Why switch opioid in renal failure? A: Morphine metabolites (M3G, M6G) accumulate; switch to fentanyl or buprenorphine.

5. 5. Q: Opioid-induced hyperalgesia vs tolerance? A: OIH = increased pain sensitivity to opioid, treated by reducing dose or switching; tolerance = decreased effect of same dose, treated by increasing dose.
