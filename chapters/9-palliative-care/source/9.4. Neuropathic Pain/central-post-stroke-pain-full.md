# Central post-stroke pain

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Neuropathic Pain**

## 1. Definition

Central post-stroke pain (CPSP, thalamic pain syndrome, Dejerine-Roussy syndrome) is neuropathic pain arising from a CNS lesion (typically thalamic stroke, but any stroke). Onset weeks-months after stroke. Features: burning, electric, aching in the affected side, often hemibody distribution; sensory loss; allodynia, hyperalgesia. Treatment: TCAs (amitriptyline — first-line for CPSP specifically), gabapentin, pregabalin, lamotrigine; tramadol; neuromodulation (deep brain stimulation, motor cortex stimulation).

## 2. Clinical Features and Presentation

Central post-stroke pain (CPSP, thalamic pain syndrome) is neuropathic pain developing after a stroke (typically thalamic, brainstem, or other CNS lesions), in the body region corresponding to the lesion. Affects 10% of stroke patients, often months-years after. Features: burning, aching, pressing, lacerating, often with allodynia, hyperalgesia, dysaesthesia. Often associated with sensory loss (thalamic pain = Dejerine-Roussy syndrome).


## 3. Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
MRI brain (infarct or other lesion), correlate with pain distribution. Trial of analgesia with monitoring.

### Management
First-line: amitriptyline 25-150mg nocte (most evidence), gabapentin, pregabalin, duloxetine. Second-line: lamotrigine, carbamazepine, fluvoxamine. Refractory: motor cortex stimulation, deep brain stimulation, spinal cord stimulation. Limited evidence for opioids.

| Differential | Distinguishing Feature |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Spasticity | Muscle tone, not pain |
| Complex regional pain | Localised, autonomic features |
| Cervical/lumbar radiculopathy | Pre-existing, not post-stroke |



## 6. Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: What is thalamic pain syndrome? A: Central post-stroke pain (Dejerine-Roussy) from thalamic infarct; contralateral burning pain with sensory loss.

2. 2. Q: First-line treatment? A: Amitriptyline (TCA, most evidence).

3. 3. Q: When does pain develop after stroke? A: Days to years (most within 1 year).

4. 4. Q: Brain regions most associated? A: Thalamus, brainstem, parietal cortex, insula.

5. 5. Q: Prevalence after stroke? A: 10% of stroke patients (up to 30% of sensory strokes).
