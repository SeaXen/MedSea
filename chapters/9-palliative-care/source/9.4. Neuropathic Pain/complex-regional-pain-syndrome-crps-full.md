# Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)

Related: [[../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> Davidson 24e Ch 8 (Colvin, Fallon) — **Neuropathic Pain**

## 1. Definition

Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS, formerly reflex sympathetic dystrophy, causalgia) is a chronic neuropathic pain condition affecting a limb, often after trauma, surgery, or stroke, with disproportionate pain, sensory changes, vasomotor, sudomotor, motor/trophic changes. Two types: Type 1 (no nerve injury, was RSD) and Type 2 (definitive nerve injury, was causalgia). Budapest diagnostic criteria. Treatment: multidisciplinary — physiotherapy (graded motor imagery, mirror therapy), TCAs, gabapentin, ketamine (oral/topical/IV), bisphosphonates, sympathetic blocks, SCS.

## 2. Clinical Features and Presentation

Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) is a chronic neuropathic pain disorder, type 1 (no nerve injury, was reflex sympathetic dystrophy) or type 2 (with nerve injury, was causalgia). Often after trauma (fracture, sprain, surgery, stroke). Budapest criteria: persistent pain disproportionate to injury, with sensory (allodynia, hyperalgesia), vasomotor (temperature, colour asymmetry), sudomotor (oedema, sweating) abnormalities, motor/trophic (weakness, dystonia, atrophy, hair/nail changes) signs. CRPS typically affects distal limb, often unilateral.


## 3. Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Clinical (Budapest criteria — 4 categories, need ≥1 sign in ≥3 categories for diagnosis). Imaging: X-ray (patchy demineralisation in late), MRI (oedema, atrophy, bone marrow oedema). Bone scan: increased uptake in affected limb (early).

### Management
Multimodal, multidisciplinary: early mobilisation (key), physiotherapy (graded exercise, desensitisation, mirror therapy), occupational therapy. Pharmacological: NSAIDs, paracetamol, gabapentin, pregabalin, amitriptyline, duloxetine, ketamine (oral or IV), bisphosphonates (especially if bone involvement), corticosteroids (early), topical capsaicin or lidocaine. Interventional: sympathetic blocks (stellate ganglion for upper limb, lumbar sympathetic for lower), spinal cord stimulation (for refractory). Psychological support.

## 6. Viva Questions

1. 1. Q: Budapest criteria for CRPS? A: Persistent pain disproportionate to injury + sensory/vasomotor/sudomotor/motor signs (≥1 sign in ≥3 categories).

2. 2. Q: Difference CRPS type 1 vs 2? A: Type 1: no nerve injury (was RSD); Type 2: with nerve injury (was causalgia).

3. 3. Q: First-line treatment? A: Physiotherapy (graded, desensitisation, mirror therapy) + adjuvant analgesia.

4. 4. Q: Sympathetic block indication? A: Refractory CRPS with sympathetic features (temperature, sweating).

5. 5. Q: Bisphosphonates in CRPS? A: Yes, can help especially with bone oedema, allodynia, stiffness.
