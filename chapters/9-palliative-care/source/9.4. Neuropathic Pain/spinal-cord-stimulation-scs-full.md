# Spinal cord stimulation (SCS)

Related: [[../../../Pain and Palliative Care MOC]], [[../../../Davidson Chapter 8 - Pain and Palliative Care Hierarchy]]

> [!info] Davidson alignment: **Chapter 8: Pain and Palliative Care** (24th Edition, Colvin, Fallon) — Neuropathic Pain

## 1. Definition

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is an interventional neuromodulation technique for chronic refractory neuropathic pain. Implanted electrode in epidural space delivers electrical stimulation to dorsal columns, modulating pain signals. Indications: failed back surgery syndrome (FBSS, most common), CRPS, refractory neuropathic pain (peripheral, PHN, diabetic neuropathy), refractory angina, ischaemic limb pain (controversial). Patient selection: trial (temporary electrode, 1-2 weeks), then permanent implant if ≥50% pain relief. Mode: traditional (paresthesia-based), high-frequency (10kHz, paresthesia-free), burst, dorsal root ganglion (DRG). Evidence: good for FBSS and CRPS; less robust for other indications. Multidisciplinary team (pain, neurosurgery, psychology). Contraindications: untreated psychiatric disease, drug abuse, infection, anticoagulation (relative).

## 2. Clinical Features and Presentation

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is an interventional neuromodulation technique for chronic refractory neuropathic pain. Implanted epidural electrode delivers electrical stimulation to dorsal columns, modulating pain signals (gate control theory). Indications: failed back surgery syndrome (FBSS, most common, 70% success), complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS, 70% success), refractory neuropathic pain (PHN, diabetic neuropathy), refractory angina, ischaemic limb pain (controversial). Modes: traditional (paresthesia-based, 30-60Hz), high-frequency (10kHz, paresthesia-free), burst, dorsal root ganglion (DRG) stimulation. Patient selection: trial (temporary electrode, 1-2 weeks), then permanent implant if ≥50% pain relief. Multidisciplinary team (pain, neurosurgery, psychology).


## 3. Investigation and Management Summary

### Investigations
Patient selection: trial period (1-2 weeks temporary electrode). Response threshold: ≥50% pain reduction. Psychological evaluation. Imaging: MRI spine (rule out lesion, assess epidural space). Trial outcomes: pain, function, QoL, opioid use. Permanent implant: device placement, programming, follow-up.

### Management
Trial: temporary electrode, 1-2 weeks; ≥50% pain reduction → proceed to permanent. Permanent implant: generator (battery — rechargeable ~10 years, non-rechargeable replace), electrode (cervical, thoracic, lumbar based on pain location), programming. Modes: traditional (paresthesia required), high-frequency 10kHz (paresthesia-free), burst, DRG. Multidisciplinary follow-up. Complications: lead migration (10-20%), fracture, infection, paralysis (rare), hardware failure, battery depletion, CSF leak. Cost: high (~£10-20k) but cost-effective if successful.
