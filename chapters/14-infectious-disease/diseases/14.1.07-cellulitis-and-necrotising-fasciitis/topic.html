INFECTIOUS DISEASE
Cellulitis and Necrotising Fasciitis
1 Learning Objectives
  • Differentiate cellulitis, erysipelas, and necrotising fasciitis
  • Apply clinical scoring (LRINEC, Eron) for severity
  • Choose empirical antibiotics by syndrome and severity
  • Recognise surgical emergency criteria for NF
  • Manage diabetic foot and special populations
2 Cellulitis and Erysipelas
Definitions
ConditionDepthTypical Appearance
-------------------------------------
**Erysipelas**Upper dermis + superficial lymphatics**Well-demarcated**, raised, bright red, shiny
**Cellulitis**Deep dermis + subcutaneous tissue**Poorly demarcated**, deeper red, less raised
**Necrotising fasciitis**Deep fascia + subcutaneous tissue**Rapid progression**, pain out of proportion, crepitus, necrosis
Aetiology
SyndromeCommon Pathogens
----------------------------
**Erysipelas****Strep pyogenes (GAS)** > Staph aureus
**Cellulitis (non-purulent)**Strep pyogenes, other Strep (Groups B, C, G)
**Cellulitis (purulent)****Staph aureus** (MSSA/MRSA)
**Cellulitis (diabetic foot)**Polymicrobial (Staph, Strep, Gram-neg, anaerobes)
**Bite wounds**Pasteurella, Capnocytophaga, Staph, anaerobes
**Water exposure**Vibrio vulnificus, Aeromonas, Mycobacterium marinum

ClassFeaturesManagement

LRINEC Score (Life-threatening Necrotising Soft Tissue Infection)
ParameterPoints
-------------------
**CRP >150 mg/L**4
**WBC >25 ×10⁹/L**2
**WBC 15–25**1
**Haemoglobin <11 g/dL**1
**Sodium <135 mmol/L**2

Score ≥6 = high risk NF; 6–7 intermediate; ≥8 high probability. Not diagnostic — clinical suspicion paramount.

3 Necrotising Fasciitis
Types
TypePathogensTypical Context
----------------------------------
**Type I (Polymicrobial)**Aerobes + anaerobes (Staph, Strep, Enterobacterales, Bacteroides, Clostridium)Post-surgical, perineal (Fournier's), diabetic
**Type II (Monomicrobial)****Strep pyogenes (GAS) ± Staph aureus**Healthy, trauma, varicella
**Type III (Marine)**Vibrio vulnificus, AeromonasSaltwater exposure, liver disease
**Type IV (Fungal)**Candida, ZygomycetesImmunocompromised
Clinical Diagnosis (Clinical Suspicion > Scores)
Red FlagSignificance
------------------------
**Pain out of proportion** to exam**Hallmark** of NF
**Rapid progression** (hours)Surgical emergency
**Pain beyond erythema**Deep involvement
**Crepitus** (gas in tissues)Gas-forming organisms
**Skin anaesthesia** (nerve destruction)Deep fascia involvement
**Bronzing/dusky discoloration**Necrosis
**Bullae/haemorrhagic blisters**Vascular thrombosis
**Rapidly expanding**Hourly progression
Management

#### Immediate (Emergency)

  1. Call surgery/orthopaedics — debridement within hours
  2. IV fluids + vasopressors (septic shock common)
  3. Empirical IV antibiotics (broad-spectrum, see below)
  4. Tetanus prophylaxis

#### Empirical Antibiotics (NF) <table class="ctable"><tr><th>Regimen</th><th>Coverage</th></tr><tr><td>---------</td><td>----------</td></tr><tr><td>Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h</td><td>Broad (Gram+, Gram-, anaerobes)</td></tr><tr><td>OR Meropenem 1g IV q8h</td><td>If penicillin allergy / severe sepsis</td></tr><tr><td>+ Clindamycin 900mg IV q8h</td><td>Toxin inhibition (GAS, Clostridium)</td></tr><tr><td>+ Vancomycin 15–20mg/kg q12h</td><td>If MRSA suspected</td></tr></table>

Clindamycin essential for toxin-mediated NF (GAS, Clostridium) — inhibits protein synthesis

#### Surgical Management

  • Early, aggressive, repeated debridement (every 24–48h until healthy tissue)
  • Fasciotomy if compartment syndrome
  • Amputation if limb non-viable
  • Negative pressure wound therapy post-debridement
  • Hyperbaric oxygen (adjunct, evidence limited)
4 Cellulitis Treatment
Outpatient (Eron I)
RegimenDoseDuration
-------------------------
**Flucloxacillin 500mg QDS PO**1st-line (MSSA, Strep)5–7d (extend if slow response)
**Clarithromycin 500mg BD** (penicillin allergy)Alternative5–7d
**Doxycycline 100mg BD** (if MRSA risk)Alternative5–7d
Inpatient (Eron II–IV)
SeverityRegimen
-------------------
**Non-purulent (Eron II)****Flucloxacillin 2g IV q6h** (+/− benzylpenicillin 2.4g IV q6h if Strep suspected)
**Purulent / MRSA risk****Vancomycin 15–20mg/kg q12h IV** (trough 15–20)
**Severe / sepsis (Eron III–IV)****Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g q6h** +/− Vancomycin
**Diabetic foot / polymicrobial****Pip-tazo** or **Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole**
Special Situations
ScenarioKey Points
----------------------
**Diabetic foot**Offloading + vascular assessment + polymicrobial cover (Pip-tazo)
**Lymphoedema / venous insufficiency**Compression + long-term penicillin prophylaxis if recurrent
**Periorbital / orbital**Ceftriaxone + metronidazole; ENT/ophthalmology review
**Bite wounds**Co-amoxiclav (Pasteurella, anaerobes); rabies/tetanus
**Water exposure**Doxycycline + ceftriaxone (Vibrio, Aeromonas)
**Recurrent (>2/year)**Prophylactic penicillin V 250mg BD / erythromycin
5 Fournier's Gangrene (Perineal NF)
FeatureDetails
------------------
**Site**Scrotum, perineum, genitalia, abdominal wall
**Common**Diabetic males, perineal trauma, colorectal surgery
**Pathogens**Polymicrobial (Type I)
**Urethral involvement**Suprapubic catheter if needed
**Management****Emergency debridement** + broad antibiotics + orchidectomy if testicular involvement
6 Complications
ComplicationRisk Factors
----------------------------
**Sepsis / septic shock**Delayed treatment, NF, immunocompromise
**Amputation**NF, severe diabetic foot, vascular compromise
**Scarring / contracture**Deep NF, delayed debridement
**Recurrent cellulitis**Lymphoedema, venous insufficiency, immunosuppression
**Osteomyelitis**Diabetic foot, contiguous spread
7 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • NF = surgical emergency — debridement within hours, not days
  • Pain out of proportion = hallmark of NF
  • LRINEC ≥6 = consider NF (but clinical suspicion > score)
  • Clindamycin essential for toxin-mediated NF (GAS, Clostridium)
  • Erysipelas = well-demarcated, raised, superficial (Strep)
  • Cellulitis = poorly demarcated, deep (Staph/Strep)
  • Eron classification guides admission/IV vs oral
  • Recurrent cellulitis → prophylactic penicillin V 250mg BD

9 Common Confusions / Exam Traps
  • Waiting for imaging/LRINEC before surgery in NF = WRONG (clinical diagnosis)
  • Using flucloxacillin alone for NF = inadequate (need broad + clindamycin)
  • Confusing erysipelas/cellulitis = erysipelas = raised, well-demarcated
  • Missing diabetic foot = check pulses, sensation, offloading
  • Not giving clindamycin in NF = misses toxin inhibition
  • LRINEC <6 does not rule out NF = clinical suspicion > score
10 Mnemonics
  • NF signs: Pain out of proportion, Rapid progression, Anaesthesia, Crepitus, Erythema spreading = PRACE
  • LRINEC: CRP, WBC, Hb, Na, Cr, Glucose = CWHNCG
  • Cellulitis vs Erysipelas: Erysipelas = Elevated, Edge well-defined
  • NF antibiotics: Pip-tazo + Clindamycin + Vanco = PCV
  • Clindamycin in NF: Toxin Inhibition = TI
11 One-Page Revision Summary

Cellulitis = deep skin infection; NF = fascial necrosis (surgical emergency)

  • Cellulitis: Flucloxacillin PO/IV; admit if systemic (Eron II–IV)
  • Erysipelas: Superficial, well-demarcated, Strep
  • NF: Pain out of proportion, rapid progression, crepitus, anaesthesia
  • LRINEC ≥6 = suspect NF; clinical suspicion > score
  • NF antibiotics: Pip-tazo + Clindamycin + Vancomycin
  • Surgery: Debridement within hours, repeat q24–48h
  • Fournier's = perineal NF; emergency debridement
  • Recurrent cellulitis → Penicillin V 250mg BD prophylaxis
