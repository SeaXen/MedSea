SEA KNOWLEDGE STUDY NOTES
INFECTIOUS DISEASE
Intra-abdominal Infections (Secondary Peritonitis, Abscess)
1 Learning Objectives
  • Classify IAI: primary (SBP), secondary (perforation), tertiary (recurrent)
  • Diagnose using clinical, laboratory, imaging criteria
  • Apply source control principles (surgery, percutaneous drainage)
  • Select empirical antibiotics by acquisition (community vs healthcare) and MDR risk
  • Determine treatment duration (4 days post-source-control)
  • Manage special scenarios: SBP, tertiary peritonitis, pancreatic necrosis infection, liver abscess
  • Recognise when antifungal coverage needed
2 Classification
TypeDefinitionTypical Pathogens
-------------------------------------
**Primary (SBP)**Ascites fluid infection **without** surgically treatable source (cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome)*E. coli*, *Klebsiella*, *Streptococcus* spp., *Enterococcus* (usually monomicrobial)
**Secondary****Perforation/rupture** of GI tract (appendicitis, diverticulitis, peptic ulcer, ischaemic bowel, trauma, anastomotic leak)**Polymicrobial**: Gram-neg (*E. coli*, *Klebsiella*) + **Anaerobes** (*Bacteroides fragilis*) ± Enterococcus
**Tertiary****Recurrent/persistent** infection ≥48h after adequate source control + antibiotics**MDR**: *Pseudomonas*, *Acinetobacter*, *Enterococcus* (VRE), *Candida*, *Enterobacteriaceae* (ESBL/CRE)
[!key] Source control (surgery/drainage) = PRIMARY treatment. Antibiotics alone FAIL without source control. Tertiary peritonitis = failure of source control or host factors.

4 Source Control — The DEFINITIVE Treatment
SourceIntervention
----------------------
**Perforated peptic ulcer**Laparoscopic/primary repair + omental patch
**Appendicitis**Appendicectomy (+/- drainage if abscess)
**Diverticulitis (perforated)**Resection (Hartmann's) or laparoscopic lavage (selected)
**Anastomotic leak**Re-laparotomy, diversion, drainage
**Ischaemic bowel**Resection of necrotic segment
**Intra-abdominal abscess****Percutaneous drainage (CT/US-guided)** ± surgery
**Infected pancreatic necrosis****Step-up approach**: percutaneous drainage → endoscopic necrosectomy → minimally invasive surgery
[!critical] Source control within 6–12h of diagnosis. Antibiotics WITHOUT source control = treatment failure. Damage control surgery for unstable: quick source control, temporary abdomen closure, ICU resuscitation, planned re-laparotomy.

6 Treatment Duration — STOP-IT Trial Evidence
ScenarioDuration
--------------------
**Adequate source control achieved****4 days FIXED** after source control (non-inferior to 8–10 days)
**Source control NOT achievable / inadequate**Continue until clinical resolution + source control achieved
**SBP**5 days (extend if slow response)
**Infected pancreatic necrosis**Individualised (usually 2–4 weeks after drainage)
[!key] STOP-IT trial (2015): 4 days post-source-control = non-inferior to longer courses. Adequate source control = all purulent material removed, no ongoing leak, drains output <200mL/day enteric content.
7 Special Scenarios
Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)
  • Diagnosis: Ascitic fluid PMN ≥250/mm³ (send in blood culture bottles at bedside)
  • Empirical: Cefotaxime 2g IV 8h ×5d (or Ceftriaxone 2g IV 24h)
  • Albumin: 1.5g/kg day 1 + 1g/kg day 3 (prevents HRS, ↓ mortality)
  • Prophylaxis: Secondary prophylaxis after 1st episode (Norfloxacin 400mg PO daily or TMP-SMX) — until transplant or resolution of ascites
  • Primary prophylaxis: In cirrhosis with ascitic protein <1.5g/dL + Child-Pugh ≥9 or variceal bleed (Norfloxacin 400mg daily)
Tertiary Peritonitis
  • Definition: Recurrent/persistent peritonitis ≥48h after adequate source control + antibiotics
  • Pathogens: MDR Gram-neg (Pseudomonas, Acinetobacter, ESBL/CRE), Enterococcus (VRE), Candida
  • Management: Source control re-assessment (CT, re-laparotomy), broad antibiotics (Meropenem ± Vancomycin ± Fluconazole/Echinocandin), immunomodulation, nutrition
Infected Pancreatic Necrosis
  • Step-up approach (PANTER trial):
  1. Percutaneous drainage (first)
  2. Endoscopic transluminal necrosectomy (if drainage fails)
  3. Minimally invasive surgery (VARD) → Open necrosectomy (last)
  • Antibiotics: Meropenem (penetrates necrosis) ± anti-MRSA ± antifungal
  • Timing: Delay intervention ≥4 weeks if possible (walled-off necrosis)

8 Antifungal Coverage Indications
IndicationPreferred Agent
-----------------------------
**Candida isolated from intra-abdominal culture****Echinocandin (Caspofungin/Micafungin/Anidulafungin)** if unstable/fluconazole-R; Fluconazole if stable/susceptible
**High-risk clinical scenario (no culture yet):** recent broad abx, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric/oesophageal leak, prolonged ICU**Empirical echinocandin** (preferred over fluconazole for critically ill)
**Candida in pancreatic necrosis****Echinocandin**
[!tip] Echinocandin preferred for empirical/definitive therapy in critically ill (fungicidal, no drug interactions, broad Candida coverage). Fluconazole if stable, susceptible, no recent azole exposure.
9 FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Points
  • IAI classification: primary (SBP), secondary (perforation), tertiary (recurrent post-source-control)
  • Source control = DEFINITIVE treatment (surgery/drainage); antibiotics = adjunct
  • Community secondary peritonitis: Pip-tazo 4.5g 6h (1st line) covers Gram-neg + anaerobes + some Enterococcus
  • Healthcare-associated/high MDR risk: Meropenem 1g 8h (preferred) ± Vancomycin ± Fluconazole/Echinocandin
  • Enterococcus coverage: NOT routine; add if prosthetic material, severe sepsis, immunosuppression, prior Enterococcus
  • Candida coverage: add if recent broad abx, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric leak
  • Duration: 4 days FIXED post-adequate source control (STOP-IT trial)
  • SBP: Ascitic PMN ≥250 → Cefotaxime 5d + Albumin 1.5g/kg D1 + 1g/kg D3
  • SBP prophylaxis: secondary (after 1st episode) norfloxacin/TMP-SMX; primary if ascitic protein <1.5 + Child-Pugh ≥9
  • Infected pancreatic necrosis: step-up (drainage → endoscopic → surgery); Meropenem (penetrates necrosis)
  • Liver abscess: drainage + antibiotics 2–4w; Klebsiella → metastatic screening (eye, brain)
10 Common Viva Questions
  1. What is the difference between primary, secondary, and tertiary peritonitis? Primary = SBP (no surgical source); Secondary = GI perforation; Tertiary = recurrent after adequate source control.
  2. What is the most important treatment for secondary peritonitis? Source control (surgery/drainage) — antibiotics alone fail.
  3. Empirical antibiotics for community-acquired secondary peritonitis? Pip-tazo 4.5g 6h (covers Gram-neg, anaerobes, some Enterococcus).
  4. When do you add Enterococcus coverage? Prosthetic material, severe sepsis, immunosuppression, valvular disease, prior Enterococcus, tertiary peritonitis.
  5. When do you add antifungal coverage? Recent broad abx, TPN, immunosuppression, tertiary peritonitis, gastric/oesophageal leak, prolonged ICU.
  6. What is the recommended duration after adequate source control? 4 days fixed (STOP-IT trial).
  7. SBP diagnosis? Ascitic fluid PMN ≥250/mm³.
  8. SBP treatment? Cefotaxime 2g IV 8h ×5d + Albumin 1.5g/kg D1 + 1g/kg D3.
  9. Infected pancreatic necrosis management? Step-up: percutaneous drainage → endoscopic necrosectomy → minimally invasive surgery.
  10. Klebsiella liver abscess complication? Metastatic infection (endophthalmitis, meningitis, brain abscess) — ophthalmology review.
