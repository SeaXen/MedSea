# Short stature and growth disorders

---

## Definition

Short stature is defined as height <2nd centile for age and sex, or >2 SD below the mean, or a height centile substantially below mid-parental height. Causes include familial short stature, constitutional delay of growth and puberty, endocrine (GH deficiency, hypothyroidism, Cushing), chronic disease (coeliac, IBD, CKD, cardiac), chromosomal (Turner), and skeletal dysplasias. Evaluation includes detailed history, growth-chart plotting, bone age, parental heights, bloods (FBC, TFTs, IGF-1, coeliac serology, karyotype in girls), and imaging. GH therapy is indicated for proven GH deficiency and specific syndromes (Turner, Prader-Willi, chronic renal failure, SGA without catch-up).

## Core Clinical Content

### Definition
Height <2nd centile or >2 SD below mean

### Most common causes
Familial short stature + constitutional delay (normal variants)

### Pathological causes
Endocrine (GH def, hypothyroid, Cushing), chronic disease, Turner, skeletal dysplasia

### Mid-parental height
Boys: (dad+ mum +13)/2 +/- 8.5 cm; Girls: (mum + dad -13)/2 +/- 8.5 cm

### Bone age
Delayed in CDGP, GH deficiency, hypothyroid; normal in familial

### Investigations
FBC, ferritin, TFTs, IGF-1, coeliac serology, karyotype (girls), urinary organic acids

### GH stimulation test
Clonidine, glucagon or insulin tolerance test; peak GH <7 ug/L = deficiency

### GH therapy
Daily SC injection; indicated in GH def, Turner, Prader-Willi, SGA, CRF

### Side effects GH
Idiopathic intracranial hypertension, slipped epiphyses, hyperglycaemia

### Tall stature
Causes: familial, Klinefelter, Marfan, Sotos; consider if >2 SD above mean

**Q3. How is Short stature and growth disorders recognised clinically?**
> Presentation and key clinical features include: Mid-parental height - Boys: (dad+ mum +13)/2 +/- 8.5 cm; Girls: (mum + dad -13)/2 +/- 8.5 cm; Bone age - Delayed in CDGP, GH deficiency, hypothyroid; normal in familial. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q4. What investigations are required for Short stature and growth disorders?**
> Investigations include: Investigations - FBC, ferritin, TFTs, IGF-1, coeliac serology, karyotype (girls), urinary organic acids; GH stimulation test - Clonidine, glucagon or insulin tolerance test; peak GH <7 ug/L = deficiency. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q5. What is the first-line management of Short stature and growth disorders?**
> First-line management: GH therapy - Daily SC injection; indicated in GH def, Turner, Prader-Willi, SGA, CRF; Side effects GH - Idiopathic intracranial hypertension, slipped epiphyses, hyperglycaemia. Refer to specialist adolescent services for definitive management where indicated.

**Q6. What are the key complications or red flags of Short stature and growth disorders?**
> Red flags and complications: Tall stature - Causes: familial, Klinefelter, Marfan, Sotos; consider if >2 SD above mean. Always consider safeguarding, mental health comorbidity, and transition planning in adolescent presentations.

## Confusions & Mnemonics

- **Mnemonic for Short stature and growth disorders** - see the FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table above for the 10 high-yield facts
- **'HEEADSSS' assessment** - Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression, Safety (3.0: + Eating, Sleep, Screen) - always use in adolescent history
- **'Gillick competence'** - under-16 with sufficient understanding to make own medical decisions
- **'Fraser guidelines'** - specific criteria for contraceptive/sexual health advice without parental knowledge
- **'HEADSS' / 'SSHADESS'** - alternative psychosocial assessment frameworks (Strengths-based)
