# Contraception in adolescents

---

## Definition

Contraception in adolescents requires confidential, individualised counselling covering efficacy, side effects, reversibility, and STI prevention. Long-acting reversible contraception (LARC) - IUS, IUD, implant - is first-line for most adolescents due to highest efficacy and adherence. Combined oral contraception (COC) is widely used; progestogen-only pill (POP) is suitable when oestrogen contraindicated. Emergency contraception (copper IUD most effective; ulipristal/levonorgestrel) within 5 days. Condoms are essential for STI prevention. UK: under-13 sexual activity mandates safeguarding referral; 13-15 assessed case-by-case.

## Core Clinical Content

### LARC first-line
IUS (Mirena), IUD, implant (Nexplanon) - efficacy >99%

### Implanon/Nexplanon
Subdermal etonogestrel; 3 years; amenorrhoea common

### IUS Mirena
Levonorgestrel-releasing; 5 years; reduced bleeding, dysmenorrhoea

### COC
21 days + 7-day break; missed pill rules; VTE risk, weight, mood effects

### POP
Cerazette (desogestrel) - 12-hr window; traditional POPs 3-hr window

### DMPA (depot)
3-monthly injection; reduced BMD with prolonged use; weigh benefit

### Emergency contraception
Copper IUD (most effective, 5 days), ulipristal (5 days), levonorgestrel (3 days)

### Condoms
Essential for STI prevention; dual method with hormonal ideal

### Fraser guidelines
Under-16 contraception without parental knowledge if mature minor

### Sexual activity <13
Mandatory safeguarding referral

**Q2. What are the most important FCPS/MRCP high-yield points for Contraception in adolescents?**

**Q3. How is Contraception in adolescents recognised clinically?**
> Presentation and key clinical features include: COC - 21 days + 7-day break; missed pill rules; VTE risk, weight, mood effects; POP - Cerazette (desogestrel) - 12-hr window; traditional POPs 3-hr window. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q4. What investigations are required for Contraception in adolescents?**
> Investigations include: DMPA (depot) - 3-monthly injection; reduced BMD with prolonged use; weigh benefit; Emergency contraception - Copper IUD (most effective, 5 days), ulipristal (5 days), levonorgestrel (3 days). See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q5. What is the first-line management of Contraception in adolescents?**
> First-line management: Condoms - Essential for STI prevention; dual method with hormonal ideal; Fraser guidelines - Under-16 contraception without parental knowledge if mature minor. Refer to specialist adolescent services for definitive management where indicated.

**Q6. What are the key complications or red flags of Contraception in adolescents?**
> Red flags and complications: Sexual activity <13 - Mandatory safeguarding referral. Always consider safeguarding, mental health comorbidity, and transition planning in adolescent presentations.

## Confusions & Mnemonics

- **Mnemonic for Contraception in adolescents** - see the FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table above for the 10 high-yield facts
- **'HEEADSSS' assessment** - Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression, Safety (3.0: + Eating, Sleep, Screen) - always use in adolescent history
- **'Gillick competence'** - under-16 with sufficient understanding to make own medical decisions
- **'Fraser guidelines'** - specific criteria for contraceptive/sexual health advice without parental knowledge
- **'HEADSS' / 'SSHADESS'** - alternative psychosocial assessment frameworks (Strengths-based)
