# Adolescent development and puberty

---

## Definition

Adolescence is the developmental transition between childhood and adulthood, typically 10-19 years (WHO), characterised by rapid physical, cognitive, psychosocial and sexual maturation driven by puberty. Puberty is the biological process of sexual maturation (adrenarche then gonadarche) producing adult reproductive capacity; it typically begins 8-13 in girls and 9-14 in boys, lasting 2-5 years. Adolescents require a distinct clinical approach because biological, psychological and social development are asynchronous, risk-taking peaks mid-adolescence, and chronic disease/adherence/transition issues cluster in this age group.

## Core Clinical Content

### WHO definition
10-19 years (early 10-14, mid 15-17, late 18-19)

### Puberty timing
Girls 8-13, Boys 9-14 (2-5 years duration)

### Tanner staging
5 stages for breast (B), pubic hair (PH), genital (G), testicular volume (ml)

### Adrenarche
Adrenal androgen rise ~6-8 yrs; pubarche (pubic hair) often first sign

### Gonadarche
HPG axis activation; first sign is testicular enlargement >=4 mL in boys, thelarche in girls

### Growth spurt
Girls peak height velocity 11-12 yrs; boys 13-14 yrs; ~20-25% final height

### Brain development
Prefrontal cortex matures into mid-20s; limbic system earlier -> risk-taking

### Psychosocial tasks
Identity, autonomy, peer relationships, sexual identity, education/vocation

### Identity vs role confusion
Erikson: 5th stage of psychosocial development

### Risk behaviour window
Mid-adolescence (15-17 yrs) is the peak for risk-taking, novelty-seeking

**Q2. What are the most important FCPS/MRCP high-yield points for Adolescent development and puberty?**
> WHO definition: 10-19 years (early 10-14, mid 15-17, late 18-19). Puberty timing: Girls 8-13, Boys 9-14 (2-5 years duration). Tanner staging: 5 stages for breast (B), pubic hair (PH), genital (G), testicular volume (ml). See FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table below.

**Q3. How is Adolescent development and puberty recognised clinically?**
> Presentation and key clinical features include: Adrenarche - Adrenal androgen rise ~6-8 yrs; pubarche (pubic hair) often first sign; Gonadarche - HPG axis activation; first sign is testicular enlargement >=4 mL in boys, thelarche in girls. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q4. What investigations are required for Adolescent development and puberty?**
> Investigations include: Growth spurt - Girls peak height velocity 11-12 yrs; boys 13-14 yrs; ~20-25% final height; Brain development - Prefrontal cortex matures into mid-20s; limbic system earlier -> risk-taking. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q5. What is the first-line management of Adolescent development and puberty?**
> First-line management: Psychosocial tasks - Identity, autonomy, peer relationships, sexual identity, education/vocation; Identity vs role confusion - Erikson: 5th stage of psychosocial development. Refer to specialist adolescent services for definitive management where indicated.

**Q6. What are the key complications or red flags of Adolescent development and puberty?**
> Red flags and complications: Risk behaviour window - Mid-adolescence (15-17 yrs) is the peak for risk-taking, novelty-seeking. Always consider safeguarding, mental health comorbidity, and transition planning in adolescent presentations.

## Confusions & Mnemonics

- **Mnemonic for Adolescent development and puberty** - see the FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table above for the 10 high-yield facts
- **'HEEADSSS' assessment** - Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression, Safety (3.0: + Eating, Sleep, Screen) - always use in adolescent history
- **'Gillick competence'** - under-16 with sufficient understanding to make own medical decisions
- **'Fraser guidelines'** - specific criteria for contraceptive/sexual health advice without parental knowledge
- **'HEADSS' / 'SSHADESS'** - alternative psychosocial assessment frameworks (Strengths-based)
