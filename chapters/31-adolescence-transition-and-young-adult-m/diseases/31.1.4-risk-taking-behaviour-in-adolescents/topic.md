# Risk-taking behaviour in adolescents

---

## Definition

Risk-taking behaviour in adolescents includes substance use, unsafe sexual activity, dangerous driving, violence, self-harm, and disordered eating. It peaks in mid-adolescence (15-17) and is driven by the asynchronous maturation of the socio-emotional (limbic) system, which is reward-sensitive, and the cognitive control (prefrontal) system, which matures into the mid-20s. Most risk-taking is exploratory and developmentally normal; persistent risk-taking or harm-promoting risk-taking signals distress. Anticipatory guidance, motivational interviewing, and harm-reduction are evidence-based approaches; punitive responses worsen outcomes.

## Core Clinical Content

### Peak age
Mid-adolescence 15-17 years; declines by 20-25

### Dual systems model
Limbic (reward) matures early; prefrontal (control) matures late

### Sensation-seeking
Normal developmental drive; novelty and peer influence amplify it

### Common risks
Alcohol, cannabis, nicotine/vaping, unsafe sex, dangerous driving, fighting, self-harm

### Protective factors
Connected family, school engagement, <20 hr/wk work, religious/community ties

### Risk factors
Adverse childhood events, mental illness, peer substance use, family disruption

### Anticipatory guidance
Discuss before risk occurs; not as a single lecture but ongoing

### Motivational interviewing
Open questions, reflective listening, elicit change talk

### Harm reduction
Safer-use advice, naloxone for opioids, contraception access, designated driver

### Red flags
Risk-taking + mood change, school decline, social withdrawal, sleep change
