# Acne in adolescents

---

## Definition

Acne vulgaris is the commonest skin disease in adolescents, affecting 80-90% of teenagers, with significant psychological impact (depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, social withdrawal). Pathogenesis: follicular hyperkeratinisation, sebum production, Cutibacterium acnes, inflammation. Comedonal, inflammatory, and nodulocystic forms. Treatment is severity-based: mild (topical retinoids, benzoyl peroxide), moderate (add topical/oral antibiotics - doxycycline, lymecycline), severe (isotretinoin, only under specialist supervision with pregnancy prevention programme). Hormonal therapy (OCP, spironolactone) in females.

## Core Clinical Content

### Prevalence
80-90% of adolescents; peak 14-17 yrs (girls) / 16-19 yrs (boys)

### Pathogenesis
Follicular hyperkeratinisation, increased sebum, C. acnes, inflammation

### Severity grading
Mild (comedonal), moderate (inflammatory), severe (nodulocystic)

### Psychological impact
Depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, social withdrawal, suicidality

### Mild treatment
Topical retinoid (adapalene) +/- benzoyl peroxide (BPO)

### Moderate treatment
Add topical/oral antibiotic (doxycycline 100 mg, lymecycline 408 mg, 3 months)

### Severe treatment
Oral isotretinoin (0.5-1 mg/kg) - 16-24 weeks; cumulative 120-150 mg/kg

### Isotretinoin monitoring
LFTs, lipids, mood; pregnancy prevention (PPP - iPLEDGE in US)

### Hormonal therapy
Combined OCP (with cyproterone/levonorgestrel), spironolactone (off-label)

### Scarring
Treat early; isotretinoin for scarring acne regardless of severity

**Q2. What are the most important FCPS/MRCP high-yield points for Acne in adolescents?**
> Prevalence: 80-90% of adolescents; peak 14-17 yrs (girls) / 16-19 yrs (boys). Pathogenesis: Follicular hyperkeratinisation, increased sebum, C. acnes, inflammation. Severity grading: Mild (comedonal), moderate (inflammatory), severe (nodulocystic). See FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table below.

**Q3. How is Acne in adolescents recognised clinically?**
> Presentation and key clinical features include: Psychological impact - Depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, social withdrawal, suicidality; Mild treatment - Topical retinoid (adapalene) +/- benzoyl peroxide (BPO). See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q4. What investigations are required for Acne in adolescents?**
> Investigations include: Moderate treatment - Add topical/oral antibiotic (doxycycline 100 mg, lymecycline 408 mg, 3 months); Severe treatment - Oral isotretinoin (0.5-1 mg/kg) - 16-24 weeks; cumulative 120-150 mg/kg. See Core Clinical Content for full details.

**Q5. What is the first-line management of Acne in adolescents?**
> First-line management: Isotretinoin monitoring - LFTs, lipids, mood; pregnancy prevention (PPP - iPLEDGE in US); Hormonal therapy - Combined OCP (with cyproterone/levonorgestrel), spironolactone (off-label). Refer to specialist adolescent services for definitive management where indicated.

**Q6. What are the key complications or red flags of Acne in adolescents?**
> Red flags and complications: Scarring - Treat early; isotretinoin for scarring acne regardless of severity. Always consider safeguarding, mental health comorbidity, and transition planning in adolescent presentations.

- **Mnemonic for Acne in adolescents** - see the FCPS/MRCP High-Yield Summary table above for the 10 high-yield facts
- **'HEEADSSS' assessment** - Home, Education, Activities, Drugs, Sexuality, Suicide/depression, Safety (3.0: + Eating, Sleep, Screen) - always use in adolescent history
- **'Gillick competence'** - under-16 with sufficient understanding to make own medical decisions
- **'Fraser guidelines'** - specific criteria for contraceptive/sexual health advice without parental knowledge
- **'HEADSS' / 'SSHADESS'** - alternative psychosocial assessment frameworks (Strengths-based)
