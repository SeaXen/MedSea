# Medically unexplained symptoms and somatisation in adolescents

---

## Definition

Medically unexplained symptoms (MUS) or somatisation are physical complaints without identifiable organic disease, common in adolescents (10-30% of presentations). Presentations include recurrent abdominal pain, headache, fatigue, chest pain, and limb pain. They often reflect distress, anxiety, or psychosocial difficulty. Diagnosis is positive (based on clinical features) rather than by exclusion. Management is multidisciplinary with CBT, family therapy, school engagement, and judicious medical investigation. Red flags: weight loss, night symptoms, abnormal signs. Avoid excessive investigation that reinforces illness behaviour.

## Core Clinical Content

### Prevalence
10-30% of adolescent medical presentations; F > M after puberty

### Common presentations
Recurrent abdominal pain, headache, chest pain, limb pain, fatigue

### Terminology
Somatic symptom disorder (DSM-5), somatoform autonomic dysfunction, functional

### Risk factors
Anxiety, depression, family history, recent illness, school stress, ACEs

### Maintaining factors
Avoidance, parental anxiety, school absence, secondary gain

### Positive diagnosis
Symptoms inconsistent with known physiology; normal exam; no red flags

### Red flags
Weight loss, night sweats, abnormal signs, focal neuro deficit, growth failure

### Management
Validate symptoms; explain mechanism; CBT; family therapy; school reintegration

### Avoid
Repeated normal investigations; 'there is nothing wrong'; dismissive reassurance

### Prognosis
60-80% improve with appropriate intervention; risk of chronicity if mismanaged
